# Mīmāṃsā under the symbolism-shift reading

## A perspective layer, explicitly flagged

### Reading discipline

This is an interpretive reading. The school's own self-understanding — Jaimini's *Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra*, Śabara's *Bhāṣya*, the Bhāṭṭa and Prābhākara elaborations — is preserved in the timeline entries on Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, and Prabhākara Miśra, and in the engagement file on Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā. What is offered here is something different in kind: an account of what the corpus's interpretive perspective implies about the tradition, addressed to a contemporary reader who is encountering Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā from outside the symbol-environment in which the rite was originally lived. The school itself does not speak in this register, and may very well disagree with the framing on offer. The disagreement is anticipated and named below in §6.

The point of marking this as a perspective layer is not modesty for its own sake. It is that two things are easy to confuse. One is the philosophical reconstruction of what a school says about itself, on its own categories, with arguments the school recognises. The other is the comparative-philosophical reading of what the school looks like from outside, in vocabulary the school did not generate, addressed to a reader the school did not have in view. Both kinds of reading are legitimate; they do different work; they should not be presented as if they were the same kind of operation. The timeline entries present the first. This perspective layer presents the second, and does so under explicit notice.

### Where the school's voice is

Throughout, where the school's self-articulation is engaged — the reading of *codanā* as the autonomous *pramāṇa* for *dharma* ([*Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra* 1.1.2](cite://jaimini/mimamsa-sutra/1.1.2) with Śabara's *Bhāṣya* ad loc.), the *bhāvanā* analysis of injunctive force, the *adhikāra* doctrine, the *apūrva* category, the *aṅga-aṅgi-vibhāga* — the engagement is conducted in the school's terms, with citations to primary text where the corpus has them on disk, and with the school's coherence taken as the starting point. Where the perspective layer goes beyond what the school says about itself, the move is marked with phrases like "on the reading taken here," "the perspective offered here," or "from outside the original symbol-environment." The reader can therefore see, at every step, where the school is being reported and where the perspective is being articulated.

## §1 — Five claims that structure the reading

The perspective taken here is summarised in five claims. They are stated up front so that the reader can see, before any argumentation, what the layer commits to and what it does not.

First, **Mīmāṃsā worked, on its own terms, for the practitioners who inhabited the original cultural matrix.** The school is coherent. Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, and Prabhākara Miśra articulated a practice-philosophy whose technical apparatus does the work the school takes it to do. The textual claim is straightforward and is engaged in the timeline entries on those four figures. There is no internal incoherence and no need for an external supplement to make the school's own categories operate. The perspective layer accepts this and does not contest it.

Second, **the rite's lived register depended on the symbol-environment**. The matrix in which the Vedic vocabulary — *Agni*, *Soma*, *Vāc*, *go*, *aśva*, the *sapta sindhavaḥ* — carried both an external referential force and an inner correlate was the public-cultural fact of how the words were used in the daily life of the community that performed the rite. The kindled fire on the *gārhapatya* hearth and the inner aspiration the tradition associates with *Agni* were one utterance, not two utterances in need of bridging. The two registers were held together not by an interpretive operation performed by the practitioner, but by the structure of the symbol-environment itself.

Third, **the symbol-environment changed across centuries, by ordinary cultural-historical processes**. New ritual idioms layered over the older one. *Bhakti* registers grew from local cult into pan-Indian religious form; Tantric ritual systems built new inner-yogic correspondences in new vocabulary; *Purāṇic* mythography re-routed the deities of Vedic ritual through new narrative substrates. The lived associations between *Agni* and inner aspiration, between *Soma* and *ānanda*, between *go* and light, attenuated for the wider cultivated Hindu life — not as a deliberate displacement, but as the slow re-saturation of the religious vocabulary by new symbolic substrates over many generations. The change is structural-cultural, of the kind *Religionsgeschichte* studies, not personal-moral.

Fourth, **for participants outside the original substrate, the rite no longer carries the inner register the symbolism originally bore — without the operations of the rite themselves having changed**. The *codanā* still prompts. The *bhāvanā* still structures the volitional re-orientation under the verbal injunction. The *apūrva* still names the persistent residue. What is no longer in place is the substrate that, in the original context, made these operations carry their inner-yogic correlates immediately, without intervening interpretation. The school as a *philosophy* of action, language, and persistence keeps working. What stops working is the *immediate spiritual-symbolic carrier* of the rite for participants who lack the symbol-environment.

Fifth, **the school-internal "decline" narrative misdiagnoses what changed**. The traditional internal Hindu narrative often attributes the eclipse of *karma-kāṇḍa* to Brahmin moral corruption, the loss of qualified ritual performers, the decay of Sanskrit fluency, or the moral deterioration intrinsic to *kali-yuga*. Each of these locates the cause of structural-cultural change in personal-moral or competence-based factors, and runs the causal arrow backwards. The perspective taken here rejects that translation. *Yuga*-discourse is a theological cosmology with its own framework; it is not a historiography of ritual systems. To attribute structural-cultural change to moral failure is a category mistake.

These five claims are the perspective. They are not claims the Mīmāṃsakas themselves make in their own categories, and they are not what the timeline entry on Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā argues. They are what the corpus's interpretive perspective implies about how the school looks when read from outside the original symbol-environment.

## §2 — What is conceded to the school's self-understanding

Before the interpretive layer is developed, three concessions are made to the school's own categories. Each rules out a misreading the perspective taken here might invite if it were not stated explicitly.

First, **the rite is not symbolism in the sense of being merely-symbolic**. The Mīmāṃsakas refuse the *nirvacana* tradition's psychological-symbolic decoding of ritual terms; their refusal is principled. Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* on Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika* (on disk in Sanskrit in the project corpus) carries arguments to the effect that ritual terms are not stand-ins for inner states but operate as the public-procedural specifications they appear to be. The perspective offered here does not propose that the Mīmāṃsakas were mistaken about this. It accepts that, on the school's own categories, the rite is what the school says it is: a publicly-specifiable operation involving these substances, these formulas, these qualified performers. The "symbol-environment" thesis is not a thesis about what the rite *is* on the school's categories. It is a thesis about the lived substrate in which the publicly-specifiable rite carried, additionally, its inner-yogic correlate for those who performed it.

Second, **Mīmāṃsā does not claim to produce *mokṣa* in the Advaita sense**. The school's soteriological economy is its own. Śabara on [*Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra* 1.1.2](cite://jaimini/mimamsa-sutra/1.1.2) articulates the operation as one in which the *artha* indicated by *codanā* "conjoins the *puruṣa* with the highest good" (*niḥśreyasa*) — and the school's *niḥśreyasa* is not the *kaivalya* of Advaita's later articulation. The perspective taken here does not import an Advaita soteriology and then judge Mīmāṃsā by it. The Sureśvara critique of *karma* in [*Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* 1.67](cite://sureshvara/naishkarmya-siddhi/1.67) is intra-Vedāntic; it engages Mīmāṃsā as a rival theoretical position about how action figures in the path to *niḥśreyasa* / *mokṣa*, but does not show that Mīmāṃsā has stopped working on its own register.

Third, **the school articulates an interior dimension internal to its own self-understanding**. The reflective-introspective reading associated with Francis X. Clooney, S.J. — that the *karmin* is constitutively a thinking subject and that the school's apparatus articulates the cognitive-volitional structure of how a verbal injunction operates on a qualified agent — is the most defensible English-language reading of the school currently available. **Honest scope on Clooney 1990 and Clooney 2022.** Neither Clooney's *Thinking Ritually* (1990) nor the 2022 encyclopedia entry "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy" is on disk in the project's corpus; engagement here proceeds via the title-thesis (which is reported across the secondary literature without controversy), published abstracts and reviews, and arguments standardly summarised in scholarly discussion of Clooney's work, not via page-level direct quotation. Within those limits, Clooney's reading suffices to establish the proposition that Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā is a coherent reflective practice-philosophy whose interior dimension belongs to the school's own self-understanding.

These three concessions are not throat-clearing. They are the work the perspective layer cannot do for itself. Without them the interpretive reading would either (a) collapse the rite into a *nirvacana*-style decoding the Mīmāṃsakas explicitly reject, (b) judge Mīmāṃsā by a soteriological standard external to it, or (c) treat the school as a thoughtless ritualism awaiting external rescue. None of those moves is on offer here.

## §3 — What the symbol-environment thesis claims, and what it does not

The phrase "symbol-environment" carries the load. It is worth stating precisely what the perspective taken here means by it, and what it does not.

A symbol-environment is the matrix of lived public-cultural usage in which a given symbol-system carries its referential force. It is not a doctrine, not a private mental state, and not a body of esoteric knowledge transmitted to initiates. It is the public fact that, in a given community at a given period, certain symbols carry certain meanings as part of the daily practice of speaking, eating, kindling fire, marrying, dying, naming children, addressing elders. The symbols and the meanings are held together by repeated use, by embedded ritual, by shared narrative, and by the simple transmissive force of one generation handing the system to the next.

For the early Vedic practitioner, on the reading taken here, *Agni* was at once the literal fire on the hearth and the seer-will the contemporary Aurobindonian reading decodes; *Soma* was at once the pressed plant juice and the *ānanda*-current; *go* was at once the cow yielding milk and the light streaming into the chamber of mind; *aśva* was at once the horse drawing the chariot and the embodied energy. The two registers were not held together by a learned interpretive operation; they were held together by the structure of the symbol-environment. To say "*Agni*" was, in that environment, to say both at once, without an act of decoding intervening.

Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* (CWSA Vol. 15, on disk in the project corpus at `sources/aurobindo_mother/D_secret_of_veda_deepmine.md`) is the canonical contemporary articulation of how the Vedic vocabulary did this work. The "double language" thesis (CWSA 15, pp. 8–9) says that the Veda's expressions are at once outer-referential (the fire, the cow, the horse, the rivers) and inner-referential (the seer-will, the light, the embodied energy, the streams of inspiration), and that the Vedic *ṛṣis* spoke in a register where the two were one utterance. The thesis describes a feature of the symbol-environment, not a private code.

The perspective taken here extends this Aurobindonian reading from the Veda back into Mīmāṃsā as the school of *karma-kāṇḍa*. Mīmāṃsā externalises the rite — articulates it as a determinate, publicly specifiable operation. The externalisation is not a flattening. Under the conditions of the original symbol-environment, the externally-specifiable operation carried its inner correlates by the structure of the environment itself; explicit decoding would have been redundant, possibly distorting. The Mīmāṃsakas' insistence on the literal-ritual reading was, on this account, a faithfulness to the integrity of a practice whose inner register was already carried in lived practice.

What the thesis **does not claim**:

1. It does not claim that the rite, on the school's own self-understanding, has an inner-yogic register that the school articulates and the perspective recovers. The school does not so articulate, and would on its own categories resist such an articulation.
2. It does not claim that classical Mīmāṃsakas were practising a hidden inner yoga under cover of ritual procedure. They were practising the rite the school describes, in the symbol-environment in which they lived; the inner correlate is, on this perspective, a feature of that environment rather than an esoteric doctrine the school transmits.
3. It does not claim that the rite has stopped working in any universal sense. There remain communities — *śrauta* lineages, *Veda-pāṭhaśālā*-trained Brahmins, traditional ritualist circles — for whom substantial portions of the original symbol-environment remain lived, and for whom the rite continues to operate as the Mīmāṃsakas describe.

## §4 — How the symbol-environment changes

Symbol-environments change by ordinary historical processes. The change is not produced by sudden moral catastrophe, by deliberate corruption of a tradition, or by the moral failure of any particular generation of practitioners. It is the slow re-saturation and de-saturation of religious symbols under the pressure of new linguistic registers, new ritual forms, new political configurations, new economic substrates, new patterns of devotional practice. *Religionsgeschichte* studies these processes as its standard subject matter.

Three concrete dynamics, in the Indian case, are worth naming. The naming follows the conceptual vocabulary of standard scholarly discussion; finer-grained empirical reconstruction of the Vedic-symbol-environment shift across the relevant centuries is a project for further work and is not undertaken here.

First, **the rise of devotional registers**. From the centuries leading up to the early-medieval period, *bhakti* registers grew from local cult-practice into pan-Indian religious form. *Pūjā* in the iconic register, *darśana* of the deity-image, *kīrtana* and *bhajana* established themselves as the lived religious substrate of a wider population than ever participated in *śrauta* ritual. The Vedic vocabulary remained in liturgical use, but the lived associations migrated: *Agni* became one of many deities in a much larger pantheon, no longer the centre of daily ritual life for the cultivated Hindu.

Second, **the rise of Tantric ritual systems**. Alexis Sanderson's mapping of the absorption of the Vedic ritual idiom into Śaiva, Vaiṣṇava, and Śākta Tantric systems through the early-medieval period documents the layering. Tantric ritual builds new symbol-environments — *cakras*, *mantras* with new theologies of efficacy, new initiatory structures, new inner-yogic correspondences — that re-articulate the inner register of ritual in a substantially different vocabulary. **Honest scope on Sanderson.** Sanderson's body of work is not on disk in the project corpus; the conceptual frame of Vedic-Tantric layering is invoked at the level of standard scholarly summary, not page-level engagement. Within those limits, the layering is widely reported across the secondary literature.

Third, **the antiquarisation of the Vedic substrate**. By the early-modern and modern periods, the Vedic ritual idiom is preserved by specialist communities but is no longer the substrate of cultivated Hindu life. The language itself becomes a learned register, accessible by training rather than native immersion. By the twenty-first century, even the inheritors of the tradition rarely have, as their daily lived substrate, the symbol-environment in which *Agni* is at once hearth-fire and seer-will. The substrate has shifted.

The shift is diachronic and uneven. There were practitioners in the eleventh and twelfth centuries who still inhabited substantial portions of the original symbol-environment, alongside others for whom it had already become antiquarian. By the time of Maṇḍana and Sureśvara, the philosophical conversation about *karma* and *jñāna* is happening primarily among interlocutors for whom the *śrauta* substrate is one inheritance among several rather than the primary lived register. By the time of the early-modern commentators, the *śrauta* substrate is largely the inheritance of specialist communities. The unevenness matters: the perspective taken here does not predict that the rite has stopped working for everyone, only that the proportion of participants who inhabit the substrate has shifted across centuries.

## §5 — What this implies for reading Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila, Prabhākara

The four Mīmāṃsaka thinker entries in the timeline present the school on its own terms. The perspective layer offered here adds a second register, addressed to the contemporary reader who is encountering those entries from outside the original substrate.

**On Jaimini.** The opening *sūtra* — *athāto dharma-jijñāsā* ([*Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra* 1.1.1](cite://jaimini/mimamsa-sutra/1.1.1)) — declares the school as *jijñāsā*, inquiry. The school is, by its own opening word, a school of *thinking* about what *dharma* requires. The timeline entry on Jaimini presents this in the school's own register. The perspective layer adds: for the reader who inhabits the original symbol-environment, the inquiry into *dharma* is also an inquiry conducted under the lived weight of the Vedic vocabulary's dual reference; for the contemporary reader, the same *sūtra* opens an inquiry that needs an additional interpretive step before its inner-yogic correlate becomes available. The *sūtra*'s philosophical content is not changed by the addition; what is added is an account of why the contemporary reader can find the *sūtra* both fully analysable on the school's terms and yet not immediately spiritually weighted in the way the original substrate carried.

**On Śabara.** Śabara's *Bhāṣya* on [*Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra* 1.1.2](cite://jaimini/mimamsa-sutra/1.1.2) carries the *codanā-lakṣaṇo'rtho dharmaḥ* — "*dharma* is what is characterised by the *codanā*" — and the *Bhāṣya*'s gloss *kriyāyāḥ pravartakaṃ vacanam*, "the speech that prompts to action." The further gloss — *tayā yo lakṣyate, so 'rthaḥ puruṣaṃ niḥśreyasena saṃyunaktīti pratijānīmahe*, "what is indicated by it, *that* is the *artha*; we declare that this *artha* conjoins the *puruṣa* with the highest good (*niḥśreyasa*)" — defines the *artha* of *codanā* in a register that is constitutively agent-binding rather than world-describing. The on-disk Sanskrit is at `materials/primary_texts/sanskrit/comparator/shabara_mimamsa_sutra_bhasya.txt`, lines 8–9. The school-internal reading takes the *artha* as the *yāga* itself — the rite as the meaning of the prescriptive sentence. The perspective layer adds: for the reader inhabiting the original symbol-environment, the *codanā*'s prompting carries with it the inner re-orientation the symbol-environment supplies; for the reader outside that substrate, the prompting still operates on its own register but does not bring the inner correlate without an additional step. Aurobindo's hermeneutic, on this perspective, is what supplies the additional step for the contemporary reader.

**On Kumārila Bhaṭṭa.** Kumārila's elaboration of the *bhāvanā* doctrine in the *Tantra-Vārttika*, with the *śābdī bhāvanā* and *ārthī bhāvanā* pair, articulates the school's account of the cognitive-volitional core of the rite. The *śābdī bhāvanā* is the action of the optative suffix on the hearer's intention; the *ārthī bhāvanā* is the agent's volitional energy directed toward the prescribed result. The school's reading takes both halves as interior, both as constitutive of how the rite operates on a competent agent. The perspective layer adds: under the original symbol-environment, the *ārthī bhāvanā* directs the agent's volitional energy toward a prescribed result whose inner-yogic correlate is carried by the substrate; under contemporary conditions, the volitional energy still organises around the prescribed result, but the inner correlate must be made visible by interpretive amplification rather than by lived substrate. The doctrine itself is not modified by the perspective; the perspective accounts for why the same doctrine reads differently for differently-substrated readers.

**On Prabhākara Miśra.** Prabhākara's *Bṛhatī* and the Prābhākara analysis of the imperative — the *kāryatā* doctrine, on which what the optative form prescribes is *kārya*, that-which-is-to-be-done, a category sui generis that does not reduce to belief about anything else — articulate the school's strongest claim that the imperative is an irreducible primitive of cognition. The school-internal reading is that *kāryatā* is the proper category for analysing how injunctive language binds an agent. The perspective layer adds: for the agent inhabiting the original symbol-environment, what is enjoined is *kārya* in a sense whose inner-yogic correlate is supplied by the lived substrate; for the contemporary agent, *kāryatā* still names the irreducible imperative force, but the inner correlate is no longer immediately carried. The Prābhākara analysis remains valid as an analysis of how injunctions work; the perspective accounts for why the same analysis reads differently for differently-substrated readers.

In each of the four cases, the school's own categories are presented intact, and the perspective layer is added as a second-order observation about how a contemporary reader can hold the school's categories together with the corpus's own interpretive perspective. The school's analyses are not corrected. The perspective is not attributed to the school. The two registers are kept apart so that a reader wanting only the philosophical engagement gets it from the school's categories and the timeline entries; a reader wanting also the contemporary-reader amplification gets the additional layer this perspective adds.

## §6 — Where the school may very well disagree

The perspective taken here anticipates four places the school's own self-understanding would resist the framing on offer.

**First, the anti-*nirvacana* polemic.** Classical Mīmāṃsā explicitly rejects psychological-symbolic decoding of ritual terms; Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* carries arguments to that effect. The perspective layer accepts that Aurobindo's hermeneutic is *not* what classical Mīmāṃsā would endorse. The hermeneutic is a contemporary-reader stance, available to a reader outside the original symbol-environment, that the Mīmāṃsakas would on their own self-understanding reject. The reader who finds this an unacceptable interpretive liberty has a real ground for refusing the perspective.

**Second, the principled refusal to make the rite about anything other than the rite.** A central commitment of Mīmāṃsā — perhaps the central commitment — is that *codanā* is autonomous; the rite's authority does not derive from anything outside the *codanā*'s own operation. To read the rite as carrying an inner-yogic correlate, even via a symbol-environment, can look from the school's vantage like an attempt to ground the *codanā* in something the school takes care to refuse to ground it in. The perspective taken here does not propose to ground the *codanā*; the inner correlate is offered as a feature of the lived substrate, not as the source of the *codanā*'s authority. But the school could still object that even the feature-of-substrate account smuggles in an external register the school is principled to refuse. The objection has bite. The perspective records it as an honest ground for disagreement.

**Third, the soteriological asymmetry.** The Mīmāṃsakas are the most disciplined refusers of *mokṣa* as a *puruṣārtha*; the school's *niḥśreyasa* is the highest good *codanā* conjoins the *puruṣa* with, and is not Advaita's *kaivalya*. The perspective taken here uses Aurobindo's hermeneutic, which is itself a soteriologically-committed framework. The Mīmāṃsakas would object that bringing Aurobindo's hermeneutic to bear on their analyses imports a soteriological economy they have, on principle, declined to commit to. The perspective accepts the objection and notes that the contemporary reader is not asked to import Aurobindo's soteriology; the hermeneutic is used to make visible the inner correlate the symbol-environment carried, not to relocate Mīmāṃsā within an Advaita-style soteriology.

**Fourth, the empirical underspecification.** The perspective claims that symbol-environments shift, layer, and re-form across centuries by ordinary cultural-historical processes, and gestures toward Eliade's vocabulary of saturation and de-saturation, Sanderson's mapping of Tantric layering, and Halbfass's treatment of cultural transformation (Halbfass, *Tradition and Reflection*, SUNY 1991, on-disk research file `sources/research/R5_halbfass.md`). It does not undertake a detailed historical reconstruction of the Vedic-symbol-environment shift. A historian of religions could legitimately ask for finer-grained empirical detail than the perspective provides. The perspective is, at the level of empirical historical detail, programmatic rather than fully worked out.

These four grounds for disagreement are honest. The perspective taken here is defensible as a structural-cultural reading addressed to a contemporary reader; it does not foreclose the school's right to refuse the framing on its own terms. The two registers — the school's own self-understanding and the contemporary-reader perspective — coexist without one displacing the other.

## §7 — Where to read further

For the school on its own terms, the timeline entries on Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, and Prabhākara Miśra are the place to begin. Each presents the relevant figure's contribution in the school's categories, with citations to primary text. The Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā engagement file (in the engagements stream) presents the school's coherence as a system of thought without the perspective-layer overlay.

For the symbol-environment thesis in fuller form, the article *Mīmāṃsā worked — the symbol-environment thesis and the contemporary reader's encounter* (slug `mimamsa-aurobindo-v4`) develops the position at length, with the seven-section structure on which the present perspective layer draws.

For the Aurobindonian reading on which the contemporary-reader hermeneutic depends, *The Secret of the Veda* (CWSA Vol. 15) is the principal source. The "double language" thesis (pp. 8–9) is the locus for the claim that the Vedic vocabulary carries dual reference as a feature of its symbol-environment. *The Synthesis of Yoga* (CWSA Vols. 23–24) extends the hermeneutic from the Vedic *saṃhitā* to the broader category of *yajña*, with the locus at I.IV "The Sacrifice, the Triune Path and the Lord of the Sacrifice" (CWSA 23, p. 89) and the triple-character analysis of works, love, and knowledge at I.V–VI (CWSA 23, p. 134).

For Clooney's reading of Mīmāṃsā as a coherent reflective practice-philosophy, the principal English-language source is *Thinking Ritually: Rediscovering the Pūrva Mīmāṃsā of Jaimini* (Publications of the De Nobili Research Library XVII, Vienna 1990); the 2022 sharpening is in "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy" in the Springer Encyclopedia of Indian Religions. **Honest scope: neither title is on disk in the project corpus; the engagement here proceeds via title-thesis, scholarly summary, and standardly-reported argument, not page-level direct quotation.**

## Closing note on register

The perspective offered here is not the school. The school is in the timeline entries and the engagement file. What is offered here is one interpretive reading of what the corpus's contemporary-reader-encounter framing implies about how the school looks from outside the original symbol-environment. The reading is defensible within its scope. It is not the only reading available. The Mīmāṃsakas may very well disagree with it; the disagreement is real and is recorded above. The reader is asked to keep the two layers apart and to use each for what it can do.
