## Editorial introduction

The doctrinal center of Sureśvara's *Taittirīya-Vārttika* lies later, in the *Brahmānanda-Vallī* discussions of *satyaṃ jñānam anantaṃ brahma*, the *ānandamaya-kośa*, and the graded sheath-analysis. If a readable primary Sanskrit witness were available on disk, that would be the obvious extended unit to choose. But the local archive does not permit that. Its English layer is extractable; its Sanskrit layer is not. The responsible course is therefore to take the earliest recoverable natural unit and to state the limitation plainly.

Verses 1-8 of the *Śīkṣā-Vallī* are not a random substitute. They are the work's threshold. The first three verses establish the authorial stance: salutation to Brahman, salutation to Śaṅkara, and declaration of exegetical purpose. Verses 4-8 then give the architectonic program that governs the whole work: the ritual portion has already done its work; the Upaniṣadic portion must now be treated separately; knowledge of Brahman alone destroys action together with its causes; desire is the immediate cause of bondage; ignorance is the prior cause of desire; and knowledge of the Self therefore terminates the pursuit of action. In compressed form, the whole anti-*jñāna-karma-samuccaya* orientation is already present here.

That is why this opening is the right fallback unit. It stands at the head of the work, it states the relation between *karma-kāṇḍa* and *vedānta*, and it anticipates themes Sureśvara elaborates elsewhere with far greater dialectical pressure, especially in the *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* (Book 1 on the impossibility of *samuccaya*; Book 2 on sentence-meaning; Book 4 on the dependence of *anātman* on *ātman*) and in the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka-Vārttika*'s *Sambandha-Vārttika*. What follows is therefore not a fabricated Sanskrit edition, but an exact report of what the on-disk witness does and does not presently allow.

## Editorial closing

What this opening cluster settles is the work's order. Sureśvara will not permit the reader to mistake Vedānta for a higher refinement of ritualism. The transition is sharper than that. Ritual action belongs to a different textual and soteriological register; the Upaniṣad begins where the saving office of action ends. Ignorance gives rise to desire, desire to action, and knowledge therefore destroys bondage not by assisting action but by annulling its root.

What this cluster leaves open is everything that later makes the *Taittirīya-Vārttika* doctrinally indispensable: the analysis of *satyaṃ jñānam anantaṃ brahma* in the *Brahmānanda-Vallī*; the status of the *ānandamaya-kośa*; the relation between *kośa*-analysis, *avasthā-traya*, and *adhyāsa*; and the precise shape of the anti-*samuccaya* doctrine in full dialectical dress. Those matters are taken up later in this same work, and in parallel by *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* 1.55-56, 1.98-99, 2.9-10, and 4.3, as well as by the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka-Vārttika*'s *Sambandha-Vārttika*. A future replacement of this file should be made from a readable primary Sanskrit witness.
