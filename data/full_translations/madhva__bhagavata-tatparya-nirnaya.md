## Editorial introduction

This opening sequence states the terms on which Madhva will read the *Bhāgavata*. The argument is not yet the full ontology of the second chapter; it is the prior hermeneutic settlement. Who is the proper hearer? What sort of text is this? What relation binds Vyāsa, Śuka, Sūta, and the sages? What is the soteriological efficacy of Hari's name and avatāra-narrative? Madhva answers each question by a sharply selective gloss. In 1.1.2, Śuka's departure is read through the negation of *dehābhimāna* (identification with body and adjuncts). In 1.1.4, the narrative frame is justified as *ākhyāyikā* (a narrative episode) introduced for the removal of doubt concerning the *puruṣārthas* (the human ends). In 1.1.14, the saving force of the divine name is tied to repeated habituation. In 1.1.18, *ātma-māyā* is not the deluding *guṇa-māyā* of a lower ontic register, but the Lord's own *svarūpa-bhūta-icchā* (will belonging to his very nature).

That is why this recoverable slice is the natural unit to translate. It is the smallest secure opening complex in the local witness, and it already displays the decisive Madhva moves: a hierarchy of teachers, a restriction of interpretive authority, the conversion of apparently descriptive epithets into doctrinal markers, and a refusal to let the Lord's agency dissolve into impersonal process. The larger claims are then made explicit in the immediately following *dvitīyo'dhyāyaḥ*, especially on *Bhāg.* 1.2.11-13, where the ontology of Brahman, Paramātman, and Bhagavān is stated directly. But the first chapter already fixes the direction of reading. It establishes, before the ontology is unfolded, that the *Bhāgavata* is to be heard as a Hari-centered revelation whose narrative surface is itself purposive.

### Bhāg. 1.1.2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *yaṃ pravrajantam anupetam apetakṛtyaṃ dvaipāyano virahakātara ājuhāva | putreti tan-mayatayā taravo 'bhineduḥ taṃ sarvabhūtahṛdayaṃ munim ānato 'smi ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> I bow to that sage, the heart of all beings, who was going forth, unaccompanied, his obligations left behind.

### Bhāg. 1.1.2 — gloss 1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *anupetaṃ dehādibhiḥ.*

**English (line-by-line):**
> "Unaccompanied" means: not accompanied by body and the rest.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| anupetam | an-upeta | acc sg masc | unaccompanied |
| dehādibhiḥ | deha-ādi | instr pl masc | by body and the like |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *dehādi* [dvandva-like enumerative/tatpuruṣa]: *dehaḥ ādiḥ yeṣāṃ te*; body and the rest of the adjunct-series.

**Kāraka structure:**
- karaṇa: *dehādibhiḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb; elliptical gloss.

**Note:**
Madhva narrows the semantic field at once. The point is not social incompletion but freedom from association with the bodily-adjunct complex.

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *anabhimānāt.*

**English (line-by-line):**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| anabhimānāt | an-abhimāna | abl sg masc | from non-identification, because there is no egoic appropriation |

**English (line-by-line):**
> "Self-manifest power" means Brahman.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |

| svānubhavam | sva-anubhava | acc sg masc | self-manifest power, own directly manifest efficacy |

## Editorial closing

This opening sequence settles three matters. First, the *Bhāgavata* is to be heard under a restricted pedagogy: what Sūta may tell is what descends through Vyāsa and the authorized ṛṣis. Second, narrative framing is doctrinally functional; the *ākhyāyikā* itself removes doubt regarding the *puruṣārthas*. Third, Hari's agency is interpreted as free and intrinsic, not as a concession to lower-order illusion. What the passage does not yet settle is the full ontological articulation of that agency. That comes at once in the next chapter, especially on *Bhāg.* 1.2.11-13, where Madhva states the relation among Brahman, Paramātman, and Bhagavān, and again on 1.2.22, 24, 27-32, where the hierarchy of worship and the anti-reductive reading of divine manifestation are made explicit. The same line is then carried into the larger *Bhāgavata-Tātparya-Nirṇaya* through the cosmogonic chapters of Skandha 2 and Skandha 3, and across Madhva's companion *Tātparya* works, above all the *Gītā-Tātparya-Nirṇaya*, where *īśvarārpaṇa*, *bhakti*, and dependence on Viṣṇu are systematically coordinated.
