## Editorial introduction

These opening six sections form the natural proem of the *Vedārtha-Saṃgraha*. The work has no chapter divisions in the local witness, but it does have a clear argumentative threshold. Sections 1-2 are not ornamental preliminaries. They state, first, the Viśiṣṭādvaitin theological frame in compressed form and, second, the anti-Advaitin polemical orientation through praise of Yāmuna as the dispeller of the delusion that makes the ignorant Brahman itself transmigrate. Section 3 then states the governing thesis of the treatise: the whole Upaniṣadic corpus, taken at its doctrinal summit, teaches an ordered path of right understanding of *jīva* and *Paramātman*, normatively structured life, and devotion directed toward the feet of the supreme Person. Sections 4-6 immediately identify the textual field to be interpreted, define the essential nature of the individual self, and give the first dense statement of Brahman as *antaryāmin*, the inner controller who is both the material and governing cause of the *cid-acid* cosmos.

This is the right stopping-point because section 7 begins the formal statement of rival readings, above all the *nirviśeṣa* reading later rejected in detail. In that sense, §§1-6 are the doctrinal aperture of the whole work. They anticipate what later becomes explicit in the *tat tvam asi* discussion (§§19-26), in the body-soul construal of non-difference, and in the later definition of *śarīra* and *apṛthak-siddhi* (§76 and following). They also stand in clear continuity with Rāmānuja's broader corpus: compare the opening orientation of the *Śrī-Bhāṣya* at *Brahma-Sūtra* 1.1.1, the *antaryāmin* asymmetry presupposed in *Gītā-Bhāṣya* 9.4-5 and 13.2, and the later analysis of the liberated self's manifestation in *Vedārtha-Saṃgraha* §§88-93. The proem already contains the architecture. The rest of the work will defend it.

## Translation

### §1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *aśeṣacidacidvastuviśeṣiṇe śeṣaśāyine*  
> *nirmalānantakalyāṇanidhaye viṣṇave namaḥ*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Salutation to Viṣṇu, who is qualified by the totality of conscious and non-conscious entities, who reclines upon Śeṣa.  
> Salutation to him who is the treasury of stainless and infinite auspicious qualities.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| aśeṣacidacidvastuviśeṣiṇe | aśeṣa-cit-acit-vastu-viśeṣin | dat. sg. m. | to the one qualified by all conscious and non-conscious entities |
| śeṣaśāyine | śeṣa-śāyin | dat. sg. m. | to the one reclining on Śeṣa |
| nirmalānantakalyāṇanidhaye | nirmala-ananta-kalyāṇa-nidhi | dat. sg. m. | to the treasury of stainless, endless auspiciousness |
| viṣṇave | viṣṇu | dat. sg. m. | to Viṣṇu |
| namaḥ | namas | indeclinable | salutation, reverence |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *aśeṣa-cid-acid-vastu-viśeṣin* [bahuvrīhi-like possessive / tatpuruṣa-chain]: *aśeṣāṇi ca cit-acit-vastūni yasya viśeṣaṇāni saḥ*.
- *śeṣa-śāyin* [saptamī-tatpuruṣa]: *śeṣe śete iti*.
- *nirmala-ananta-kalyāṇa-nidhi* [karmadhāraya/tatpuruṣa-chain]: *nirmalāni cānantāni ca kalyāṇāni yasmin nidhīyante saḥ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- sampradāna: *aśeṣacidacidvastuviśeṣiṇe*, *śeṣaśāyine*, *nirmalānantakalyāṇanidhaye*, *viṣṇave*
- kriyā-artha (elliptical): *namaḥ* functions as the salutation-act

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed; the verse is an elliptical nominal salutation built on *namaḥ* governing the dative.

**Note:**
The opening is already doctrinal. Viṣṇu is not praised as an isolated deity but as the one qualified by the whole field of *cit* and *acit*. That is the compressed Viśiṣṭādvaita thesis later unfolded through *śarīra-śarīrin* language. Compare the more explicit prose statement at §6 and the later body-soul analysis at §§19-24 and §76.

### §2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *paraṃ brahmaivājñaṃ bhramaparigataṃ saṃsarati*  
> *tatparopādhyālīḍhaṃ vivaśam aśubhasyāspadam iti*  
> *śrutinyāyāpetaṃ jagati vitataṃ mohanam idaṃ*  
> *tamo yenāpāstaṃ sa hi vijayate yāmunamuniḥ*

**English (line-by-line):**
> "The supreme Brahman itself, as ignorant, seized by error, transmigrates;  
> it is overrun by adjuncts other than itself, helpless, the abode of evil"  
> such is this delusion, spread throughout the world, devoid of scripture and reasoning.  
> The sage Yāmuna, by whom that darkness was driven away, he indeed triumphs.

**Kāraka structure:**

**Note:**
The verse states the target before the argument begins. The delusion is not merely generic ignorance but the thesis that Brahman itself, under *upādhi*, becomes the transmigrating, suffering subject. The charge that this doctrine is *śruti-nyāyāpeta* anticipates the two fronts of the whole work: scriptural harmonization and rational scrutiny. Yāmuna is invoked as the predecessor who cleared this ground; compare the anti-Advaitin orientation of *Śrī-Bhāṣya* 1.1.1 and the later *sapta-vidhānupapatti* sequence.

### §3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *aśeṣajagaddhitānuśāsanaśrutinikaraśirasi samadhigato 'yam arthaḥ jīvaparamātmayāthātmyajñānapūrvakavarṇāśramadharmetikartavyatākaparamapuruṣacaraṇayugaladhyānārcanapraṇāmādir atyarthapriyas tatprāptiphalaḥ*

**English (line-by-line):**
> At the head of the mass of scriptural texts that instruct for the welfare of the whole world, this meaning has been established:  
> meditation upon, worship of, prostration before, and the like, directed to the pair of feet of the supreme Person, and preceded by knowledge of the true nature of the *jīva* and the supreme Self and by the proper carrying out of the duties of *varṇa* and *āśrama*, are supremely dear to him;  
> and the fruit is the attainment of him.

| aśeṣajagaddhitānuśāsanaśrutinikaraśirasi | aśeṣa-jagad-hita-anuśāsana-śruti-nikara-śiras | loc. sg. n. | at the summit of the host of scriptural texts that teach the welfare of the entire world |
| samadhigataḥ | sam-adhi-gam | past passive participle, nom. sg. m. | ascertained, fully established |
| ayam | idam | nom. sg. m. | this |
| arthaḥ | artha | nom. sg. m. | meaning, purport |
| jīvaparamātmayāthātmyajñānapūrvakavarṇāśramadharmetikartavyatākaparamapuruṣacaraṇayugaladhyānārcanapraṇāmādiḥ | jīva-paramātma-yāthātmya-jñāna-pūrvaka-varṇāśrama-dharma-itikartavyatāka-parama-puruṣa-caraṇa-yugala-dhyāna-arcana-praṇāma-ādi | nom. sg. m. | meditation, worship, prostration and the like toward the supreme Person's pair of feet, preceded by knowledge of the true nature of the jīva and Paramātman and by the due performance of varṇa-āśrama duty |
| atyarthapriyaḥ | atyartha-priya | nom. sg. m. | exceedingly dear |
| tatprāptiphalaḥ | tat-prāpti-phala | nom. sg. m. | having the attainment of him as fruit |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *aśeṣa-jagad-hita-anuśāsana-śruti-nikara-śiras* [tatpuruṣa-chain]: *aśeṣasya jagataḥ hitaṃ anuśāsanti ye śrutayaḥ, teṣāṃ nikarasya śirasi*.
- *jīva-paramātma-yāthātmya-jñāna* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *jīvasya ca paramātmanaś ca yāthātmya-jñānam*.
- *varṇāśrama-dharma-itikartavyatāka* [tatpuruṣa-chain]: *varṇāśrama-dharmasya itikartavyatā*; "the precise mode of obligatory performance."
- *parama-puruṣa-caraṇa-yugala* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *paramapuruṣasya caraṇayugalam*.
- *dhyāna-arcana-praṇāma-ādi* [dvandva-chain]: meditation, worship, prostration, and the rest.
- *tat-prāpti-phala* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *tasya prāptiḥ phalam yasya*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- adhikaraṇa: *aśeṣajagaddhitānuśāsanaśrutinikaraśirasi*
- kartṛ (main clause): *ayam arthaḥ*
- kriyā (elliptical/passive sense): *samadhigataḥ*
- predicate of the established purport: *...dhyānārcanapraṇāmādiḥ atyarthapriyaḥ tatprāptiphalaḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- *samadhigataḥ*: past passive participle functioning predicatively; no finite verb is expressed.

**Note:**
The sentence gives the practical grammar of the whole treatise. Knowledge of *jīva* and *Paramātman* does not terminate in bare cognition; it orders ritual-ethical life and culminates in devotional orientation to the supreme Person. The syntax is important: epistemic clarification, normative duty, and worship are not three unrelated tracks. Compare the more developed soteriological sequence at §§91-93 and the parallel insistence in *Gītā-Bhāṣya* that liberating knowledge matures as *bhakti-rūpāpannā upāsanā*.

### §4

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *asya jīvātmano 'nādyavidyāsaṃcitapuṇyapāparūpakarmapravāhahetukabrahmādisuranaratiryaksthāvarātmakacaturvidhadehapraveśakṛtatattadabhimānajanitāvarjanīyabhavabhayavidhvaṃsanāya dehātiriktātmasvarūpatatsvabhāvatadantarayāmiparamātmasvarūpatatsvabhāvatadupāsanatatphalabhūtātmasvarūpāvirbhāvapūrvakānavadhikātiśayānandabrahmānubhavajñāpane pravṛttaṃ hi vedāntavākyajātam, tat tvam asi | ayam ātmā brahma | ya ātmani tiṣṭhann ātmano 'ntaro yam ātmā na veda yasyātmā śarīraṃ ya ātmānam antaro yamayati sa ta ātmāntaryāmy amṛtaḥ | eṣa sarvabhūtāntarātmāpahatapāpmā divyo deva eko nārāyaṇaḥ | tam etaṃ vedānuvacanena brāhmaṇā vividiṣanti yajñena dānena tapasānāśakena | brahmavid āpnoti param | tam evaṃ vidvān amṛta iha bhavati nānyaḥ panthā ayanāya vidyata ity ādikam*

**English (line-by-line):**
> For the destruction of the inescapable fear of becoming, born from the several identifications produced by this individual self's entry into the four kinds of bodies consisting in Brahmā, gods, humans, animals, and immovables, an entry caused by the stream of karmas in the form of merit and demerit accumulated through beginningless ignorance,  
> the whole body of Vedāntic sentences is indeed set in motion to make known: the self's nature as distinct from body; its intrinsic character; the nature of the supreme Self who is its inner controller; that supreme Self's intrinsic character; the worship of him; and the experience of Brahman as limitless and unsurpassed bliss, preceded by the manifestation of the self's own nature that is the fruit of that worship.  
> "That thou art."  
> "This self is Brahman."  
> "He who stands in the self, who is within the self, whom the self does not know, whose body is the self, who from within controls the self, he is thy Self, the inner controller, the immortal."  
> "This one, the inner Self of all beings, free from evil, divine, the one god, is Nārāyaṇa."  
> "By recitation of the Veda, by sacrifice, by gift, and by austerity unaccompanied by self-seeking, the Brāhmaṇas desire to know that very one."  
> "The knower of Brahman attains the supreme."  
> "One who knows him thus becomes immortal here; there is no other path for going there" and the like.

- kartṛ: *brahmavit*; karman: *param*; kriyā: *āpnoti*

**Verbal modality:**
- *pravṛttam*: past passive participle
- *asi*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active
- *veda*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active
- *yamayati*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active
- *vividiṣanti*: desiderative, laṭ / parasmaipada / active
- *āpnoti*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active
- *bhavati*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active
- *vidyate*: laṭ / ātmanepada / existential-passive usage

**Note:**
This section gives the work's scriptural canon in miniature. The sequence is deliberate: first the existential problem of embodied misidentification, then the pedagogical task of distinguishing self from body and Brahman from neither-self-nor-world, then the cited *mahāvākyas* and *antaryāmin* texts that will govern the hermeneutic. The quoted *Bṛhadāraṇyaka* formula, "*yasyātmā śarīram*," is especially load-bearing. The later body-soul reading of *tat tvam asi* at §§19-26 is already latent here, and the same asymmetry reappears in *Gītā-Bhāṣya* 9.4-5 and 13.2.

### §5

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *jīvātmanaḥ svarūpaṃ devamanuṣyādiprakṛtipariṇāmaviśeṣarūpanānāvidhabhedarahitaṃ jñānānandaikaguṇaṃ, tasyaitasya karmakṛtadevādibhede 'padhvaste svarūpabhedo vācām agocaraḥ svasaṃvedyaḥ, jñānasvarūpam ity etāvad eva nirdeśyam | tac ca sarveṣām ātmanāṃ samānam*

**English (line-by-line):**
> The essential nature of the individual self is free from the many distinctions constituted by the particular transformations of matter such as gods, humans, and the rest; it has knowledge and bliss alone as its defining qualities.  
> When the distinctions such as godhood, produced by karma, have been destroyed, the self's own specific nature is beyond the reach of speech and knowable only by itself; it can be indicated only thus far: "it is of the nature of knowledge."  
> And this is common to all selves.

- *jñāna-ānanda-eka-guṇa* [dvandva/tatpuruṣa]: *jñānaṃ cānandaś ca eva guṇau yasya*.
- *karma-kṛta-deva-ādi-bheda* [tatpuruṣa]: *karmaṇā kṛto devādi-bhedaḥ*.
- *svarūpa-bheda* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: distinctness belonging to its own nature.
- *sva-saṃvedya* [bahuvrīhi / karmadhāraya]: that which is cognizable by itself alone.

**Kāraka structure:**

- kartṛ/predicate topic: *svarūpam*

- adhikaraṇa-condition: *karmakṛtadevādibhede apadhvaste*
- predicate of second clause: *svarūpabhedaḥ ... agocaraḥ svasaṃvedyaḥ ... nirdeśyam*
- final predicate: *tat ... samānam*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed. The section proceeds by nominal predication.
- *nirdeśyam*: gerundive/predicative adjective, "can be indicated."

**Note:**
Rāmānuja sharply distinguishes the self's essential nature from its karmically produced embodiments. The passage does not erase plurality of selves; it says what is common to them at the level of essential constitution. Later sections on liberation (§§88-93) will develop how the obscured self becomes manifest without ceasing to be an individual self. The line also anticipates Rāmānuja's refusal to treat individuality as a mere illusion generated by *avidyā*.

### §6

> And the śrutis that aim to proclaim his glory teach, by terms such as "his power," "his part," "his manifestation," "his form," "his body," "his person," and the like, and also by their co-reference with him, that he is the inner self of every class of sentient and non-sentient entity other than himself and that he governs them all.

- second sentence kartṛ: *śrutayaḥ*

- kriyā: *pratipādayanti*

**Verbal modality:**
- *pratipādayanti*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active
- The first sentence is again nominal, giving a definitional predication without an expressed finite verb.

**Note:**
This is the compressed doctrinal heart of the proem. Two things matter grammatically. First, the qualifiers do not sit externally beside Nārāyaṇa; they accumulate into a single definition of the *antaryāmin*. Second, the final phrase *tatsāmānādhikaraṇyena* anticipates the later hermeneutic use of co-reference in the reading of *tat tvam asi*. The relation is not bare identity but identity of reference under differentiated qualifying conditions. Compare especially §§19-26, the fuller ontological definition of *śarīra* at §76, and the cosmological asymmetry rearticulated in *Gītā-Bhāṣya* 9.4-5.

## Editorial closing

This opening unit settles the interpretive horizon of the *Vedārtha-Saṃgraha*. It has already fixed the three indispensable coordinates: the *jīva* is not the body; Brahman is not a featureless remainder but Nārāyaṇa possessed of boundless *kalyāṇa-guṇas*; and the Upaniṣadic corpus must be read through the *antaryāmin* relation in which the world of *cit* and *acit* stands to Brahman. It also settles the soteriological shape of the work: knowledge, normatively ordered life, worship, and the manifestation of the self are internally connected rather than competitively ranked.

What remains open here is the exact grammatical and ontological mechanism by which non-difference-language functions. That work begins in earnest with the rival views at §7, the purification of negation-language in §§17-24, the explicit body-soul reading of *tat tvam asi* in §§19-26, the technical definition of *śarīra* in §76, the anti-Advaitin critique from §76 onward, the *nirguṇa* construal at §84, and the account of the self's manifested state in §§88-93. The proem therefore does not conclude the argument. It gives its terms.
