## Editorial introduction

This opening segment does not yet argue the whole first *adhikaraṇa*. It does something prior. It fixes the conditions under which that argument may begin. Madhva starts by naming *Nārāyaṇa* as the one to be known, attained, and saluted, then places the *Brahma-Sūtra* under a specific history of disclosure: knowledge had become confused in the Dvāpara age; Viṣṇu descended as Vyāsa; the obscured Veda was re-manifested and divided; only then were the *sūtras* composed for the sake of decisive determination. The work therefore begins neither with a bare exhortation to inquire nor with a neutral philosophical curiosity. It begins with a theologically charged account of why inquiry is needed, who authorizes it, and what its proper object already is.

That is why BBsBh 1.1.1.1-20 is a natural extended unit. Sections 1-18 form a proem in the strict sense: invocation, textual genealogy, scriptural citation, and meta-comment on what counts as a *sūtra*. Sections 19-20 then state the first sūtra, *athāto brahmajijñāsā*, and give the first lexical key: *atha* signifies auspicious commencement and succession after qualification; *ataḥ* signifies reason. The fuller analysis of *brahma* and *jijñāsā* lies beyond this file, later in the same sūtra-commentary. But the threshold is already unmistakable. Inquiry begins because Viṣṇu's grace, Vyāsa's descent, Vedic disclosure, and the seeker's qualification have together made it both possible and necessary. This is fully continuous with Madhva's broader position. *Anuvyākhyāna* 1.1.13-15 states the same dependence-grammar more directly; *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna* 1-2 gives the ontological synopsis; *Viṣṇu-Tattva-Vinirṇaya* returns to the identification of *brahman* with Viṣṇu in independent treatise form.

### BBsBh 1.1.1.1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *nārāyaṇaṃ guṇaiḥ sarvair udīrṇaṃ doṣavarjitam /*  
> *jñeyaṃ gamyaṃ gurūṃś cāpi natvā sūtrārtha ucyate //*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Having bowed to Nārāyaṇa, exalted by all qualities and devoid of defects,  
> to him who is to be known, to be attained, and also to the teachers, the meaning of the sūtras is stated.

### BBsBh 1.1.1.2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**

### BBsBh 1.1.1.3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *atheṣṭāniṣṭaprāptiparihārecchūnāṃ tadyogam avijānatāṃ tajjñāpanārthaṃ vedam utsannaṃ vyañjayaṃś caturdhā vyabhajat, caturviṃśatidhā ekaśatadhā sahasradhā dvādaśadhā ca /*

## Editorial closing

This opening threshold settles three matters. First, the inquiry of the *Brahma-Sūtra* is not neutral; it is already oriented to Nārāyaṇa. Second, the text's authority is grounded in a descent-history: confusion of knowledge, Vyāsa's avatāra, Vedic re-manifestation, and then sūtra-composition. Third, the very first particles of the opening sūtra are doctrinally loaded: *atha* marks auspicious commencement and qualified succession; *ataḥ* marks reason. What this segment does not yet settle is the full semantic weight of *brahmajijñāsā* itself, nor the extended demonstration that *brahma* here means Viṣṇu and not some other candidate referent. Those matters are taken up immediately afterwards in BBsBh 1.1.1.21-38 and 1.1.1.81-113, and are restated in a more condensed inferential register at *Anuvyākhyāna* 1.1.13-15. For Madhva's broader ontological codification of the same asymmetry, the nearest parallel is *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna* 1-2; for the independent doctrinal dossier identifying Viṣṇu as the supreme referent of scripture, compare *Viṣṇu-Tattva-Vinirṇaya* 1-10.
