## Editorial introduction

The natural extended unit for this work would ordinarily be the opening *svaprakāśatva-pariccheda*, since Citsukha's definition of *svaprakāśatva* (self-luminosity) is one of the load-bearing theses by which the *Tattva-Pradīpikā* enters later Advaita polemics. But the present repository does not contain a continuous primary Sanskrit text of the *Citsukhī* itself. The search across `materials/primary_texts/sanskrit/vedanta/full_corpus/`, adjacent Sanskrit directories, and the wider repository yielded no direct transcription or scan-derived text of the work. It did, however, yield a small set of explicit secondary quotations and attributions: one modern handbook quotation preserving Citsukha's famous definition of self-luminosity; one polemical secondary source preserving a variant formulation of the same definition; and one further citation, explicitly ascribed to Citsukha, clarifying that *aparokṣatva* (immediacy) cannot be reduced to yogic perceptibility.

That limitation determines the scope of this file. It is not a substitute for a chapter-translation of the *svaprakāśatva-pariccheda*, and it does not pretend to be one. Rather, it gives a disciplined translation dossier of the lines that can be responsibly constructed from the on-disk witnesses now available. Even this narrow dossier matters. The first definition states, with great compression, Citsukha's mature Advaitin claim that consciousness is directly available without first becoming an object. The second and third lines show the polemical edge of the definition: *aparokṣa* is not mere objecthood, nor is it exhausted by yogic extraordinary perception. That conceptual package shapes later Vivaraṇa-Advaita, is presupposed by Madhusūdana's *Advaita-Siddhi*, and remains one of the clearest points at which Citsukha hardens an earlier Śaṅkarite intuition into scholastic precision.

## Translation

### Secondary witness A: self-luminosity definition quoted in the Bloomsbury handbook

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *avedyatve sati aparokṣa-vyavahāra-viṣayatvam*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Self-luminosity is: while there is non-objecthood, there is being the object-domain of immediate discourse and practice.  
> More plainly: it is direct availability for immediate employment, without first becoming an object of another cognition.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| avedyatve | a-vedyatva | loc sg neut | in the condition of not being an object of knowledge |
| sati | सत् / as | pres act participle, loc sg | while being, when there is |
| aparokṣa-vyavahāra-viṣayatvam | aparokṣa-vyavahāra-viṣayatva | nom/acc sg neut | the state of being the object/domain of immediate discourse or practical employment |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *avedyatva* [nañ-tatpuruṣa]: *na vedyatvam*.
- *aparokṣa-vyavahāra* [karmadhāraya / tatpuruṣa nuance]: *aparokṣo vyavahāraḥ*.
- *aparokṣa-vyavahāra-viṣayatvam* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *aparokṣasya vyavahārasya viṣayatvam*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- No finite verbal construction is expressed.
- The phrase functions as a *lakṣaṇa* (definition), with *avedyatve sati* as a locative-absolute qualifier.

**Verbal modality:**
- *sati*: present participial form of *as* used in a locative-absolute construction; no finite *lakāra* is expressed.

**Note:**
This wording is preserved in the on-disk Bloomsbury handbook chapter, which cites Trivedi 1987, p. 122 n. 7. Because the primary *Citsukhī* text is not available on disk, the exact editional context cannot here be verified directly. Philosophically, the force is decisive: self-luminosity is not self-objectification. The self is immediately available without becoming a second-order object. Compare the related exclusion of yogic-perceptual reduction in the third witness below.

### Secondary witness B: variant formulation preserved in *AdvaitasiddhiVsNyayamrta*

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *avedyatve sati aparokṣa-vyavahāra-yogyatvam*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Self-luminosity is: while there is non-objecthood, there is fitness for immediate discourse and practice.  
> The emphasis here falls less on "being the object-domain" and more on "being fit for direct employment."

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| avedyatve | a-vedyatva | loc sg neut | in the condition of not being an object of knowledge |
| sati | सत् / as | pres act participle, loc sg | while being, when there is |
| aparokṣa-vyavahāra-yogyatvam | aparokṣa-vyavahāra-yogyatva | nom/acc sg neut | the state of being fit for immediate discourse or practical employment |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *avedyatva* [nañ-tatpuruṣa]: *na vedyatvam*.
- *aparokṣa-vyavahāra* [karmadhāraya / tatpuruṣa nuance]: *aparokṣo vyavahāraḥ*.
- *aparokṣa-vyavahāra-yogyatvam* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *aparokṣasya vyavahārasya yogyatvam*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- No finite verbal construction is expressed.
- As in witness A, the phrase is a nominal *lakṣaṇa* with a locative-absolute qualifier.

**Verbal modality:**
- *sati*: present participial form of *as* used in a locative-absolute construction; no finite *lakāra* is expressed.

**Note:**
This variant occurs in the on-disk `AdvaitasiddhiVsNyayamrta.txt` as part of an English discussion of Citsukha's definition. The same source preserves witness C below and explicitly cites `Citsukhi, p. 10. N. S. Press. Edn.` for that citation. Since the direct primary page is unavailable, I cannot settle whether *viṣayatvam* and *yogyatvam* are variant readings, paraphrastic reformulations, or different ways later authors glossed the same definition. The conceptual difference is small but not trivial: *viṣayatva* stresses availability as content-domain, whereas *yogyatva* stresses pragmatic fitness.

### Secondary witness C: exclusion of yogic-perceptual reduction

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *na ca yogi-pratyakṣa-gamyatayā aparokṣatvaṃ dharmādīnāṃ codanāika-pramāṇa-yogyatvāt*

**English (line-by-line):**
> And immediacy does not consist in being accessible to yogic perception,  
> because dharma and the like are fit for one pramāṇa only, namely scriptural injunction.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| na | na | indecl | not |
| ca | ca | indecl | and |
| yogi-pratyakṣa-gamyatayā | yogi-pratyakṣa-gamyatā | instr sg fem | by/through the state of being accessible to yogic perception |
| aparokṣatvam | aparokṣatva | nom/acc sg neut | immediacy, non-mediate status |
| dharmādīnām | dharmādi | gen pl masc | of dharma and the like |
| codanāika-pramāṇa-yogyatvāt | codanā-eka-pramāṇa-yogyatva | abl sg neut | because of being fit for a single means of knowledge, namely injunction/scripture |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *yogi-pratyakṣa* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *yoginaḥ pratyakṣam*.
- *yogi-pratyakṣa-gamyatā* [tṛtīyā / tatpuruṣa sense]: *yogi-pratyakṣeṇa gamyatā*.
- *dharmādi* [dvandva-elliptic / tatpuruṣa convention]: *dharmaḥ ādiś ca yeṣām*.
- *codanāika-pramāṇa* [karmadhāraya within tatpuruṣa]: *codanā eva ekaṃ pramāṇam*.
- *codanāika-pramāṇa-yogyatva* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *codanāikapramāṇasya yogyatvam*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- Qualified topic: *aparokṣatvam*.
- Instrumental of rejected basis: *yogi-pratyakṣa-gamyatayā*.
- Genitive reference: *dharmādīnām*.
- Hetu: *codanāika-pramāṇa-yogyatvāt*.

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed.
- The sentence is an elliptical scholastic assertion of exclusion; the copular force is understood.

**Note:**
This line is preserved in ASCII transliteration in the on-disk `AdvaitasiddhiVsNyayamrta.txt`, immediately followed by the citation `Citsukhi, p. 10. N. S. Press. Edn.`. IAST has been normalized here from that transliteration. The philosophical point is sharp: *aparokṣa* cannot be defined so narrowly that only yogically extraordinary perceptibility would count as immediacy. Otherwise entities such as *dharma*, whose epistemic access is classically scriptural, would be wrongly excluded. The line thus protects Citsukha's definition from collapse into a merely perceptual model of directness.

## Editorial closing

This dossier settles one thing and only one thing securely: even without the missing primary witness on disk, the repository preserves enough attributed material to show the shape of Citsukha's first-*pariccheda* doctrine of self-luminosity. The self is not self-luminous by becoming an object to itself; it is self-luminous by being directly available without such objectification. The third witness further settles that *aparokṣatva* is not identical with yogic extraordinary perception. What remains open is the wider argumentative architecture in which Citsukha defended these claims: the missing continuous text would be needed to establish his exact sequence of objections, replies, and proofs, and to settle whether *viṣayatvam* and *yogyatvam* are true variant readings or later paraphrases. Later Advaita unquestionably picks this up. Madhusūdana's *Advaita-Siddhi* presupposes Citsukha's definition in its opening epistemological disputes, and the broader Vivaraṇa line carries the same anti-objectificatory account of consciousness forward into debates over *avidyā*, *mithyātva*, and *aparokṣa-jñāna*. The next responsible step, therefore, is not further reconstruction from memory, but acquisition of the absent primary edition or a reliable transcription of the *svaprakāśatva-pariccheda* itself.
