## Editorial introduction

The first chapter of the *Madhyamakāvatāra*, the *Prathamaścittotpādaḥ*, does not yet stage Candrakīrti's most famous negative dialectic. That work belongs chiefly to chapter 6, where the denial of *svabhāva* is unfolded at length. Chapter 1 does earlier and no less necessary work. It states the causal order by which a bodhisattva-path becomes possible at all: *mahākaruṇā* (great compassion), *advayabuddhi* (non-dual cognition), and *bodhicitta* (the resolve for awakening) are named as the causes of the Victor's sons; the first *bhūmi* is then marked under the title *Pramuditā*; and the predominance of *dāna-pāramitā* is immediately argued. This is therefore the natural opening unit of the work. It is chapter 1 of an eleven-chapter composition, and it fixes the ethical and soteriological architecture presupposed by chapters 2–5 on the remaining *pāramitā*s and by chapter 6 on *prajñā*.

The present file translates only the root verses 1–17. That restriction is deliberate and honest. The witness on disk is an interleaved transcription of the *Madhyamakāvatāra-Bhāṣya* rather than a separately edited verse-only text, and a full treatment of the embedded prose would exceed the present natural unit. Even so, these verses are doctrinally load-bearing. Candrakīrti begins neither with a bare celebration of emptiness nor with a merely private ideal of release. He begins with compassion, then vow, then the hierarchy of *bhūmi*s, then generosity as the practical form of the first ground. The ordering matters. By the time chapter 6 gives the philosophical center of the work, the reader is already expected to know that emptiness, for Candrakīrti, is not a world-denying abstraction but the wisdom without which the bodhisattva's compassionate resolve cannot be completed.

## Translation

### 1.1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *munīndrajāḥ śrāvakamadhyabuddhāḥ*  
> *buddhodbhavāḥ khalv api bodhisattvāt |*  
> *kāruṇyacittādvayabuddhibodhicittāni hetur jinaputrakāṇām ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> The disciples and the intermediate buddhas are born from the Lords of Sages.  
> The Buddhas too, indeed, arise from a bodhisattva.  
> Compassionate mind, non-dual cognition, and the awakening-mind are the cause of the Victor's sons.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| munīndrajāḥ | munīndra-ja | nom. pl. m. | born from the lords of sages |
| śrāvakamadhyabuddhāḥ | śrāvaka-madhya-buddha | nom. pl. m.; compound | disciples and intermediate buddhas |
| buddhodbhavāḥ | buddha-udbhava | nom. pl. m. | arisen from buddhas |
| khalu | khalu | indecl. particle | indeed |
| api | api | indecl. particle | also |
| bodhisattvāt | bodhisattva | abl. sg. m. | from a bodhisattva |
| kāruṇyacittādvayabuddhibodhicittāni | kāruṇya-citta-advaya-buddhi-bodhi-citta | nom. pl. n.; aggregate compound | compassionate mind, non-dual cognition, and bodhi-mind |
| hetuḥ | hetu | nom. sg. m. | cause |
| jinaputrakāṇām | jina-putraka | gen. pl. m. | of the sons of the victors |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *munīndrajāḥ* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *munīndrāt jātāḥ*.
- *śrāvakamadhyabuddhāḥ* [dvandva-like aggregate]: *śrāvakāś ca madhyabuddhāś ca*.
- *kāruṇyacittādvayabuddhibodhicittāni* [itaretara-dvandva in aggregate use]: *kāruṇyaṃ cittaṃ ca advayā buddhiś ca bodhicittaṃ ca*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- apādāna: *bodhisattvāt*
- sambandha: *jinaputrakāṇām*
- viśeṣya-predicate relation: *kāruṇyacittādvayabuddhibodhicittāni ... hetuḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb; the verse is a nominal predication.

**Note:**
The verse states a hierarchy of causal dependence rather than a merely chronological sequence. Candrakīrti begins with bodhisattva-causality, not with an isolated Buddha-ideal. Compare the chapter-6 insistence that wisdom without the bodhisattva orientation is insufficient for the full Mahāyāna path.

### 1.2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *bījaṃ kṛpā yaj jinaśasyarāśes*  
> *tadvṛddhaye vārisamā cirāya |*  
> *bhogāya pākaś ca yathaiva mānyaḥ,*  
> *ādau mamātaḥ karuṇāpraśaṃsā ||*

**English (line-by-line):**

Seed, water, and ripening mark beginning, middle, and end. Candrakīrti's point is not ornamental. Compassion is not merely the first impulse; it remains structurally necessary through the whole maturation of buddhahood.

### 1.3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *ātmābhisaktau tv aham eti pūrvaṃ*  
> *rāgodbhave bhāva idaṃ mameti |*  
> *arhaṭṭacaryāvadadhīnaloke*  
> *kāruṇyavān yo'sti namo'stu tasmai ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> In attachment to self, first there arises the thought, "I."  
> When passion arises, with respect to things there arises, "This is mine."  
> As for the world, dependent on the course of the water-wheel,  
> homage be to the one who is compassionate toward it.

| rāgodbhave | rāga-udbhava | loc. sg. m. | when passion arises |
| bhāvaḥ | bhāva | nom. sg. m. | the object / existent thing |

- *eti*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active
- *asti*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active
- *astu* (in *namo'stu*): loṭ / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
The lexical crux is *arhaṭṭa*, a water-wheel or bucket-wheel image, which the prose commentary explicitly develops. The verse links ego-possession, possessiveness, and saṃsāric circulation before introducing compassion as the only adequate response.

### 1.4

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *yad asya citte khalu bodhisattve*

- sambandha: *asya*

**Note:**
The first *bhūmi* is defined not by a bare psychological event but by compassion, vow, and orientation toward universal liberation. The chapter thus makes explicit that *bodhicitta* is already structured as commitment, not mere aspiration.

## Editorial closing

This chapter settles the beginning of the path. A bodhisattva is not defined by sentiment alone, nor by wisdom alone, nor by verbal self-designation. Compassion, non-dual cognition, and *bodhicitta* together generate the bodhisattva; the first *bhūmi* is publicly legible above all through *dāna*; and the path already differs from śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha attainments even while borrowing some of their classificatory grammar. What the chapter leaves open is equally important. It does not yet show, in full philosophical detail, why all dharmas are empty of *svabhāva*; that labor is taken up in chapter 6, especially in the long treatment of dependent origination, selflessness, and the refutation of Yogācāra idealism. Chapters 2–5 distribute the other *pāramitā*s across the next grounds; chapter 7 and beyond carry the bodhisattva through the higher *bhūmi*s. For Candrakīrti's broader position, this opening should be read together with *Madhyamakāvatāra* 6 and with the *Prasanna-Padā* on MMK 24, where the non-substantialist force of dependent arising is argued with far greater explicitness.
