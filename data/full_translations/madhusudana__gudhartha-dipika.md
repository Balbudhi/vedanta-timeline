## Editorial introduction

This is the verse in which Arjuna ceases, formally, to speak as Kṛṣṇa's equal and begins to speak as his disciple. That is why *Bhagavad-Gītā* 2.7, together with Madhusūdana's full gloss, is the natural unit to isolate. The verse itself is short. The bhāṣya is not. Madhusūdana uses it to settle three matters before the doctrinal teaching on the self begins at 2.11. First, he construes Arjuna's collapse as *kārpaṇya* (spiritual smallness, the condition of the *kṛpaṇa* who remains outside self-knowledge), not merely as battlefield hesitation. Second, he marks the rise of *adhikāra* (fitness for instruction): one who has become deeply disenchanted with saṃsāra and who has properly approached a teacher becomes eligible for *vidyā-grahaṇa* (the reception of liberating knowledge). Third, he specifies what Arjuna is now asking for: not a contingent good, but an *aikāntika* and *ātyantika* *śreyas* (a good that is both infallibly effective and final).

Within the chapter's architecture, this is the hinge between lament and teaching. Verses 2.1-6 still belong to the unstable zone of grief, scruple, and moral confusion. Verse 2.7 institutes *guru-upasatti* (approach to the teacher), and verse 2.11 presupposes precisely that shift. Madhusūdana makes the point explicit by reading the verse through Upaniṣadic and disciplinary categories: *bhikṣācaryā* as a symptom of renunciatory inclination, *śiṣya*-status as a surrender of parity, and the demand for a good beyond finite heaven or royal success. The same architecture governs later turning-points in his corpus. Compare *Siddhānta-Bindu* *Upodghāta*, where doubt does not cancel scriptural validity but occasions disciplined inquiry, and *Bhakti-Rasāyana* 1.1, where the distinctness of the highest end must first be fixed before the rest of the treatise can proceed. Here the issue is not yet *bhakti* or ontology in the full sense. It is the prior condition of being teachable.

## Translation

### Bhagavad-Gītā 2.7a

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *kārpaṇya-doṣopahata-svabhāvaḥ pṛcchāmi tvāṃ dharma-saṃmūḍha-cetāḥ*

**English (line-by-line):**
> My native disposition has been struck down by the fault of miserly smallness.  
> My mind is confounded with regard to dharma.  
> I ask you.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| kārpaṇya-doṣopahata-svabhāvaḥ | kārpaṇya-doṣa-upahata-svabhāva | nom sg masc | one whose nature is struck down by the fault of *kārpaṇya* |
| pṛcchāmi | √prach | 1sg pres ind P | I ask |
| tvām | tvad | acc sg | you |
| dharma-saṃmūḍha-cetāḥ | dharma-saṃmūḍha-cetas | nom sg masc | one whose mind is bewildered regarding dharma |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *kārpaṇya-doṣopahata-svabhāva* [tatpuruṣa]: *kārpaṇyasya doṣeṇa upahataḥ svabhāvaḥ yasya saḥ*.
- *dharma-saṃmūḍha-cetas* [tatpuruṣa]: *dharme saṃmūḍhaṃ cetaḥ yasya saḥ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: implied first-person speaker in *pṛcchāmi*
- karman: *tvām*

**Verbal modality:**
- *pṛcchāmi*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
Arjuna does not say merely "I am sad." He identifies a corruption of *svabhāva* itself. Madhusūdana will take this as the threshold condition for genuine discipleship: self-confidence in one's own practical judgment has broken.

## Editorial closing

What this passage settles is preliminary but indispensable. It settles that Arjuna's crisis has ripened into *adhikāra*: he has moved from counter-argument to petition, from parity to discipleship, and from finite goods to the demand for a final good. It also settles the hermeneutic status of 2.7 within the chapter. Madhusūdana does not read it as ornamental humility. He reads it as the formal condition under which the teaching beginning at 2.11 can properly occur.

What the passage does not yet settle is the content of that *śreyas*. The self is not yet analysed, the relation of body and self is not yet disentangled, and the status of action is not yet reassigned. Those tasks begin immediately at *Bhagavad-Gītā* 2.11-30, then pass into the argument for *svadharma* at 2.31-38, and later into the relation between knowledge and action at 3.3 ff. For Madhusūdana's larger Advaita framing of eligibility, compare *Siddhānta-Bindu* *Upodghāta*; for the later elevation of devotion within an Advaitic soteriology, compare *Gūḍhārtha-Dīpikā* on 18.54-55 and *Bhakti-Rasāyana* 1.1.
