## Editorial introduction

This file is necessarily narrower than the ideal Cambridge-style deliverable, and the reason is textual rather than doctrinal. The repository does not contain a full Sanskrit transcription of Baladeva Vidyābhūṣaṇa's *Govinda-Bhāṣya*. What it does contain, however, is enough to identify the opening argumentative unit to which the local Baladeva notes repeatedly return: the catuḥsūtrī, *Brahma-Sūtra* 1.1.1–1.1.4, at the head of adhyāya 1, pāda 1. The opening is the natural unit because it fixes three things at once. First, it determines what *brahman* means for the whole commentary. Second, it determines what sort of causality the world exhibits in relation to that *brahman*. Third, in 1.1.4 it fixes *samanvaya* as the hermeneutic rule by which apparently divergent *abheda* and *bheda* texts are to be read together. The on-disk secondary materials are unanimous that this is where Baladeva's Gauḍīya signature enters the commentary: *brahman* is identified as Govinda, the causal sūtra is read through real *śakti*, and the harmonization-sūtra is read as requiring an *acintya-bhedābheda* settlement rather than a unilateral privileging of either identity-statements or difference-statements.

The result is a text that can be translated fully only at the level of the sūtras themselves, while Baladeva's distinctive bhāṣya-doctrine must be registered in the notes with explicit source caution. That is still philosophically worthwhile. The catuḥsūtrī is where Baladeva's later moves on 2.1.14, 2.1.27, 2.1.33, and 2.3.43 are already adumbrated: real causality, real *śakti*, non-eliminable *bheda*, and an insistence that the Vedāntic corpus must be read without sacrificing either pole of the scriptural evidence. In that sense, even this evidentially modest file shows the governing pressure of the *Govinda-Bhāṣya* as a whole.

## Translation

### Brahma-Sūtra 1.1.1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *athāto brahma-jijñāsā ||1.1.1||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Now, therefore, the inquiry into *brahman*.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| atha | indeclinable particle | avyaya | now; then; at this point |
| ataḥ | pronoun-base *etad* used adverbially | ablative singular, adverbialized; avyayībhūta in use | therefore; from this; consequent upon this |
| brahma-jijñāsā | *brahman* + desiderative nominal from √*jñā* | feminine nominative singular compound action-noun | inquiry into Brahman; desire to know Brahman |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *brahma-jijñāsā* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *brahmaṇaḥ jijñāsā*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- No overt finite verb is stated.
- Implied predication: *jijñāsā* is the action to be undertaken.

**Verbal modality:**
- No explicit finite verb.
- *jijñāsā* is a desiderative action-noun derived from √*jñā* and carries the prescriptive force of “one should inquire.”

**Note:**
The sūtra is radically compressed. Its whole burden falls on what *atha*, *ataḥ*, and above all *brahma* are taken to presuppose. The on-disk Baladeva materials report that he identifies *brahman* here not with an impersonal remainder but with Govinda, Kṛṣṇa as *svayaṃ-bhagavān*, read through *Bhāgavata-Purāṇa* 1.3.28. Since the bhāṣya's Sanskrit wording is not locally available, that identification must be treated here as a secondary reconstruction rather than as a directly transcribed Baladeva sentence.

### Brahma-Sūtra 1.1.2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *janmādy asya yataḥ ||1.1.2||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> [Brahman is that] from which the origination and the rest of this [world proceed].

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| janmādi | *janman* + *ādi* | neuter singular compound, sūtra-elliptic | origination and the rest; birth etc. |
| asya | pronoun *idam* | genitive singular neuter | of this |
| yataḥ | relative pronoun *yat* | ablative singular neuter | from which |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *janmādi* [elliptic samāsa with *ādi*-second member]: *janma-sthiti-pralaya-ādi*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- apādāna: *yataḥ*
- sambandha / ṣaṣṭhī-relation: *asya*
- The generated series (*janmādi*) is the implied resultant proceeding from the apādāna.

**Verbal modality:**
- No explicit finite verb.
- An implied verb such as *bhavati* / *pravartate* / *sambhavati* must be supplied from the syntactic economy of the sūtra.

**Note:**
This sūtra gives the causal definition of *brahman*. The local Baladeva notes repeatedly connect it to *Bhāgavata-Purāṇa* 1.1.1, *janmādy asya yataḥ anvayād itarataś cārtheṣv abhijñaḥ*, in order to argue that the source of the world is not merely causal but cognitively sovereign. The reported Baladeva line of reasoning is therefore strongly personalist: if conscious selves emerge within the causal order, the ultimate source cannot be a *niḥśaktika* or purely featureless absolute. That doctrinal move is secure in the secondary notes even though the exact bhāṣya Sanskrit is not locally recoverable.

### Brahma-Sūtra 1.1.3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *śāstra-yonitvāt ||1.1.3||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Because of being the source of scripture.
>
> Or, on the other classical construal: because scripture is the source [for the knowledge of Brahman].

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| śāstra-yonitvāt | *śāstra* + *yoni* + *tva* | ablative singular neuter abstract noun | because of scripture-source-hood; because of being the source of scripture / because scripture is the source |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *śāstra-yoni* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa, one construal]: *śāstrasya yoniḥ*.
- *śāstra-yoni* [upapada-like interpretive construal, second classical reading]: *śāstraṃ yoniḥ yasya jñānasya*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- hetu: *śāstra-yonitvāt*

**Verbal modality:**
- No explicit finite verb.
- The ablative marks reason or ground.

**Note:**
This is the most semantically contested of the opening four sūtras. Some commentators read it as saying that Brahman is the source of scripture; others read it as saying that scripture is the source of Brahman-knowledge. The on-disk Baladeva summaries do not preserve a discrete Sanskrit gloss for 1.1.3, so it would be dishonest to force a uniquely Baladevan wording here. Still, Baladeva's broader local profile, especially his insistence that the *Bhāgavata* functions as Vyāsa's self-commentary on the sūtras, suggests a strong scripturalist orientation whichever of the two classical parses is foregrounded.

### Brahma-Sūtra 1.1.4

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tat tu samanvayāt ||1.1.4||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> But that [is Brahman], because of harmonization.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| tat | pronoun *tad* | nominative/accusative singular neuter | that |
| tu | indeclinable particle | avyaya | but; however; rather |
| samanvayāt | *samanvaya* | ablative singular masculine | because of harmonization; from coherent coordination |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- No lexical samāsa in the transmitted wording.

**Kāraka structure:**
- hetu: *samanvayāt*
- *tat* is the topic resumed from the previous sūtras.

**Verbal modality:**
- No explicit finite verb.
- The particle *tu* marks adversative correction or restriction within the inferential sequence.

**Note:**
This is the hermeneutic hinge of the opening. The local Baladeva notes make 1.1.4 the decisive site at which *abheda*-texts and *bheda*-texts are held together rather than hierarchized away from one another. On that reconstruction, *samanvaya* does not mean reducing plurality to a single favored register; it means showing how identity-statements and difference-statements disclose one *para-tattva* under distinct but jointly valid aspects. That is the precise opening pressure out of which the later Gauḍīya *acintya-bhedābheda* reading is said to emerge.

## Editorial closing

What this opening settles is the frame. By the end of 1.1.4, *brahman* has been placed under inquiry, defined causally, tied essentially to *śāstra*, and located under a rule of *samanvaya* rather than exegetical exclusion. Even with the local absence of Baladeva's bhāṣya prose, the secondary materials make clear what he is doing with that frame: identifying the sūtra's *brahman* with Govinda, preserving real causality through *śakti*, and reading harmonization as requiring both *bheda* and *abheda* to stand. What the passage does not yet settle is the full metaphysics of cause and effect, the exact status of *śakti* in relation to *śaktimān*, or the ontological place of the *jīva*. Those questions are picked up later, above all at *Govinda-Bhāṣya* on *Brahma-Sūtra* 2.1.14 (*tad-ananyatvam ārambhaṇa-śabdādibhyaḥ*), 2.1.27 (*śabda-mūlatvāt* in the local Baladeva dossier), 2.1.33 (*lokavat tu līlā-kaivalyam*), and 2.3.43 (*aṃśo nānā-vyapadeśād*), with compressed doctrinal restatement in *Prameya-Ratnāvalī* 6–9.
