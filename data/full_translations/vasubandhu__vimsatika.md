## Editorial introduction

These opening ten verses with their auto-commentary form the first complete argumentative arc of the *Viṃśatikā*. Vasubandhu begins not with a qualified proposal, but with a flat Mahāyāna thesis: the *traidhātuka* (threefold world) is *vijñaptimātra* (nothing but representation). He then allows the realist opponent to press the four classical difficulties in their strongest form: if there are no external objects, how can there be restriction of place, restriction of time, agreement across continua, and determinate efficacy? Verses 2-7 and their vṛtti answer these objections by appeal to dream, preta-vision, and hell-experience, all governed by karmically conditioned *vāsanā* (impressions). The point is not that waking life is casually reduced to dream, but that the explanatory burden the realist places on external objects can, in principle, be discharged by patterned cognitive transformation itself.

The unit becomes still more important at verse 8. The opponent now turns from phenomenology to scripture: if only representation exists, why does the Buddha speak of *rūpāyatana* and the other sense-spheres? Vasubandhu's reply is hermeneutic as well as metaphysical. Such discourse is *ābhiprāyika* (intention-governed), spoken with a view to the disciple who must be led onward. Verses 9-10 then make the pedagogic end explicit: first entry into *pudgala-nairātmya* (the non-selfhood of the person), then into *dharma-nairātmya* (the non-selfhood of dharmas). This is the natural stopping point. From verse 11 onward, the work turns to the special refutation of atomic external-object theories; from verses 16-22 it turns to perception, memory, moral efficacy, and other minds. The present unit therefore states the thesis, answers the first battery of objections, and explains how the thesis is to be heard within Buddhist pedagogy. For the larger Yogācāra framework compare *Triṃśikā* 17-25; for later Indian opposition, compare *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya* 2.2.28-32 and Yāmuna, *Saṃvit-Siddhi* 416-439.

### Vvs 1

> And in that very place it is seen at some time, not at all times. Thus, even without an object, restriction of place and time is established.  
> Again, restriction across continua is established, as in the case of pretas.  
> It is established, that is to say, just as among pretas. How is it established? In the seeing by all alike of a river of pus and the like.  
> A river full of pus is a "river of pus," like a jar of ghee.  
> For pretas, all of them being in the condition of an equal karmic maturation, all see a river full of pus, not only one.  
> And by the "and the like" one should include a river full of urine and excrement, and one occupied by men bearing clubs and swords.  
> Thus regularity across continua is established for representations even though the object is unreal.

- *svargasaṃbhavaḥ* [saptamī-tatpuruṣa]: *svarge saṃbhavaḥ*.
- *tiryakpretaviśeṣāṇām* [dvandva]: "of special kinds of animals and pretas."
- *narakapālādīnām* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: "of hell-guards and the rest."

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *narakapālāḥ ... śvānaḥ vāyasāḥ ca*
- hetu: *ayogāt*, *tadduḥkhāpratisaṃvedanāt*, later *yataḥ ... nānubhavanti*
- kartṛ of hypothetical torment: *yātayatām*
- adhikaraṇa: *narake*, *svarge*

**Verbal modality:**
- *iṣyante*: laṭ / passive / doctrinal admission
- *yujyante*: laṭ / ātmanepada / suitability
- *syāt*, *yātayeyuḥ*: optative / hypothetical consequence
- *anubhavanti*: laṭ / parasmaipada / experiential present

**Note:**
Vasubandhu refuses the realist's repair. The hell-guards cannot simply be admitted as ordinary sentient beings inhabiting hell, because that would dissolve the asymmetry of terror and punishment that the hell-experience is meant to explain.

**English (line-by-line):**
> "Then, for those hell-beings, special elements arise there from their karmas, distinguished by color, shape, size, and strength, and these acquire the designation 'hell-guards' and the like. And they transform in that way, since they are seen making twofold gestures of the hands and the like for the sake of producing fear, just as sheep-shaped mountains are seen coming and going, and just as in the forest of iron śālmalī the thorns become downward-facing and upward-facing." No: those things do not come into being in that way at all.  
> If it is admitted that by those karmas there is there an arising of elements, and also a transformation, why is a transformation of consciousness not admitted?  
> Why is precisely a transformation of consciousness by those karmas not admitted? Why instead are elements imagined?  
> And again: the impression of the karma is posited in one place, the fruit in another. Why is it not admitted precisely there where the impression is? What reason is there?  
> For by the karma through which such an arising of elements there for the hell-beings is imagined, and also a transformation, the impression of that karma is lodged in their continuum of consciousness, not elsewhere.  
> And precisely where the impression is, why is not its fruit there, namely such a transformation of consciousness, admitted?  
> If the fruit of that impression is imagined where the impression is not, what reason is there for that?  
> "'Scripture' is the reason. If only consciousness were the appearance of form and the rest, and there were no external thing such as form and the rest, then the Blessed One would not have taught the existence of the sense-spheres of form and the rest."  
> That is no reason. For: "The existence of the sense-spheres of form and the rest was spoken, with regard to those disciples to be trained by that teaching, by force of intention, just as [the teaching of] a spontaneously born being."  
> Just as the Blessed One, by force of intention, spoke of "a being that is spontaneously born," intending the non-interruption of the stream of consciousness in the future, because of the statement, "Here there is no being and no self; these are only causal dharmas," so too the existence of the sense-spheres of form and the rest was spoken by the Blessed One with regard to those to be trained by that teaching. That statement is therefore intentional.

### Vvs 9 and Entry into *Pudgala-Nairātmya*

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *ko 'trābhiprāyaḥ | yataḥ svabījād vijñaptir yadābhāsā pravartate | dvividhāyatanatvena te tasyā munir abravīt || 9 || kim uktaṃ bhavati | rūpapratibhāsā vijñaptir yataḥ svabījāt pariṇāmaviśeṣaprāptād utpadyate tac ca bījaṃ yat pratibhāsā ca sā te tasyā vijñapteś cakṣūrūpāyatanatvena yathākramam bhagavān abravīt | evaṃ yāvat spraṣṭavyapratibhāsā vijñaptir yataḥ svabījāt pariṇāmaviśeṣaprāptād utpadyate tac ca bījaṃ yat pratibhāsā ca sā te tasyā kāyaspraṣṭavyāyatanatvena yathākramam bhagavān abravīd ity ayam abhiprāyaḥ || evaṃ punar abhiprāyavaśena deśayitvā ko guṇāḥ | tathā pudgalanairātmyapraveśo hi | tathā hi deśyamāne pudgalanairātmyaṃ praviśanti | dvayād vijñānaṣaṭkaṃ pravartate | na tu kaścid eko draṣṭāsti na yāvan manteti evaṃ viditvā ye pudgalanairātmyadeśanāvineyās te pudgalanairātmyaṃ praviśanti |*

**English (line-by-line):**
> What is the intention here?  
> When a representation, bearing a given appearance, proceeds from its own seed, the Sage spoke of those two as the twofold sense-sphere of that representation.  
> What is meant is this. When a representation that has the appearance of form arises from its own seed, a seed that has reached a specific transformation, then that seed and that appearance, those two, the Blessed One described, in order, as the eye-sphere and the form-sphere of that representation.  
> So too, up to the representation that has the appearance of the tangible: when it arises from its own seed that has reached a specific transformation, that seed and that appearance, those two, the Blessed One described, in order, as the body-sphere and the tangible-sphere of that representation. This is the intention.  
> What advantage is there in teaching in this way, by force of intention? There is entry into *pudgala-nairātmya* (the non-selfhood of the person).  
> For when it is taught in this way, those fit to be disciplined by the teaching of the person's non-selfhood enter into that non-selfhood.  
> They understand that from the two the sixfold consciousness proceeds, but that there is no one single seer, nor indeed one single thinker, and so on.

### Vvs 10 and Entry into *Dharma-Nairātmya*

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *anyathā punaḥ | deśanā dharmanairātmyapraveśaḥ | anyatheti vijñaptimātradeśanā | kathaṃ dharmanairātmyapraveśaḥ | vijñaptimātram idaṃ rūpādidharmapratibhāsam utpadyate na tu rūpādilakṣaṇo dharmaḥ ko 'py astīti viditvā | yadi tarhi sarvathā dharmo nāsti tad api vijñaptimātraṃ nāstīti kathaṃ tarhi vyavasthāpyate | na khalu sarvathā dharmo nāstīty evaṃ dharmanairātmyapraveśo bhavati | api tu | kalpitātmanā || 10 || yo bālair dharmāṇāṃ svabhāvo grāhyagrāhakādiḥ parikalpitas tena kalpitenātmanā teṣāṃ nairātmyaṃ na tv anabhilāpyenātmanā yo buddhānāṃ viṣaya iti | evaṃ vijñaptimātrasyāpi vijñaptyantaraparikalpitenātmanā nairātmyapraveśāt vijñaptimātravyavasthāpanayā sarvadharmāṇāṃ nairātmyapraveśo bhavati na tu tadastitvāpavādāt | itarathā hi vijñapter api vijñaptyantaram arthaḥ syād iti vijñaptimātratvaṃ na sidhyetārthavatītvād vijñaptīnāṃ ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Again, on another reading, the teaching is an entry into *dharma-nairātmya* (the non-selfhood of dharmas).  
> "On another reading" means: as a teaching of *vijñaptimātra* (representation-only).  
> How is it an entry into the non-selfhood of dharmas? By understanding that this alone is representation, arising with the appearance of dharmas such as form, whereas there is no dharma at all having the defining character of form and the rest.  
> "If then there is in every way no dharma, then this representation-only too does not exist; how then is it established?"  
> Entry into the non-selfhood of dharmas does not occur by teaching that there is absolutely no dharma at all. Rather, it occurs with respect to an imagined selfhood.  
> The own-nature of dharmas that childish people imagine, namely object, subject, and the like, it is with that imagined selfhood that their selflessness is intended, not with an inexpressible selfhood which is the object of the Buddhas.  
> Thus, since even representation-only is entered as selfless with respect to a selfhood imagined by another representation, the establishment of representation-only brings about entry into the selflessness of all dharmas, and not a denial of their existence.  
> Otherwise, for representation too, another representation would become its object. Then representation-only would not be established, because representations would have objective content.

## Editorial closing

This passage settles three things. First, external objects are not needed to explain regularity, shared appearance, or even efficacy; dream, preta-vision, and hell-experience suffice to show that karmically structured cognition can bear that explanatory weight. Second, Buddhist scriptural language about *āyatanas* is not thereby abandoned; it is reclassified as pedagogic discourse governed by *abhiprāya*. Third, the doctrine's point is soteriological: it loosens first the fiction of a single personal subject, then the fiction of self-grounded dharmas.

What remains open is equally important. Verses 11-15 take up the special refutation of atomic external-object theories. Verses 16-17 turn to perception and memory; verses 18-20 to intersubjective governance and moral efficacy; verse 21 to other-mind knowledge; verse 22 to the modest claim that the full depth of *vijñaptimātratā* is finally a Buddha's domain. The doctrinal compression of the same project appears later in *Triṃśikā* 17-25. Its most influential Indian opponents are visible in *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya* 2.2.28-32, Yāmuna's *Saṃvit-Siddhi* 416-439, and Udayana's *Ātma-Tattva-Viveka* chapter 3.
