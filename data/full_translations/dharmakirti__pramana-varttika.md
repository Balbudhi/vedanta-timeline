**By:** Dharmakīrti  
**Locus scope:** *Pratyakṣa*-pariccheda, opening definitional unit (`Pramāṇav_2.1–5` in the local corpus; often cited in modern scholarship as PV III 1-5 because of chapter-order variation)

## Editorial introduction

These five verses state the load-bearing thesis with which the *Pratyakṣa* chapter begins. Dharmakīrti first argues that *pramāṇa* (a means of valid cognition) must be twofold because the *meya* (what is to be validly known) is itself twofold. He then immediately specifies the distinction: what is genuinely capable of *arthakriyā* (causal efficacy, successful practical function) is *paramārthasat* (ultimately real), whereas what lacks that efficacy yet still figures in cognition and discourse is *saṃvṛtisat* (conventionally real). The paired terms that organize the rest of the chapter are thereby introduced in compressed form: *svalakṣaṇa* (particular, uniquely efficacious particular) and *sāmānyalakṣaṇa* (generalized or conceptual object). This is the natural opening unit of the chapter because verses 2.1-5 move from thesis, to compressed grounds, to definition, to objection, to first rebuttal without yet dispersing into the later technical debates about conceptual construction, self-awareness, and the exact definition of perception.

The philosophical force of the passage is considerable. Dharmakīrti does not begin by defining perception as non-conceptual; that comes later in the same chapter at `Pramāṇav_2.123` (`pratyakṣaṃ kalpanāpoḍham`). He begins instead by fixing ontology through efficacy. The distinction between the real and the merely conceptually posited is not first a semantic distinction but a practical one: what does work, and what only appears within cognition as if it were workable. The same compression appears in the *Nyāya-Bindu* (`NB 1.12-17`), where the *svalakṣaṇa* alone is said to be *paramārthasat* because a thing's nature is marked by efficacy. What remains open here, and is pursued later in the chapter (`Pramāṇav_2.51-63`, `2.123-170`), is how inference can still count as *pramāṇa* if its object is a *sāmānyalakṣaṇa* and not the ultimately real particular directly.

## Translation

### Pramāṇavārttika 2.1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *pramāṇaṃ dvividhaṃ meyadvaividhyāc chaktyaśaktitaḥ / arthakriyāyāṃ keśādir nārtho 'narthādhimokṣataḥ //*

**English (line-by-line):**
> A *pramāṇa* (means of valid cognition) is twofold, because the *meya* (the knowable object) is twofold, in virtue of efficacy and inefficacy.  
> With respect to *arthakriyā* (causal efficacy), hairs and the like are not objects, because there is no settled taking of them as objects.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| pramāṇam | pra-√mā / pramāṇa | nom./acc. sg. neut. | means of valid cognition |
| dvividham | dvi-vidha | nom./acc. sg. neut. adjective | twofold |
| meyadvaividhyāt | meya-dvaividhya | abl. sg. neut. | because of the twofoldness of the knowable |
| śaktyaśaktitaḥ | śakti-aśakti | ablatival adverbial formation | from efficacy and inefficacy; in terms of capacity and incapacity |
| arthakriyāyām | artha-kriyā | loc. sg. fem. | in respect of causal efficacy |
| keśādiḥ | keśa-ādi | nom. sg. masc. collective | hairs and the like |
| na | na | indeclinable | not |
| arthaḥ | artha | nom. sg. masc. | object; pragmatically relevant thing |
| anarthādhimokṣataḥ | an-artha-adhimokṣa | abl. sg. masc./neut. abstract | because of non-commitment to it as an object |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *meyadvaividhya* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *meyasya dvaividhyam*.
- *śaktyaśakti* [dvandva]: *śaktiś cāśaktiś ca*.
- *arthakriyā* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa, technicalized]: *arthasya kriyā*.
- *keśādi* [dvandva-like enumerative compound]: *keśaḥ ādiḥ yasya saḥ*.
- *anarthādhimokṣa* [upapada-tatpuruṣa with privative prefix]: *arthe adhimokṣaḥ*; negated as *an-artha-adhimokṣa*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- predicative subject: *pramāṇam*
- predicative qualifier: *dvividham*
- hetu / ablative ground: *meyadvaividhyāt*, *śaktyaśaktitaḥ*
- adhikaraṇa: *arthakriyāyām*
- negated predicative subject: *keśādiḥ*
- negated predicate: *arthaḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- no explicit finite verb; the verse operates through implied nominal predication (*pramāṇam ... [asti]*, *keśādiḥ na arthaḥ [asti]*)

**Note:**
The on-disk witness is visibly corrupt here (`mānaṃ`, `viṣayadvai...`, `athaṃkriyāyām`); the normalized reading given above follows the widely cited scholarly form of the verse. The philosophical point is not merely classificatory. Dharmakīrti ties the twofoldness of *pramāṇa* to a prior twofoldness in its object, and he states that distinction in terms of practical efficacy, not first in terms of language. The dismissal of *keśādi* refers to standard illusion-cases such as the “hairs” seen by an impaired eye: they may appear, but they do not enter successful practice as objects. Compare `NB 1.12-17`.

### Pramāṇavārttika 2.2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *sadṛśāsadṛśatvācca viṣayāviṣayatvataḥ / śabdasyānyanimittānāṃ bhāve dhīḥ sadasattvataḥ //*

**English (line-by-line):**
> And because there is similarity and dissimilarity, and because there is objecthood and non-objecthood,  
> and because, when a word and other auxiliary causes are present, cognition occurs or does not occur.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| sadṛśāsadṛśatvāt | sadṛśa-asadṛśa-tva | abl. sg. neut. | because of similarity and dissimilarity |
| ca | ca | indeclinable | and |
| viṣayāviṣayatvataḥ | viṣaya-aviṣaya-tva | abl. sg. neut. | because of objecthood and non-objecthood |
| śabdasya | śabda | gen. sg. masc. | of a word |
| anyanimittānām | anya-nimitta | gen. pl. neut. | of other causes / auxiliary conditions |
| bhāve | bhāva | loc. sg. masc. | when present; in the presence |
| dhīḥ | dhī | nom. sg. fem. | cognition |
| sadasattvataḥ | sat-asattva | abl. sg. neut. | because of occurrence and non-occurrence |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *sadṛśāsadṛśatva* [dvandva-tatpuruṣa]: *sadṛśasya asadṛśasya ca tvam*.
- *viṣayāviṣayatva* [dvandva-tatpuruṣa]: *viṣayatvaṃ cāviṣayatvaṃ ca*.
- *anyanimitta* [karmadhāraya/tatpuruṣa]: *anyāni nimittāni*.
- *sadasattva* [dvandva-tatpuruṣa]: *sattvaṃ cāsattvaṃ ca*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- the verse is an ablative chain supplying further grounds for 2.1
- genitive qualifier: *śabdasya*, *anyanimittānām*
- locative condition: *bhāve*
- notional subject: *dhīḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- no explicit finite verb; the verse continues the compressed inferential syntax of 2.1

**Note:**
The verse is syntactically elliptical and is best read as a continuation of the grounds stated in 2.1. The final member is difficult in the legacy corpus witness; I construe it as saying that the rise or non-rise of cognition depends on linguistic and other conditions. That matters because *sāmānyalakṣaṇa* is not merely “there” the way an efficacious particular is “there”; it is bound up with conditions such as word, exclusion, and conceptual prompting. The compressed ablatives prepare the explicit distinction of 2.3.

### Pramāṇavārttika 2.3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *arthakriyāsamarthaṃ yat tad atra paramārthasat / anyat saṃvṛtisat proktaṃ te sva-sāmānyalakṣaṇe //*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Whatever is capable of causal efficacy, that here is *paramārthasat* (ultimately real).  
> The other is said to be *saṃvṛtisat* (conventionally real). Those two are the particular and the general characteristic.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| arthakriyāsamartham | artha-kriyā-samartha | nom./acc. sg. neut. | capable of causal efficacy |
| yat | yad | nom./acc. sg. neut. relative pronoun | whatever |
| tat | tad | nom./acc. sg. neut. correlative pronoun | that |
| atra | atra | indeclinable | here; in this system |
| paramārthasat | paramārtha-sat | nom./acc. sg. neut. | ultimately real |
| anyat | anya | nom./acc. sg. neut. | the other |
| saṃvṛtisat | saṃvṛti-sat | nom./acc. sg. neut. | conventionally real |
| proktam | pra-√vac / prokta | past passive participle, nom./acc. sg. neut. | said; called |
| te | tad | nom. dual neut. | those two |
| sva-sāmānyalakṣaṇe | sva-lakṣaṇa + sāmānya-lakṣaṇa | nom. dual neut. | the particular and the general characteristic |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *arthakriyāsamartha* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *arthakriyāyāṃ samartham*.
- *paramārthasat* [karmadhāraya]: *paramārtha eva sat*.
- *saṃvṛtisat* [tatpuruṣa]: *saṃvṛtyā sat* / *saṃvṛtau sat*.
- *sva-sāmānyalakṣaṇe* [itaretara-dvandva, elliptic for *svalakṣaṇa-sāmānyalakṣaṇe*]: *svalakṣaṇaṃ ca sāmānyalakṣaṇaṃ ca*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- relative-correlative subject pair: *yat ... tat*
- locative frame: *atra*
- secondary subject: *anyat*
- predicative complement: *saṃvṛtisat*
- classificatory dual: *te ... sva-sāmānyalakṣaṇe*

**Verbal modality:**
- *proktam*: past passive participle (`kta`), passive-adjectival
- no explicit finite copula; predication is again implicit

**Note:**
This is the chapter's decisive ontological sentence. Dharmakīrti does not say that the conventionally real is sheer nothing; he says that it is the “other” with respect to the efficacious real. The pairing of *svalakṣaṇa* and *sāmānyalakṣaṇa* is restated in compressed prose at `NB 1.12-17`: the *svalakṣaṇa* alone is *paramārthasat*, while the *sāmānyalakṣaṇa* is the object of inference. Later in this same chapter, however, Dharmakīrti complicates the initial duality by arguing that the thing finally to be known is only the particular (`Pramāṇav_2.53-54`). That later refinement does not cancel the present distinction; it reinterprets how the second member functions epistemically.

### Pramāṇavārttika 2.4

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *aśaktaṃ sarvam iti ced bījāder aṅkurādiṣu / dṛṣṭā śaktir matā sā cet saṃvṛtyāstu yathā tathā //*

**English (line-by-line):**
> If it is said that everything is inefficacious, then consider sprouts and the like from seeds and the like.  
> If a power there is admitted because it is observed, then let it stand conventionally, however that may be.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| aśaktam | a-śakta | nom./acc. sg. neut. | inefficacious; not capable |
| sarvam | sarva | nom./acc. sg. neut. | everything |
| iti | iti | indeclinable | thus |
| cet | cet | indeclinable | if |
| bījādeḥ | bīja-ādi | abl. sg. neut. | from seed and the like |
| aṅkurādiṣu | aṅkura-ādi | loc. pl. masc./neut. | in sprouts and the like |
| dṛṣṭā | √dṛś / dṛṣṭa | past passive participle, nom. sg. fem. | observed |
| śaktiḥ | śakti | nom. sg. fem. | power; efficacy |
| matā | √man / matā | past passive participle, nom. sg. fem. | admitted; considered |
| sā | tad | nom. sg. fem. | that |
| cet | cet | indeclinable | if |
| saṃvṛtyā | saṃvṛti | instr. sg. fem. | conventionally; at the conventional level |
| astu | √as | loṭ, 3rd sg., parasmaipada, active | let it be |
| yathā | yathā | indeclinable | as; however |
| tathā | tathā | indeclinable | so; in that way |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *bījādi* [enumerative compound]: *bījaṃ cādiś ca*.
- *aṅkurādi* [enumerative compound]: *aṅkuraś cādiś ca*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- quoted predicate: *aśaktam sarvam*
- apādāna: *bījādeḥ*
- adhikaraṇa: *aṅkurādiṣu*
- kartṛ of the concessive predication: *śaktiḥ*
- karaṇa / epistemic basis: *dṛṣṭā* (observed)
- instrumental of standpoint: *saṃvṛtyā*

**Verbal modality:**
- *astu*: loṭ / parasmaipada / active
- *matā*, *dṛṣṭā*: past passive participles, adjectival

**Note:**
The verse answers an obvious objection: if only what is ultimately real is efficacious, how can ordinary causal talk about seeds and sprouts survive? Dharmakīrti's answer is not to deny such talk but to relocate it. Let the power ascribed there stand *saṃvṛtyā*; that is, let it stand at the conventional level. The force of *astu* is concessive, not celebratory. He grants the practical idiom while withholding ultimate status from the generalities through which that idiom is ordinarily framed.

### Pramāṇavārttika 2.5

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *sāsti sarvatra ced buddher nānvayavyatirekayoḥ / sāmānyalakṣaṇe 'dṛṣṭeḥ cakṣūrūpādibuddhivat //*

**English (line-by-line):**
> If that power exists everywhere, then there is no positive and negative concomitance for cognition.  
> For the general characteristic is not observed, just as an eye-cognition [does not apprehend a universal apart from] color and the like.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| sāsti | sā + asti | sandhied form: nom. sg. fem. pronoun + laṭ, 3rd sg., parasmaipada | that exists |
| sarvatra | sarvatra | indeclinable | everywhere |
| cet | cet | indeclinable | if |
| buddheḥ | buddhi | gen. sg. fem. | for cognition; of cognition |
| na | na | indeclinable | not |
| anvayavyatirekayoḥ | anvaya-vyatireka | gen./loc. dual masc. | of concomitant presence and absence |
| sāmānyalakṣaṇe | sāmānya-lakṣaṇa | loc. sg. neut. | in the general characteristic |
| adṛṣṭeḥ | a-dṛṣṭi / a-dṛṣṭa | abl. sg. fem./neut. abstractive use | because of non-observation |
| cakṣūrūpādibuddhivat | cakṣus-rūpādi-buddhi-vat | adverbial comparative formation | like the eye's cognition of color and the like |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *anvayavyatireka* [dvandva]: *anvayaś ca vyatirekaś ca*.
- *sāmānyalakṣaṇa* [karmadhāraya]: *sāmānyaṃ lakṣaṇam*.
- *cakṣūrūpādibuddhi* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *cakṣuṣo rūpādīnāṃ buddhiḥ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *sā* (in *sāsti*)
- adhikaraṇa / distributive field: *sarvatra*
- genitive reference: *buddheḥ*
- basis denied: *anvayavyatirekayoḥ*
- locus of non-observation: *sāmānyalakṣaṇe*
- hetu: *adṛṣṭeḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- *asti* (in sandhi as *sāsti*): laṭ / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
This is the first rebuttal to the concessive move of 2.4. If one diffuses “power” everywhere, one destroys the very possibility of tracking a cognition by *anvaya* and *vyatireka*, that is, by its presence where the relevant factor is present and its absence where that factor is absent. The reason is that the *sāmānyalakṣaṇa* is not itself given as a perceptual item. This anticipates the later discussion in `Pramāṇav_2.51-63`, where Dharmakīrti distinguishes the ultimately knowable particular from the secondary, concept-laden mode in which it is reached, and it coheres with `NB 1.12-20`.

## Editorial closing

What this opening unit settles is the chapter's governing axis. A cognition is not valid merely because something appears; it is valid in relation to an object that can be sorted by efficacy. From that sorting Dharmakīrti derives the paired categories of *svalakṣaṇa* and *sāmānyalakṣaṇa*, and with them the paired levels of *paramārthasat* and *saṃvṛtisat*. What is not yet settled is how the second member of the pair can still sustain genuine knowledge rather than mere error. That question is pressed later in the same chapter when Dharmakīrti refines the claim that there are two objects of knowledge (`Pramāṇav_2.51-63`) and when he defines perception proper as *kalpanāpoḍha* (`Pramāṇav_2.123-170`). The compressed restatement in *Nyāya-Bindu* (`NB 1.12-21`) shows that Dharmakīrti regarded this opening distinction as stable doctrine, even as its exact epistemic deployment required fuller defense elsewhere.
