## Editorial introduction

These seven verses are the natural middle-unit of the *Stotra-Ratna*'s theology of *prapatti* (surrender by taking refuge). Verses 1-19 had established the addressee: Nārāyaṇa as supreme Lord, possessor of inexhaustible auspicious qualities, ruler of creation, and object of Vedic praise. Verse 20 turns that cosmic theology toward the dependent soul. The Lord's acts of world-creation, preservation, dissolution, and liberation are described as his *līlā* (non-necessitous divine play), yet not as arbitrary display; they are said to follow his profound intention and to be Veda-grounded. Verse 21 then compresses the governing paradox: he is beyond speech and mind, yet also their sole ground; immeasurable in manifestation, yet singly bent toward compassion. Only after that theological preparation does Yāmuna place on the speaker's lips the decisive speech of the *ākiñcana* (one stripped of all resource): not morally accomplished, not self-knowing, not rich in devotion, and yet taking refuge.

This scope is exact because verses 22-24 alone, though famous, are not the whole movement. Verse 22 gives the surrender formula. Verse 23 places it under the pressure of karmic fruition. Verse 24 turns that pressure into discovery of the Lord as shore and of the self as the newly found recipient of compassion. Verses 25-26 then prevent a sentimental reading: the plea is juridical as much as affective, since the disgrace of one who has sought refuge would compromise the Lord's own status as protector; and the maternal simile makes dependence more basic than merit. In the larger architecture of the hymn, verses 27-29 extend this same relation into irreversible attachment to the Lord's feet, and verses 48-55 restate it in a still more concentrated mode of confession and self-offering. In Yāmuna's broader position, the passage is the devotional counterpart of the *Siddhi*-treatises: what the philosophical works argue about dependence on the supreme Person, the hymn here enacts as speech.

## Translation

### YStr 20

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tvadāśritānāṃ jagadudbhavasthitapraṇāśasaṃsāravimocanādayaḥ*  
> *bhavanti līlāvidhayaś ca vaidikās tvadīyagambhīramano'nusāriṇaḥ*

**English (line-by-line):**
> For those who have taken refuge in you, the creation, maintenance, and dissolution of the world, release from *saṃsāra* (transmigratory bondage), and the rest become modes of play;  
> and they are Veda-grounded, following the depth of your own intention.

- *bhavanti*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
The syntax is deliberately compressed. I take *vaidikāḥ* predicatively with the same subject as *līlāvidhayaḥ*: the Lord's cosmic and salvific acts are sportive, but not whimsical. Their being called *vaidika* means that divine play is not outside order; it is the very form in which the Veda's world-governing and liberation-governing intention is carried out. This framing matters for verse 22: surrender is not an escape from order, but the soul's right placement within it.

**Note:**
The verse is a carefully balanced theological hinge. The Lord is beyond speech and mind, yet also their sole ground; transcendent without being absent. The final epithet turns from majesty to *dayā* (compassion), and that is why the surrender of verse 22 is not irrational. Compare the earlier accumulation of divine qualities at YStr 17-19 and the later feet-centered attachment at YStr 27-29.

### YStr 22

- *'smi*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
This is the locus classicus of Yāmuna's *ākiñcanya* logic. The negatives are not ornamental self-abasement. They strip away the claimant qualifications by which one might imagine oneself entitled to salvation, and only then does the middle-voice *prapadye* occur. The grammar is decisive: the act of refuge is not the crowning achievement of an accomplished self, but the speech-act of one who confesses that he has nothing to advance. Compare the deeper confession of YStr 48-50 and, in reception, the use later made of this verse in Pillai Lokācārya's *Śrī-Vacana-Bhūṣaṇa* §83.

This is why the cry in the second line is not general devotional speech but a plea uttered at the point where karmic consequence has become unavoidable. Compare YStr 48, where the same pattern reappears in more imperative form.

- *hātum*: tumun-infinitive
- *jihāsati*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active desiderative

**Note:**
The simile prevents any reading of *prapatti* as a contract sustained only by successful reception. Dependence is prior to desert. Even imagined rejection does not annul attachment, just as the suckling child does not renounce the mother when pushed away in irritation. This verse therefore stabilizes the surrender of verse 22 against fluctuations of felt assurance. The immediate continuation at YStr 27-29 develops the same point through bee-and-lotus imagery and the language of irreversible relish.

---

## Editorial closing

What this passage settles is substantial. It settles that in Yāmuna's hymn *prapatti* is not an ornamental synonym for devotion, but a formally distinct act of refuge articulated from incapacity, guilt, karmic exposure, and total dependence. It also settles that divine grace is not presented as arbitrary interruption: verses 20-21 have already placed it inside the Lord's Veda-grounded governance and endless compassion. What the passage does not yet settle, because a hymn is not yet a later rahasya-manual, is the fully scholastic ranking of *prapatti* against *bhakti-yoga* and the later analysis of its constituent *aṅga*-s. Within the *Stotra-Ratna* itself, that unfinished work is taken forward immediately at YStr 27-29 through attachment to the feet, and again at YStr 48-55 through a second cycle of confession and self-offering. Within Yāmuna's own corpus, the same reliance on divine mediation ripens in the *Catuḥ-Ślokī*, especially verses 3-4 on Śrī's saving intercession. In later Śrīvaiṣṇava systematization, Rāmānuja's *Śaraṇāgati-Gadya* 1 and Vedānta Deśika's *Rahasya-Traya-Sāra* take up precisely the terrain this passage opens.
