## Editorial introduction

The first *pariccheda* of the *Iṣṭa-Siddhi* is where Vimuktātman fixes the grammar that the rest of the treatise will only elaborate. He is not yet answering every later objection in full. He is deciding what kinds of answer are even available. The present scope therefore gathers the five load-bearing loci already keyed in the corpus from that first *pariccheda*: the definition of *māyā* as *avidyā* and as *anirvacanīyā* (indeterminable), the statement of Vedānta's *viṣaya* (subject-matter) as *Brahman* (absolute reality), the claim that *jīvanmukti* (liberation while living) follows at the rise of self-knowledge, the determination that *avidyā* belongs to the Self alone, and the closing formula that whatever appears in error cannot be certified as either *bhāva* (being) or *abhāva* (non-being). These loci occur in the first *pariccheda* at printed pp. 35, 38, 77, 212, and 229 respectively.

This bounded cluster is the natural scope for the present file even though it is not a single contiguous page-run. The existing corpus keys all the decisive first-chapter theses here, while the whole chapter is too large for one translation file under the project's own ceiling. Read together, these passages show the argumentative spine of the chapter. They move from textual jurisdiction, to ontology, to soteriology, and then back to the locus-and-object problem of ignorance, before closing with the error-theory formula that later Advaita will repeat. What later Vivaraṇa and post-Vivaraṇa authors expand, they already inherit here. In this sense the first *pariccheda* is not a preface to Vimuktātman; it is his doctrinal matrix. Śrīharṣa's *Khaṇḍana-Khaṇḍa-Khādya* upodghāta, Vidyāraṇya's *Vivaraṇa-Prameya-Saṃgraha*, and Madhusūdana's *Advaita-Siddhi* do not replace these sentences. They work inside them.

## Translation

### IṢ 1.1, *Prapañcasyānirvacanīyatvam*, p. 35, line 1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *māyeti sadasadbhyām anirvacanīyā avidyocyate.*

**English (line-by-line):**
> By the term *māyā* (the power of appearance), *avidyā* (ignorance) is called *anirvacanīyā* (indeterminable) with respect to *sat* and *asat*.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| māyā | māyā | nom sg fem | māyā, the power of appearance |
| iti | iti | indecl | thus; "by the word ..." |
| sadasadbhyām | sat / asat | instr-abl du neut | by / from being and non-being |
| anirvacanīyā | anirvacanīya | nom sg fem | indeterminable in predication |
| avidyā | avidyā | nom sg fem | ignorance |
| ucyate | √vac | laṭ, 3sg, ātmanepada, passive | is called |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *sadasadbhyām* [dvandva]: *sat ca asat ca, tābhyām*.
- *anirvacanīyā* [negative-tatpuruṣa]: *na nirvacanīyā*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *avidyā*
- karaṇa / apādāna-delimitation: *sadasadbhyām*

**Verbal modality:**
- *ucyate*: laṭ / ātmanepada / passive

**Note:**
The opening definition is already polemical. Vimuktātman does not say that *māyā* is merely obscure or merely ineffable. He says that it fails the forced alternative of *sat* and *asat*. This is the first chapter's load-bearing refusal, and later Advaita repeats it in precisely this form; compare *Khaṇḍana-Khaṇḍa-Khādya*, upodghāta, and *Advaita-Siddhi*, *anirvacanīyatā-pariccheda*.

### IṢ 1.1, *Prapañcasyānirvacanīyatvam*, p. 35, line 3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**

**English (line-by-line):**
> Nor, again, can it be simply "it is not"; because, by its being different from both being and non-being, it is unfit to be spoken of in those terms.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |

| tadvacanānarhatvāt | tadvacanānarhatva | abl sg neut | because of unfitness for being described by those terms |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *sadasadvilakṣaṇatvena* [other]: *sat ca asat ca, tābhyāṃ vilakṣaṇatvena*.

This line supplies the reason for the previous denial. The force of *vilakṣaṇa* is not merely "different in some respect," but "different in kind from both poles under consideration." That is why the line matters for the project's standing prohibition against collapsing *mithyā* into *asat*.

### IṢ 1.1, *Prapañcasyānirvacanīyatvam*, p. 35, line 4

**Sanskrit (IAST):**

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| ataḥ | atas | indecl | therefore; for that reason |
| eva | eva | indecl | just; precisely |
| anirvacanīyā | anirvacanīya | nom sg fem | indeterminable |

### IṢ 1.9, *Jīvanmuktiḥ*, p. 77, line 1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *ato jñānodayakāle eva vidvān vimuktaḥ.*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Therefore, at the very moment when knowledge arises, the knower is liberated.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| ataḥ | atas | indecl | therefore |
| jñānodayakāle | jñānodayakāla | loc sg masc | at the time of the rise of knowledge |
| eva | eva | indecl | just; precisely |
| vidvān | vidvas | nom sg masc | the knower |
| vimuktaḥ | vimukta | nom sg masc, kta-participle | liberated |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *jñānodayakāle* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *jñānasya udayaḥ tasya kālaḥ tasmin*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- adhikaraṇa: *jñānodayakāle*
- kartṛ: *vidvān*

**Note:**
I adopt the locative *jñānodayakāle*, which is already presupposed by the corpus morphology and by the sentence's sense. The point is the immediacy thesis of *jīvanmukti*: liberation does not wait for bodily death. Compare *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya* 4.1.13-15, *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* 4, and later *Jīvanmukti-Viveka*.

### IṢ 1.140, *Jaḍe'py avidyāpādanam*, p. 212, line 3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *na tv anātmano 'nātmani ceti siddham.*

**English (line-by-line):**
> because, with respect to the being and the non-being of that whole appearing item, there is no *pramāṇa* (means of valid cognition),

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| tasya | tad | gen sg neut pron | of that |
| sarvasya | sarva | gen sg neut | of the whole of it |
| bhāve | bhāva | loc sg masc | with respect to being |
| abhāve | abhāva | loc sg masc | with respect to non-being |
| ca | ca | indecl | and |
| pramāṇābhāvāt | pramāṇābhāva | abl sg masc | because of the absence of a means of valid cognition |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *pramāṇābhāvāt* [negative-tatpuruṣa]: *pramāṇasya abhāvāt*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- sambandha: *tasya sarvasya*
- adhikaraṇa: *bhāve abhāve ca*
- apādāna / hetu: *pramāṇābhāvāt*

**Verbal modality:**
- no finite verb; causal phrase carried into the conclusion

**Note:**
The denial is epistemic in form and ontological in consequence. Since no *pramāṇa* secures either pole, the phenomenon cannot be straightforwardly certified as real or unreal. This is why Vimuktātman's theory is not simple skepticism: the appearance is admitted, but its ontological sorting is denied.

## Editorial closing

This first-pariccheda cluster settles four things. It settles that Vedānta's proper object is Brahman; that *māyā*-*avidyā* is neither *sat* nor *asat* but *anirvacanīya*; that liberation follows at the rise of self-knowledge; and that ignorance belongs to the Self alone rather than to some external non-self substrate. What it does not yet settle in full is the later, more technical unpacking of these claims: the exact mechanism of *cidābhāsa*, the comparative force of *avaccheda-vāda* and *pratibimba-vāda*, and the full interpretation of the *mahāvākya*s. Those are taken up further in the treatise's later chapters, especially the third chapter's treatment of the locus and object of ignorance and of *tat tvam asi*. Downstream, the same argumentative line reappears in *Pañcapādikā-Vivaraṇa*, varṇaka 4 (on *āśraya*-*viṣaya*), *Khaṇḍana-Khaṇḍa-Khādya*, upodghāta (on *anirvacanīyatā*), Vidyāraṇya's *Vivaraṇa-Prameya-Saṃgraha*, and Madhusūdana's *Advaita-Siddhi*, especially the *avidyā-pariccheda* and *anirvacanīyatā-pariccheda*. The later tradition does not abandon Vimuktātman's first-chapter theses. It merely makes them answer harsher questions.
