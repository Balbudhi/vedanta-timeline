## Editorial introduction

The third *pariccheda* of Madhva’s *Viṣṇu-Tattva-Vinirṇaya* takes up the third adjective of the opening benediction: Nārāyaṇa is *nirdoṣāśeṣasadguṇa* , free from every defect and possessed of an unexhausted plenitude of auspicious qualities. The argument is compact, but its compactness is deceptive. Madhva here settles three questions at once. First, he answers the classical avatāra-objection: if Viṣṇu is faultless, why does he appear as human, and why do narratives present him as subject to fatigue, wounds, grief, or ignorance? Second, he states in one of his sharpest short formulations that Viṣṇu’s qualities are not adventitious properties added to a bare substrate; *guṇa*, *kriyā*, and the rest are spoken of as his very *svarūpa*. Third, he coordinates this doctrine of intrinsic attributes with the language of non-dual *śruti*, insisting that *abheda* in such passages does not erase real structure but excludes hostile difference while leaving room for *viśeṣa*.

This is the natural extended unit because the chapter is formally complete, doctrinally self-contained, and expressly keyed to the maṅgala-śloka’s third predicate. The first *pariccheda* establishes that Nārāyaṇa is known only through right scripture; the second establishes his transcendence over the *kṣara* and *akṣara* orders; the third concludes the triad by fixing what sort of being that supreme Nārāyaṇa is. Its claims are not isolated. The defense of divine manifestation against imputations of defect should be read with *Mahābhārata-Tātparya-Nirṇaya* 2.84 on the Lord’s delusive display for the sake of unfit beings; the insistence on the reality of differentiating structure anticipates the more programmatic use of *viśeṣa* in *Brahma-Tarka* and in later Mādhva scholastic elaboration. What is short here becomes architectonic elsewhere.

## Translation

### 3.1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *varjitaḥ sarvadoṣair yo guṇasarvasvamūrtimān |  
> svatantro yadvaśāḥ sarve sa viṣṇuḥ paramo mataḥ ||  
> iti paramopaniṣadi |*

**English (line-by-line):**
> He who is devoid of all defects, whose very embodiment is the whole treasure of qualities, under whose control all beings stand, that Viṣṇu is held to be supreme.  
> Thus in the *Paramopaniṣad*.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| varjitaḥ | varjita (PPP from √vṛj/varj) | nominative singular masculine | excluded from, devoid of |
| sarvadoṣaiḥ | sarva-doṣa | instrumental plural masculine compound | by all defects; from all faults |
| yaḥ | yad | relative pronoun, nominative singular masculine | who |
| guṇasarvasvamūrtimān | guṇa-sarvasva-mūrtimat | nominative singular masculine compound | embodied as the whole plenitude of qualities |
| svatantraḥ | svatantra | nominative singular masculine | independent |
| yadvaśāḥ | yad-vaśa | nominative plural masculine compound | subject to whom; under whose control |
| sarve | sarva | nominative plural masculine | all |
| saḥ | tad | correlative pronoun, nominative singular masculine | that one |

| paramaḥ | parama | nominative singular masculine | supreme |
| mataḥ | mata (PPP from √man) | nominative singular masculine | regarded, held |

| paramopaniṣadi | parama-upaniṣad | locative singular feminine | in the *Paramopaniṣad* |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *sarvadoṣaiḥ* [tatpuruṣa]: *sarvaiḥ doṣaiḥ*.
- *guṇasarvasvamūrtimān* [bahuvrīhi]: *guṇānāṃ sarvasvaṃ mūrtiḥ yasya saḥ*.
- *yadvaśāḥ* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *yasya vaśāḥ*.
- *paramopaniṣadi* [karmadhāraya]: *paramā upaniṣat*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *sa viṣṇuḥ*
- karaṇa / apādāna nuance: *sarvadoṣaiḥ*
- adhikaraṇa: *paramopaniṣadi*

**Verbal modality:**
- *mataḥ*: past passive participial predicate; finite copula understood.

**Note:**
The verse is built on a relative-correlative frame: *yaḥ ... saḥ*. The grammar does not merely list predicates; it binds supremacy to faultlessness, plenitude of qualities, and universal lordship together.

### 3.2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *nityapūrṇākhilaguṇo nirdoṣaḥ sarvadaiva yaḥ |  
> svatantraḥ paramo viṣṇur janmamṛtyādivarjitaḥ ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> He who is eternally full in all qualities, and faultless at all times without exception,  
> that supreme and independent Viṣṇu is devoid of birth, death, and the rest.

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|

**Note:**
The adverbial force of *sarvadaiva* matters. Madhva is not saying merely that defects are absent in Viṣṇu’s essential state and present in manifestation; he denies them absolutely.

## Editorial closing

This chapter settles the immediate objection that avatāra compromises transcendence. Madhva’s answer is not that defects are somehow ultimately unreal in a way that swallows the narratives, but that the Lord’s manifestation is a real manifestation of his own eternal body, while the marks of suffering are displayed for delusive and pedagogical purposes. It also settles a second point with lasting systematic force: Viṣṇu’s *guṇa*s are not external predicates appended to a bare divine subject; they are spoken of as *svarūpa* itself, and the chapter therefore requires a doctrine of internal differentiability without decomposition. What remains open here is the full technical defense of that last claim. For that one must move to the scholastic elaborations signaled here by citation, especially *Brahma-Tarka*, and to later Mādhva work on *viśeṣa*. The avatāra question, meanwhile, is taken up again in narrative-theological register in *Mahābhārata-Tātparya-Nirṇaya* 2.84 and in the school’s later sub-commentarial literature on divine embodiment.
