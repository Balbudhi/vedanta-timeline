## Editorial introduction

This file cannot yet be what its title promises. The reason is plain and should be stated without embroidery: no primary Sanskrit text of Maṇḍana Miśra's *Vidhi-Viveka* is present in the local corpus from which these extended translations are to be made. The repository's own thinker record for Maṇḍana presently represents only the *Brahma-Siddhi*; it does not list *Vidhi-Viveka* among the engaged works. A second internal note, in `MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS_v2.md`, states the same thing more directly: *Vidhi-Viveka* is “not on disk” and is there used only through secondary literature, specifically Andrew Ollett's 2013 article on *bhāvanā*.

Even so, the importance of the missing work is not in doubt. On the evidence preserved within this repository, *Vidhi-Viveka* is Maṇḍana's major Mīmāṃsā treatment of *vidhi* (injunction) and *bhāvanā* (productive or enactive prompting), and it stands behind the later Vedāntic transformation of those categories in the *Brahma-Siddhi*'s *Niyoga-Kāṇḍa*. The clearest local primary pointer occurs in *Brahma-Siddhi* 2.90 prose (KKS 112), where Maṇḍana says: *tac cedaṃ vidhivivekabhāvanāvivekābhyām avagantavyam*, “and this is to be understood from the *Vidhi-Viveka* and the *Bhāvanā-Viveka*.” That remark settles one thing. The work is structurally load-bearing for Maṇḍana's theory of injunction. It does not settle another. Without the text itself, no exact chapter, clause-sequence, or lineation can be edited, translated, or grammatically unpacked in the present file.

### Indirect primary anchor

The following sentence from the locally available *Brahma-Siddhi* is the only on-disk primary locus that names the work:

**Cross-reference locus:** *Brahma-Siddhi* 2.90 prose continuation (KKS 112, in the local GRETIL transcription)

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tac cedaṃ vidhivivekabhāvanāvivekābhyām avagantavyam /*

**English:**
> And this matter is to be understood from the *Vidhi-Viveka* and the *Bhāvanā-Viveka*.

**Editorial significance:**
This line does not belong to *Vidhi-Viveka* itself. It belongs to the later *Brahma-Siddhi*. Its importance is evidential. It shows that Maṇḍana treats those earlier treatises as the proper place for fuller analysis of the injunction-theory presupposed in the *Niyoga-Kāṇḍa*. It therefore gives the most defensible statement of scope now available: once the primary text is added to the corpus, the first natural unit to translate should be the opening doctrinal block in which *vidhi* and *bhāvanā* are defined and distinguished.

## Editorial closing

What is settled by this file is negative but important. A responsible extended translation of *Vidhi-Viveka* cannot yet be made from the present local materials. What is also settled is the work's place in Maṇḍana's corpus: the *Brahma-Siddhi*'s *Niyoga-Kāṇḍa* explicitly points back to *Vidhi-Viveka* and *Bhāvanā-Viveka* for the deeper analysis of injunction. What remains open is everything that depends on the missing primary witness: the work's exact internal divisions, the lineation of the opening argument, the precise wording of Maṇḍana's bhāvanā-analysis, and the relation between that earlier Mīmāṃsā vocabulary and the later Vedāntic *prasaṅkhyāna* doctrine. Once a Sanskrit edition or scan is added under `materials/primary_texts/`, the immediate next loci to revisit are *Brahma-Siddhi* 2.85–95, where Maṇḍana reconstructs the theory of *vidhi*, and the broader *Niyoga-Kāṇḍa*, where the Mīmāṃsā inheritance is carried into Advaita.
