**By:** Gauḍapāda

## Editorial introduction

The present file covers three linked argumentative blocks: Āgama 1.13-18, Vaitathya 2.1-6, and Advaita 3.1-21. This is the smallest natural extended scope that contains all of Gauḍapāda's load-bearing loci for this work while still preserving the argumentative motion internal to each *prakaraṇa*. In 1.13-18 the text moves from the distinction between *prājña* (the deep-sleep self) and *turīya* (the "fourth") to the decisive claim that the awakened *jīva* knows the unborn non-dual. In 2.1-6 Gauḍapāda opens the *vaitathya* argument, that is, the argument that dream and waking share a common status of merely projected differentiation. In 3.1-21 he begins the positive exposition of *ajāti* (non-origination), first by subordinating *upāsanā* (meditative worship) to a lower standpoint and then by arguing that the unborn cannot really become multiple without ceasing to be unborn.

These units together show the architecture of the whole work more clearly than any single isolated cluster can. The first block gives the epistemic transition from ignorance to awakening; the second gives the parity-argument by which the ordinary assurance of waking experience is destabilized; the third gives the ontological thesis that the real is not merely one item among many but the unborn ground in which origination cannot finally obtain. The later verses of the work, especially MK 2.32, 3.31-39, 3.48, and the reprises in 4.22-31 and 4.57-58, are already compressed in germ here. Gauḍapāda's broader position is therefore not adequately described as a bare denial of the world; it is, rather, a graded argument that moves from phenomenological states, through critique of dual cognition, to the thesis that what is ultimately real cannot enter into production, alteration, or partition without contradiction.

## Translation

### MK 1.13

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *dvaitasyāgrahaṇaṃ tulyam ubhayoḥ prājñaturyayoḥ*  
> *bījanidrāyutaḥ prājñaḥ sā ca turye na vidyate*

**English (line-by-line):**
> In both, the non-apprehension of duality is the same, namely in the *prājña* and in the *turīya*.  
> But the *prājña* is joined to seed-sleep; and that is not present in the *turīya*.

|---|---|---|---|
| dvaitasya | dvaita | gen sg neut | of duality |

| ubhayoḥ | ubhaya | gen dual | of both |

**Note:**
The verse distinguishes *prājña* from *turīya* not by the presence of dual objects, since both lack dual apprehension, but by the persistence in *prājña* of a causal seed-state. This prepares the awakening language of MK 1.16.

### MK 1.14

### MK 1.17

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *prapañco yadi vidyeta nivarteta na saṃśayaḥ*

**English (line-by-line):**
> If the manifold world truly existed, it would cease; of this there is no doubt.

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *māyāmātram* [tatpuruṣa]: *māyā eva mātram*.

## Editorial closing

What this passage settles is the argumentative sequence by which Gauḍapāda reaches *ajāti*. First, *turīya* is distinguished from deep sleep not by a fresh object but by the removal of causal sleep itself (1.13-18). Second, waking loses its privileged realism when dream and waking are shown to share the same differentiating structure (2.1-6). Third, once plurality is redescribed as merely apparent differentiation, the unborn cannot coherently be said to become many without ceasing to be what it is (3.1-21).

What remains open here is the fuller phenomenology of mind and mind-cessation, and the explicit discipline by which the non-dual is stabilized in realization. Gauḍapāda takes those up later in MK 3.31-39 and 3.42-46, before giving the lapidary conclusion at 3.48. The causal critique is then resumed in a still more explicitly dialectical form in MK 4.22-31 and 4.57-58, where the same *ajāti* thesis is restated against a wider field of opponents.
