# Brahma-Siddhi — Extended Translation
**By:** Maṇḍana Miśra  
**Locus scope:** Original first kāṇḍa in its doctrinal spine: the maṅgala verse, the proem on Vedāntic *prāmāṇya*, the *ānanda* and *avidyā* hinge-passages of the Brahma-kāṇḍa, the concluding summary verse (*MBs* 1.3), and the Tarka-kāṇḍa incipit (*MBs* 1.4), i.e. the arc preserved in the corpus as the opening of the originally undivided first kāṇḍa.  
**Source edition transcribed from:** S. Kuppuswami Sastri (ed.), *Brahma-Siddhi*, Madras Government Oriental Series 4, Madras: Government Press, 1937, as represented in the GRETIL transcription (with a few older readings noted there).  
**Translator:** Pāṇinian-rich extraction by Codex 5.4 (reasoning=high), 2026-05-09.

---

## Editorial introduction
The opening movement of Maṇḍana's *Brahma-Siddhi* does not proceed by devotional overture and then, only afterward, by argument. The maṅgala verse itself is already doctrinal. The Brahman saluted there is one, unborn, deathless, self-luminous consciousness and bliss. The subsequent prose makes explicit what the verse has compressed: ordinary pleasure is only a delimited measure of intrinsic *ānanda*; plurality is not denied as a brute datum of experience but redescribed as *māyā*-conditioned appearance; *avidyā* is neither simply real nor simply unreal, and its locus is said to be the *jīvas*; and, even after *śabda*-generated ascertainment of Brahman-self-identity, entrenched contrary appearance may persist, so *tattvadarśanābhyāsa* remains necessary. The Tarka-kāṇḍa then opens with the epistemic formula that governs the sequel: perception is a *vidhātṛ* (positive establisher), not a *niṣeddhṛ* (universal canceller), and therefore has no right to defeat the Upaniṣadic teaching of non-duality.

This is the natural extended unit for the site because the keyed passages already concentrate here, and because they belong to one argumentative arc. The Brahma-kāṇḍa fixes Maṇḍana's characteristic positions on *brahman*, *ānanda*, *māyā*, and *avidyā*; the Tarka incipit states the epistemic sequel without which those claims would lack a defensible relation to perception. The same doctrinal cluster is what later differentiates Maṇḍana from Sureśvara's more austere *jñāna*-alone line. Compare *Brahma-Siddhi* 2.1-4, where Maṇḍana again insists that Upaniṣadic cognition arises from the sentence as sentence, and compare the later *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* polemic that rejects supplementary contemplative assimilation.

## Translation

### MBs 1.1 (maṅgala verse)

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *ānandam ekam amṛtam ajaṃ vijñānam akṣaram / asarvaṃ sarvam abhayaṃ namasyāmaḥ prajāpatim //* 

**English (line-by-line):**
> We reverence the Lord of creatures as bliss, as one, as deathless, as unborn, as consciousness, as imperishable.  
> We reverence him as not one item among all things, yet as all; and as free from fear.

| ekam | eka | acc. sg. neut. | one, single |

| ajam | aja | acc. sg. neut. | unborn |

| namasyāmaḥ | namas / namasy | 1st pl. pres. ind. Ā | we reverence, we bow to |
| prajāpatim | prajāpati | acc. sg. masc. | the Lord of creatures |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *prajāpati* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *prajānāṃ patiḥ* — the lord of creatures.

**Kāraka structure:**
- karman: *prajāpatim*
- kriyā: *namasyāmaḥ*
- predicate accusatives: *ānandam*, *ekam*, *amṛtam*, *ajam*, *vijñānam*, *akṣaram*, *asarvam*, *sarvam*, *abhayam*

**Verbal modality:**
- *namasyāmaḥ*: laṭ / ātmanepada / active-middle devotional present

**Note:**
The accusative cascade is not ornamental. It prefigures the prose argument by laying side by side the predicates that Maṇḍana will spend the opening kāṇḍa defending: unity, consciousness, bliss, freedom from fear, and the paradoxical formula that Brahman is not one object among objects and yet is all. The collocation *asarvaṃ sarvam* anticipates the later insistence that Brahman is not itself the manifold *prapañca* and yet is the reality of all that appears.

### MBs 1.1 (proem on Vedāntic authority)

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *vedānteṣu vipratipadyante vipaścitaḥ — kecid aprāmāṇyaṃ manyante, ātmanaḥ pramāṇāntarasiddhatve teṣām anuvādakatvam, asiddhatve saṃbandhāgrahaṇāt, apadārthatve vākyasyāviṣayatvāt, pravṛttinivṛttyanupadeśe cāpuruṣārthatvāt / anye tu pratipattikartavyatāprāmāṇyavyājenāprāmāṇyam evāhuḥ / anye tu karmavidhivirodhāt pratyakṣādivirodhāc ca śrutārthaparigrahe upacaritārthān manyante / tannirāsārtham ucyate — ānandam iti //* 

**English (line-by-line):**
> The learned are at variance over the Vedāntas. Some hold them to be non-authoritative.  
> If the self is already established by another *pramāṇa*, then the Vedāntic sentences are mere reiteration. If it is not established, then, because the relation is not grasped, the sentence cannot function. If there is no denotable referent, the sentence has no object. And if it gives no instruction toward action or cessation, it has no human point.  
> Others, under cover of granting authority only to what enjoins something to be cognitively carried out, declare them non-authoritative all the same.  
> Others, again, because of conflict with ritual injunction and because of conflict with perception and the like, take the scriptural meaning here in a secondary sense.  
> To remove those views, he says: “*ānandam* …”

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *vipaścitaḥ*, *kecit*, *anye*, *anye*
- karman: *aprāmāṇyam*, *upacaritārthān*

- prayojana: *tannirāsārtham*

**Verbal modality:**
- *vipratipadyante*: laṭ / ātmanepada / active-middle
- *manyante*: laṭ / ātmanepada / active-middle
- *āhuḥ*: liṭ / parasmaipada / perfect used as authoritative report
- *ucyate*: laṭ / ātmanepada / passive

**Note:**
This proem already fixes the polemical field. Maṇḍana does not argue first against Buddhists or Naiyāyikas in the abstract; he begins with three internal challenges to Upaniṣadic authority: redundancy, injunction-dependence, and conflict with other pramāṇas. Compare *MBs* 2.1-4, where he again insists that sentence-born cognition does not wait upon an additional injunction.

### MBs 1.1 (Brahman as self-luminous bliss)

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tasmād ātmaprakāśaprakṛṣṭātmasvabhāvam eva brahma iti yuktam / evaṃ ca laukikānanda etasya mātrā iti yujyate, avacchedāt / itaś cānandasvabhāva ātmā, parapremāspadatvāt //* 

**English (line-by-line):**
> Therefore it is fitting that Brahman is precisely of the nature of supreme self-luminosity.  
> And thus it is coherent to say that worldly pleasure is only a measure of this, because it is delimited.  
> And furthermore the self is of the nature of bliss, because it is the locus of supreme love.

- *laukikānanda* [karmadhāraya]: *laukikaḥ ānandaḥ*.
- *ānandasvabhāva* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *ānandaḥ svabhāvaḥ yasya saḥ*.
- *parapremāspadatva* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *parasya premaṇa āspadatvam*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *laukikānandaḥ* (for *yujyate*)
- sambandha: *etasya*
- hetu / apādāna: *avacchedāt*, *parapremāspadatvāt*
- viśeṣaṇa to *brahma*: *ātmaprakāśaprakṛṣṭātmasvabhāvam eva*

**Verbal modality:**
- *yujyate*: laṭ / ātmanepada / passive-middle of fitness

**Note:**
This is the decisive positive passage of the opening argument. Maṇḍana does not define *ānanda* as mere cessation of pain; he makes worldly pleasure derivative, delimited participation in a more primary intrinsic bliss. The appeal to *parapremāspadatva* is philosophically load-bearing: what is loved for its own sake must be intrinsically desirable, and therefore not merely painlessness.

### MBs 1.1 (plurality as māyā-conditioned appearance)

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *indro māyābhiḥ pururūpa īyate iti vyaktam eva nānārūpāvagamo māyānibandhano darśitaḥ / tathā vyakto nānātvaniṣedho — neha nānāsti kiñcana iti //*

### MBs 1.1 (avidyā as neither real nor unreal; of the jīvas)

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *nāvidyā brahmaṇaḥ svabhāvaḥ, nārthāntaraṃ, nātyantam asatī, nāpi satī / evam eveyam avidyā māyā mithyāvabhāsa ity ucyate / atyantāsattve khapuṣpasadṛśī na vyavahārāṅgam / tasmād anirvacanīyā / yat tu kasyāvidyeti, jīvānām iti brūmaḥ //* 

**English (line-by-line):**
> *Avidyā* is not the intrinsic nature of Brahman; it is not some other real thing; it is not absolutely unreal; nor is it simply real.  
> Just so, this very *avidyā* is called *māyā*, a false appearance.  
> If it were absolutely unreal, like a sky-flower, it would not enter into practical commerce.  
> Therefore it is *anirvacanīyā* (indeterminable).  
> And as for the question, “whose *avidyā* is it?”, we say: of the *jīvas*.

- *atyantāsattva* [tatpuruṣa]: *atyantam asattvam*.
- *khapuṣpasadṛśī* [tatpuruṣa]: *khapuṣpasya sadṛśī*.
- *vyavahārāṅga* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *vyavahārasya aṅgam*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- sambandha denied: *brahmaṇaḥ svabhāvaḥ*
- adhikaraṇa: *atyantāsattve*
- sambandha asserted: *jīvānām*
- kriyā: *ucyate*, *brūmaḥ*

- *ucyate*: laṭ / ātmanepada / passive

**Note:**
This is the clearest compressed statement of Maṇḍana's mature *avidyā* doctrine. The order matters. He first excludes identity with Brahman's nature, then excludes sheer external otherness, then excludes both *sat* and *asat*. Only after those exclusions does he license the technical term *anirvacanīyā*. The final first-person plural, *jīvānām iti brūmaḥ*, is not incidental; it marks the doctrinal fork later associated with the Bhāmatī line.

## Editorial closing
This opening arc settles four things for Maṇḍana. First, Brahman is intrinsically self-luminous and blissful, not merely pain-free. Second, plurality is admitted only as *māyā*-conditioned appearance, not as ultimate fact. Third, *avidyā* is *anirvacanīyā* and is said to belong to the *jīvas*. Fourth, sentence-born certainty is necessary but not, by itself, existentially final; entrenched contrary appearance may demand *tattvadarśanābhyāsa*.

What remains open here is the full epistemic defense of these claims against perception and against Mīmāṃsā insistence on injunction. Maṇḍana takes that up at once in *MBs* 1.4 and the ensuing Tarka-kāṇḍa, and then reformulates the relation between scriptural cognition, injunction, and further discipline in *MBs* 2.1-9. Later Advaita will divide on precisely these points: Sureśvara's *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* rejects the need for supplementary contemplative assimilation, while Vācaspati's *Bhāmatī* preserves a Maṇḍana-shaped concern with residual error and its removal.
