**By:** Nāgārjuna

## Editorial introduction

VV 21–29 is the first stretch of the *Vigrahavyāvartanī* in which Nāgārjuna stops merely turning back the opponent's charge and states, with compression, what the opponent has failed to understand. The charge from VV 1–20 is familiar: if all things are without *svabhāva* (own-being), then the sentence asserting that very absence must itself be without *svabhāva* and so incapable of negating anything. VV 21 answers that the emptiness of the statement is no defect, because its lack of own-being is precisely parallel to the lack of own-being in all other *bhāvas* (entities, occurrences). VV 22 then states the decisive identity: the dependent arising of things is what is called *śūnyatā* (emptiness). VV 23–27 defend the efficacy of an empty negation by appeal to illusion-logic: one illusion can cancel another without thereby becoming an essence. VV 28 makes explicit that this dialectic does not repudiate *saṃvyavahāra* (conventional transaction, ordinary discourse), and the adjacent prose gloss expressly invokes the MMK's two-truths discipline at MMK 24.10. VV 29 then reaches the famous anti-thesis culmination: if there were a thesis of Nāgārjuna's own, the fault would stick; but there is none.

This is the natural unit for an extended reader's translation because it contains the tract's central clarification in one argumentative arc: from the self-referential objection, through the equation of emptiness and dependent arising, to the denial that Madhyamaka advances a rival metaphysical *pratijñā* (thesis). The broader work continues into pramāṇa-critique at VV 30ff., but the present sequence already fixes the grammar of the whole polemic. It also shows how close this text stands to the MMK's central loci: compare especially MMK 24.10 and 24.18–19. A reader who understands VV 21–29 understands why Nāgārjuna thinks that the emptiness of the sentence "all things are empty" does not annul its dialectical force but explains it.

Editorial note: the local full-text witness contains minor corruption in the neighborhood of VV 22 and VV 29. The metrical text printed below follows the cleaner GRETIL kārikā transform where needed; the adjacent Yonezawa-based prose witness controls the sense.

## Translation

### VV 21

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *hetupratyayasāmagryāṃ ca pṛthak cāpi madvaco na yadi*  
> *nanu śūnyatvaṃ siddhaṃ bhāvānām asvabhāvatvāt*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Even if my statement is not in the aggregate of causes and conditions, nor separately either,  
> still the emptiness of entities is surely established, because they are without own-being.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| hetupratyayasāmagryām | hetu-pratyaya-sāmagrī | loc. sg. f. compound | in the aggregate of causes and conditions |
| ca | ca | indecl. | and |
| pṛthak | pṛthak | indecl. | separately |
| ca | ca | indecl. | and |
| api | api | indecl. | also / even |
| madvacaḥ | mad-vacas | nom. sg. n. | my statement |
| na | na | indecl. | not |
| yadi | yadi | indecl. | if / even if |
| nanu | nanu | indecl. | surely / indeed |
| śūnyatvam | śūnyatva | nom. sg. n. | emptiness |
| siddham | siddha | nom. sg. n. PPP | established |
| bhāvānām | bhāva | gen. pl. m. | of entities |
| asvabhāvatvāt | a-svabhāva-tva | abl. sg. n. | because of lack of own-being |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *hetupratyayasāmagryām* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa within a larger tatpuruṣa]: *hetūnāṃ pratyayānāṃ ca sāmagryām*.
- *asvabhāvatvāt* [nañ-tatpuruṣa + *tva*]: *asvabhāvasya bhāvaḥ; tasmāt*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- adhikaraṇa (concessively denied): *hetupratyayasāmagryām*, *pṛthak*
- sambandha: *madvacaḥ*, *bhāvānām*
- hetu: *asvabhāvatvāt*
- predicate: *śūnyatvaṃ siddham*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed; the verse uses elliptical predication with *siddham*.

**Note:**
The opponent's point had been that an empty sentence cannot negate anything. Nāgārjuna's reply is exact: if his sentence too lacks *svabhāva*, that confirms, rather than undermines, the general thesis of emptiness. The prose gloss immediately after the verse makes this explicit by saying that the statement, precisely as dependently arisen, is empty in the same way as the things of which it speaks.

### VV 22

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *yaś ca pratītyabhāvo bhāvānāṃ śūnyateti sā proktā*  
> *yaḥ pratītyabhāvo bhavati hi tasyāsvabhāvatvam*

**English (line-by-line):**
> And that dependent arising of entities, that is what is called emptiness.  
> For whatever comes to be dependently, of that there is lack of own-being.

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *pratītyabhāvaḥ* [avyayībhāva-like compression]: *pratītya bhāvaḥ*, "a being / arising that occurs dependently."
- *asvabhāvatvam* [nañ-tatpuruṣa + *tva*]: *asvabhāvasya bhāvaḥ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- sambandha: *bhāvānām*
- predicate identification: *pratītyabhāvaḥ ... śūnyatā iti sā proktā*

**Verbal modality:**
- *proktā*: PPP used predicatively
- *bhavati*: laṭ / parasmaipada / present

**Note:**
This is the doctrinal center of the selected unit. The adjacent prose commentary glosses the verse by saying that what depends on causes and conditions cannot be *sasvabhāva*; if it were so, it would not stand in dependence at all. The line is the prose-tract counterpart to MMK 24.18, though here the idiom is *pratītyabhāva* rather than the fuller *pratītyasamutpāda*. The local prose witness is damaged in pāda a; the kārikā reading adopted here is confirmed by the commentary's explanation.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ/predicate pair: *etat vākyaṃ ... na svābhāvikam*

- *nāsti*: laṭ / parasmaipada / existential-present

**Note:**

The prose commentary explains that asymmetry would arise only if the sentence were admitted to be *aśūnya* while all other things remained *śūnya*; Nāgārjuna refuses that move.

### VV 25

**Sanskrit (IAST):**

> This analogy, "like the prohibitive word *mā*," is not the example you can sustain.

- *śabdavat* [*-vat* comparative formation]: *śabda iva*.

- karaṇa: *tvayā*, *śabdena*
- sambandha: *śabdasya*

**Note:**
The opponent's thought is that the sentence "do not make a sound" uses a sound to stop a sound, so an empty sentence could similarly stop emptiness. Nāgārjuna's reply is that the cases are non-parallel: in the *mā* example one sound checks another sound, whereas here the point is not to negate emptiness but to negate the false imputation of *svabhāva*.

### VV 26

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *naiḥsvābhāvyānāṃ cen naiḥsvābhāvyena vāraṇaṃ yadi hi*

> If indeed there were a stopping of non-own-being things by means of non-own-being,

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *naiḥsvābhāvyanivṛttau* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *naiḥsvābhāvyasya nivṛttau*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- sambandha: *naiḥsvābhāvyānām*
- karaṇa: *naiḥsvābhāvyena*
- adhikaraṇa: *naiḥsvābhāvyanivṛttau*

**Verbal modality:**

This is the reductio against the *mā-śabda* analogy. If emptiness were itself what got negated, the result would be the restoration of *svabhāva*. But Nāgārjuna's point is exactly the opposite: the discourse of emptiness removes reification, not emptiness itself.

| doṣaḥ | doṣa | nom. sg. m. | fault |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- No major samāsa requires separate resolution in this verse.

**Kāraka structure:**
- subject: *pratijñā*, *doṣaḥ*
- sambandha: *me*, *mama*
- inferential transition: *tata*, *tasmān*

**Verbal modality:**
- *syāt*: विधिलिङ् / parasmaipada / conditional-optative
- *bhaved*: विधिलिङ् / parasmaipada / conditional-optative
- *nāsti*, *asti*: laṭ / parasmaipada / existential-present

**Note:**
This is the famous anti-thesis statement, but it should not be flattened into mere quietism. The adjacent prose says that if Nāgārjuna had a *pratijñā* in the opponent's sense, the earlier fault would follow; but since all things are empty and quiescent, no such thesis-character properly attaches to him. Compare VV 59, where the same point is restated, and compare the method implicit throughout MMK: the Mādhyamika advances *prasaṅga* (undesired consequence), not a counter-metaphysics with its own essence-bearing ground.

## Editorial closing

What this passage settles is the shape of Nāgārjuna's answer to the charge of self-referential incoherence. It settles, first, that the emptiness of the statement about emptiness is no embarrassment but part of the point (VV 21). It settles, second, that *śūnyatā* names dependent arising itself rather than a further reality behind it (VV 22). It settles, third, that conventional discourse remains available to Madhyamaka, and indeed indispensable to it (VV 28; compare MMK 24.10). And it settles, finally, that Nāgārjuna refuses to occupy the dialectical place of a positive *pratijñā* (VV 29).

What the passage does not yet settle is the full destruction of the opponent's pramāṇa-realism. That work is taken up immediately at VV 30–51, where the mutual dependence of pramāṇa and prameya is pressed into regress and circularity. The broader doctrinal horizon is then supplied by MMK 24.18–19 and, in compressed restatement, by the later Nāgārjunan verses of the *Śūnyatā-Saptati* and *Yukti-Ṣaṣṭikā*. The point of contact remains unchanged: whether intelligibility is preserved by *svabhāva*, or only by its surrender.
