**By:** Yāmunācārya

## Editorial introduction

Verses 14–18 form the natural closing unit of the *Īśvara-Siddhi*. The earlier movement of the work has already done two kinds of labor. Verses 2–3 restrict the scope of direct perception; verses 4–13 stage and answer objections to the inference from *kāryatva* (effect-status) to an intelligent cause. What remains, at the end, is not more preliminaries but the conclusion itself. Verse 14 reasserts that earth and the rest really do bear the marks of an effect. Verse 15 extends that claim by listing the ontological features under which the world stands in the same inferential class as familiar artifacts. Then verses 16–18 compress the positive *anumāna* (inference): the *jagat* (ordered world-process) is perceptible because it is a knowable entity; it follows one will because it is non-conscious; embodied conscious agents themselves act only under governance; therefore the contested world, composed of *cetana* (conscious beings) and *acetana* (non-conscious entities), has one principal person.

This is the right stopping-point because the argument here is architectonic. Yāmuna does not infer a bare first cause and then add personality by devotion. The conclusion is already *eka-pradhāna-puruṣa*, one principal person. That matters for the larger Viśiṣṭādvaita trajectory. The same asymmetry later becomes explicit in Rāmānuja's *Vedārtha-Saṃgraha* §§1–6, where Brahman is stated as the *antaryāmin* of the *cid-acid* whole, and in *Śrī-Bhāṣya* 1.1.1, where inquiry into Brahman is fixed as inquiry into the supreme personal Lord rather than into an indeterminate absolute. In Yāmuna, however, that later theological clarity is still in inferential compression. These five verses are where the compression is sharpest.

## Translation

### Verse 14

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *atra brūmo na kāryatvaṃ kṣityādau śakyanihnavam |*  
> *sabhāgatvāt kriyāvattvān mahattvena viśeṣitāt ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Here we say: the status of being an effect cannot be denied in earth and the like.  
> That is because they have parts, because they possess activity, and because they are marked by magnitude.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| atra | atra | indeclinable | here, at this point |
| brūmo | √brū | laṭ, 1 pl, parasmaipada | we say, we state |
| na | na | indeclinable | not |
| kāryatvam | kāryatva | nom/acc sg neut | effect-status, being an effect |
| kṣityādau | kṣiti-ādi | loc sg masc (collective) | in earth and the rest |
| śakyanihnavam | śakya-nihnava | nom/acc sg neut | fit to be denied, susceptible of concealment |
| sabhāgatvāt | sa-bhāga-tva | abl sg neut | because of having parts |
| kriyāvattvāt | kriyāvat-tva | abl sg neut | because of possessing activity |
| mahattvena | mahattva | inst sg neut | by magnitude, by largeness |
| viśeṣitāt | viśeṣita | abl sg neut, past passive participle used predicatively | because of being qualified or distinguished |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *śakyanihnavam* [karmadhāraya / gerundival]: *śakyo nihnavaḥ yasya tat*.
- *sabhāgatva* [bahuvrīhi-derived abstract]: *bhāgāḥ santi yasmin tat, tasya bhāvaḥ*.
- *kriyāvattva* [matup-derived abstract]: *kriyāvān iti tasya bhāvaḥ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- speaker / kartṛ of speech-act: implicit in *brūmo* ("we")
- karman of assertion: *kāryatvaṃ ... na śakyanihnavam*
- adhikaraṇa: *kṣityādau*
- hetu: *sabhāgatvāt*, *kriyāvattvāt*, *mahattvena viśeṣitāt*

**Verbal modality:**
- *brūmo*: *laṭ* / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
This verse answers the objection just stated at *Īśvara-Siddhi* 13 that earth, mountains, and oceans are too unlike ordinary crafted objects to count as effects in the relevant sense. Yāmuna's reply is exact. The inferential basis is not artisan-scale manufacture as such, but a cluster of objective marks: partite structure, activity, and measurable largeness. The world need not look like a pot in every respect; it must only fall under the same causal type.

### Verse 15

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tādṛśād eva mūrtatvād bāhyapratyakṣatānvitāt |*  
> *sasāmānyaviśeṣatvād ityādibhyo ghaṭādivat ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> And precisely because of corporeality of that very sort, because of being endowed with external perceptibility,  
> because of possessing universal and particular, and because of such further features, [its effect-status is established] like that of a pot and the like.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| tādṛśāt | tādṛśa | abl sg neut/masc | because of being of that kind |
| eva | eva | indeclinable | precisely, indeed |
| mūrtatvāt | mūrtatva | abl sg neut | because of corporeality, because of extended form |
| bāhyapratyakṣatānvitāt | bāhya-pratyakṣatā-anvita | abl sg neut, past passive participial adjective | because of being endowed with external perceptibility |
| sasāmānyaviśeṣatvāt | sa-sāmānya-viśeṣa-tva | abl sg neut | because of possessing universal and particular |
| ityādibhyaḥ | iti-ādi | abl pl | because of such features and the like |
| ghaṭādivat | ghaṭa-ādi | indeclinable comparative | like a pot and the like |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *bāhyapratyakṣatānvita* [tṛtīyā-tatpuruṣa]: *bāhyapratyakṣatayā anvitam*.
- *sasāmānyaviśeṣatva* [dvandva-based possessive abstract]: *sāmānyaṃ ca viśeṣaś ca staḥ yasmin, tasya bhāvaḥ*.
- *ghaṭādivat* [comparative indeclinable]: *ghaṭa-ādivat*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- hetu: *tādṛśāt mūrtatvāt*, *bāhyapratyakṣatānvitāt*, *sasāmānyaviśeṣatvāt*, *ityādibhyaḥ*
- dṛṣṭānta: *ghaṭādivat*
- carried-over predicate from the prior verse: *kāryatvaṃ na śakyanihnavam*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed; the verse remains syntactically dependent on verse 14's assertion.

**Note:**
The syntax is elliptical, but the argument is not. Verse 15 extends verse 14 by showing that the world is comparable to familiar effects not merely by partite structure but by a wider set of ontological marks: extendedness, external perceptibility, and determinate universal-particular constitution. Yāmuna is careful here. The inference rests on typed similarity, not on superficial resemblance.

### Verse 16

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *pratyakṣaṃ tat prameyatvāt padārthatvād ghaṭādivat /*  
> *ekecchānuvidhāyīdam acaitanyāt svadehavat ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> That is perceptible, because it is an object of knowledge, because it is an entity, like a pot and the like.  
> This follows one will, because it is non-conscious, like one's own body.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| pratyakṣam | pratyakṣa | nom/acc sg neut | perceptible, directly manifest |
| tat | tad | nom/acc sg neut | that, that world |
| prameyatvāt | prameya-tva | abl sg neut | because of being an object of knowledge |
| padārthatvād | padārtha-tva | abl sg neut | because of being an entity, a real item |
| ghaṭādivat | ghaṭa-ādi | indeclinable comparative | like a pot and the like |
| ekecchānuvidhāyi | eka-icchā-anuvidhāyin | nom/acc sg neut | conforming to one will |
| idam | idam | nom/acc sg neut | this |
| acaitanyāt | acaitanya | abl sg neut | because of non-consciousness |
| svadehavat | sva-deha | indeclinable comparative | like one's own body |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *prameyatva* [abstract]: *prameyasya bhāvaḥ*.
- *padārthatva* [abstract]: *padārthasya bhāvaḥ*.
- *ekecchānuvidhāyi* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *ekasyā icchāyā anuvidhāyi*.
- *svadehavat* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa within a comparative form]: *svasya deha iva*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- subject / pakṣa: *tat*, *idam* (the world under discussion)
- predicate 1: *pratyakṣam*
- predicate 2: *ekecchānuvidhāyi*
- hetu: *prameyatvāt*, *padārthatvāt*, *acaitanyāt*
- dṛṣṭānta: *ghaṭādivat*, *svadehavat*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed; the predicates are nominal.

**Note:**
This is the first half of Yāmuna's compressed four-step proof. The move from *prameyatva* and *padārthatva* to *pratyakṣatva* keeps the world within the same ontological field as ordinary knowable entities, and the move from *acaitanya* to one-will-governance introduces teleological subordination. The comparison to one's own body is decisive: a non-conscious complex is not self-directing; it is directed.

### Verse 17

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *ekenābhiṣṭhitāḥ kāryaṃ kurvate sarvacetanāḥ |*  
> *dehasambandhasāpekṣakāryakṛttvāt tvagādivat ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> All conscious beings, being governed by one, carry out activity.  
> That is because their agency depends upon connection with a body, like skin and the rest.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| ekena | eka | inst sg masc/neut | by one |
| abhiṣṭhitāḥ | abhi-√sthā | past passive participle, nom pl masc | governed, directed, presided over |
| kāryam | kārya | acc sg neut | work, activity |
| kurvate | √kṛ | laṭ, 3 pl, ātmanepada | perform, carry out |
| sarvacetanāḥ | sarva-cetana | nom pl masc | all conscious beings |
| dehasambandhasāpekṣakāryakṛttvāt | deha-sambandha-sāpekṣa-kārya-kṛt-tva | abl sg neut | because of possessing agency that depends on relation to a body |
| tvagādivat | tvak-ādi | indeclinable comparative | like skin and the rest |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *sarvacetanāḥ* [karmadhāraya]: *sarve cetanāḥ*.
- *dehasambandhasāpekṣakāryakṛttva* [tatpuruṣa-chain]: *dehasambandhaṃ sāpekṣaṃ kāryakṛttvaṃ yasya tat*.
- *tvagādivat* [comparative indeclinable]: *tvag-ādivat*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *sarvacetanāḥ*
- karaṇa / agent of participial governance: *ekena*
- karman: *kāryam*
- hetu: *dehasambandhasāpekṣakāryakṛttvāt*
- dṛṣṭānta: *tvagādivat*

**Verbal modality:**
- *kurvate*: *laṭ* / ātmanepada / active-middle
- *abhiṣṭhitāḥ*: past passive participle

**Note:**
Verse 17 is the bridge from inert order to sentient order. Yāmuna does not treat conscious agents as independent explanatory termini once they are embodied. Their very capacity to function in the world is body-conditioned, and body-conditioned agency is governance-conditioned. The example of skin and the other bodily members makes the point concrete: organs act, but not as self-ruling principals.

### Verse 18

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *ekapradhānapuruṣaṃ vivādādhyāsitaṃ jagat |*  
> *cetanācetanātmatvād ekarājakadeśavat ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> The world that is under dispute has one principal person.  
> That is because it has conscious and non-conscious entities as its constitution, like a realm under one king.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| ekapradhānapuruṣam | eka-pradhāna-puruṣa | nom/acc sg neut | having one principal person |
| vivādādhyāsitam | vivāda-adhyāsita | nom/acc sg neut | posited in the present dispute, the debated subject-matter |
| jagat | jagat | nom/acc sg neut | the world, the world-process |
| cetanācetanātmatvād | cetana-acetana-ātmatva | abl sg neut | because of having conscious and non-conscious as its nature |
| ekarājakadeśavat | eka-rājaka-deśa | indeclinable comparative | like a land with a single king |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *ekapradhānapuruṣa* [bahuvrīhi]: *ekaḥ pradhānaḥ puruṣo yasya tat*.
- *vivādādhyāsita* [saptamī-tatpuruṣa]: *vivāde adhyāsitam*.
- *cetanācetanātmatva* [dvandva-based abstract]: *cetanam cācetanaṃ cātmānaḥ svarūpaṃ yasya, tasya bhāvaḥ*.
- *ekarājakadeśavat* [comparative compound]: *eka-rājakaḥ deśa iva*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- subject / pakṣa: *jagat*
- predicate: *ekapradhānapuruṣam*
- viśeṣaṇa of subject: *vivādādhyāsitam*
- hetu: *cetanācetanātmatvād*
- dṛṣṭānta: *ekarājakadeśavat*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed; the inferential conclusion is presented as nominal predication.

**Note:**
This is the formal conclusion of the whole block, and the word *puruṣa* does real work. Yāmuna does not conclude merely to a principle, law, or impersonal intelligence. He concludes to one principal person. What has been settled is governance, unity, and personality together. What has not yet been supplied within these verses alone is the fuller scriptural identification of that person as Nārāyaṇa and the later Viśiṣṭādvaita specification of the *cit-acit* whole as his body; those developments are unfolded downstream in Rāmānuja's *Vedārtha-Saṃgraha* §§1–6 and *Śrī-Bhāṣya* 1.1.1.

## Editorial closing

This closing block settles the inferential core of the *Īśvara-Siddhi*. Yāmuna has shown, first, that the world cannot be exempted from *kāryatva*; second, that the world's non-conscious dimension implies subordination to one will; third, that embodied conscious agency is itself not explanatorily ultimate; and therefore, fourth, that the *jagat* as a *cetana-acetana* complex stands under one principal person. What remains open here is not whether there is such a ruler, but the fuller theological and scriptural articulation of who that ruler is and how the world stands to him. That fuller articulation is taken up later in the Śrīvaiṣṇava line: compare Rāmānuja's *Vedārtha-Saṃgraha* §§1–6 for the first explicit statement of Brahman as *antaryāmin* of the *cid-acid* whole, and *Śrī-Bhāṣya* 1.1.1 for the doctrinal fixing of Brahman-inquiry as inquiry into the supreme personal Lord. In that sense these five verses do not finish the system. They secure its causal and ontological hinge.
