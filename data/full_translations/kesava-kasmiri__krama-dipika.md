## Editorial introduction

This file does not translate a contiguous chapter, and that is deliberate. The *Krama-Dīpikā* is not a treatise that lays out its theology in one continuous *adhikaraṇa*; it is a ritual manual whose doctrinal claims are distributed across eligibility rules, mantra-exegesis, and *phalaśruti*. The natural extended unit for this work is therefore the doctrinal spine formed by the passages already marked as load-bearing in the site’s own apparatus: KD 1.4–1.5, 2.16–2.18, 6.4–6.5, and 8.95–8.96. Taken together, these verses state what the manual presupposes about access, grace, divine power, surrender, and liberation.

The sequence is tight. KD 1.4 universalizes the efficacy of the Gopāla-mantra across *varṇa* (class-order), *āśrama* (life-stage), and gendered social location; KD 1.5 immediately qualifies that openness by making *bhakti* (devotional participation), born of Acyuta’s grace, the basis of real eligibility before Hari and guru. KD 2.16–2.18 then turns from access to ontology: in the exegesis of *gopījana-vallabha*, Vallabha is not set over against his powers as an external ruler only; he is their indwelling supporter and pervader, and in 2.18 the aggregate of world-protecting *śaktis* (powers) is said to be *svānanya*, non-other than him. KD 6.4–6.5 shifts from doctrinal gloss to enacted religion: the Mukunda-mantra yields cognitive and poetic excellence, but its resolved form is explicitly one of *prapatti* (self-surrender), “I resort to the feet of Śrī Mukunda as refuge.” KD 8.95–8.96 gathers the result into the work’s mature promise: this mantra is pre-eminent among Vaiṣṇava mantras, and its proper performance breaks the knot of karma and leads to Murāri’s eternally pure station.

This is not the whole theology of Keśava-Kāśmīrī. It is, however, the minimum doctrinal arc without which the surrounding ritual architecture would be unintelligible.

## Translation

### KD 1.4

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *sarveṣu varṇeṣu tathāśrameṣu*  
> *nārīṣu nānāhvaya-janmabheṣu |*  
> *dātā phalānām abhivāñchitānāṃ*  
> *drāg eva gopālaka-mantra eṣaḥ ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Among all *varṇas*, and likewise among the *āśramas*;  
> among women, and among those marked by diverse names and natal designations;  
> this Gopālaka-mantra is the giver  
> of the fruits that are desired, and that quickly indeed.

|---|---|---|---|
| sarveṣu | sarva | loc pl | among all |

| nārīṣu | nārī | loc pl fem | among women |
| nānāhvaya-janmabheṣu | nānāhvaya-janmabha | loc pl masc | among those of diverse names and birth-designations |
| dātā | dātṛ | nom sg masc | giver |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *nānāhvaya-janmabheṣu* [mixed tatpuruṣa / coordinative compound]: *nānāni āhvayāni janmabhāni ca yeṣu teṣu*.
- *gopālaka-mantra* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *gopālakasya mantraḥ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- adhikaraṇa: *sarveṣu varṇeṣu*

**Note:**
Govindabhaṭṭācārya construes the verse very broadly, but he also limits the claim to the specific daśākṣara Gopāla-mantra under discussion, not to every mantra concerning Gopāla. His commentary explicitly raises the problem of mantras with *praṇava* and *svāhā* being given to a Śūdra and avoids that consequence by taking this verse as a statement of efficacy and scope for the present mantra-form. The social breadth claimed here reappears in practice-oriented form at KD 5.12.

### KD 1.5

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *nūnam acyuta-kaṭākṣa-pātena*  
> *kāraṇaṃ bhavati bhaktir añjasā |*  
> *tac-catuṣṭaya-phalāptaye tato*  
> *bhaktimān adhikṛto harau gurau ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Certainly, by the fall of Acyuta’s sidelong glance,  
> *bhakti* becomes the cause, directly.  
> Therefore, for the attainment of that fourfold fruit,  
> one possessed of *bhakti* is qualified with respect to Hari and with respect to the guru.

| añjasā | añjas | indecl | directly, straightforwardly |
| tac-catuṣṭaya-phalāptaye | tac-catuṣṭaya-phalāpti | dat sg fem | for the attainment of that fourfold fruit |

**Kāraka structure:**
- karaṇa: *acyuta-kaṭākṣa-pātena*
- kartṛ: *bhaktiḥ*
- predicative complement: *kāraṇam*

- *bhavati*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
The commentary identifies the “fourfold fruit” as the *puruṣārtha-catuṣṭaya*. More important than that commonplace identification is the order of dependence: grace first, *bhakti* second, and eligibility after that. Govindabhaṭṭācārya also draws out an implication not explicit in the verse’s surface wording, namely a practical non-separation of guru and deity in meditation. Later loci continue the same line: KD 2.7 makes the mantra a *bhakti*-augmenter, KD 6.8 promises steady *bhakti*, and KD 8.23 returns to the ideal of the *bhakti-namra* practitioner.

### KD 2.16

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *anayor gopī-janayoḥ samīraṇād āśrito vyāptyā |*

- *gopī-janayoḥ* [dvandva treated as a pair]: *gopī ca janaś ca tayoḥ*.

The printed source’s *āśritaḥ* is awkward in context; the commentary clearly reads the phrase as carrying the force of supportive indwelling, not mere “being resorted to.”

### KD 2.17

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *svāhety ātmānaṃ gamayāmīty atejase tasmai |*  
> *yaḥ kārya-kāraṇeśaḥ paramātmety acyutaikatā asya manoḥ ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> “‘Svāhā’ means: ‘I convey my very self to that radiance.’”  
> “‘He who is the lord of cause and effect is the Supreme Self’: thus the purport of this mantra is unity with Acyuta.”

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| svāhā | svāhā | indecl | “svāhā” |
| iti | iti | indecl | thus |
| ātmānam | ātman | acc sg masc | the self, my very self |
| gamayāmi | gam-aya (caus. of √gam) | 1 sg laṭ parasmaipada | I convey, I offer over |
| iti | iti | indecl | thus |
| atejase | atejas; commentary construes *sva-tejase* | dat sg neut | to that radiance |
| tasmai | tad | dat sg masc/neut | to that |
| yaḥ | yad | nom sg masc | who |
| kārya-kāraṇeśaḥ | kārya-kāraṇa-īśa | nom sg masc | lord of cause and effect |

| acyutaikatā | acyuta-ikatā / acyuta-ekatā | nom sg fem | unity with Acyuta |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *kārya-kāraṇeśaḥ* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *kāryasya kāraṇasya ca īśaḥ*.
- *acyutaikatā* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *acyutasya ekatā*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- karman: *ātmānam*
- sampradāna / goal: *atejase tasmai*
- kartṛ: implicit first person in *gamayāmi*
- kartṛ of nominal clause: *yaḥ kārya-kāraṇeśaḥ paramātmā*
- sambandha: *asya manoḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- *gamayāmi*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active, causative

**Note:**
Two textual points matter. First, the source reads *atejase*, but Govindabhaṭṭācārya unmistakably glosses *sva-tejase*, “to his own radiance,” and the translation follows that sense. Second, the sandhied phrase is best resolved as *acyutaikatā asya manoḥ*: the mantra’s purport is identity or non-separate union with Acyuta. The line therefore carries the self-offering force of *svāhā* far beyond mere liturgical closure.

### KD 2.18

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *athavā gopījana iti samasta-jagad-vana-śakti-samudāyaḥ |*  
> *tasya svānanyasya svāmī vallabha iti ha nirdiṣṭaḥ ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Or else, “gopījana” is the aggregate of the powers that protect the whole world.  
> Of that, which is non-other than himself, Vallabha is indeed indicated as the Lord.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| athavā | athavā | indecl | or else |
| gopījana | gopījana | quoted expression | “gopījana” |

| samasta-jagad-vana-śakti-samudāyaḥ | samasta-jagad-vana-śakti-samudāya | nom sg masc | aggregate of the powers that protect the whole world |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *samasta-jagad-vana-śakti-samudāyaḥ* [multi-member tatpuruṣa]: *samastasya jagataḥ avana-śaktīnāṃ samudāyaḥ*.
- *svānanyasya* [negative tatpuruṣa]: *svasmād ananyasya*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *samasta-jagad-vana-śakti-samudāyaḥ*
- sambandha: *tasya svānanyasya*
- predicative designation: *vallabha iti*
- kartṛ of passive predication: *svāmī ... nirdiṣṭaḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- no finite verb; passive predication through *nirdiṣṭaḥ*

**Note:**
The source’s *jagad-vana-śakti* is clarified by the commentary as *sakala-viśva-rakṣaṇa-śakti-samudāyaḥ*. The important doctrinal move is not the lexical detail alone but the phrase *svānanyasya*: the power-aggregate is not alien to the Lord whose lordship it manifests. Govindabhaṭṭācārya makes this explicit with *svābhinnasya* and even names the point as one of *śakti-śaktimator abheda-vivakṣā*. Within this ritual manual, that is as clear a statement of Nimbārka-line non-separateness as one could ask for.

This prepares the transition to the Mukunda-mantra not as a departure from the prior current, but as its equivalent in fruit and superior in explicit self-surrender.

### KD 6.5

In particular, *prapadya* here stands for the resolved *prapadye*.

> *śrī-mukunda-caraṇau sadā śaraṇam ahaṃ prapadye |*

- *śrī-mukunda-caraṇau* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *śrī-mukundasya caraṇau*.

- kartṛ: *ahaṃ*
- karman: *śrī-mukunda-caraṇau*
- predicative accusative: *śaraṇam*

- *prapadye*: laṭ / ātmanepada / middle

The middle voice matters: the self is not merely directed toward an object but implicated in the very act of surrender. Read with KD 2.17, the sequence moves from self-offering in the explanation of *svāhā* to explicit refuge-taking at Mukunda’s feet.

### KD 8.95

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *akhila-manuṣu mantrā vaiṣṇavā vīryavanto*  
> *mahitatara-phalāḍhyās teṣu gopāla-mantrāḥ |*  
> *prabalatara ihaiṣo 'mīṣu saṃmohanākhyo*  
> *manur anupama-sampat-kalpanākalpa-śākhī ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Among all mantras, the Vaiṣṇava mantras are possessed of power.  
> Among them, the Gopāla-mantras are richer in especially exalted fruit.  
> Among these, this so-called Saṃmohana-mantra is stronger still.  
> This mantra is a wish-tree branch for bringing forth incomparable prosperity.

**Word-by-word:**

The *kalpa-śākhī* image also matters: the mantra is not only powerful, but generative and branching in result.

**Note:**
This is the work’s clearest soteriological capstone. The commentary glosses *Murāri* etymologically as the destroyer of *murā*, taken as *avidyā*, but the root verse itself is content to speak of Murāri’s eternally pure *pada*. What is crucial in the grammar is the sequence of predicates attached to the practitioner: devotion, meditative enactment, severed karmic bondage, awakened consciousness, and only then arrival. The same trajectory is anticipated in KD 6.8, which promises unwavering *bhakti*, and restated in KD 7.155, where one who performs such practice reaches Viṣṇu’s world.

## Editorial closing

These passages settle five things. The *Krama-Dīpikā* treats the Gopāla-mantra as broadly efficacious; it makes divine grace the source of *bhakti* and *bhakti* the basis of eligibility; it reads the Lord’s powers as non-separate from their lord; it renders mantra-practice explicitly as *prapatti*; and it ends by promising not mere worldly advantage but Murāri’s eternally pure *pada*. What they do not settle is the full scholastic articulation of that ontology. Keśava states non-separateness here, but he does not in these verses develop the longer metaphysical defense that a commentary on scripture would require.

The work itself picks up these themes later. KD 2.22–2.24 continues the hierarchy and secrecy of the expanded Gopāla-mantras; KD 6.8 turns the Mukunda-mantra toward “steady *bhakti*”; KD 7.15 and 7.57 resume the logic of *śakti* and *śrī* in further mantra-formations; KD 7.155 and KD 8.23 restate the devotional and salvific end-state in more explicitly practical terms. The ritual manual thus does not abandon doctrine after these loci. It disperses doctrine into use.
