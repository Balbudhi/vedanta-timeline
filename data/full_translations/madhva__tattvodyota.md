**By:** Madhva  
**Locus scope:** Opening anti-*anirvacanīya* section: from **Maṅgalācaraṇam** through the first *śruti*-cluster under **Nāsadāsīd iti śrutyarthavicāraḥ**; i.e. up to **Iti Mādhyandinaśrutiḥ**, before **Arthāpattinirāsaḥ** begins

## Editorial introduction

This opening section of the *Tattvodyota* does not yet attempt the whole anti-Advaitin campaign. It does something more exact. Madhva first states the issue as one of *mukta-bheda*, difference even in liberation, and then blocks the opponent's most convenient escape-route: the proposal that the relevant difference is neither simply real nor simply unreal, but *anirvacanīya* (indefinable). The structure is deliberately austere. An inferential thesis is announced. The possible alternatives are narrowed. Then each candidate *pramāṇa* (means of knowledge) that might sustain the indefinable middle is stripped away in turn: perception, inference, and scripture. Only after this does Madhva turn, still within the same opening movement, to a first cluster of Vedic citations, especially *Ṛgveda* 10.129, in order to show that the familiar “neither sat nor asat” language does not license the ontological middle that Advaitins need.

This is the natural extended unit because the next heading, **Arthāpattinirāsaḥ**, begins a fresh dialectical stage. Up to **Iti Mādhyandinaśrutiḥ**, the argument remains one continuous task: to deny that *anirvacanīyatva* has any stable purchase in logic or scripture. In the larger tract, this section lays the groundwork for the later, more expansive interpretive and polemical moves. In Madhva's corpus more broadly, it stands close to the opening anti-*māyāvāda* drive of *Anuvyākhyāna* 1.1 and to the more concentrated assault of the *Mithyātvānumāna-khaṇḍana*. What is being defended here is not crude dualism, but Madhva's insistence that once *bheda* is given in experience and preserved in revelation, it cannot be dissolved by installing an allegedly ineffable middle-term.

## Translation

### Maṅgalācaraṇam

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *maṅgalācaraṇam*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Invocatory benediction.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| maṅgalācaraṇam | maṅgala-ā-caraṇa | neut. nom./acc. sg.; compound | auspicious invocation; benedictory opening |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *maṅgalācaraṇam* [tatpuruṣa]: *maṅgalasya ācaraṇam* — “the performance of an auspicious invocation.”

**Kāraka structure:**
- none: heading, not a finite sentence

**Verbal modality:**
- no finite verb; nominal heading

**Note:**
The heading announces function rather than doctrine. Madhva's polemical tract begins, as so often in the *Sarva-mūla*, by locating argument under auspicious theistic address.

### Maṅgalācaraṇa Verse, line 1

**Word-by-word:**

| oṃ | oṃ | indeclinable | Om |

| aśeṣadarśanaḥ | aśeṣa-darśana | nom. sg. masc.; compound | one whose seeing leaves nothing over; all-seeing |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**

**Verbal modality:**

**Note:**
The compressed diction already states the tract's metaphysical bias. The Lord is not an undifferentiated witness, but an independent knower whose power ranges over a field of genuinely real entities.

### Indefinability Is Nowhere Established

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *anirvacanīyasya sarvatrāprasiddhatvād eva।*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Precisely because the “indefinable” is nowhere established.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| anirvacanīyasya | anirvacanīya | gen. sg. neut./masc. | of the indefinable |
| sarvatra | sarvatra | indeclinable | everywhere |
| aprasiddhatvāt | aprasiddhatva | abl. sg. neut. | because of non-establishment; because it is unproved |
| eva | eva | indeclinable | precisely; indeed |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *anirvacanīya* [nañ-tatpuruṣa]: *na nirvacanīyam* — “not determinately sayable.”
- *aprasiddhatvāt* [taddhita abstraction]: from *aprasiddha* + *-tva* — “from the condition of not being established.”

**Kāraka structure:**
- hetu: *aprasiddhatvāt*
- genitive relation: *anirvacanīyasya*

**Verbal modality:**
- no finite verb; causal ablative continuing previous sentence

**Note:**
The argument is epistemic before it is ontological. Madhva's move is: if the category cannot be established by any accepted route, it cannot function as the hidden third option in the debate.

### Hare's Horn, Cow's Horn

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *śaśaviṣāṇaṃ goviṣāṇaṃ vocyata itivat।*

**Note:**
The contrast is sharp: one can utter both expressions, but only one has a real referent. Mere utterability does not secure an ontological middle term.

> *na ca dhūmatvavat mithyātvam ity ubhayasampratipannaṃ sāmānyam asti।*

> Nor is there, as there is in the case of smokiness, any universally admitted common property called “*mithyātva*.”

| mithyātvam | mithyātva | nom./acc. sg. neut. | falsity; mithyā-status |
| iti | iti | indeclinable | thus; called |

### No Scripture Teaches a Category Beyond Being and Non-Being

**Sanskrit (IAST):**

This is the thesis that the next citations must vindicate. Madhva's claim is not that scripture lacks paradoxical language, but that such language does not yield the exact ontological middle the opponent requires.

## Editorial closing

What has been settled in this opening section is narrow but decisive. Madhva has not yet proved every positive thesis of the tract. He has done something prior: he has denied the opponent a stable middle category between *sat* and *asat*, and he has denied that such a category can be retrieved by lexical stipulation, formal inference, or the first line of Vedic negation. That is why the argument matters. Once *anirvacanīyatva* is deprived of epistemic and scriptural standing, the later non-dualist assimilation of liberated self and Brahman loses its most useful protective screen.

What remains open here is everything that depends on further positive articulation: the more elaborate handling of “indescribable” language in later citations, the *arthāpatti* stage, and the explicit tightening of anti-*mithyātva* argument that Madhva will press elsewhere. Those later tasks are taken up immediately in the next section of the *Tattvodyota*, and across the corpus in the *Mithyātvānumāna-khaṇḍana*, the *Upādhi-khaṇḍana*, and the opening anti-*māyāvāda* run of *Anuvyākhyāna* 1.1. The present passage is therefore foundational rather than exhaustive.
