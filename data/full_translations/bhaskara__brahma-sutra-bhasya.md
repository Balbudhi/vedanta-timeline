## Editorial introduction

This file is necessarily a fallback dossier rather than a direct translation of Bhāskara's *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya*. No Sanskrit witness of the *Bhāṣya* itself is presently on disk in the repository's Vedānta corpus, its `full_corpus` subdirectory, or the associated corpus inventory. Under that constraint, the only honest procedure is to say so plainly, then translate a natural representative unit from the root *sūtras* that Bhāskara comments on and keep every Bhāskara-specific claim explicitly marked as reconstruction from secondary materials. The natural unit is the opening *Catuḥsūtrī* of *Adhyāya* 1, *Pāda* 1: `BS` 1.1.1-4. Any *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya* necessarily begins here, and the local secondary witnesses repeatedly treat this opening as the place where Bhāskara's anti-illusionist, causal, *bhedābheda* orientation first becomes legible.

The four *sūtras* set the program of the whole commentary. `BS` 1.1.1 opens the inquiry into *brahman* (the absolute). `BS` 1.1.2 defines *brahman* through the world's origin and continuation, a causal opening that later Bhāskara materials on disk press with unusual severity. `BS` 1.1.3 makes *śāstra* (scripture) decisive. `BS` 1.1.4 turns to *samanvaya* (systematic coordination of scriptural statements), and the project's internal Bhāskara materials repeatedly point to this locus when discussing his simultaneous insistence on *bheda* (difference) and *abheda* (non-difference). The larger shape of Bhāskara's position is then carried by later loci cited in the local dossier: `BSB` 2.1.14 on real transformation, `BSB` 2.3.43 on the real *upādhi* (conditioning adjunct) that differentiates *jīva* (individual self) from *brahman*, `BSB` 3.4.26-27 on *jñāna-karma-samuccaya* (the joint efficacy of knowledge and action), and `BSB` 4.4.1-7 on liberation. What follows therefore translates only what is actually extant on disk, and does not pretend to quote a Bhāskara Sanskrit text that the repository does not yet possess.

## Translation

The Sanskrit translated in this section is the root *Brahma-Sūtra* text, not Bhāskara's absent prose *bhāṣya*. Every Bhāskara-specific note below is therefore inferential and is restricted to what the local secondary materials actually support.

### BS 1.1.1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *athāto brahmajijñāsā*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Now, therefore, the inquiry into *brahman* begins.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| atha | atha | indecl. | now; at this juncture |
| ataḥ | atas | indecl.; ablatival adverb | therefore; from this prior condition |
| brahmajijñāsā | brahma-jijñāsā | nom sg fem; ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa compound with desiderative action noun from √jñā | inquiry into Brahman; desire to know Brahman |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *brahmajijñāsā* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *brahmaṇaḥ jijñāsā*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- embedded karman within the compound: *brahma*
- no overt finite predication; the sūtra is an ellipsis whose procedural force is "the inquiry is to be undertaken"

**Verbal modality:**
- No explicit finite verb occurs.
- *jijñāsā* is a desiderative action noun from √*jñā* with *san*, expressing the impulse or undertaking to know.

**Note:**
Because Bhāskara's own Sanskrit is unavailable on disk, one cannot responsibly specify how he glossed *atha* and *ataḥ* here, or whether he front-loaded later commitments into the opening sentence. What the local dossier does support is only the larger architectural point: Bhāskara's later defense of *jñāna-karma-samuccaya* at `BSB` 3.4.26-27 does not erase the priority of *brahma-jijñāsā*; it changes what counts as the full path appropriate to that inquiry.

### BS 1.1.2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *janmādyasya yataḥ*

**English (line-by-line):**
> *Brahman* is that from which are the origination and the rest of this [world].

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| janmādi | janma-ādi | nom/acc sg neut; enumerative compound | origination and the rest |
| asya | idam | gen sg neut | of this; of this [world] |
| yataḥ | yad | abl sg neut/masc; relative pronoun | from which; from whom |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *janmādi* [enumerative compound]: *janma-prabhṛti*; "origination and what follows from it."

**Kāraka structure:**
- sambandha: *asya*
- apādāna/source: *yataḥ*
- implied predicand: the *brahman* carried over from the preceding and following *sūtras*

**Verbal modality:**
- No explicit finite verb occurs.
- The sentence is a compressed relative expression with the copular predication understood: "that from which..."

**Note:**
This is the load-bearing causal *sūtra* for the local reconstruction of Bhāskara. The repository's Bhāskara profile and research note both treat the causal definition of *brahman* as the pressure-point that later forces Bhāskara toward real *pariṇāma* (transformation) and toward refusal of any reading that makes manifestation merely apparent. The direct textual proof for that stronger claim lies later, especially at `BSB` 2.1.14; but the local evidence is consistent that Bhāskara reads the opening causal formula more literally than an illusionist reading can comfortably allow.

### BS 1.1.3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *śāstrayonitvāt*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Because of being the source of *śāstra* (scripture).  
> The compressed wording also admits the traditional alternative: because scripture is its source of knowledge.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| śāstrayonitvāt | śāstra-yoni-tva | abl sg neut | because of being the source of scripture; or because of having scripture as source |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *śāstrayonitva* [compound with abstract *-tva*; grammatically ambiguous]: either *śāstrasya yoniḥ yasya tasya bhāvāt* ("because of being that whose source is scripture") or *śāstrasya yoniḥ* in the sense "source of scripture," with *-tvāt* marking the abstract state.

**Kāraka structure:**
- hetu: *śāstrayonitvāt*
- no overt finite clause; the ablative gives the reason for the preceding identification of *brahman*

**Verbal modality:**
- No explicit finite verb occurs.
- The ablative abstract *-tvāt* carries inferential or justificatory force: "because of the condition/property that..."

**Note:**
The local materials do not preserve Bhāskara's exact handling of this famous ambiguity, so any more determinate attribution would be fabrication. What can still be said is narrower and safer. The local dossier consistently places Bhāskara on the scriptural-realist side of Vedānta: *śāstra* matters because it does not dissolve the world-causal teaching into a merely provisional level. That broader orientation is confirmed later by the repository's references to `BSB` 2.1.14 and `BSB` 3.4.26-27, even though the Sanskrit argument for `1.1.3` itself is missing.

### BS 1.1.4

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tat tu samanvayāt*

**English (line-by-line):**
> But that [*brahman*] is to be established from systematic coordination.  
> That is, it is known from the consistent integration of the scriptural passages.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| tat | tad | nom/acc sg neut | that; that very thing, namely Brahman |
| tu | tu | indecl. | but; however; by contrast |
| samanvayāt | samanvaya | abl sg masc | from coordination; from coherent integration |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- None as a lexical *samāsa* in the line.
- *samanvaya* is a nominal formation built on *sam-anvaya*, "bringing together in a consistent sequence or fit."

**Kāraka structure:**
- topic/object of establishment: *tat*
- inferential or justificatory basis: *samanvayāt*

**Verbal modality:**
- No explicit finite verb occurs.
- The ablative again carries the force of ground or warrant: "from coordination," "on account of coherent integration."

**Note:**
This is the most important opening locus for Bhāskara that the local repository can presently identify, even without his Sanskrit prose. The internal Bhāskara profile and glossary materials repeatedly point to `BSB` 1.1.4 when stating that both *bheda* and *abheda* are scripturally unavoidable for him. The claim, more exactly, is not that identity-statements vanish, but that they do not erase real plurality or real causation. On the local evidence, this opening hermeneutic later issues in Bhāskara's realist reading of causality at `BSB` 2.1.14 and his insistence on real *upādhi* at `BSB` 2.3.43.

## Editorial closing

What this fallback passage settles is only the opening program of Bhāskara's *Bhāṣya*, not its completed doctrine. It settles that the local evidence requires us to begin Bhāskara from causality, scripture, and coordinated exegesis, and not from any world-negating reduction of plurality to mere seeming. It also settles that `BSB` 1.1.4 is the repository's own recurrent anchor for Bhāskara's paired insistence on *bheda* and *abheda*. What it leaves open is precisely what only the missing Sanskrit *bhāṣya* can settle directly: the detailed argument of `BSB` 2.1.14 on real transformation, the exact construal of real *upādhi* at `BSB` 2.3.43, the textual form of the *jñāna-karma-samuccaya* defense at `BSB` 3.4.26-27, and the grammar of liberation at `BSB` 4.4.1-7. Those later loci are named consistently in the local secondary dossier and should govern any future direct translation once a Sanskrit witness of the *Bhāskarīya* is added to the corpus.
