# Pañcapādikā — Extended Translation
**By:** Padmapāda  
**Locus scope:** *Pañcapādikā* V.15–VI.16, the *mithyājñāna* / *avidyāśakti* argumentative unit within Padmapāda's opening exposition of Śaṅkara's *Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya*. Because no complete primary Sanskrit edition of the *Pañcapādikā* could be verified on disk in this run, the scope is confined to the smallest natural continuous unit for which the local project preserves continuous Sanskrit.  
**Source edition transcribed from:** Local secondary Sanskrit transcription of *Pañcapādikā* V.15–VI.16 preserved in the project thinker data; checked against D. Venkataramiah, trans., *The Pancapadika of Padmapada* (Baroda: Oriental Institute, 1948), pp. 11–12.  
**Translator:** Pāṇinian-rich extraction by Codex 5.4 (reasoning=high), May 10, 2026.

## Editorial introduction

This brief unit is where Padmapāda's reading of Śaṅkara's opening *adhyāsa* analysis first becomes doctrinally irreversible. The immediate question is how the bhāṣya's phrase *mithyājñāna-nimittaḥ* is to be understood. Padmapāda refuses the easy reading on which the phrase would mean only that superimposition is caused by an ordinary erroneous cognition. He splits the compound into *mithyā* and *ajñāna*, takes the first member to indicate *anirvacanīyatā* (indeterminability), and then, decisively, takes the second to indicate a *jaḍātmikā avidyāśakti* (an ignorance-power whose nature is insentient). The result is not a mere sharpening of the theory of error. It is the beginning of a positive ontology of *avidyā*, offered as necessary if the appearance of false objects is to be intelligible at all. That is why V.15–VI.16 is the right extended scope here. It is a self-contained objection-and-reply sequence: first the compound is parsed, then the causal force of the parsing is stated, then the obvious objection is raised, namely how what is causally conditioned can nevertheless be called *naisargika* (beginningless, congenital, stream-like), and then the answer is given. The later architectonic loci of the work depend upon this move. At *Pañcapādikā* XIX.62–63 Padmapāda defines *adhyāsa* as *smṛtirūpaḥ paratra pūrvadṛṣṭāvabhāsaḥ*; at XXIX.108 he deploys the same ontology in reading *tat tvam asi* through the *bimba-pratibimba* relation. Those later moves would lose their ground if this earlier passage did not already force the admission of a positive *avidyāśakti*.

## Translation

### V.15.1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *mithyā ca tad ajñānaṃ ca mithyājñānam |*

**English (line-by-line):**
> That which is both *mithyā* (indeterminable, not straightforwardly real or unreal) and *ajñāna* (ignorance) is *mithyājñāna*.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| mithyā | mithyā | indeclinable | indeterminably; as *mithyā* |
| ca | ca | indeclinable | and |
| tad | tad | pronoun, nom./acc. sg. neut. | that; that very thing |
| ajñānam | ajñāna | nom./acc. sg. neut. | ignorance |
| ca | ca | indeclinable | and |
| mithyājñānam | mithyājñāna | nom. sg. neut. | *mithyājñāna*; indeterminable ignorance |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *mithyājñānam* [analytic re-reading of a compact compound]: *mithyā ca tad ajñānaṃ ca*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- predicative explication: *mithyā* and *ajñānam* are given as the two explanatory members of *mithyājñānam*.

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed; the line is a nominal exegetical equation.

**Note:**
Padmapāda begins by forcing the compound open. The point is not stylistic. Once the compound is analytically split, the passage can no longer be domesticated as if it concerned only a passing false cognition.

### V.15.2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *mithyeti anirvacanīyatocyate |*

**English (line-by-line):**
> By the word "*mithyā*," *anirvacanīyatā* (indeterminability) is stated.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| mithyeti | mithyā + iti | indeclinable expression; sandhi | "`mithyā`," thus |
| anirvacanīyatā | anirvacanīyatā | nom. sg. fem.; preserved under sandhi in *anirvacanīyatocyate* | indeterminability; inexpressibility |
| ucyate | √vac | laṭ, 3rd sg., passive, ātmanepada form | is said; is meant |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *anirvacanīyatā* [abstract derivative]: *na nirvacanīyā iti anirvacanīyā; tasyā bhāvaḥ anirvacanīyatā*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *anirvacanīyatā*
- viṣaya of lexical explication: *mithyeti*

**Verbal modality:**
- *ucyate*: laṭ / passive / ātmanepada form.

**Note:**
This line matters because it blocks the collapse of *mithyā* into sheer *asat*. Padmapāda is already working with the determinative pressure that later Advaita will formulate more systematically: the false object is not straightforwardly nothing.

### V.15.3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *ajñānam iti ca jaḍātmikāvidyāśaktiḥ*

**English (line-by-line):**
> And by the word "*ajñāna*" there is meant the *avidyāśakti* (power of ignorance) whose nature is insentient.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| ajñānam | ajñāna | nom./acc. sg. neut. | ignorance |
| iti | iti | indeclinable | thus; by this expression |
| ca | ca | indeclinable | and |
| jaḍātmikāvidyāśaktiḥ | jaḍātmikā-avidyā-śakti | nom. sg. fem. | the ignorance-power whose nature is insentient |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *jaḍātmikāvidyāśaktiḥ* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa with internal karmadhāraya force]: *jaḍātmikā yā avidyā, tasyāḥ śaktiḥ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- predicate nominative: *jaḍātmikāvidyāśaktiḥ*
- lexical trigger: *ajñānam iti*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb appears in this line; the predication is completed in the next line.

**Note:**
Here the ontological turn occurs. *Ajñāna* is not left as a mere blank or absence; it is read as a positive *śakti*. This is the load-bearing move later inherited by the *Vivaraṇa* line.

### V.15.4

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *jñānaparyudāsenocyate |*

**English (line-by-line):**
> It is stated by exclusion from knowledge.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| jñānaparyudāsena | jñāna-paryudāsa | instr. sg. masc.; preserved under sandhi in *jñānaparyudāsenocyate* | by exclusion of knowledge |
| ucyate | √vac | laṭ, 3rd sg., passive, ātmanepada form | is stated; is denoted |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *jñānaparyudāsaḥ* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *jñānasya paryudāsaḥ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- karaṇa: *jñānaparyudāsena*
- implicit kartṛ: the predicative content supplied from the previous line, namely *jaḍātmikāvidyāśaktiḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- *ucyate*: laṭ / passive / ātmanepada form.

**Note:**
The instrumental is philosophically exact. Padmapāda is not saying simply that ignorance equals the absence of knowledge. Rather, the exclusion of knowledge serves as the route by which a positive explanatory remainder, *avidyāśakti*, is marked out.

---

### V.15.5

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tannimittaḥ tadupādāna ity arthaḥ |*

**English (line-by-line):**
> The meaning is: it has that as its occasioning cause, and that as its material cause.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| tannimittaḥ | tad-nimitta | nom. sg. masc. | having that as cause; caused by that |
| tadupādāna | tad-upādāna | predicative nominal compound; cited in the transmitted line without further inflection | having that as material cause |
| iti | iti | indeclinable | thus |
| arthaḥ | artha | nom. sg. masc. | the meaning; the purport |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *tannimittaḥ* [tatpuruṣa]: *tat nimittaṃ yasya saḥ*.
- *tadupādāna* [tatpuruṣa]: *tat upādānaṃ yasya tat*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- predicative qualifier 1: *tannimittaḥ*
- predicative qualifier 2: *tadupādāna*
- metalinguistic subject: *arthaḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed; *arthaḥ* supplies the exegetical predication.

**Note:**
Padmapāda sharpens causal language here. The relevant order is not merely triggered by ignorance; it stands to it as to *upādāna* as well. That is why this passage became central in later disputes over whether Advaita had reified ignorance into a quasi-substantial causal principle.

### VI.16.1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *kathaṃ punar naimittikavyavahārasya naisargikatvam |*

**English (line-by-line):**
> But then how can a transactional order that is causally occasioned possess *naisargikatva* (beginningless, congenital status)?

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| kathaṃ | katham | indeclinable interrogative | how |
| punar | punar | indeclinable | then again; further |
| naimittikavyavahārasya | naimittika-vyavahāra | gen. sg. masc. | of a causally occasioned practical order |
| naisargikatvam | naisargikatva | nom./acc. sg. neut. | naturalness; congenital or beginningless status |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *naimittikavyavahāraḥ* [tatpuruṣa]: *nimitta-sambandhī vyavahāraḥ*.
- *naisargikatvam* [abstract derivative]: *nisarga-sambandhitvaṃ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- sambandha/genitive: *naimittikavyavahārasya*
- interrogated predicate: *naisargikatvam*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed; the line is an elliptical interrogative nominal clause.

**Note:**
This is the unavoidable objection. If superimposition is explained through a cause, why is it still called *naisargika* in Śaṅkara's opening discussion? The rest of the unit exists to answer that question without relinquishing either side.

### VI.16.2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *atrocyate |*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Here the reply is stated.

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| atra | atra | indeclinable | here; in this connection |
| ucyate | √vac | laṭ, 3rd sg., passive, ātmanepada form; preserved in sandhi as *atrocyate* | it is said; the answer is given |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**

### VI.16.3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *avaśyam eṣā avidyāśaktir bāhyādhyātmikeṣu vastuṣu*

**English (line-by-line):**
> This *avidyāśakti* must indeed be admitted with respect to external and internal things.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| avaśyam | avaśyam | indeclinable | necessarily; certainly |
| eṣā | etad | nom. sg. fem. | this |
| avidyāśaktiḥ | avidyā-śakti | nom. sg. fem.; preserved in sandhi as *avidyāśaktir* | the power of ignorance |
| bāhyādhyātmikeṣu | bāhya-adhyātmika | loc. pl. | in external and internal |
| vastuṣu | vastu | loc. pl. neut. | things; entities |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *avidyāśaktiḥ* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *avidyāyāḥ śaktiḥ*.
- *bāhyādhyātmikeṣu* [dvandva used adjectivally]: *bāhyeṣu ca adhyātmikeṣu ca*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- adhikaraṇa: *bāhyādhyātmikeṣu vastuṣu*
- subject to be qualified further: *eṣā avidyāśaktiḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- The line prepares the gerundive predication completed in the next line.

**Note:**
The reach of the claim is already visible here. The *avidyāśakti* is not confined to one odd case of illusion. It must be admitted across both outer and inner domains.

---

### VI.16.4

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tatsvarūpasattāmātrānubandhinī abhyupagantavyā*

**English (line-by-line):**
> It must be admitted as attached only to the bare existence that belongs to their very nature.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| tatsvarūpasattāmātrānubandhinī | tat-svarūpa-sattā-mātra-anubandhinī | nom. sg. fem. | attached only to the mere existence of that very nature |
| abhyupagantavyā | abhi-upa-√gam | gerundive, nom. sg. fem. | must be admitted; has to be conceded |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *tatsvarūpasattāmātrānubandhinī* [complex tatpuruṣa]: *tasya svarūpasya sattāmātre anubandhinī*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- subject/predicate complex: *tatsvarūpasattāmātrānubandhinī ... abhyupagantavyā*

**Verbal modality:**
- *abhyupagantavyā*: gerundive of obligation.

**Note:**
The gerundive is decisive: this is not an optional speculative gloss but a required admission. The restriction to *sattā-mātra* is equally important. Padmapāda does not say that ignorance veils every determinate object just as such; he ties it to sheer there-being, which is why the later tradition can connect the argument to the concealment and projection powers of *avidyā* without making every ordinary cognition destroy root-ignorance.

---

### VI.16.5

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *anyathā mithyārthāvabhāsānupapatteḥ |*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Otherwise, the appearance of false objects would be inexplicable.

**Word-by-word:**

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| anyathā | anyathā | indeclinable | otherwise |
| mithyārthāvabhāsānupapatteḥ | mithyā-artha-avabhāsa-anupapatti | abl. sg. fem. | because of the impossibility of accounting for the appearance of false objects |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *mithyārthāvabhāsānupapattiḥ* [extended tatpuruṣa]: *mithyasyārthasya avabhāsasya anupapattiḥ*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- hetu/ablative of reason: *mithyārthāvabhāsānupapatteḥ*

**Verbal modality:**
- No finite verb is expressed; the ablative supplies the reason for the required admission.

**Note:**
This is the argument's hard stop. Padmapāda is claiming explanatory compulsion: deny the positive *avidyāśakti*, and false-object appearance becomes unintelligible. The subsequent English discussion in Venkataramiah's pp. 11–12 develops the consequence further by distinguishing the non-manifestation of *brahman* from ordinary failures of object-cognition; but the decisive premise is already fully present here.

---

## Editorial closing

What this passage settles is the causal and ontological minimum of Padmapāda's doctrine of *adhyāsa*. *Mithyājñāna* cannot here be reduced either to sheer absence of knowledge or to a passing erroneous cognition; it names, or at least forces the admission of, a positive *avidyāśakti* required to explain the very possibility of false-object appearance. What it leaves open is equally important. The passage has not yet given the refined phenomenological definition of superimposition, and it has not yet shown how this ontology bears on liberating sentences. Those later steps come at *Pañcapādikā* XIX.62–63, where *adhyāsa* is defined as *smṛtirūpaḥ paratra pūrvadṛṣṭāvabhāsaḥ*, and at XXIX.108, where *tat tvam asi* is read through the *bimba-pratibimba* relation rather than through sheer annihilation of the *jīva*. Prakāśātman's *Pañcapādikā-Vivaraṇa* later amplifies precisely this V.15–VI.16 move into the more explicit *Vivaraṇa* account of positive *mūlāvidyā*. The present unit, then, does not complete Padmapāda's philosophy; it settles the premise without which the rest of it would not stand.
