## Editorial introduction

In the ninth chapter of the *Gītā-Bhāṣya*, after Kṛṣṇa has identified himself with sacrifice, offering, mantra, heat, rain, immortality, and death (9.16-19), Madhva turns to a stricter discriminative question: if all sacrificial efficacy already depends upon the supreme Lord, what exactly distinguishes exclusive devotion to him from Vedic heaven-seeking ritual and from the worship of other deities? Verses 9.20-29 are the natural unit because the argument here is continuous and architectonic. Verses 9.20-21 mark the instability of desire-driven ritual: heaven is real, but transient, and therefore cannot be the terminus of the text's theology. Verse 9.22 states the positive rule of *ananya-upāsanā* (exclusive worship). Verses 9.23-25 explain why worship directed elsewhere still reaches Kṛṣṇa, but reaches him *avidhipūrvakam* (without right cognitive form), because the worshipper fails to know him as sole enjoyer and lord of all sacrifice. Verses 9.26-28 then democratise access to the supreme object of worship: a leaf, a flower, a fruit, water, indeed any action at all, can be rendered salvific when offered as *mad-arpaṇa* (offering to me). Verse 9.29 closes the sequence by excluding arbitrariness. Divine reciprocity with devotees is not caprice or partiality; it is the fitting asymmetry of a relation knowingly entered.

This section is therefore a miniature of Madhva's broader position. It already contains the grammar of dependence that elsewhere appears in more explicitly Vedāntic form: Viṣṇu is the supreme *svatantra* (independent reality), others are effective only as *paratantra* (dependently real) agents or objects. The passage is especially important for reading Madhva against identity-mode construals of the *Gītā*: universality does not erase hierarchy, and divine immanence does not collapse lordship into numerical identity. The same pressure reappears later at *Gītā-Bhāṣya* 10.20, 15.15, and 18.65-66.

## Translation

### Bhagavad-Gītā 9.20

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *traividyā māṃ somapāḥ pūtapāpā yajñair iṣṭvā svargatiṃ prārthayante | te puṇyam āsādya surendralokaṃ aśnanti divyān divi devabhogān ||20||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Those possessed of the threefold Vedic learning, drinkers of Soma, their sins purified, having worshipped me by sacrifices, seek the heavenly goal.  
> They, having attained merit and the world of the lord of the gods, enjoy divine enjoyments in heaven.

**Note:**
Madhva will immediately refuse to deny the efficacy of Vedic ritual; the point is not that heaven is unreal, but that it is inferior because non-final. This is already a characteristic move in his anti-identity reading of the *Gītā*: hierarchy is preserved without reducing lower attainments to mere nullity.

### Bhagavad-Gītā 9.21

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *te taṃ bhuktvā svargalokaṃ viśālaṃ kṣīṇe puṇye martyalokaṃ viśanti | evaṃ trayīdharmaṃ anuprapannā gatāgataṃ kāmakāmā labhante ||21||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Having enjoyed that vast heavenly world, they enter the mortal world when their merit is exhausted.

- *martyalokam* [karmadhāraya]: *martyaḥ lokaḥ*.
- *trayīdharmaṃ* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *trayyā dharmaḥ*.
- *gatāgatam* [dvandva in lexicalised form]: *gataṃ cāgataṃ ca*.
- *kāmakāmāḥ* [upapada-like compound]: *kāmān kāmayante*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *te ... anuprapannāḥ ... kāmakāmāḥ*
- karman: *tam svargalokam*, *martyalokam*, *gatāgatam*

**Verbal modality:**

**Note:**
The decisive term is *gatāgatam*. Madhva does not need to deny heavenly fruition; he needs only to insist that cyclical return is categorically distinct from liberation. Compare his repeated insistence elsewhere that non-final attainments remain within dependence and are not to be confused with *aparokṣa-jñāna* or *mokṣa*.

### Gītā-Bhāṣya on 9.20-21

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tathāpi madbhajanam eva anyadevatābhajanāt varaṃ iti darśayati - traividyā ityādinā ||20-21||*

| Pada | Stem / root | Pratyaya / morphology | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| tathāpi | tathā api | indeclinable | even so, nevertheless |
| madbhajanam | mad-bhajana | nom./acc. sg. n. compound | worship of me |
| eva | eva | particle | precisely, alone |
| anyadevatābhajanāt | anya-devatā-bhajana | abl. sg. n. compound | than worship of other deities |
| varam | vara | nom./acc. sg. n. | better, superior |

| darśayati | √dṛś (caus.) | laṭ, 3 sg., parasmaipada | he shows |
| traividyā | traividya | citation form | "traividyā" |
| ityādinā | iti-ādi | instr. sg. n. compound | by the passage beginning with |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *madbhajanam* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *mama bhajanam*.
- *anyadevatābhajanāt* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *anyadevatānāṃ bhajanāt*.
- *ityādinā* [avyayībhāva-like citation compound]: *iti ādi yasmin tena*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: implied Kṛṣṇa / the verse-sequence as construed by the commentator
- karman: the clause *madbhajanam eva ... varam iti*
- apādāna: *anyadevatābhajanāt*
- karaṇa: *traividyā ityādinā*

**Verbal modality:**
- *darśayati*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
This is one of the clearest summary-statements in the passage. Madhva does not merely compare ritual paths; he ranks objects of worship. The comparative *varam* does philosophical work: the issue is not sincerity alone, but cognitive and ontological rectitude concerning the supreme recipient.

### Bhagavad-Gītā 9.22

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *ananyāś cintayanto māṃ ye janāḥ paryupāsate | teṣāṃ nityābhiyuktānāṃ yogakṣemaṃ vahāmy aham ||22||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Those persons who, thinking on me with no other object, worship me all around,  
> for them, who are ever steadfastly joined, I carry acquisition and preservation.

| aham | aham | nom. sg. pronoun | I |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *nityābhiyuktānām* [karmadhāraya/tatpuruṣa]: *nityaṃ abhiyuktāḥ yeṣāṃ teṣām*.
- *yogakṣemam* [dvandva in lexical pair]: *yogaṃ ca kṣemaṃ ca*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *ye janāḥ ananyāḥ cintayantaḥ* / *aham*
- karman: *mām*, *yogakṣemam*
- sambandha / recipient: *teṣām nityābhiyuktānām*

**Verbal modality:**
- *paryupāsate*: laṭ / ātmanepada / active-middle
- *vahāmi*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
Madhva's later glosses show that *ananya* and *nityābhiyukta* are the two load-bearing terms. The verse is not a vague commendation of piety; it specifies exclusive orientation and continuous relation. Compare *Gītā-Bhāṣya* 18.66, where surrender is likewise read as directed dependence, not identity-discovery.

### Gītā-Bhāṣya on 9.22, line 1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *ananyāḥ anyad acintayitvā | tathā hi gautamakhileṣu -*

**Word-by-word:**

|---|---|---|---|
| ananyāḥ | an-anya | nom. pl. m. | exclusive ones |
| anyat | anya | acc. sg. n. | anything else |
| acintayitvā | a-√cint / cintay | absolutive | having not thought |
| tathā | tathā | indeclinable | thus |
| hi | hi | particle | indeed |
| gautamakhileṣu | gautama-khila | loc. pl. n. | in the Gautama Khila texts |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *gautamakhileṣu* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *gautamasya khileṣu*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- viṣaya-karman: *anyat* (understood with *acintayitvā*)

**Note:**
Madhva takes the verse's exclusion literally. *Ananya* is not merely intense devotion; it is devotion that excludes rival intentional objects. The gloss is therefore epistemic before it is affective.

- *anyadevatābhaktāḥ* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *anyadevatānāṃ bhaktāḥ*.

- karman: *mām*

- *yajante*, *yajanti*: laṭ / ātmanepada and parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
This verse is central for Madhva. Universality is conceded: all worship terminates in Kṛṣṇa. But equality of all worship is denied by *avidhipūrvakam*. The issue is not emotional sincerity alone; it is right knowledge of the supreme recipient.

## Editorial closing

This passage settles four things. First, heaven-seeking ritual is effective but non-final (9.20-21). Second, all worship does in some sense terminate in Kṛṣṇa, yet not all worship is of one order, since cognition of the supreme recipient matters (9.23-25). Third, the smallest material offering, and then the whole field of action, can be transfigured when rendered as *mad-arpaṇa* (9.26-28). Fourth, divine equality is not cancelled by special reciprocity with devotees; rather, reciprocity is the form taken by a rightly known dependence-relation (9.29).

What remains open here is the larger metaphysical spelling-out of that relation. The chapter does not yet fully develop inner control, memory, and surrender. Those themes are taken up again at *Gītā-Bhāṣya* 10.20 (*aham ātmā guḍākeśa*), 13.2 (*kṣetrajñaṃ cāpi māṃ viddhi*), 15.15 (*mattaḥ smṛtir jñānam apohanaṃ ca*), and climactically 18.65-66. On the side of Madhva's wider corpus, the primacy of devotion as the path to direct realisation is picked up with greater technical explicitness at *Anuvyākhyāna* 3.3.1.
