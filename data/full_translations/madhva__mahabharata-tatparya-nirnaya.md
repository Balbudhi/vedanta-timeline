# Mahābhāratatātparyanirṇaya — Extended Translation
**By:** Madhva  
**Locus scope:** *Mahābhāratatātparyanirṇaya* 1.1–1.6, the opening maṅgala and pre-creation deliberation of the *sarvaśāstratātparyanirṇaya* chapter  
**Source edition transcribed from:** GRETIL plain-text / TEI transformation of the Dvaita.net input, legacy file `m_mbhtnu.htm`, TEI conversion dated 2020-07-31  
**Translator:** Pāṇinian-rich extraction by Codex 5.4 (reasoning=high), 2026-05-10.

## Editorial introduction

The *Mahābhāratatātparyanirṇaya* begins, significantly, not with epic summary but with a theological compression. The first chapter, explicitly titled *sarvaśāstratātparyanirṇaya*, aims to state what all scripture, rightly read, is about; and verses 1.1–1.6 already fix the grammar of that claim. Nārāyaṇa is introduced as *paripūrṇaguṇārṇava* (an ocean of fully perfect qualities) and as the giver of the universe's arising, maintenance, dissolution, and regulation. Creation is then presented as deliberate *vihāra* (sport, play), but not as arbitrary play. The crucial phrase is 1.3's *bhūtyai nijāśritajanasya*: the turn toward creation is for the good of those dependent upon him. That teleological claim governs the rest of the chapter.

This six-verse unit is the natural extended scope because it contains the complete prelude: invocation (1.1), pre-creational state (1.2), the divine glance toward creation (1.3), inspection of the jīvas (1.4), the explicit declaration of creation's purpose (1.5), and the first differentiation into the *Vāsudeva* and *Māyā* forms (1.6). The next verses (1.7–1.21) continue the same movement by unfolding the *vyūha* sequence and then the hierarchy of beings; later in the same chapter Madhva states the *pañcabheda* doctrine in overt form (1.67–1.70) and ties liberation to hierarchical dependence and *bhakti* (1.77–1.85). But the conceptual seed is here already. The Lord is active without compulsion; dependent beings are brought forth into manifestation for the unfolding of their natures; and liberation is something granted from above, not manufactured from below. That same asymmetry governs the longer epic theology of the work, including the *vāyu-tritaya* arc signalled in chapters 2 and 32.

*Text note:* the only local primary witness available for this work is the GRETIL / Dvaita.net electronic text, which contains occasional spacing and segmentation noise. The Sanskrit below regularizes only obvious faults such as `j ñāna` to `jñāna` and `nijāṇsrita` to `nijāśrita`; harder readings are left explicit and discussed in notes.

## Translation

### 1.1

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *nārāyaṇāya paripūrṇaguṇārṇavāya viśvodayasthitilayonniyatipradāya | jñānapradāya vibudhāsurasaukhyaduḥkhasatkāraṇāya vitatāya namo namaste ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> Reverence to Nārāyaṇa, to the ocean of completely perfect qualities, to the giver of the universe's arising, maintenance, dissolution, and regulation.  
> To the giver of knowledge, to the true cause of the happiness and suffering of gods and anti-gods, to the all-pervading one: obeisance, repeated obeisance.

| jñānapradāya | jñānaprada | `-āya`, dat sg masc | to the giver of knowledge |

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**

- *viśvodayasthitilayonniyatiprada* [itaretara-dvandva + tatpuruṣa]: *viśvasya udayaś ca sthitiś ca layaś ca niyatiś ca, teṣāṃ pradaḥ*.
- *vibudhāsurasaukhyaduḥkhasatkāraṇa* [complex tatpuruṣa]: *vibudhānām asurāṇāṃ ca saukhyasya duḥkhasya ca sat kāraṇam*.

**Kāraka structure:**
- sampradāna: *nārāyaṇāya*, *paripūrṇaguṇārṇavāya*, *viśvodayasthitilayonniyatipradāya*, *jñānapradāya*, *vibudhāsurasaukhyaduḥkhasatkāraṇāya*, *vitatāya*
- kriyā-artha salutation: *namo namaste*

**Verbal modality:**
- No overt finite verb. The verse is a dative litany governed by the salutation *namo namaste*.

**Note:**
The opening verse does doctrinal work immediately. Madhva does not begin with narrative ornament but with a definitional invocation. The cluster *udaya-sthiti-laya-niyati* anticipates the later explicit summary of divine functions at 1.82–1.83, while the dative sequence keeps all predicates directly tethered to Nārāyaṇa rather than distributing them across subordinate deities.

### 1.2

### 1.3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *tasyodarasthajagataḥ sadamandasāndrasvānandatuṣṭavapuṣo 'pi ramāramasya | bhūtyai nijāśritajanasya hi sṛjyasṛṣṭau īkṣā babhūva paranāmanimeṣakānte ||*

This verse is textually the hardest in the present scope. The witness is noisy, but the doctrinal center is secure: the divine turn toward creation is *for the welfare of dependants* (*bhūtyai nijāśritajanasya*), a point explicitly singled out in traditional paraphrase. The closing compound *paranāmanimeṣakānte* has therefore been translated cautiously rather than forced into spurious exactness.

### 1.5

**Note:**
This is the chapter's decisive teleological statement. Creation is *vihāra*, but the sport is not idle; it is ordered to the manifestation of the natures of embodied beings. Traditional Mādhva exegesis reads the phrase as meaning that saṃsāric embodiment furnishes the arena in which the latent constitution of each dependent being comes fully to expression, a line the chapter later develops through *taratamya*, *bhakti*, and liberative eligibility (1.71–1.90).

Madhva's handling of Māyā here is therefore already asymmetric and subordinate, not co-ordinate with the Lord.

## Editorial closing

What this passage settles is the point from which the whole chapter must be read. Creation is neither compelled nor accidental. It is a divine *vihāra* ordered toward the manifestation and sorting of dependent beings, and it arises from a beneficent glance cast for the welfare of those who belong to the Lord. It also settles, already at the threshold, the subordination of Ramā's secondary form called Māyā, and it introduces the Vāsudeva differentiation that the following verses carry forward.

What it does not yet settle is the full map of dependence. The graded hierarchy of beings is unfolded in 1.13–1.21; the scriptural proof-cluster begins at 1.22; the *pañcabheda* doctrine is stated overtly at 1.67–1.70; and the path of liberation through knowledge, hierarchy, and *bhakti* is formulated at 1.77–1.85. Beyond chapter 1, the same asymmetry becomes epic-historical in the *vāyu-avatāra* identification of chapter 2 and self-referentially closes in chapter 32's *vāyu-tritaya* declaration.
