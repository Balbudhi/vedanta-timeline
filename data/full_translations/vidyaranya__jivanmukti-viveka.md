# Jīvanmukti-Viveka — Extended Translation
**By:** Vidyāraṇya  
**Locus scope:** Caturtha-prakaraṇa, opening unit on the five *prayojanas* of *jīvanmukti* and the first of them, *jñāna-rakṣā* (Sanskrit text pp. 130–133 in the Adyar edition)  
**Source edition transcribed from:** Pandit S. Subrahmanya Sastri and T. R. Srinivasa Ayyangar (eds. and trans.), *Jīvanmuktiviveka of Vidyāraṇya*, rev. ed., Adyar Library and Research Center, 1978, Sanskrit text pp. 130–133.  
**Translator:** Pāṇinian-rich extraction by Codex 5.4 (reasoning=high), 2026-05-10.

## Editorial introduction

The fourth *prakaraṇa* asks a question that the first three do not yet answer. If *jīvanmukti* has already been shown to be scripturally grounded, causally attainable, and yogically stabilized, what is it for? Vidyāraṇya’s answer is not decorative. It is architectural. He states five *prayojanas* of embodied liberation: preservation of knowledge, austerity, absence of conflict, destruction of suffering, and manifestation of bliss. The present opening unit is the natural place to begin because it fixes the order of the chapter and gives the first argument for why liberation-in-life is not redundant once *tattva-jñāna* has arisen. Knowledge produced by a *pramāṇa* may still require protection, not because the *pramāṇa* was defective, but because the knower may not yet have attained *citta-viśrānti*, settled repose of mind. Without that repose, doubt and misapprehension can recur.

That claim is important for Vidyāraṇya’s larger position. It lets him preserve the strict Advaitin thesis that liberating knowledge is generated by knowledge alone, while also making room for the post-cognitive disciplines of *vāsanā-kṣaya* and *manonāśa* developed in the earlier chapters. The grammar of this opening unit does real philosophical work. The five purposes are not listed as optional embellishments but as predicates of accomplished *jīvanmukti*; and the objection is framed precisely in terms of *pramāṇotpanna-tattvajñāna*, knowledge already validly arisen. This is why the chapter opens here rather than with bliss. Vidyāraṇya first secures the continuing need for stabilization. Later in this same chapter he will unfold *tapas*, *avisaṃvāda*, *duḥkha-nāśa*, and *sukhāvirbhāva* in turn.

## Translation

### Caturtha-prakaraṇa opening (p. 130)

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *keyaṃ jīvanmuktiḥ, kiṃ vā tatpramāṇam, kathaṃ vā tatsiddhiḥ; ity etasya praśnatrayasyottaraṃ nirūpitam. siddhyā vā kiṃ prayojanam ity asya caturthapraśnasyottaram idānīm abhidhīyate — jñānarakṣā, tapaḥ, visaṃvādābhāvaḥ, duḥkhanāśaḥ, sukhāvirbhāvaś ca santi pañca prayojanāni.*

**English (line-by-line):**
> “What is this *jīvanmukti* (liberation while living)? Or what is the valid means of knowing it? Or how is its accomplishment to be understood?” The answer to this triad of questions has been set out.
>
> Now the answer to the fourth question, namely, “What purpose is served by that accomplishment?”, is stated: the safeguarding of knowledge, austerity, absence of discord, destruction of suffering, and manifestation of bliss are five purposes.

- *tatsiddhi* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *tasya siddhiḥ* — the accomplishment of that.
- *caturthapraśna* [karmadhāraya]: *caturthaḥ praśnaḥ* — the fourth question.
- *jñānarakṣā* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *jñānasya rakṣā* — preservation of knowledge.
- *visaṃvādābhāva* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *visaṃvādasya abhāvaḥ* — absence of contradiction or discord.
- *duḥkhanāśa* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *duḥkhasya nāśaḥ* — destruction of suffering.
- *sukhāvirbhāva* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *sukhasya āvirbhāvaḥ* — manifestation of bliss.

**Kāraka structure:**
- kartṛ: *jñānarakṣā tapaḥ visaṃvādābhāvaḥ duḥkhanāśaḥ sukhāvirbhāvaś ca*
- kriyā: *santi*
- pradhāna-predicative expression: *pañca prayojanāni*

**Verbal modality:**
- *nirūpitam*: past passive participle; completed exposition.
- *abhidhīyate*: laṭ / passive / ātmanepada-form; present statement in an exegetical register.
- *santi*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active.

**Note:**
The chapter opens not with bliss but with *prayojana*. That order matters. Vidyāraṇya first secures the intelligibility of post-cognitive discipline by showing that even accomplished *jīvanmukti* has functions. The list also governs the architecture of what follows in this chapter: *jñāna-rakṣā* first, then *tapas*, then social non-conflict, then the negative and positive affective fruits.

### Objection and reply on the need to protect knowledge (p. 130)

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *nanu pramāṇotpannasya tattvajñānasya ko nāma bādhaprasaṅgo yena rakṣāpekṣyeta iti ced ucyate: cittaviśrāntyabhāve saṃśayaviparyayau prasajyetām.*

**English (line-by-line):**
> If it be objected: “For knowledge of reality that has arisen from a valid means of cognition, what possible occasion of obstruction could there be, such that protection should be required?” the reply is this:
>
> In the absence of mental repose, doubt and error would arise.

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *pramāṇotpanna* [tṛtīyā-tatpuruṣa]: *pramāṇena utpannam* — arisen through valid cognition.
- *tattvajñāna* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *tattvasya jñānam* — knowledge of reality.
- *bādhaprasaṅga* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *bādhasya prasaṅgaḥ* — occasion for sublation, disturbance, obstruction.
- *rakṣāpekṣyeta* (conceptually): *rakṣāyā apekṣā syāt* — there would be need of protection.
- *cittaviśrāntyabhāva* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *cittasya viśrānteḥ abhāvaḥ* — absence of the mind’s settled repose.
- *saṃśayaviparyaya* [dvandva]: doubt and misapprehension.

**Kāraka structure:**
- sambandha: *pramāṇotpannasya tattvajñānasya*
- kartṛ (in the objection): *kaḥ ... bādhaprasaṅgaḥ*
- hetu/condition: *yena*
- locative condition in the reply: *cittaviśrāntyabhāve*
- kartṛ (reply): *saṃśayaviparyayau*
- kriyā: *prasajyetām*

**Verbal modality:**
- *rakṣāpekṣyeta*: liṅ / passive sense; hypothetical requirement.
- *ucyate*: laṭ / passive / ātmanepada-form; scholastic reply-formula.
- *prasajyetām*: liṅ / dual / middle; contingent recurrence.

**Note:**
This is the conceptual hinge of the passage. Vidyāraṇya does not weaken *pramāṇa*-generated knowledge; he distinguishes its rise from its stabilization. The threat is not that valid knowledge becomes invalid, but that without *citta-viśrānti* the knower remains vulnerable to *saṃśaya* and *viparyaya* at the level of lived mental functioning.

### Laghu-Yogavāsiṣṭha citation on *viśrānti* (pp. 130–131)

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *na rāghava tavāsty ajñeyaṃ jñānavatāṃ vara |*  
> *svayaṃ sūkṣmayā buddhyā sarvaṃ vijñātavān asi ||*  
> *bhagavadvyāsaputrasya śukasyeva matis tava |*  
> *viśrāntimātram evādya jñātasyāpekṣate ||*

**English (line-by-line):**
> O Rāghava, there is nothing unknown to you, best of the knowers.
>
> By your own subtle intellect you have come to know everything.
>
> Your understanding is like that of Śuka, the divine Vyāsa’s son.
>
> What has been known now requires only repose.

**Compound resolution (samāsa-vigraha):**
- *bhagavadvyāsaputra* [ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa]: *bhagavato vyāsasya putraḥ* — the venerable Vyāsa’s son.
- *viśrāntimātra* [karmadhāraya/tatpuruṣa nuance]: *viśrāntir eva mātram* — repose alone, nothing beyond repose.

**Kāraka structure:**
- संबोधन / vocative: *rāghava*, *jñānavatāṃ vara*
- kartṛ: *tvam* (understood through *asi* and *vijñātavān asi*)
- karaṇa: *sūkṣmayā buddhyā*
- karman: *sarvam*
- subject (second verse): *matiḥ tava*
- predicate comparison: *śukasya iva*
- subject (fourth pāda): *jñātasya* (genitive of relation with implied neuter content)
- object/predicate sought: *viśrāntimātram eva*

**Verbal modality:**
- *asti*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active.
- *vijñātavān asi*: periphrastic perfect-like completed cognition through past active participle + present *asi*.
- *apekṣate*: laṭ / ātmanepada / active-middle sense, “requires/awaits.”

**Note:**
The citation does exactly what Vidyāraṇya needs. It grants fullness of knowledge and yet insists on *viśrānti*. The philosophical point is not epistemic deficiency but post-epistemic settlement. The comparison with Śuka also foreshadows the broader Advaitic theme, developed further in this chapter, that degrees of mental rest differentiate forms of embodied liberation without compromising non-dual knowledge itself.

## Editorial closing

What this opening settles is the formal non-redundancy of *jīvanmukti*. The rise of *tattva-jñāna* does not by itself exhaust the problem, because unsettled mind can still admit the practical recurrence of doubt and distortion. What it does not yet settle is how that repose is to be deepened, graded, and socially or cosmically consequential. Vidyāraṇya takes those up immediately in the remainder of the fourth *prakaraṇa*: first the second *prayojana*, *tapas*, then *avisaṃvāda*, then *duḥkha-nāśa*, then *sukhāvirbhāva* (JMV IV, pp. 134–148 in this edition). The larger doctrinal frame is carried back into the third *prakaraṇa* on *manonāśa* and forward into the fifth on *vidvat-saṃnyāsa*. Within Vidyāraṇya’s wider corpus, the affective and experiential side of this same question is taken up at length in *Pañcadaśī* XI–XIV, where the gradations of *ānanda* are unfolded more analytically.
