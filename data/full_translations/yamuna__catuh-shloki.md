## Editorial introduction

Yāmuna's *Catuḥ-Ślokī* is short, but it is not slight. In four verses it fixes the structure of *Śrī-tattva*: Śrī is neither an ornamental consort nor a dispensable devotional intermediary, but the eternally inseparable and personally distinct partner of Nārāyaṇa, the one through whose *dayā* (mercy) the grammar of surrender becomes possible. The natural unit here is therefore the whole hymn, YCs 1-4. Any narrower selection would break the argument. Verse 1 situates Śrī cosmologically: the Lord, the serpent couch, Garuḍa, Māyā, and the gods are all redescribed from within her household. Verse 2 turns that cosmic placement into soteriology: if even the Lord does not exhaustively measure her greatness, the speaker may nevertheless approach her as *dāsa* (servant) and *prapanna* (one who has surrendered), because her defining trait is perpetual favorability. Verse 3 sharpens the claim: the saving efficacy of her gracious glance extends across the whole range of human destinations. Verse 4 then states the metaphysical nerve of the hymn: wherever Hari appears, in whatever form, Śrī is co-present in a corresponding form. The inseparability is not episodic.

This compact hymn stands in exact continuity with Yāmuna's larger position. The *Stotra-Ratna* gives the existential and liturgical voice of *prapatti* (especially YStr 22-24, 60); the *Siddhi* texts give the school its formal ontology and epistemology. The *Catuḥ-Ślokī* supplies what those works presuppose but do not isolate: the place of Śrī in the economy of grace. That is why later Śrīvaiṣṇava debates return to it with such intensity. Pillai Lokācārya's *Śrī-Vacana-Bhūṣaṇa* §§9-42 and Maṇavāḷa Māmunigaḷ's commentary read it as fixing Śrī's saving role in the strongest way; Vedānta Deśika's *Rahasya-Traya-Sāra*, especially the *puruṣakāra-vaibhava-adhikāra*, construes the same verses more narrowly. The hymn is thus brief in extent, but architectonic in consequence.

**Sanskrit (IAST):**

Who is Puruṣottama?" perform the same mutual specification from the other direction. The point is not mere ornament. It is ontological placement.

### YCs 2

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *yasyās te mahimānam ātmana iva tvadvallabho 'pi prabhur nālaṃ mātum iyattayā niravadhiṃ nityānukūlaṃ svataḥ*  
> *tām tvāṃ dāsa iti, prapanna iti ca stoṣyāmy ahaṃ nirbhayo lokaikeśvari lokanāthadayite dānte dayāṃ te vidan*

> Even your beloved, the Lord himself, is not equal to measuring your greatness as though it were his own, delimiting it by any fixed extent; for it is boundless and by nature ever favorable.

Later Śrīvaiṣṇava soteriology repeatedly treats this shift from theology to address as decisive.

### YCs 3

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *śreyo tvatkaruṇānirīkṣaṇasudhāsandhukṣaṇād rakṣyate naṣṭaṃ prāk tadalābhas tribhuvanaṃ saṃpraty anantodayam*

**Kāraka structure:**
- prayojya-subject of passive: *śreyaḥ*

**Note:**
The doctrinal pressure of the verse lies in the range-marker *saṃsṛtyakṣaravaiṣṇavādhvasu*. Later Śrīvaiṣṇava readers hear here the full field of possible human destinations, not merely one narrowly soteriological end. The word *akṣara* is therefore best left slightly open in translation: in later usage it often points toward the imperishable or *kaivalya* attainment, but the verse's own argument is broader. Whatever the end under discussion, Śrī's favor is not ancillary to it.

**Verbal modality:**
- *āhuḥ*: laṭ / parasmaipada / active

**Note:**
This is the most compressed and, for later tradition, the most consequential verse of the hymn. The grammar itself states inseparability across forms: wherever Hari appears, Śrī is there in a correspondingly fitting mode. Later Śrīvaiṣṇava exegesis will often hear in the sequence a taxonomy of *para*, manifest, and sportive forms, but the verse does not force a rigid scholastic mapping by grammar alone. What it certainly does force is co-presence. That is enough to underwrite the later argument, conducted differently by Deśika and by Pillai Lokācārya, over whether Śrī's role is to be construed as *puruṣakāra* (mediating intercession) only, or as a stronger saving accompaniment.

## Editorial closing

This passage settles three things. First, Śrī is not external to Nārāyaṇa's being or work; she is inseparable from him across the whole range of his manifestation. Second, her defining trait is not bare majesty but intrinsic favorability, *nityānukūlatā*. Third, surrender is grammatically licensed through her: the speaker may say *dāsa* and *prapanna* because mercy is already on her side. What the hymn does not settle is the later scholastic question of exact soteriological classification. It does not, by bare grammar alone, decide whether Śrī is to be called an *upāya* in the same strong sense as Nārāyaṇa, or whether she is more exactly *puruṣakāra*. That later question is taken up explicitly in Pillai Lokācārya's *Śrī-Vacana-Bhūṣaṇa* §§9-42 and, on the Vaḍakalai side, in Vedānta Deśika's *Rahasya-Traya-Sāra* *puruṣakāra-vaibhava-adhikāra*. Yāmuna's own devotional continuation is easier to state: *Stotra-Ratna* 22-24 and 60 give the existential voice of the same theology. The four verses here provide its concentrated doctrinal base.
