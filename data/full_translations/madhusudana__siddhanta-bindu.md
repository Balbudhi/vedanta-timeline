## Editorial introduction

This opening composite unit states, in compressed form, the two theses without which the rest of the *Siddhānta-Bindu* cannot be read correctly. The *Upodghāta* argues, first, that the reality of the self (*ātmatattva*) is not uselessly familiar merely because ordinary persons say “I”; what is familiar there is a self mixed with body, sense-organs, and mind. Hence the decisive *pramāṇa* is the Vedāntic *mahāvākya* itself, above all “*tattvam asi*” and “*ahaṃ brahmāsmi*,” working through prior ascertainment of the significanda of *tat* and *tvam*. The same preface then adds a second point of method: although the Upaniṣadic sentence is intrinsically valid, philosophical inquiry remains necessary because doubt generated by rival schools obstructs the rise of liberating certainty. That is the place of the *Upodghāta* within the work.

The second locus, taken from the prose under verse 1 in the *Prathamo Vibhāgaḥ*, supplies the first settled doctrinal result of that inquiry. After rival accounts of the self have been canvassed and rejected, Madhusūdana says what must be posited if the ordinary erroneous complex “I am a man, an agent, an enjoyer” is to be explained at all: an *anirvācyam ajñānam* (indefinable ignorance), not a mere absence of knowledge, directly attested in the witness-consciousness by the cognition “I do not know.” The linkage is exact. The *mahāvākya* is the *pramāṇa* because what binds is neither non-being nor bare conceptual error, but a beginningless obscuration and superimposition complex that only self-knowledge can remove. Later sections of the work extend the same architecture to *tat-padārtha* (verse 4 ff.) and to the sentence-meaning of *tattvam asi* (the *Tṛtīyo Vibhāgaḥ*, especially verse 7 ff.). Here the structure is laid down.

### Upodghāta 5

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *na, cidbhāsyatvena lakṣaṇena idaṅkārāspadānām api dehendriyamanasāṃ pratibhāsato 'haṅkārāspadatvena tadavivekāt tena viśuddhe 'py ātmani duḥkhitvādyabhimānāt śāstrīyeṇaiva brahmatmaikyajñānena samūlasya tasya nivṛtteḥ /*

**English (line-by-line):**
> No. Because body, senses, and mind too appear, under the mark of being illuminated by consciousness, as loci of the “this”-notion, and because through failure to discriminate them they are taken as loci of the “I”-notion, there arises in the pure self itself the conceit of suffering and the like; and that whole thing is removed, root and branch, only by the scriptural knowledge of the identity of Brahman and self.

- kartṛ: implicit argument

- karaṇa: *śāstrīyeṇaiva brahmatmaikyajñānena*

**Verbal modality:**

**Note:**
This is the first decisive distinction of the preface. Ordinary “I”-experience is not denied; it is said to be vitiated by *aviveka*, non-discrimination. Compare Śaṅkara’s *Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya*: the self is not unknown absolutely, but wrongly mixed.

### Upodghāta 8

**Sanskrit (IAST):**
> *vākyaṃ ca padārthajñānadvāreṇaiva jñāpakam iti tattvampadārthayoḥ prakṛtavākyānukūlayor anyato 'siddhatvāt tāv api śāstreṇaiva pramātavyau yūpāhavanīyapadārthavat /*

> And a sentence conveys only through the cognition of its word-meanings. ### Upodghāta 9

## Editorial closing

This opening scope settles three things. It settles, first, that the *mahāvākya* is the decisive *pramāṇa* for *ātmatattva*. It settles, second, that the semantic route to its force runs through the distinction between *vācya* and *lakṣya*. And it settles, third, that what is removed by that knowledge is not a mere absence but a beginningless, *anirvācyā* ignorance that both conceals and supports superimposition. What remains open here is the fuller account of *tvam-padārtha*, the status of *tat-padārtha*, and the exact sentence-unity of “*tattvam asi*.” Those are taken up immediately in the continuing prose of verse 1, then in the *Dvitīyo Vibhāgaḥ* on *tat-padārtha* (verse 4 ff.), and in the *Tṛtīyo Vibhāgaḥ* on the *mahāvākya* itself (especially verse 7 ff.). Outside this work, Madhusūdana returns to the positive, non-privative status of ignorance in the first *pariccheda* of the *Advaita-Siddhi*, where the explanatory indispensability of *ajñāna* is argued at much greater length.
