# Engagement: Vijñānabhikṣu’s Yoga-Vārttika

## 1. What the text is

*Yoga-Vārttika* is Vijñānabhikṣu’s substantial sub-commentary on Vyāsa’s *Yoga-Bhāṣya* on Patañjali’s *Yoga-Sūtra*: not a bare gloss on the sūtras, but a layered commentary on the sūtra-through-bhāṣya tradition. Vijñānabhikṣu is usually placed in the sixteenth century and belongs neither to a simple “classical Yoga” isolation nor to a straightforward Advaita register; the text is better read as a syncretic Sāṃkhya-Yoga work with explicit Vedāntic commitments and a marked bhedābheda tendency. The file’s own prefatory summary states that the work has four `pāda`s and briefly assigns each its function: `samādhi`, `sādhana`, `vibhūti`, and `kaivalya` (pure-Sanskrit file, opening summary at lines 58-76). The most important architectural fact is that Vijñānabhikṣu uses the Yoga frame not only to explicate practice, but to intervene in larger disputes about Vedānta, cognition, world-status, and the relation between `jīva`, `īśvara`, `citta`, and `puruṣa`.

## 2. Sampling and scope statement

Engaged at depth: roughly 15% of the file, concentrated in the prefatory `upasaṃhāra`; the first `pāda` around `YS 1.24` and the long doctrinal digression attached to it; the opening of the third `pāda` on `dhāraṇā`; and the fourth `pāda` around `YS 4.2` and `YS 4.24-26`. Structural summary only: the rest of all four `pāda`s, including the long runs on `samāpatti`, the `kleśa` and `aṣṭāṅga` materials, and most of the `vibhūti` catalog. Not engaged: the appended `śuddhipatra` / errata leaves at the end, except insofar as they confirm a few OCR uncertainties.

## 3. Structural walk-through

The file opens with a programmatic `upasaṃhāra` rather than with `atha yogānuśāsanam`. That opening matters. It first places Yoga within an ordered `darśana` ecology: Vedānta for the highest `adhikārin`, Mīmāṃsā for karmic purification, Sāṃkhya for discriminating `prakṛti` and `puruṣa`, Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika for object-analysis, and Yoga for the `nididhyāsana`-like realization that carries discrimination into direct attainment (opening leaves; page marker not cleanly retrieved). The same prefatory material also states, unusually plainly, that the work will not only expound its own view but also show the strengths and defects of other śāstric positions (lines 58-63). The polemical architecture is therefore announced before the commentary proper begins.

The `Samādhi-pāda` ends explicitly on p. 87 and is functionally about what Yoga is for the highest aspirant: `citta-vṛtti-nirodha`, the classification of mental states, `abhyāsa`, `vairāgya`, the graded `samāpatti`s, `īśvara`, `praṇava`, obstacles, and the transition toward `asaṃprajñāta` (p. 87 colophon). But the `pāda` is not merely practical. Already here Vijñānabhikṣu interrupts the flow to attack reflection-theories, self-luminosity theories, and newer Vedāntic readings that collapse the waking world into mere mentality. The result is that the first `pāda` constructs both a phenomenology of meditative cognition and a realist metaphysics of what that cognition is about.

The `Sādhana-pāda` begins explicitly on p. 88. The file’s own prefatory summary says that this `pāda` addresses the non-excellent `adhikārin` through `kriyāyoga`, then unfolds `yama`, `niyama`, `āsana`, `prāṇāyāma`, and `pratyāhāra` as the outer limbs, while making room for different levels of aspirants (opening summary, lines 64-75; `dvitīyaḥ pādaḥ`, p. 88). In the body of the `pāda`, the architecture is standard but not inert: `kleśa`, karmic residue, `duḥkha`, the distinction between seer and seen, and `viveka-khyāti` as `hānopāya` all prepare the ascetic-technological material. The polemical function here is constructive rather than refutational: against any merely declarative liberationism, Vijñānabhikṣu insists on an ordered discipline of `citta`, body, breath, and senses.

The `Vibhūti-pāda` begins immediately after the explicit `sādhana` colophon; the page marker at the opening leaf is OCR-frayed, but the next preserved page sequence runs up to p. 259, where the fourth `pāda` starts. This third section turns inward from the outer limbs to `dhāraṇā`, `dhyāna`, `samādhi`, their combination as `saṃyama`, and the resulting knowledges and powers. The opening commentary on `deśa-bandhaḥ cittasya dhāraṇā` already shows what kind of Yoga this is: the loci are not merely abstract objects but bodily and supra-bodily sites, including `nābhi`, `hṛdaya`, `kaṇṭha`, `nāsikā`, and external objects such as sun and moon. The polemical architecture here is directed less at named opponents than at reduction: Yoga is treated as a real psychophysical science, not as a metaphor for ethical concentration.

The `Kaivalya-pāda` begins on p. 259 and carries the heaviest metaphysical load. The file’s own opening summary says that it both specifies the `citta` fit for `kaivalya` and expounds the five kinds of `siddhi` that had only been mentioned generally before (lines 73-76; p. 259). The body then moves through `janma-oṣadhi-mantra-tapaḥ-samādhi` as sources of `siddhi`, `jātyantara-pariṇāma`, the status of constructed minds, the `parārthatva` of `citta`, the cessation of `ātmabhāva-bhāvanā`, and finally the turn to `kaivalya`. Polemically, this `pāda` is the clearest anti-idealist and anti-world-nullifying stretch of the text. It is also the place where the friction with the user’s thesis becomes sharpest, because Vijñānabhikṣu makes `avidyā` a `citta-dharma`, not a property of pure `puruṣa` or Brahman.

## 4. Load-bearing passages — engaged at depth

### Passage 1

**Locus:** Prefatory `upasaṃhāra`, anti-`nava-vedāntin` discussion of world-status (opening leaves; page marker not cleanly retrieved).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `na [OCR? svapnādivad iti] vedāntasūtraṃ ca jāgrat-prapañcasya kevala-mānasatvam eva nirākaroti.`

**Close English:** “And the Vedānta-sūtra, in saying ‘not as in dream and the like’ [OCR uncertain], rejects precisely the thesis that the waking manifold is nothing but mere mentality.”

**Engagement:** This is one of the text’s most useful sentences for the present corpus. Vijñānabhikṣu is not merely saying that the world is pragmatically serviceable or provisionally admitted. He is making a sharper claim: an interpretation of Vedānta that reduces the waking world to bare mentality misreads Vedānta’s own sūtra-logic. The target is not `mithyātva` as such, but a stronger idealizing move in which the world loses any robust status whatever.

This is also the right place to see why the text matters for the position’s T2 claim. The sentence does not say “the world is absolutely independent” or “Yoga is naïve realism.” But it does say that the world is not well handled by collapsing it into dream-like or purely mental status. That is precisely the kind of late scholastic pressure that helps recover `mithyā ≠ asat`.

**Pāṇinian-rich treatment:** `vedāntasūtraṃ` is the grammatical subject (`kartṛ`) of the finite verb `nirākaroti`. `kevala-mānasatvam` is the direct object (`karman`) of that verb; the particle `eva` restricts it sharply: “mere mentality, and nothing more.” `jāgrat-prapañcasya` is a genitive dependent on `kevala-mānasatvam`: not mentality in general, but the mere-mentality *of the waking manifold*. `svapnādivad iti` functions as a quoted doctrinal marker. Samāsa-vigraha: `jāgrat-prapañca` = `jāgrati avasthāyāṃ prapañcaḥ`; `kevala-mānasatva` = `kevalaṃ mānasatvam`. The force of the sentence lies in the verb: `nir-ā-kṛ`, “to reject, set aside, disallow.” Vijñānabhikṣu is not offering a conciliatory gloss; he is excluding an interpretation.

### Passage 2

**Locus:** `Samādhi-pāda`, `YS 1.24`, p. 49.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `kleśa-karma-vipākāśayair aparāmṛṣṭaḥ puruṣa-viśeṣa īśvaraḥ.`

**Close English:** “Īśvara is a special puruṣa untouched by afflictions, actions, their ripenings, and latent deposits.”

**Engagement:** The obvious point is theism, but the more important point is the exact form of theism. `Īśvara` is not placed outside the puruṣa-framework as a wholly alien category; he is a `puruṣa-viśeṣa`. This preserves continuity with Yoga-Sāṃkhya analysis while refusing the atheistic or merely formal construal of Yoga. Vijñānabhikṣu’s commentary makes the polemical target explicit: he introduces the sūtra precisely against the Sāṃkhya objection that nothing beyond `prakṛti` and ordinary `puruṣa`s need be admitted.

What follows in the long digression is even more important for corpus-placement. Vijñānabhikṣu does not move from here into strict non-dual identity; he uses the discussion to reject both `avacchedavāda` and `pratibimbavāda`, while also refusing sheer plural disconnection. The constructive position is a real difference that does not cancel a more general non-difference. That is why the text cannot be folded either into a purely illusionist Advaita or into an uncompromising plurality-without-commonality model.

### Passage 3

**Locus:** `Vibhūti-pāda`, `YS 3.1`, opening of the `pāda` (immediately after the third-pāda heading; opening page marker not cleanly retrieved).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `deśa-bandhaś cittasya dhāraṇā.`  
`yasmin deśe dhyeyaṃ cintanīyaṃ tatra dhyānādhāra-deśa-viṣaye cittasya tad-ekāgraṃ dhāraṇā ity arthaḥ.`

**Close English:** “Fixing the mind to a place is `dhāraṇā`. That is: with respect to the place that serves as the support of meditation, wherever the object to be contemplated is to be thought, the one-pointedness of the mind there is `dhāraṇā`.”

**Engagement:** This is straightforward Yoga doctrine, but it matters because it displays the kind of ontology presupposed by the whole work. Vijñānabhikṣu immediately glosses the “place” through a repertoire of internal loci such as navel, heart, throat, nose-tip, and other subtle-body sites, and then allows external objects such as sun and moon. This is not a purely formal theory of attention. The body, the subtle body, cosmological objects, and cognition are all treated as real and articulable supports of practice.

For the position, the value here is methodological. The text gives a primary-source basis for taking Yoga as a somato-psychic register rather than as ethics plus concentration metaphors. That helps T1 and T11 more than T2 or T7: the world is not denied, but more importantly, cognition is not treated as self-enclosed.

### Passage 4

**Locus:** `Kaivalya-pāda`, `YS 4.2`, pp. 259-290.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `jāty-antara-pariṇāmaḥ prakṛty-āpūrāt.`  
`manuṣyādi-jāti-rūpaḥ pariṇāmaḥ sa prakṛty-āpūrād udbhavati, na tu saṃkalpa-mātrāt ity arthaḥ.`

**Close English:** “Transformation into another species-form occurs from the overflowing completion of nature. That is: a transformation having the form of another species, such as human and the like, arises from nature’s filling-out, not from mere thought or will alone.”

**Engagement:** This is one of the most fertile passages in the whole text. Vijñānabhikṣu reads transformation as a real process internal to `prakṛti`, not as a merely projective event of consciousness. `Saṃkalpa` is not denied all role, but the denial of `saṃkalpa-mātra` is decisive: becoming is not mental fiat. The examples that follow in the commentary, including yogic transformations and mythic expansions of form, all still presuppose an operative natural basis.

For the position’s T11, this is a strong ally. It is not Aurobindonian evolution, and one should not pretend otherwise. But it does underwrite a real ontology of manifestation and transformation. Experience is not all there is; nor is becoming a subjective overlay. Something in nature really unfolds.

### Passage 5

**Locus:** `Kaivalya-pāda`, `YS 4.24-25` with commentary, pp. 295-296.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `tad asaṃkhyeya-vāsanābhiś citram api parārthaṃ saṃhatya-kāritvāt.`  
`eṣa bhāva-rūpo vicitraḥ pariṇāma ātmanaḥ na bhavati, kiṃ tu cittasyaivāvidyā-janyatvāt; avidyāyāś ca citta-dharmatvāt.`

**Close English:** “Though variegated by innumerable latent impressions, that mind is still for the sake of another, because it functions as a composite. This manifold modification, having the form of a positive state, does not belong to the self; rather it belongs to mind alone, because it is produced by ignorance, and because ignorance is a property of mind.”

**Engagement:** The first sentence denies self-sufficiency to `citta`: however intricate it becomes, it remains `parārtha`, for another. The reason given, `saṃhatya-kāritvāt`, is classically Sāṃkhya-like: what is composite functions for another. Vijñānabhikṣu uses that inferential style to keep the distinction between `citta` and `puruṣa` firm even at the most refined end of the path.

The second sentence is where the gain and the friction both lie. It is excellent for anti-idealism: manifold becoming belongs to mind, not to pure self; therefore liberation requires discrimination, not inflation of mind into ultimate reality. But it is a resistant text for T7. `Avidyā` is not here treated as Brahman’s own positive self-veiling; it is said explicitly to be a `citta-dharma`. That means the text supports a realist critique of world-nullification more readily than it supports the Vivaraṇa-style ontology of `avidyā` that the position sometimes wants to preserve.

## 5. The text’s relation to the position

This text is best placed as a late premodern ally of the realism-recovery trajectory, not as a direct rung in the Bhartṛprapañca → Bhāskara → Madhva → Caitanya → Aurobindo sequence. Its affinities are strongest wherever the project opposes the nullification of manifestation. The prefatory anti-`nava-vedāntin` material is especially useful here: Vijñānabhikṣu explicitly denies that Vedānta requires `atyantatucchatā` or sheer dream-like mentalization of the waking world. In that respect the text sits closer to Bhāskara, Nimbārka, Vallabha, and anti-illusionist Vedāntic currents than to the Vivaraṇa school’s more aggressive defensive strategies.

Against the Vivaraṇa line specifically, the text is often polemical rather than cooperative. The digression on `īśvara` rejects `avacchedavāda` and `pratibimbavāda`, and later passages attack “modern Vedāntins” and `dṛṣṭi-sṛṣṭi`-style views as effectively self-undermining. So on T2 the text is quite strong: it gives direct primary support for the claim that `mithyā` must not be flattened into `asat`, and that mere mentalism is not the inevitable logic of Vedānta. On T9 as well it is helpful: the world and divine governance are not reduced to symbol or illusion, and `īśvara` is a live metaphysical factor.

As to targets: in the material inspected I did not retrieve historical names such as Padmapāda, Prakāśātman, or Rāmānuja as direct objects of refutation. The text’s opponents are usually typed doctrinally rather than historically: `nava` or `ādhunika vedāntin`, `dṛṣṭi-sṛṣṭi`-style theorists, `avaccheda`- and `pratibimba`-vāda, Sāṃkhya resistance to `īśvara`, and recurring anonymous `pūrva-pakṣin`s on self-luminosity, reflection, world-status, and the independence of `citta`.

On T7, however, the text resists the position’s synthesis. Vijñānabhikṣu’s own formulation makes `avidyā` a `citta-dharma`; he does not move toward the claim that Brahman is both `āśraya` and `viṣaya` of `avidyā`, nor toward a fully positive ontology of divine self-ignorance. That means the text cannot simply be used as evidence for the Vivaraṇa-Madhusūdana line of argument. If the position wants both Vijñānabhikṣu and the Vivaraṇa account, it will have to stage them as different resources with real tension between them, not as mutually transparent witnesses.

The same caution applies to the Madhva and Viśiṣṭādvaita lineages. Vijñānabhikṣu is not Tattvavāda: he keeps a general non-difference even while insisting on real difference in specification, so the text does not share Madhva’s full foreclosure of non-dual language. Nor is it Viśiṣṭādvaita: the architecture remains Yoga-Sāṃkhya, not `śeṣa-śeṣi` theology. Yet with both lineages he shares an anti-Mayāvāda instinct and a refusal to treat manifestation as sheer nothing. That is the point of contact the project should use.

## 6. What the user can productively take

This text changes the corpus in three concrete ways. First, it supplies a valuable primary witness that late Sanskrit scholasticism itself could read Vedānta against waking-world mentalism; that is direct support for T2. Second, it gives the project a serious Yoga-side ontology of practice: bodily loci, `citta`-discipline, and transformation are all treated as real, which strengthens T11. Third, it forces a clarification in the `avidyā` chapter: if the position wants the stronger divine-self-veiling thesis, Vijñānabhikṣu cannot simply be cited as if he already held it. He is a strong ally against world-nullification, but a resistant witness for the Vivaraṇa route.

The most productive use, then, is selective. Cite Vijñānabhikṣu where the project needs a learned Sanskrit source against the reduction of the world to sheer unreality or mere mentality, and where it needs Yoga to count as a real register of being and practice. Do not conscript him for claims he resists. The text is most valuable precisely when its alliance and its friction are both kept in view.
