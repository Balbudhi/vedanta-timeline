# Engagement: Madhva, Māyāvāda-Khaṇḍana

## 1. What the text is

*Māyāvāda-Khaṇḍana* is a short polemical *prakaraṇa* attributed to Madhva (Ānandatīrtha), traditionally placed in the thirteenth century and situated in the Tattvavāda lineage. The work itself has no chapter-division: in the on-disk witness the mūla occupies only printed pp. 2-4 of the pure-Sanskrit file, after which the edition expands into six commentarial layers explicitly listed on the title page as Padmanābha Tīrtha's *Prācīnīkā*, Jayatīrtha's *Ṭīkā*, Vyāsatīrtha's *Mandāramañjarī*, Śrīnivāsa Tīrtha's *Durgamārthavyākhyā*, Pāṇḍuraṅga Keḍāvācārya's *Viṣamapadavākyārthavivaraṇa* [OCR?], and Satyanātha's *Paraśu*. It is best read as a compressed anti-Mayāvāda dossier whose scholastic afterlife is much larger than the root text.

## 2. Sampling and scope statement

Engaged at depth: the complete mūla on printed pp. 2-4 of the pure-Sanskrit file, roughly lines 162-251, plus Padmanābha Tīrtha's immediate ṭīkā through printed p. 5 for orientation. Structural summary only: the later restart of the mūla and the long expansions by Jayatīrtha, Vyāsatīrtha, Śrīnivāsa Tīrtha, Pāṇḍuraṅga Keḍāvācārya, and Satyanātha in the remainder of the witness. Flagged but not engaged in depth: the later commentaries' grammatical and *nyāya* disputes around `anyathāpratipādakatva`, `mithyātva`, and `ajñāna-nivṛtti` as a "fifth kind" of mokṣa.

## 3. Structural walk-through

### Opening maṅgala and thesis-statement

Printed p. 2 opens with the invocation `narasiṃho 'khilājñānamatadhvāntadivākaraḥ ...` and immediately moves to `vimatam anārambhaṇīyam` (around lines 165-176). Narasiṃha is the light that dispels false cognition; that image is immediately converted into a technical claim: the Mayāvādin's teaching is unfit to be undertaken because it presents reality otherwise than it is. The architecture is already clear: disqualify the rival śāstra, then replace its claimed scriptural purport.

### The dilemma over brahmātmaikya

Still on printed p. 2, the mūla's next movement is the dilemma `na hi brahmātmaikyasya yāthārthyaṃ tatpakṣe ... advaitahāneḥ svarūpātireke ... anatireke ... siddhasādhanatā` (lines 175-181). If the identity of Brahman and self is something over and above the self, non-duality is destroyed; if it is not, scripture merely establishes what is already established. This is the text's central anti-Advaita move.

### The attack on avidyā and the pramāṇa definition

The same cluster continues with `nirviśeṣatvāc cātmano nānadhigato viśeṣaḥ ... nājñānaṃ kasyacid āvarakam. "anadhigatārthagantṛ pramāṇam" iti tanmatam` (printed p. 2). Madhva's claim is that a self-luminous and featureless self leaves nothing genuinely unapprehended for ignorance to cover, while the opponent's own pramāṇa-theory requires cognition of a previously unapprehended object. The polemic is thus against the coherence of the Advaitin's cognitive architecture itself.

### Consequences for scripture, mokṣa, adhikāra, and sambandha

Printed pp. 2-4 then compress the downstream consequences: `ajñānāsambhavād eva tanmatam akhilam apākṛtam`, `mithyātve caikyasya ... āgamasya syāt`, `satyatā ca bhedasya`, and then `svarūpatvān mokṣasya pūrvam eva siddhatvāt` together with the rejection of the fourth and fifth "types" of liberation. If ignorance is impossible, the Mayāvādin's ontology collapses; if identity is *mithyā*, scripture becomes a revealer of unreality; if mokṣa is the self's own nature, it cannot coherently be produced as ignorance-removal; and once *viṣaya* and *prayojana* fail, *adhikārī* and *sambandha* fail as well.

### Positive scriptural close: kṣara, akṣara, and Puruṣottama

The text closes with quotation from Bhagavad-Gītā 15 and Kaṭha Upaniṣad: `dvāv imau puruṣau loke ... uttamaḥ puruṣas tv anyaḥ ...`, followed by `indriyebhyaḥ parā hy arthā ... puruṣān na paraṃ kiñcit` (printed pp. 3-4). Scripture thus culminates not in an undifferentiated identity-claim but in an ordered ontology terminating in Viṣṇu/Puruṣottama, beyond both the perishable and the imperishable.

### The commentarial witness

After the short mūla and Padmanābha's first ṭīkā, the witness restarts the root text and expands it through Jayatīrtha and later authors beginning around line 302. Their role is to make explicit the inferential steps packed into the mūla: what counts as `anārambhaṇīya`, why `anyathāpratipādakatva` is the operative fault, how `brahmātmaikya` would produce `siddhasādhanatā`, and why the "fifth kind" of mokṣa is incoherent.

## 4. Load-bearing passages — engaged at depth

### 1. The thesis-inference

**Locus:** printed p. 2 of the pure-Sanskrit file, around lines 169-176.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `vimatam anārambhaṇīyam, anyathāpratipādakatvāt, yad itthaṃ tat tathā, yathā sampratipannam.`

**Close English:** "A disputed śāstra is not fit to be undertaken, because it presents things otherwise than they are; whatever is of that sort is so, as is commonly agreed."

**Engagement:** This is the text's governing inferential form. Madhva does not begin by saying merely that the Mayāvādin is wrong; he begins by saying that a text whose purport is false forfeits *śāstric admissibility*. The issue is not authorship or prestige but uptake: a text may exist and be influential, yet still be disqualified if it teaches `anyathā`.

**Pāṇinian-rich treatment:** `vimatam` is neuter nominative singular, functioning substantivally: "the disputed thesis/text." `anārambhaṇīyam` is a gerundive formation, likewise neuter nominative singular, giving the predication: "[it] is not to be undertaken." The principal sentence is therefore an ellipted nominal sentence: `vimatam anārambhaṇīyam [asti]`. `anyathāpratipādakatvāt` is ablative singular of an abstract compound and supplies the hetu: "because of the state of presenting otherwise." The compound may be unpacked as `anyathā pratipādayati iti anyathāpratipādakaḥ; tasya bhāvaḥ anyathāpratipādakatvam`. The correlative `yad itthaṃ tat tathā` gives the inferential schema: "whatever is like this, that is so." `yathā sampratipannam` supplies the accepted case, the conventionally admitted instance. The whole line is thus not ornamental but formally inferential: pakṣa (`vimatam`), sādhya (`anārambhaṇīyam`), hetu (`anyathāpratipādakatvāt`), and dṛṣṭānta by appeal to what is `sampratipanna`.

### 2. The dilemma about identity

**Locus:** printed p. 2, around lines 175-181.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `na hi brahmātmaikyasya yāthārthyaṃ tatpakṣe. advaitahāneḥ svarūpātireke. anatireke svaprakāratvād ātmanaḥ siddhasādhanatā.`

**Close English:** "For on that view the identity of Brahman and self is not real. If it is over and above the self's own nature, non-duality is lost. If it is not over and above it, there is the fault of proving what is already proved, since the self is self-manifest."

**Engagement:** This is the load-bearing anti-Mayāvāda dilemma. Madhva's complaint is not that identity-statements are spiritually unhelpful; it is that they cannot be assigned a coherent ontological status. If identity is something genuinely distinct, then plurality has already entered and the claim to non-duality collapses. If it is nothing over and above the self, then the śāstra does no real epistemic work.

The phrase `siddhasādhanatā` is especially important. Madhva is not satisfied with saying that the self is always already Brahman. He asks what a pramāṇa would then be doing. That makes the argument stronger than a merely doctrinal disagreement: it challenges the opponent at the level of what counts as revelation or cognition at all.

### 3. No room for an ignorance that covers

**Locus:** printed p. 2, around lines 178-181.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `nirviśeṣatvāc cātmano nānadhigato viśeṣaḥ. siddhatvāt svarūpasya viśeṣābhāvāc ca nājñānaṃ kasyacid āvarakam. "anadhigatārthagantṛ pramāṇam" iti tanmatam.`

**Close English:** "And because the self is without differentiating features, there is no unapprehended specification there. Since its nature is already established, and because there is no specification, no ignorance can be a cover over anything whatsoever. Yet their thesis is: 'A pramāṇa reaches an as-yet-unapprehended object.'"

**Engagement:** This is where the text attacks Advaita's avidyā-theory most sharply. If the self is featureless and self-established, what exactly is hidden, and by what? Madhva forces the opponent to choose between an occult covering with no intelligible object, or an admitted specification which would already compromise the claimed nirviśeṣatva. The quoted pramāṇa-definition makes the argument internal: on the opponent's own terms, liberating knowledge requires genuinely unapprehended content, and Madhva denies that such content exists here.

### 4. If identity is mithyā, scripture teaches unreality

**Locus:** printed pp. 2-3, around lines 181-184.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `ajñānāsambhavād eva tanmatam akhilam apākṛtam. mithyātve caikyasy[a] atattvāvedakatvam [OCR?] āgamasya syāt. satyatā ca bhedasya.`

**Close English:** "Precisely because ignorance is impossible, that whole doctrine is refuted. And if identity is mithyā, scripture would become a revealer of the unreal; and difference would be true."

**Engagement:** This is the passage the position most needs to treat with care. Madhva does not equate `mithyā` with straightforward `asat`; the structure of the argument is subtler. If the scriptural content centrally taught is itself not ultimately real, scripture ceases to function as truth-bearing revelation in the way the Mayāvādin needs. From there he infers `satyatā ca bhedasya`: denied identity rebounds into the truth of difference.

This is a powerful realist pressure-point, but it is not yet the position's synthesis. The position wants to preserve the thought that sublatability is not non-being. Madhva is operating with a stronger exclusionary inference: once the identity-claim is denied `yāthārthya`, the burden shifts decisively toward real difference.

### 5. Mokṣa cannot be a fifth ontological kind

**Locus:** printed p. 4, around lines 245-251, clarifying the mūla's compact line on printed p. 3.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `na cātmājñāna-nivṛttir mokṣa iti vaktuṃ śakyate, ātmanas svayaṃprakāśamānasyājñānāsambhavena tan-nivṛtty-asambhavāt. ... sad-asad-vilakṣaṇa-rūpa-caturthaprakārājñānāsambhavena pañcamaprakārasyājñāna-nivṛttirūpa-mokṣasya sutarām asambhavāt.`

**Close English:** "And one cannot say that mokṣa is the cessation of ignorance regarding the self, because, since the self is self-luminous, ignorance with respect to it is impossible, and therefore its cessation is impossible. ... Since an ignorance of a fourth kind, of the nature of being different from both being and non-being, is impossible, a fifth kind of mokṣa consisting in the cessation of that ignorance is all the more impossible."

**Engagement:** The mūla itself states this point in extremely compressed form; Padmanābha's ṭīkā makes the intended target explicit. Madhva is refusing the move whereby Advaita posits an ontologically peculiar ignorance, neither sat nor asat, and then defines liberation as the cessation of precisely that. If the ignorance has no coherent standing, the liberation built on its cessation inherits the incoherence.

What matters here is that the text does not merely reject an opponent's conclusion; it rejects the very ontological slot required to house the conclusion. This is why the attack on mokṣa is tied back to the earlier attack on avidyā. The work's polemical architecture is cumulative.

### 6. The positive close: Puruṣottama beyond kṣara and akṣara

**Locus:** printed p. 3, around lines 189-196.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `dvāv imau puruṣau loke kṣaraś cākṣara eva ca / kṣaraḥ sarvāṇi bhūtāni kūṭastho 'kṣara ucyate // uttamaḥ puruṣas tv anyaḥ paramātmety udāhṛtaḥ / yo lokatrayam āviśya bibharty avyaya īśvaraḥ //`

**Close English:** "In the world there are these two persons, the perishable and the imperishable. The perishable is all beings; the immutable is called the imperishable. But another is the highest person, called the supreme self, the undecaying Lord who, entering the three worlds, sustains them."

**Engagement:** The positive close is not a devotional appendage. It answers the question left open by the anti-Mayāvāda demolition: if the śāstra does not culminate in sheer identity, what does it culminate in? Madhva's answer is not a flat multiplicity but an ordered reality capped by Puruṣottama, who transcends both `kṣara` and `akṣara`.

That matters for reading the text philosophically. The work is not only a refusal of Mayāvāda's cognitive model; it is a redirection of Vedāntic purport toward a theologically articulated realism. The positive thesis is therefore as important as the negative one.

## 5. The text's relation to the position

On the realism-recovery chain Bhartṛprapañca -> Bhāskara -> Madhva -> Caitanya -> Aurobindo, this text is a crucial hardening point. It does not yet offer the later synthetic vocabulary of unity-in-multiplicity; it makes the anti-Mayāvāda case in an uncompromising Tattvavādin form. Against the Vivaraṇa line of Padmapāda, Prakāśātman, Vidyāraṇya, and later Madhusūdana, it names no Advaitin explicitly in the mūla: the target is an anonymous Mayāvādin *pūrva-pakṣin*. The later commentaries compare the case to Jaina and Buddhist texts for the purpose of `anārambhaṇīyatva`, but those are analogical foils, not the real opponent. Relative to Yāmuna, Rāmānuja, and Vedānta Deśika, it stands in a parallel Vaiṣṇava anti-Mayāvāda front, though with a more aggressively epistemic dialectic; relative to Vallabha and Nimbārka, it is again a parallel realism, but sharper and less accommodating.

For the position's specific theses: on T2, the text is strongly usable. It gives a primary source for the anti-Mayāvāda side of the realism-recovery trajectory and does so without equating the opponent's `mithyā` with simple nonexistence. On T9, it is likewise useful: the positive close in terms of `kṣara`, `akṣara`, and `uttamaḥ puruṣaḥ` is a clear Vaiṣṇava refusal of world-negating closure. On T7, however, the text is not support but obstacle. The position wants a positive account of avidyā or self-veiling robust enough to survive the Madhusūdana/Vivaraṇa terrain; Madhva here denies that ignorance can coherently cover a self-luminous, featureless self at all. That is a challenge the position must answer, not a resource it can simply enlist. On T11, the text contributes only indirectly: it resists reducing manifestation to a false projection, but it does not yet furnish anything like an Aurobindonian ontology of manifestation or evolution.

The central friction is therefore exact and non-trivial. The position wants to preserve some non-dual truth while refusing world-denial and crude illusionism. This text presses the question whether that middle path is stable. If `brahmātmaikya` is really distinct from the self, Madhva says non-duality is lost; if it is not distinct, then revelation becomes `siddhasādhana` and there is no real room for covering ignorance. Any synthesis downstream of Madhva has to answer that dilemma rather than moving around it.

## 6. What the user can productively take

- This text gives the position a strong primary-source citation for the claim that realism-recovery within Vedānta is argued on epistemic and scriptural grounds, not only on devotional or common-sense grounds.
- It sharpens the anti-Mayāvāda side of T2, but it also forces precision: any later synthesis retaining a non-dual register must explain why Madhva's `siddhasādhanatā` objection does not go through.
- It is especially useful as a pressure test for T7. The position cannot simply say that ignorance is real or divinely willed; it must say how such ignorance is possible without becoming the incoherent `āvaraṇa` that Madhva denies.
- In corpus terms, this text should be treated as a hard anti-Mayāvāda node: indispensable for the project's realist genealogy, but equally indispensable as a source of friction against any too-easy reconciliation.
