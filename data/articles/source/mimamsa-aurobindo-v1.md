# Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā as Externalized Yogic Process: An Investigation of an Aurobindonian Hypothesis

A philosophical investigation of the claim that the Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā ritual programme is the *externalized* form of an internal yogic process — the same internal process that Sri Aurobindo, in *The Secret of the Veda*, claims to have decoded behind the surface of the Ṛgvedic hymns. The corollary to be tested is that the historical disconnection between Mīmāṃsā exegesis and lived spiritual life — what the tradition itself frequently calls *karma-kāṇḍa-jaḍatā*, "the inertness of the ritual section" — is best explained not by ritualistic decline, priestly corruption, or linguistic decay, but by the same kind of disengagement Aurobindo describes when he says that "the letter remained after the spirit was forgotten" (*The Secret of the Veda*, CWSA 15, pp. 56–57).

The investigation is conducted in the Russell–Chakrabarti register: charitable formulation, primary-source citation where available, explicit flagging of where the claim outruns the evidence.

---

## 1. Three precisifications of the hypothesis

The original intuition admits of three philosophically distinguishable readings, increasing in strength.

### Hypothesis A (Mild — Hermeneutic)

*Aurobindo's symbolic-psychological reading of the Ṛgveda, when applied to the Mīmāṃsaka object-language (yajña, codanā, devatā, dravya-deśa-kāla), illuminates a register of internal-yogic referents that the literal-ritual register obscures.*

This is a claim about hermeneutic productivity, not about historical fact. It says: if you bring Aurobindo's lexicon (cow = ray of light; horse = vital force; Indra = illumined mind; soma = ānanda; the inner sacrifice as the upward-offering of consciousness) to the Mīmāṃsā corpus, certain otherwise obscure features of the system — its insistence that *codanā* is *autonomous* of any visible empirical state of affairs; its insistence that *śabda–artha* relations are *autpattika* (innate, not conventional); its commitment to an *apūrva* that is real but never sensorily present — become legible as references to an interior, not exterior, process.

A is the weakest claim. It does not require anyone to have *intended* this reading. It requires only that the reading is coherent and explanatorily fruitful. A is almost certainly true in some form, because hermeneutic productivity is cheap and the Mīmāṃsā lexicon is rich in formal abstractions that admit such re-pointing.

### Hypothesis B (Medium — Genealogical-Structural)

*Mīmāṃsā as historically received is the externalization of an originally internal yogic process. The externalization is the philosophical move: the Mīmāṃsakas treat the same operations of consciousness that the Vedic Ṛṣis (on Aurobindo's reading) practiced inwardly, but they treat them as outer ritual operations because the cultural-cognitive horizon in which they are working is one of externalized symbol-traffic.*

This is a structural-historical claim. It says: there is one process (call it the Vedic yajña-as-inner-sacrifice). Two registers of articulation: the Aurobindonian inward register (Agni as seer-will, soma as released delight, Sarama as intuition that finds the hidden cows of light), and the Mīmāṃsaka outward register (Agni as the literal sacrificial fire, soma as the literal pressed plant, the *yajamāna* as the literal patron of the literal rite). The Mīmāṃsakas are not wrong about the ritual; they are *committed* to the ritual register because that is where the symbol-system has, for them, settled.

B is the substantive philosophical claim. It is testable, in the limited sense that one can ask whether the Mīmāṃsakas themselves, in their explicit doctrines, leave room for an inner-yogic register or close it down.

### Hypothesis C (Strong — Causal-Diagnostic)

*The standard tradition-internal narrative of Mīmāṃsā's decline — that the karma-kāṇḍa became "empty ritual" because of priestly corruption, lack of qualified Brahmins, the loss of Sanskrit fluency, or a falling-off of śraddhā — is wrong. The actual cause of the disconnect is the same phenomenon Aurobindo identifies: the human mind no longer projects symbols onto outer ritual in the way that made the originally externalized symbol-traffic functional. Ritual became "empty" not because anyone did anything wrong but because the mode of consciousness that made the externalization work as a symbol-system had passed.*

C is the strongest claim. It is also the most original — it has the form of a *diagnostic* claim against the Mīmāṃsakas' (and their critics') own self-understanding. C says: Sureśvara, Śaṅkara, and the Vedāntins were partly right that ritual alone does not deliver mokṣa, but they misdiagnosed *why* ritual no longer delivered. They thought ritual was inherently incapable. The truer story (on the hypothesis) is that ritual once *was* capable, when the consciousness that performed it was such that ritual operations were transparent to inner operations; what failed was the cognitive substrate, not the ritual qua ritual.

C is the claim the user's voice-text most directly proposes.

The three hypotheses interlock but are independent. A could hold while B fails (the hermeneutic is fruitful but ahistorical). B could hold while C fails (the externalization happened but the decline has standard causes). C entails B but is much stronger.

## 2. Aurobindo on the Vedic interior and its historical occlusion

The textual basis for Aurobindo's reading is *The Secret of the Veda*, CWSA Vol. 15, originally serialized in *Arya* 1914–1916. The on-disk extraction (`/orcd/home/002/eeshan/philosophy/sources/aurobindo_mother/D_secret_of_veda_deepmine.md`) and the verbatim mine in `/orcd/home/002/eeshan/philosophy/sources/modern/08_aurobindo_secret_of_the_veda.md` provide page-anchored access to the relevant passages.

### 2.1 The double-language thesis

Aurobindo's central claim, stated in CWSA 15 pp. 8–9, is that "the Rig Veda is a deliberately double-language scripture: outwardly ritual, natural, cultic, and mythic; inwardly psychological and spiritual; and that the inward sense is not a late philosophical afterthought but the original organizing intention of the hymns themselves." The hymns are "conceived and constructed" on the principle of a "double sense" in which "the outer rite and physical image are preserved, but the deeper significance concerns states and powers of consciousness" (p. 8). Crucially: *the outer rite is preserved*. Aurobindo does not say it is illusory or false. He says it is "covering, support, and vehicle for a more inward discipline and knowledge" (pp. 8–9, 22–23, 34–35).

This is the structural opening for Hypothesis B. If the Vedic hymns themselves are symbolically two-layered — outer ritual / inner consciousness — then a tradition (Mīmāṃsā) that takes only the outer layer and builds a complete deontic and ritual-procedural metaphysics on it is precisely the kind of *externalization* the hypothesis names. The outer layer is not falsified by Mīmāṃsā; it is given a complete autonomous metaphysics.

### 2.2 The inner yajña

In Chapter VI ("Agni and the Truth," pp. 58–67), Aurobindo argues that Agni is "the divine will or seer-force, the conscious force that performs the inner sacrifice, mediates between mortal and immortal, and applies the truth in works." The sacrifice (yajña) is "inner transformation, not merely external rite" (p. 67 area). The seven rivers (pp. 109–122) are "the seven streams or currents or forms of movement of the one conscious existence" (p. 117). The cows recovered from the Vala cave (pp. 138–158) are rays of illumination, hidden in the cave of subconscious darkness, freed by the action of Indra, Brihaspati, Sarama, and the Aṅgirases.

Now consider the Mīmāṃsā sacrificial vocabulary alongside. The Mīmāṃsakas analyse the *yajña* into *karaṇa* (instrument), *kartṛ* (agent), *deśa-kāla* (place and time), *dravya* (substance), *devatā* (deity), *codanā* (injunction), and the *apūrva* — the unseen efficacy generated by the rite that connects the visible action to its (invisible, sometimes posthumous) fruit. If Aurobindo is right that the Vedic *yajña* was originally inward, the Mīmāṃsaka analytic — taken as a description of an *external* operation — looks like a complete and remarkably faithful externalization of the inward operation. The unseen efficacy (*apūrva*) is precisely the kind of formal-functional placeholder that an externalizing translation would have to introduce: the inner operation generated its own "fruit" by transforming consciousness; once externalized, the operation must postulate an unseen mediator (*apūrva*) to do, ex post, the work that consciousness no longer does in the present.

### 2.3 The historical occlusion: the *strongest* passage

CWSA 15 pp. 56–57 contains the formula on which everything in this investigation turns. After the symbolic power had faded, Aurobindo writes (close paraphrase from the on-disk deepmine, p. 56): "the letter lived on when the spirit was forgotten; the symbol, the body of the doctrine, remained, but the soul of knowledge had fled from its coverings." The same thought appears, in different words, in Chapter II ("A Retrospect of Vedic Theory"): "the material aspects of Vedic worship had grown like a thick crust over the inner knowledge and were stifling what they had once served to protect"; "the power had begun to disappear out of the symbolic ceremony; the light had departed from the mystic parable and left only a surface of apparent grotesqueness."

What does Aurobindo say *caused* this? Three causal strands appear in the same chapter (verified by web fetch of the full *Secret of the Veda* text on the canonical Aurobindo Ashram site):

1. *Evolutionary-cognitive*: "Once the first intensity of the search after truth had passed, periods of fatigue and relaxation were bound to intervene in which the old truths would be partially lost"; "The Ages of Intuition were passing away into the first dawn of the Age of Reason."
2. *Loss of initiatory transmission*: "Even Purohits of repute performed the rites with a very imperfect knowledge of the power and the sense of the sacred words which they repeated."
3. *Institutional ossification*: the "thick crust" passage above.

The first strand is what matters for Hypothesis C. Aurobindo *does* attribute the loss, in part, to a change in the *mode of consciousness* of humanity. He frames it with the language of evolutionary cycles ("Ages of Intuition," "Age of Reason"), which is part of his larger evolutionary metaphysics elsewhere (the *Human Cycle*, the *Life Divine*). The user's hypothesis — that the disconnect is due to "the mind changing" rather than to corruption — has a real Aurobindonian basis. It is not the only causal claim Aurobindo makes, but it is one of three, and arguably the deepest one in his system.

This is the strongest single piece of evidence for the user's hypothesis.

### 2.4 What Aurobindo does *not* say

A point of honesty: Aurobindo does not, in the searched passages of *The Secret of the Veda*, name Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā or Jaimini explicitly as the externalized form of the inner Vedic process. He criticizes Sāyaṇa (CWSA 15, pp. 20–23), who is a fourteenth-century Vedic ritual commentator embedded in the Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā exegetical tradition — but he criticizes Sāyaṇa as a particular interpreter, not Mīmāṃsā as a school. The application to Mīmāṃsā is the user's extension. It is a defensible extension — Sāyaṇa *is* operating within the Mīmāṃsā tradition's hermeneutical assumptions about Vedic injunction and ritual reference — but it is an extension, not an Aurobindonian explicit thesis.

## 3. The Mīmāṃsā position on its own terms

To test the hypothesis, one has to engage what the Mīmāṃsakas actually argue, not the popular caricature. The on-disk corpus gives access to four primary witnesses: Jaimini's *Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra* (`/orcd/home/002/eeshan/philosophy/materials/primary_texts/sanskrit/mimamsa/jaimini_mimamsa_sutra.txt`); Mādhava's *Jaimini-Nyāya-Mālā* (a navya-Mīmāṃsā summary); Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* commentary on Kumārila's *Śloka-Vārttika*; and (by inference from the cross-corpus trace) Sureśvara's *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* in the Alston translation, which contains the Advaita polemic against Mīmāṃsā.

### 3.1 The opening of Jaimini

The first six sūtras of the *Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra* (verified verbatim from the GRETIL text on disk):

> *athāto dharma-jijñāsā* ‖1.1.1‖
> "Now therefore the inquiry into dharma."
>
> *codanā-lakṣaṇo'rtho dharmaḥ* ‖1.1.2‖
> "Dharma is that *artha* characterized by *codanā* (Vedic injunction)."
>
> *tasya nimitta-parīṣṭiḥ* ‖1.1.3‖
> "The investigation of its ground/cause."
>
> *sat-saṃprayoge puruṣasyendriyāṇāṃ buddhi-janma tat pratyakṣam animittaṃ vidyamāno-palambhanatvāt* ‖1.1.4‖
> "When there is contact with an existing thing, the arising of cognition in the agent's senses is *pratyakṣa*; this is *not* a ground (for dharma), since it apprehends only what is already present."
>
> *autpattikas tu śabdasya-arthena saṃbandhas tasya jñānam upadeśo'vyatirekaś cārthe'nupalabdhe tat pramāṇaṃ bādarāyaṇasya-anapekṣatvāt* ‖1.1.5‖
> "But the relation of word and meaning is *autpattika* (innate, intrinsic, not produced by convention); the cognition of that [relation] is the teaching, and there is no deviation [of word from meaning] regarding an *artha* not [otherwise] apprehensible. Therefore [the Veda] is the *pramāṇa* there, by Bādarāyaṇa, since it requires nothing else."

These five sūtras are remarkable. Read on the surface, they are an aggressive epistemic-ritualist programme: dharma is not an empirical fact but a normative one; sense-perception cannot ground it; the Veda is autonomous *pramāṇa* for what cannot otherwise be known; word and meaning are eternally and innately related.

But notice what they actually deny. They deny that *pratyakṣa* — empirical sense-perception of present things — is a ground for dharma. The reason given is exact and important: *vidyamāno-palambhanatvāt*, "because [perception] apprehends only what is already present." Dharma, by contrast, is not "already present" — it is *what is to be done*, an injunctive modality, a future-directed ought. This is precisely the *is/ought* gap, articulated about a thousand years before Hume.

The Vedic *codanā* is then introduced as the *only* available *pramāṇa* for the deontic. Word-and-meaning are *autpattika* — eternal — because if they were merely conventional, the Veda's normative authority would be parasitic on prior agreements.

Now, consider this from the perspective of Hypothesis B. The Mīmāṃsaka argument is: there is a real domain (the deontic, the to-be-done) which is not accessible via the senses and which requires its own unique *pramāṇa* (the *codanā*, the Vedic injunction). On the externalization-of-inner-process reading, this is precisely the move one would expect from a tradition that has externalized an originally inward operation: the inward operation generated its own normative grip directly (the inner *yajña* "did" something; the consciousness operating it was transformed by it); the externalized version cannot generate that grip from the visible operation alone, and must therefore postulate the deontic as its own autonomous order, accessed through its own autonomous *pramāṇa*.

This is a very strong piece of structural evidence for B. The Mīmāṃsaka's most distinctive doctrine — the autonomy of the deontic, grounded in *codanā* alone — is exactly the kind of formal compensation one would expect when the operation that originally generated normative grip (an inner transformation of consciousness) is no longer present and the system must instead *postulate* the normative as a separate order.

### 3.2 The *bhāvanā* doctrine

Kumārila Bhāṭṭa, in the *Śloka-Vārttika*'s discussion of *codanā* and *vidhi* (and developed extensively in later Bhāṭṭa Mīmāṃsā), distinguishes two species of *bhāvanā* — "bringing into being" — generated by the Vedic injunction:

- *śābdī bhāvanā* (also *abhidhā-bhāvanā*) — the *verbal* bringing-into-being: what the optative suffix (*liṅ*) of the injunction does to the hearer's intention; the motivating force the injunction exerts, prior to any action.
- *ārthī bhāvanā* — the *agentive* bringing-into-being: what the agent does in performing the enjoined act; the agent's volitional energy directed at the prescribed result.

This is sourced in Stanford Encyclopedia's "Kumārila" article (Eltschinger / Yoshimizu), Pārthasārathi Miśra's *Nyāya-Ratna-Mālā*, and the Springer article "What is Bhāvanā?" (D'Avella, 2013). The doctrine is the heart of Kumārila's reading of Vedic injunction.

Two observations bear on the hypothesis.

First, the *bhāvanā* doctrine does name a *psychological* operation — it is not pure ritual procedure. *Śābdī bhāvanā* is the action of the *injunction* on the hearer's *mind*; *ārthī bhāvanā* is the *agent's* volitional bringing-into-being of the result. This is *not* nothing. It is something internal to the agent's psychology. To that extent, the Mīmāṃsaka system does have an internal-psychological dimension, not just an external-ritual one. Hypothesis A is supported here: there is an inner-psychological register in Mīmāṃsā itself, quite apart from any Aurobindonian decoding.

But — second observation — *bhāvanā* is *not* the inner-yogic *manas* operating on *prāṇa-śakti* and *ojas* that Aurobindo decodes from the Vedic horse-and-cow imagery. *Bhāvanā* is closer to Brentanian intentionality or volitional energy: a directedness of the agent's will toward the enjoined action and its intended result. There is no transformation of consciousness, no upward-offering of the lower registers, no recovery of the hidden light from the cave. The "internal" of *bhāvanā* is the agent's first-personal psychology of volition; the "internal" of Aurobindo's Vedic interior is the structure of consciousness itself.

This gap is important and creates trouble for B. The Mīmāṃsaka inner register exists, but it is calibrated to a different conceptual scale. To equate Kumārila's *bhāvanā* with Aurobindo's inner *yajña* requires bridge-work that neither Kumārila nor Aurobindo provides.

### 3.3 The *apūrva* doctrine

The *apūrva* — the unseen efficacy generated by the correctly performed rite — is the most metaphysically distinctive Mīmāṃsaka doctrine. It is required because the rite is performed now, but the result (e.g., svarga) is to come later or after death; something must persist between the act and its fruit; the Mīmāṃsakas, who do not posit a creator-God, identify this as the *apūrva*, an unprecedented potency generated by the rite, inhering in the agent's soul, ripening at its appointed time.

The *apūrva* doctrine cuts both ways for the hypothesis.

*For the hypothesis*: the *apūrva* is exactly the kind of internal-residue concept one would expect if the rite is the externalized form of an inner operation. The inner operation (consciousness offered upward in the Vedic *yajña*) generates its result *in* the consciousness that performed it — that is, the result is intrinsic to the operation. When the operation is externalized, the result is no longer intrinsic to the visible action; it must persist as some unseen something. *Apūrva* is the formal-metaphysical compensation for the loss of intrinsic generation. On the externalization-of-inner-process reading, *apūrva* is the trace of what was once direct.

*Against the hypothesis*: the Mīmāṃsakas themselves are explicit that *apūrva* is *not* a state of consciousness. It is a metaphysical entity — an unseen potency inhering in the *ātman* — but it is not the agent's *jñāna* or *prajñā* or *cit*. It is precisely *not* the inner-yogic transformation. The Mīmāṃsakas' insistence on *apūrva* as a sui generis metaphysical category, distinct from any psychological state, suggests that they are not (consciously) treating the rite as a vehicle for inner transformation; they are treating it as an external operation that generates a real-but-invisible metaphysical residue. This is precisely the move that Hypothesis C says is the *sign* of the disconnect: the Mīmāṃsakas had to invent *apūrva* because the operation no longer worked the way it once did. So C *predicts* that *apūrva* will look exactly the way it does — a metaphysical postulate, severed from the inner life of the agent.

The *apūrva* doctrine, then, is consistent with both directions. By itself it does not decide between B/C and the standard reading. But its very *unusualness* — Sanskrit philosophy is generally chary of unseen mediating entities, yet here the Mīmāṃsakas postulate one — is suggestive. The system has had to introduce a strange metaphysical placeholder to do work that, on the externalization reading, used to be done directly.

### 3.4 What the Mīmāṃsakas *explicitly* deny

This is where the hypothesis runs into its strongest internal resistance. The Mīmāṃsakas, far from being closet Aurobindonians, are *aggressively* polemical against any reading that locates the truth of dharma somewhere other than in the externally-performable injunction.

Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* on Kumārila (sampled from on-disk text) is dense with arguments to the effect that the Veda is *not* about anything other than what the injunction enjoins. The Mīmāṃsaka is hostile to *arthavāda* (descriptive passages) being treated as primary; arthavāda is in service of *vidhi* (injunction). The Mīmāṃsaka is hostile to *upāsanā* (meditative absorption) being treated as the deeper meaning of the rite; *upāsanā* may be commendable but it is not what the *codanā* enjoins. The Mīmāṃsaka is hostile, above all, to the Vedāntic move of treating the *jñāna-kāṇḍa* (the Upaniṣadic knowledge-section) as the higher truth of which the *karma-kāṇḍa* is the lower expression.

The Mīmāṃsaka thinks the *karma-kāṇḍa* is the primary teaching of the Veda. They mean this seriously. They are not, on their own self-understanding, externalizers of an internal practice; they are exegetes of the *external* practice that they take to be the Veda's actual concern.

This is a genuine difficulty for Hypothesis B in the strong form. Mīmāṃsakas would *reject* the claim that they are externalizing what was originally inward. They would say: the inward register that the Vedāntins (and Aurobindo) treat as the "real meaning" is at most a *secondary* or *ornamental* construction; the Veda's primary content is the *codanā-corpus* of dharmic injunctions, and that content is what it is. A hypothesis that the Mīmāṃsakas are externalizing an originally interior operation requires reading them against their own self-conception.

A defender of B has two options. The harder option: argue that the Mīmāṃsakas' aggressive externalism *is itself the symptom of the externalization* — they are *so committed* to the outer register precisely because they have lost contact with the inner one, and the loss is invisible to them. The easier option: weaken B to the claim that *the Mīmāṃsakas may be wrong in their self-understanding*, and the Aurobindonian reading is the correcter description of what the original Vedic *yajña* was, even if the Mīmāṃsakas themselves did not see it that way.

The harder option is intellectually exciting but risks being unfalsifiable. The easier option is more defensible but reduces B to a version of A plus historical conjecture about original Vedic intent — which is exactly the kind of conjecture Aurobindo himself asks the reader to make.

## 4. The Vedāntic critique: does Sureśvara read Mīmāṃsā Aurobindo's way?

A natural test for the hypothesis is to ask whether the major Vedāntic critics of Mīmāṃsā — Maṇḍana Miśra (in *Brahma-Siddhi*) and Sureśvara (in *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* and the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka-Vārttika*) — engage Mīmāṃsā as if it were the externalization of an inner process, or whether they read it (as the standard view does) as outer-ritual-only.

### 4.1 Sureśvara's polemic

Sureśvara's *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* (Alston tr. 1959; PDF on disk; cross-checked verbatim against archive.org djvu text) is an extended argument that ritual action (*karma-kāṇḍa*) cannot produce *mokṣa*. Verses 1.51–1.70 contain the sustained refutation. Verse 1.67 (Alston's translation): "The knowledge derived from Vedic revelation demolishes nescience in its form as modified into the factors necessary for action at a single stroke. Hence there can be no association between the two."

This is a direct refutation of the *jñāna-karma-samuccaya* doctrine — the view (held by Maṇḍana Miśra and Brahmadatta) that liberation comes from a *combination* of knowledge and action. Sureśvara's argument is that knowledge (*jñāna*) destroys ignorance (*avidyā*) at the root; action presupposes the agent-object-action triad, which is a *modification* of ignorance; therefore action cannot cooperate with knowledge in producing liberation, because knowledge dissolves the very framework in which action makes sense.

The shape of Sureśvara's argument matters for the hypothesis. He is *not* arguing: "the Mīmāṃsaka rite once worked because there was an inner correlate, but no longer works because the inner correlate has been lost." He is arguing: "ritual action *as such*, *qua* action, cannot produce liberation, because the structure of action presupposes the very ignorance liberation removes." This is a structural-philosophical argument, not a historical-diagnostic one.

If Sureśvara had endorsed Hypothesis C, he would have said something like: "the rite was once efficacious for inner transformation; in our age the inner correlate is lost; the rite is now empty." He says nothing of the kind. He says the rite was *never* capable of producing liberation, regardless of the agent's interior state.

This is significant negative evidence for C. The most powerful Advaita critique of Mīmāṃsā does *not* take the diagnostic shape that Hypothesis C predicts. It takes the structural-philosophical shape that the standard view assumes: ritual is intrinsically incapable of liberation.

### 4.2 Maṇḍana's mediating position

Maṇḍana Miśra's *Brahma-Siddhi* (on disk in Sanskrit as `mandana_misra_brahma_siddhi_gretil.txt`; English in Kuppuswami Sastri 1937) is the canonical statement of *jñāna-karma-samuccaya* — knowledge and action together produce liberation. Maṇḍana himself was a Mīmāṃsaka before becoming a Vedāntin (the tradition of his debate-defeat by Śaṅkara is famous, though historiographically uncertain). His position is the closest classical Indian analogue to a *bridge* between the Mīmāṃsaka external register and the Vedāntic inner register.

But notice what Maṇḍana does *not* do. He does not argue that the Mīmāṃsaka rite is the *externalization* of the Vedāntic interior. He argues that *both* the rite and the knowledge are required, in a kind of methodological pluralism (action prepares the agent; knowledge then liberates). This is a *coordination* model, not an *identity-under-different-registers* model. It does not endorse B or C.

Sureśvara, in 1.67 and elsewhere, refutes Maṇḍana by arguing that there is no methodological pluralism to be had: knowledge alone suffices, action is structurally incompatible with the dissolution of agency. The dispute between Maṇḍana and Sureśvara is therefore an *internal* dispute about how knowledge and action relate, not about whether the rite has an inner Aurobindonian register.

### 4.3 Provisional summary

The Vedāntic engagement with Mīmāṃsā does *not* take the shape Hypothesis C predicts. The Vedāntins read Mīmāṃsā as outer-ritual-only and either reject it (Sureśvara) or attempt to coordinate it with the inner register as a separate operation (Maṇḍana). They do not say: "Mīmāṃsā is the externalized form of the inner yogic process the Veda originally taught." They say: "Mīmāṃsā gets the Vedic *karma-kāṇḍa* right; we get the *jñāna-kāṇḍa* right; the question is which one is sufficient for *mokṣa*."

This is significant evidence against C in particular. The Vedāntic tradition could have made the Aurobindonian move — it had every motive — but did not.

## 5. Verdict

### 5.1 Hypothesis A (mild) — Supported

A is well-supported. The Mīmāṃsaka system, read with Aurobindo's lexicon in hand, has clear sites of fruitful re-pointing: the *autpattika* relation of word and meaning is structurally close to Aurobindo's claim that the Vedic mantras are the inherent verbal forms of consciousness-states; *codanā* as the autonomous *pramāṇa* of the deontic is structurally close to the operation of an inner injunction in the Vedic *yajña*; *bhāvanā* as the agent's volitional bringing-into-being is structurally close to the inner *kratu* (effective will) that Aurobindo finds in Agni; *apūrva* is structurally close to the inner residue of consciousness-transformation. The hermeneutic is fruitful. A is true in the modest, hermeneutic sense.

### 5.2 Hypothesis B (medium) — Partially supported

B is partially supported but runs into the resistance of the Mīmāṃsakas' own self-understanding. The structural fit between the Aurobindonian inward-yajña reading and the Mīmāṃsaka outward-yajña analysis is striking. The Mīmāṃsaka analytic of the rite reads, on Aurobindo's lens, as a remarkably faithful externalization of an originally inward operation. The Mīmāṃsaka invention of *apūrva* in particular looks, on this reading, like the formal compensation required when intrinsic generation has been lost. But the Mīmāṃsakas themselves *deny* this reading, aggressively. To hold B, one must hold either that (a) they are wrong about themselves, or (b) the externalization happened *before* the Mīmāṃsakas, who are then exegetes of an already-externalized text. (b) is the more defensible position; it is also the position Aurobindo's argument about the loss of the inner sense at pp. 56–57 actually makes.

So: B is defensible in the form "the originally inward Vedic operation has been externalized in a long historical process; the Mīmāṃsakas are the late-stage exegetes of the externalized form." B is *not* defensible in the form "the Mīmāṃsakas themselves are doing the externalizing as a deliberate philosophical move."

### 5.3 Hypothesis C (strong) — Not supported by the textual evidence; novel as a philosophical proposal

C is the user's strongest claim and is not supported by the textual evidence. The Vedāntic critics, who had every motive to make the diagnostic move that C predicts, did not make it. They made a structural argument (ritual is intrinsically incapable of liberation) rather than a diagnostic argument (ritual was once efficacious but the cognitive substrate has changed). The Mīmāṃsakas themselves did not understand their decline, where they understood it at all, in terms of cognitive evolution; they understood it in terms of ritual incompetence, lost qualifications, kali-yuga deterioration, and so on — the standard tradition-internal narrative.

But — this is important — C *is* novel as a philosophical proposal, and it has Aurobindonian resources behind it. Aurobindo himself, in "A Retrospect of Vedic Theory," does say that "the Ages of Intuition were passing away into the first dawn of the Age of Reason" — that is, he does attribute the loss in part to a change in the mode of consciousness. This is precisely the cognitive-evolutionary claim C requires. Aurobindo does not extend this claim to a diagnostic argument about Mīmāṃsā specifically; the user's hypothesis is the extension. The extension is not crazy. It is a plausible application of Aurobindo's evolutionary metaphysics to the Mīmāṃsā case. But it is not what either Aurobindo or the Mīmāṃsakas or the Vedāntins themselves say.

### 5.4 Where the hypothesis is right, overreach, and novel

*Right*: The intuition that the Mīmāṃsaka externalism is *strange* and that it cries out for a deeper explanation is correct. The Mīmāṃsaka apparatus — the *autpattika* word-meaning relation, the autonomy of *codanā*, the *bhāvanā* and *apūrva* doctrines — is a remarkable and slightly bizarre intellectual structure if read flatly as ritual exegesis. The hypothesis correctly identifies that something is going on beneath the surface.

*Overreach*: The claim that the standard tradition-internal narrative is *wrong* is too strong. The standard narrative (priestly decline, loss of qualifications, etc.) is not the deepest account, but it is not therefore false. Multiple causes can coexist; Aurobindo himself names three (cognitive-evolutionary, transmission-loss, institutional ossification). C should be reformulated as: the cognitive-evolutionary cause is the *deepest* and most overlooked, *in addition to* the standard causes. That formulation is defensible.

*Novel*: The diagnostic application of Aurobindo's evolutionary-cognitive claim to the Mīmāṃsā case is novel and worth pursuing. It reframes a long-standing puzzle (why does *karma-kāṇḍa* feel so dead? why did the most rigorously argued school of Indian philosophy become so inert?) in a way that neither the Vedāntic tradition (which says: because ritual is intrinsically inadequate) nor the Mīmāṃsaka tradition (which says: because ritualists got worse) addresses. The novel claim is: the rite *did* work, once, when the cognitive substrate that made it work was present; what failed was the substrate, not the rite.

## 6. Implications for the timeline corpus

If any form of the hypothesis is judged worth carrying into the corpus, three concrete moves are recommended.

### 6.1 A comparative-claim entry

Add a comparative-claim entry that engages the Aurobindo–Mīmāṃsā triangle. The entry should be framed as a *reading hypothesis* rather than a historical thesis: "An Aurobindonian reading of Mīmāṃsā: the *autpattika* word-meaning relation, the *codanā*-autonomy of the deontic, and the *apūrva* doctrine read, on Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* lens, as the formal-metaphysical compensations one would expect from a tradition that has externalized an originally inward Vedic operation. The Mīmāṃsakas would reject this reading. The Vedāntic critics did not make it. But the structural fit is striking and the question of *why* the Mīmāṃsaka apparatus took the precise form it did is not well answered by the tradition's own resources." That is a careful, defensible entry that does not claim more than the evidence supports.

### 6.2 An "Aurobindo on the Veda" sub-section

The aurobindo.json entry being created in a separate dispatch should include a sub-section specifically on the *Secret of the Veda* and its (a) double-language thesis, (b) inner-yajña reading, (c) cognitive-evolutionary account of the loss of the inner sense. The third component is the philosophically strongest and most distinctive — and it is the component that connects to the broader Aurobindonian doctrine of the *Human Cycle* and the evolution of consciousness. It is also the component that funds any future engagement with the Mīmāṃsā question.

### 6.3 Primary texts to acquire

The current on-disk corpus is good but incomplete for further work on this question. The highest-priority acquisitions are:

1. **Śabara-Bhāṣya on Jaimini's *Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra***, especially on 1.1.2 (*codanā-lakṣaṇo'rtho dharmaḥ*) and 1.1.5 (*autpattikas tu śabdasya-arthena saṃbandhaḥ*). Śabara is the foundational classical commentator; without him, all later Mīmāṃsā discussion floats. Sanskrit text on GRETIL; Ganganatha Jha's English translation in the Gaekwad Oriental Series.

2. **Kumārila's *Tantra-Vārttika***, especially the discussion of *arthavāda* and the relation of injunction to descriptive passages. *Tantra-Vārttika* is where Kumārila most directly engages the question of whether the Veda's non-injunctive content has its own truth-value or is merely supportive of injunction. This is the precise locus where any Aurobindonian re-reading would have to engage.

3. **Maṇḍana Miśra's *Vidhi-Viveka***, the great Mīmāṃsaka treatise on the nature of injunction (predating his Vedāntic *Brahma-Siddhi*). This is the text that most directly defends the Mīmāṃsaka externalism and that any Aurobindonian re-reading would have to come to terms with.

4. **Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* in English translation** (the on-disk Sanskrit is dense and Sucaritamiśra is the most subtle of Kumārila's commentators on the *codanā* question).

The single most important text, if only one can be acquired, is the **Śabara-Bhāṣya**. It is the indispensable starting point for any rigorous engagement with Mīmāṃsā as Mīmāṃsā.

## 7. Closing methodological note

The hypothesis, as formulated, has the form of a deep diagnostic claim against an entire darśana's self-understanding. Such claims are intellectually high-risk and high-reward. The risk is unfalsifiability: if the Mīmāṃsakas deny the reading, that itself is evidence of their captivity to the external register; nothing they say can count as counter-evidence. The reward, if the claim is right, is a genuine philosophical illumination of a long-standing puzzle (the inertness of *karma-kāṇḍa*) by means of a non-obvious causal hypothesis (cognitive evolution rather than ritual decline).

The honest position is: the hypothesis is too strong as a historical-causal claim about why Mīmāṃsā declined; it is too weak as a mere hermeneutic exercise; it is most defensible in the form of a *structural-genealogical* claim — the Mīmāṃsaka analytic is the externalized form of the inward Vedic operation, the externalization happened over a long historical process to which Aurobindo's evolutionary-cognitive account is a partial answer, and the Mīmāṃsakas themselves are late-stage exegetes of the already-externalized form rather than the agents of the externalization.

In that form, the hypothesis is worth carrying. In its strongest form, it overreaches the available textual evidence — but the user, who said "they might very much be disagreeing with my thesis," has already anticipated that.
