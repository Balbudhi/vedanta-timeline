# Reading Sāṃkhya with Anirban Bandyopadhyay

*A guided long-form reader through classical Sāṃkhya — Īśvarakṛṣṇa's Sāṃkhya-Kārikā with the commentaries of Gauḍapāda and Vācaspati Miśra, the structural twin in the Yoga-Sūtras of Patañjali with Vyāsa's Bhāṣya — read in counterpoint with Anirban Bandyopadhyay's contemporary physics formalization of the three guṇas as operations on a Phase Prime Metric, with Deleuze on the seed, the tree, and the rhizome, and a connection back to the architecture of the user's thesis.*

## Reader's Orientation

Classical Sāṃkhya — the oldest of the six *darśanas* of orthodox Indian thought, older than the canonical Yoga-Sūtras whose metaphysics it underwrites — gives us the **three guṇas**: *sattva*, *rajas*, *tamas*, the threefold "threads" of which *prakṛti* (Nature, the manifest principle) is woven. Every appearance is for Sāṃkhya a particular composition of these three. The deeper picture to keep in view is that light or consciousness is fundamental, while *prakṛti* is the veil or engine of form standing between infinity and definite appearance: tamas is the substantial thread of that veil, rajas its motion or pulsation, sattva its clarity, the transparent thread through which feedback from infinity enters manifestation. The doctrine is enumerative, austere, structural — and, until very recently, stranded. From Vijñānabhikṣu in the late sixteenth century to the present, no major thinker has tried to *formalize* the guṇas. They have been inherited as metaphysical primitives, taxonomised psychologically, or moralised. Nobody has asked: what *kind of mathematical objects* are the three guṇas? What *operations* on a definite formal substrate would instantiate them?

Cross-engagement: Abhinavagupta presses the same substrate structure axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `one-self-standing` rather than `many-coordinate-reals` in [primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

**Anirban Bandyopadhyay** — Principal Researcher at the National Institute for Materials Science (Tsukuba); experimental collaborator of Stuart Hameroff in the Penrose-Hameroff Orch-OR program; author of *Nanobrain* (2020) — is the first physicist to have given a definite answer. He claims the three guṇas are physical operations on a single mathematical primitive — the **Phase Prime Metric (PPM)**, which he identifies with Puruṣa. *Sattva* is *projection from infinity*; *rajas* is *transformation of one geometric shape into another at a singularity*; *tamas* is *bonding of geometric shapes at undefined points*. One hardware, three operations; consciousness is the simultaneous co-presence of all three modes on the same substrate. If even approximately correct, this is the first contemporary scientific formalization of Sāṃkhya since the seventeenth century. For the user's thesis, it furnishes a formal substrate for the metaphysics of form (T1), experimental traction for unity-in-multiplicity (T9), and a physics-language interlocutor for divine play through self-veiling (T7).

**Method.** Two commitments govern this reader. First, classical Sāṃkhya is engaged literally as a metaphysics of what reality IS — *prakṛti* really transforms, *puruṣa* really witnesses, the guṇas really are real constituents of the manifest world. We do not translate Sāṃkhya into "spiritual psychology" or treat guṇas as Aristotelian "qualities." Second, Anirban's polyatomic time-crystal mathematics is cited verbatim where he makes claims, but is not interpreted philosophically — the formal apparatus belongs to physics, not to the philosophical synthesis pursued here. For deeper Sanskritic engagement (Yuktidīpikā, Vijñānabhikṣu, *Mokṣa-Dharma*), see the companion reader `13_kala_cakra_clock_structures.md`.

The reader proceeds in seven parts: **I.** Sāṃkhya-Kārikā walk-through with Devanāgarī, IAST, English, and Vyāsa on YS II.18, II.20. **II.** The three guṇas in detail with the *Bhagavad-Gītā* 14 inflection. **III.** Anirban's mapping with verbatim quotation. **IV.** The PPM and primes-as-primitives. **V.** Deleuze (virtual/actual, rhizome, fold) on the seed-tree question — see also `01_deleuze_difference_repetition.md`. **VI.** The user's thesis architecture. **VII.** Reading sequence and open questions.

A small glossary, glossed on first appearance:

- **prakṛti** — Nature; the unmanifest material principle which differentiates into the manifest world; from *pra-kṛ*, "to make forth, to produce."
- **puruṣa** — pure consciousness; the witness; the non-active spectator of *prakṛti*'s spectacle; literally "person" or "man" in Vedic Sanskrit.
- **guṇa** — literally "thread, strand, quality"; in Sāṃkhya the three constitutive principles of *prakṛti*. Not "qualities" in the Aristotelian sense but the very fabric of which the manifest is woven.
- **sattva** — the guṇa of luminosity, lightness, intelligibility; produces *prakāśa* (illumination).
- **rajas** — the guṇa of activity, restless motion, passion; produces *pravṛtti* (activity, going-forth).
- **tamas** — the guṇa of heaviness, obstruction, concealment; produces *niyama* (restraint, fixation) and *varaṇa* (covering).
- **mahat / buddhi** — the "great one"; the first evolute of *prakṛti*; the intellective principle; structurally the discriminating faculty that distinguishes *puruṣa* from *prakṛti*.
- **ahaṃkāra** — the I-maker; the second evolute; the principle that appropriates experiences as "mine."
- **manas** — the lower mind; the synthesizing faculty that coordinates the senses.
- **tanmātra** — "that-only"; subtle element; the five subtle predecessors of the gross elements (sound-only, touch-only, form-only, taste-only, smell-only).
- **mahābhūta** — gross element; the five (ether/space, air, fire, water, earth).
- **prakṛti-vikṛti** — both producing and produced; the seven evolutes intermediate between *mūla-prakṛti* and the sixteen pure modifications.
- **vyakta** — the manifest.
- **avyakta** — the unmanifest; *mūla-prakṛti* itself.
- **kaivalya** — isolation; the soteriological goal of Sāṃkhya — *puruṣa*'s recognition of its eternal distinction from *prakṛti*.
- **vivarta** — apparent transformation (Advaita's preferred term).
- **pariṇāma** — real transformation (Sāṃkhya's preferred term — *prakṛti* really transforms into its evolutes; the *vivarta-pariṇāma-vāda* distinction divides Vedānta from Sāṃkhya at this point).
- **puruṣārtha** — the purpose of *puruṣa*; the teleological clause that makes *prakṛti*'s evolution intelligible.

And from Anirban Bandyopadhyay's terminology:

- **Phase Prime Metric (PPM)** — a combinatorial object derived from how *n* nodes can group into prime-sized partitions, plotted as C(n); identified by Anirban with Puruṣa.
- **time crystal** — a structure with periodic order in time (Wilczek 2012); in Anirban's usage, a polyatomic clocking architecture in which clocks nest inside clocks.
- **SOMU manifold** — Self-Operating Mathematical Universe; the cosmological extension of the PPM construction.
- **fractal information theory (FIT)** — the framework in which a Turing tape contains a Turing tape inside every cell.
- **FIT-GML** — Fractal Information Theory plus Geometric Musical Language; Anirban's information-processing primitive.
- **singularity superposition** — the operation of *rajas* in his framework; the locus where "everything of Puruṣa superposes to make ideally random."
- **DDG (Dodecanogram)** — Anirban's high-frequency EEG instrument, measuring activity from millihertz to terahertz across twelve frequency bands.
- **/Zhe operator** — the prime-symmetric topological composition operator on multinion tensors introduced in Singh *et al.* 2020.

Sāṃkhya is the metaphysics of *form* (T1) — not of *ground* (Advaita) or *relation* (Viśiṣṭādvaita, Madhva, Caitanya). It addresses what the manifest is *made of*. Its *puruṣa-prakṛti* dualism is not a competitor to Advaitic monism; the two address different questions. Take the experimental observations in Anirban's corpus as data; take the theoretical framework as a serious research program; reserve judgment on whether the *guṇa* identifications are *forced* or merely *suggested* by the formalism.

## Part I — The Sāṃkhya-Kārikā of Īśvarakṛṣṇa: A Systematic Walk

The *Sāṃkhya-Kārikā* of Īśvarakṛṣṇa (c. 4th–5th c. CE) is the foundational text of classical Sāṃkhya, seventy-two *āryā* verses codifying an older oral school traced (SK 70) from Kapila through Āsuri and Pañcaśikha. Sāṃkhya is older than the canonical *Yoga-Sūtras* (c. 2nd–4th c. CE), which presuppose its metaphysics. The principal commentaries are Gauḍapāda's *Bhāṣya* (c. 6th–7th c.), Vācaspati Miśra's *Tattva-Kaumudī* (c. 9th–10th c., the most lucid and widely studied), and the anonymous Yuktidīpikā (c. 6th–8th c.). Standard translations: Larson 1969, Burley 2007, Phukan 1960. What follows is a selective walk through the verses that establish the metaphysical structure, keeping the English renderings and the verse references that matter for later comparison.

Cross-engagement: Adi Sankara presses the same manifestation status axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `sublatable-not-null` rather than `real-transformation` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

### Kārikā 1–2 — The Threefold Suffering and the Inadequacy of Vedic Remedy

> **SK 1.** "From the blow of the threefold suffering there arises inquiry into the means that removes it. If one says this is pointless because visible remedies are already known: no, because they yield cessation that is neither certain nor absolute."

Cross-engagement: Anandabodha presses the same selfhood structure axis in the phenomenological register, but with the value `witness-self` rather than `substantial-self` in [primary text](cite://sarvajnatman/samksepa-sariraka/1.319-352). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

> **SK 2.** "The Vedic remedy resembles the worldly one, because it is bound up with impurity, wasting, and excess. Better is the contrary: discriminative knowledge of the manifest (*vyakta*), the unmanifest (*avyakta*), and the knower (*jña*)."

The threefold suffering — *ādhyātmika* (intra-personal), *ādhibhautika* (inter-personal), *ādhidaivika* (super-personal) — is the existential datum from which Sāṃkhya begins. SK 2 rules out worldly means (*dṛṣṭa* — yielding neither *aikāntika*/final nor *ātyantika*/absolute cessation) and Vedic ritual remedies (*ānuśravika* — involving impurity, decay, stratified comparison). The remedy is *vivekajñāna*; its threefold object is *vyakta* (manifest), *avyakta* (unmanifest *mūla-prakṛti*), and *jña* (knower, *puruṣa*) — the structure of everything that follows.

### Kārikā 3 — The Twenty-Five Tattvas

> **SK 3.** "*Mūla-prakṛti* is unproduced. The seven beginning with *mahat* are both productive and produced. The set of sixteen are products. *Puruṣa* is neither productive nor produced."

Cross-engagement: Alfred North Whitehead presses the same temporal mode axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `process-primary` rather than `substance-primary` in [primary text](cite://whitehead/process-and-reality/1.2). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

This is the structural verse of Sāṃkhya. The **twenty-five *tattvas***: (1) **Mūla-prakṛti**, *avikṛti* — uncaused first cause. (2–8) The seven *prakṛti-vikṛtis* (both producing and produced) — *mahat* (=*buddhi*); *ahaṃkāra*; and the five *tanmātras*. (9–24) The sixteen *vikāras* — eleven *indriyas* (five *jñānendriyas* + five *karmendriyas* + *manas*) plus the five *mahābhūtas* (ether, air, fire, water, earth). (25) **Puruṣa** — neither cause nor effect, witness only. The entire manifest, including the inner instrument (*antaḥkaraṇa* = *buddhi* + *ahaṃkāra* + *manas*), is real transformation (*pariṇāma*) of *prakṛti*. This is *sat-kārya-vāda*: the effect pre-exists in the cause; *prakṛti* differentiates from itself. SK 9 gives five reasons (*asad-akaraṇāt*, *upādāna-grahaṇāt*, *sarva-sambhavābhāvāt*, *śaktasya śakya-karaṇāt*, *kāraṇa-bhāvāt*) — the central polemic against Buddhist *pratītyasamutpāda* and Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika *asat-kārya-vāda*.

### Kārikā 8 — Why Prakṛti is Unseen

> **SK 8.** "Its non-perception comes from subtlety, not from non-existence. It is known through its effects. *Mahat* and the rest are its effects, at once unlike it and like it."

Cross-engagement: Akalanka presses the same practice-path axis in the soteriological register, but with the value `meditative-discipline` rather than `knowledge-discipline` in [primary text](cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/1.34-35). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

SK 7 enumerated eight reasons for non-perception (distance, proximity, sensory defect, inattention, subtlety, intervention, suppression, confusion with similars). *Prakṛti* falls under subtlety: known through its effects by *anumāna sāmānyatodṛṣṭa* (general-empirical inference, SK 5–6). The effects are *prakṛti-virūpaṃ sarūpaṃ ca* — *like* in tri-guṇa structure (SK 14), *unlike* in being particularised. The cause is partially but not exhaustively recoverable in its effects.

### Kārikā 11 — The Characteristics of the Manifest

> **SK 11.** "The manifest is constituted by the three *guṇas*, non-discriminating, objectual, common, non-conscious, and productive. *Pradhāna* is the same. *Puruṣa* is the reverse of these."

Cross-engagement: Anantakrishna Sastri presses the same soteric end axis in the soteriological register, but with the value `identity-with-ground` rather than `isolation-or-discriminative-release` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

SK 10 enumerated eight contrasting characteristics of manifest vs unmanifest. SK 11 fixes the six characteristics manifest and unmanifest *prakṛti* share — tri-guṇa, *aviveki* (undiscriminative), *viṣaya* (object), *sāmānya* (common), *acetana* (unconscious), *prasava-dharmi* (productive). *Puruṣa* is outside this genus on every count: discriminative, subject, individual, conscious, non-productive. The deepest line of Sāṃkhyic dualism: *puruṣa* is not in the genus of any thing made-of-guṇas.

### Kārikā 12 — The Cardinal Verse on the Three Guṇas

> **SK 12.** "The *guṇas* are of the nature of *prīti*, *aprīti*, and *viṣāda*; their functions are illumination (*prakāśa*), activity (*pravṛtti*), and restraint (*niyama*); their modes of operation are mutual overcoming, mutual support, mutual production, and mutual coupling."

Cross-engagement: Baladeva Vidyabhushana presses the same causation model axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `unchanged-ground-with-changing-power` rather than `real-transformation` in [primary text](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

The metaphysical heart of Sāṃkhya, fixing the guṇas in three triads:

1. **Affective nature** (*ātmakāḥ*): sattva = *prīti* (pleasure); rajas = *aprīti* (pain/restlessness); tamas = *viṣāda* (stupor).
2. **Operational purpose** (*arthāḥ*): sattva = *prakāśa* (illumination); rajas = *pravṛtti* (activity); tamas = *niyama* (restraint, fixation).
3. **Mutual relations** (*vṛttayaḥ*): *abhibhava* (suppression), *āśraya* (support), *janana* (generation), *mithuna* (pairing). The guṇas are *parasparam* — never pure, never isolated; every actual state is a composition in which one predominates.

The cardinal clause: *anyonya-abhibhava-āśraya-janana-mithuna-vṛttayaḥ*. This *covariance* is a structural feature any formalization must capture. Anirban's "one hardware, three souls" — three operations on a single PPM, each requiring the others to define its target — is a contemporary instantiation of this *anyonya-vṛtti*.

The user's framing deepens rather than revises this. The three guṇas are not abstract labels floating free of a substrate; they are the texture of the veil itself. *Prakṛti* is the fabric-of-reality or engine-of-form. Tamas is the actual substance-thread of that fabric, rajas the thread of motion and pulsation, sattva the clear thread. In operational terms, that clarity is not something merely adjacent to "feedback from infinity"; it *is* the feedback-from-infinity by virtue of being the transparent mode in which the unbounded reaches the bounded. This is fully continuous with Anirban's vocabulary: his "sattva = projection from infinity" is the same metaphysics stated from the side of the light passing through the veil rather than from the side of the veil's texture.

### Kārikā 13 — The Defining Functions and the Lamp

> **SK 13.** "Sattva is light and illuminating. Rajas is driving and mobile. Tamas is heavy and obscuring. Their operation, for the sake of *puruṣa*, is like a lamp."

SK 13 fixes the physical-aesthetic signatures: sattva = *laghu* (light, buoyant) + *prakāśaka* (illuminating); rajas = *upaṣṭambhaka* (stimulating) + *cala* (mobile); tamas = *guru* (heavy) + *varaṇaka* (concealing).

The closing *pradīpavat* (lamp) simile is among the most important in the *Kārikā*. The wick (tamas, the substrate), the oil (rajas, the active medium), the flame (sattva, the illumination) cooperate — despite mutually opposing physical characters — *for a single purpose*, the giving of light. The three guṇas cooperate for the single *puruṣārtha* — the seeing-by-puruṣa and eventual *kaivalya*. Any contemporary formalization recovering both opposition and cooperation is faithful to the *Kārikā*. Anirban's three simultaneous operations on the same PPM is the lamp-structure restated.

### Kārikā 14–16 — The Inference to the Unmanifest

> **SK 14.** "The manifest's non-discriminating and related character is established because it is constituted by the three *guṇas* and lacks the opposite properties. Because the effect has the nature of the cause's *guṇas*, the unmanifest too is established."

> **SK 15.** "The unmanifest is established because differentiations are limited, because there is homogeneity, because capacities issue into activity, because cause and effect are distinct, and because the universal remains undivided there."

> **SK 16.** "The unmanifest cause exists and proceeds into manifestation through the combination of the three *guṇas*, by transformation, like water, according to the differing predominance of each guṇa-base."

SK 14–16 comprise the *anvaya-vyatireka* argument for *prakṛti*. The manifest's tri-guṇa-ness requires a tri-guṇa cause; SK 15 adds six further reasons (limitation of differentiations, homogeneity, capability + activity, cause-effect distinction, non-differentiation of universal-form). SK 16 gives the *salilavat* (water) simile: as water takes the shape of each vessel without losing water-nature, so *prakṛti* differentiates by the differential predominance of each guṇa, without ceasing to be the unitary tri-guṇa substrate. This is *pariṇāma-vāda* — real transformation, not Advaita's *vivarta*.

### Kārikā 17 — The Five Reasons for Puruṣa

> **SK 17.** "*Puruṣa* exists because the aggregate exists for another, because there must be the opposite of the three *guṇas* and the rest, because there must be a ground of superintendence, because there must be an enjoyer, and because activity is for the sake of *kaivalya*."

The classical Indian argument for pure consciousness. Five abductive reasons: (i) every composite exists for-another's-sake (*saṅghāta-parārthatva*); (ii) the tri-guṇa requires its non-tri-guṇa opposite; (iii) unconscious *prakṛti* requires a conscious superintendent; (iv) the phenomenology of enjoyment requires an enjoyer; (v) *prakṛti*'s activity-for-*kaivalya* presupposes a *puruṣa* whose *kaivalya* is the goal. This is the rigorous Indian articulation that consciousness cannot be reduced to its contents — restated in K. C. Bhattacharyya's *Subject as Freedom* (1930) as the *non-objectifiable* subject, with structural affinities to Kant's transcendental I, Husserl's pure ego, Heidegger's *Dasein*.

### Kārikā 18–19 — The Plurality and the Witness-Character of Puruṣa

> **SK 18.** "The plurality of *puruṣas* is established from the distinct distribution of births, deaths, and instruments, from the non-simultaneity of activity, and from the diverse configurations of the *guṇas*."

> **SK 19.** "From that opposition it is established that *puruṣa* is witness, isolated, neutral, a seer, and non-agent."

The plurality argument distinguishes Sāṃkhya from Advaita: if there were only one *puruṣa*, liberation of one would be liberation of all. Empirical-phenomenological diversity (different births, deaths, bodies, *guṇa* proportions) requires plural *puruṣas* — each individual is eternally distinct. Aurobindo's *caitya puruṣa* descends from this. SK 19 enumerates five characteristics: *sākṣin* (witness), *kevala* (isolated), *mādhyastha* (neutral), *draṣṭṛ* (seer), *akartṛ* (non-actor) — *puruṣa* is pure subjectivity *behind* every experience but *in* none.

### Kārikā 20–21 — The Conjunction: The Lame and the Blind

> **SK 20.** "Through conjunction with *puruṣa*, that which is unconscious appears as if conscious; and the indifferent *puruṣa* appears as if an agent within the activity of the *guṇas*."

> **SK 21.** "For the sake of *puruṣa*'s seeing, and for the sake of *pradhāna*'s *kaivalya*, the two are conjoined like the lame and the blind; from that conjunction creation proceeds."

The paradox: *puruṣa* is non-active and *prakṛti* is unconscious; how does experience occur? Resolution: the *saṃyoga* (conjunction). *Prakṛti* appears conscious (illuminated by *puruṣa*); *puruṣa* appears active (the guṇas' agency reflects in its presence). The lame man (*puruṣa* — sees but cannot move) and the blind man (*prakṛti* — moves but cannot see) cooperate; the lame mounts the blind; together they accomplish what neither can alone. The cooperation is *for the sake of the seeing of puruṣa* and *for the kaivalya of pradhāna*: the *saṃyoga* is itself the engine of the eventual *vi-yoga*. Manifestation exists for the sake of its own self-undoing in *puruṣa*'s recognition of its distinctness from the spectacle.

### Kārikā 22 — The Order of Evolution

> **SK 22.** "From *prakṛti* comes *mahat*; from that, *ahaṃkāra*; from that, the set of sixteen; and from five among those arise the five *bhūtas*."

SK 22 fixes the evolutionary sequence: *mūla-prakṛti* → *mahat* → *ahaṃkāra* → sixteen, with each *tattva* the cause of the next — the apparently arborescent structure (Part V will revisit). The sixteen are sub-structured: *ahaṃkāra* in *vaikārika* (sattvic) mode produces the eleven *indriyas*; in *bhūtādi* (tamasic) mode the five *tanmātras*; *taijasa* (rajasic) contributes activity to both. The *tanmātras* produce the *mahābhūtas*: *śabda* → ākāśa, *sparśa* → vāyu, *rūpa* → tejas, *rasa* → ap, *gandha* → pṛthivī.

### Kārikā 38–40 — The Subtle and the Gross Elements

> **SK 38.** "The *tanmātras* are the non-particulars. From them arise the five *bhūtas*. These are the particulars, tranquil (*śānta*), fierce (*ghora*), and dull (*mūḍha*)."

> **SK 39.** "The particulars are of three kinds: subtle bodies, those born from mother and father, and those born together with their elements. Of these, the subtle bodies persist across rebirth, whereas the parental bodies fall away."

> **SK 40.** "The subtle body, arising from a prior creation, unconfined and determinate, extending from *mahat* down to the subtle elements, transmigrates without itself enjoying, perfumed by dispositions (*bhāvas*)."

Two refinements: (i) the *sūkṣma* (subtle) vs *sthūla* (gross) body distinction — the *liṅga-śarīra* (*mahat* + *ahaṃkāra* + *manas* + ten *indriyas* + five *tanmātras*) persists across rebirths, carrying *bhāvas* (*dharma*, *jñāna*, *vairāgya*, *aiśvarya* and opposites). (ii) Even gross *bhūtas* are tri-guṇa: *śānta* (sattvic), *ghora* (rajasic), *mūḍha* (tamasic).

### Kārikā 56–57 — The Milk Simile

> **SK 56.** "so this whole evolution from *prakṛti*, from *mahat* down to the specific *bhūtas*, is for the sake of each *puruṣa*'s liberation, though its own activity appears as if meant for another."

> **SK 57.** "Just as milk, though unconscious, flows for the calf's growth, so *pradhāna* acts for *puruṣa*'s liberation."

The *kṣīra-vatsa* simile: milk flows for the calf without consciously intending it; so *prakṛti* — unconscious — operates for *puruṣa*'s liberation without consciously intending. *Teleology without a teleologist* — internal directionality without anyone directing. This is precisely the structure that allows Anirban's PPM to be both unconscious (a mathematical pattern) and teleological (organized toward a recognizable outcome).

### Kārikā 65–68 — The Realization and Kaivalya

> **SK 65.** "Then *puruṣa*, standing firm like a spectator, sees *prakṛti* as one whose productive activity has ceased, whose seven forms have withdrawn because their purpose has been fulfilled."

> **SK 66.** "One says, 'She has been seen by me,' and stands apart in indifference. The other says, 'I have been seen,' and comes to rest. Even if conjunction remains, creation has no further purpose."

> **SK 67.** "With right knowledge attained, and *dharma* and the rest deprived of causal force, the liberated one remains embodied by the momentum of residual impressions, like a wheel still spinning after the impulse stops."

> **SK 68.** "When the body breaks up, and *pradhāna* has fully withdrawn after accomplishing its task, the liberated one attains both final and absolute *kaivalya*."

SK 65–68 give the *jīvanmukti* / *videhamukti* sequence. SK 66 is the exchange between spectator and dancer: *puruṣa* — "I have seen her" — becomes *upekṣaka*, indifferent; *prakṛti* — "I have been seen" — *uparatā*, desists. The *saṃyoga* continues, the body persists, the dance is over. SK 67: the body persists by force of *saṃskāras*, like a potter's wheel spinning after the potter lifts his hand (*cakra-bhrama-nyāya*). SK 68: at body's dissolution, *kaivalya* — both *aikāntika* and *ātyantika* — is attained.

### A Note on the Yoga-Sūtras with Vyāsa-Bhāṣya

The *Yoga-Sūtras* (c. 2nd–4th c. CE) presuppose Sāṃkhya's twenty-five *tattvas*, *puruṣa-prakṛti* dualism, three guṇas, and *kaivalya*; what they add is the eight-limbed *aṣṭāṅga* and the optional *īśvara* (YS I.23–28, a special *puruṣa* available for devotional contemplation). Two sūtras compress the guṇa metaphysics:

> **YS II.18.** "The seen has the character of illumination, activity, and stasis. It is constituted of elements and sense-faculties. Its purpose is enjoyment and emancipation."

The three characters — *prakāśa* (sattva), *kriyā* (rajas), *sthiti* (tamas) — are the three guṇas under functional names. *Sthiti* (stasis) for tamas (vs the *Kārikā*'s *niyama*) emphasizes the holding function. Vyāsa's *Bhāṣya*: the seen has a dual purpose (*dvi-vidham prayojanam*) — *bhoga* (for the *puruṣa* still in *saṃyoga*) and *apavarga* (for the *puruṣa* who attains *vivekajñāna*). Both are *for puruṣa*.

> **YS II.20.** "The seer is sight-only. Though pure, it looks along with the cognitions of the *buddhi*."

*Puruṣa* as pure witness. *Dṛśi-mātra* — sheer seeing, not a thing-that-sees but the act of seeing as such (K. C. Bhattacharyya's "subject as freedom-from-object"). *Pratyayānupaśya* — looks-along the cognitions of the *buddhi* without being modified by them. Yoga is *citta-vṛtti-nirodha*; when *citta*'s modifications cease, what is left is *puruṣa* in native *kaivalya*. Yoga is *seśvara-sāṃkhya* — same metaphysics, practical apparatus added. The three-guṇa doctrine is, through Yoga, the common inheritance of all subsequent Indian philosophy of consciousness — Śaṅkara's *māyā triguṇātmikā*, Abhinavagupta's Trika *prakṛti*.

One further reception point matters for the user's project. The anonymous *Yuktidīpikā* presses harder than the *Kārikā* itself on causation and on the guṇas' mutual implication; Vijñānabhikṣu, in the *Sāṃkhya-Pravacana-Bhāṣya*, later re-reads Sāṃkhya inside a broader Vedāntic-Yogic horizon. That later trajectory matters because it shows that classical Sāṃkhya was never only a sealed dualism. It was also a living analytic grammar for form, causation, embodiment, and liberation, one that later thinkers kept reopening.

## Part II — The Three Guṇas: Soteriological Inflection

The *Kārikā* gives the guṇas their structural definition (SK 12, 13). The *Bhagavad-Gītā* 14 (*guṇa-traya-vibhāga-yoga*) supplies a soteriological inflection that pure Sāṃkhya does not.

### Sattva

Etymology: from *sat* ("being, real"). Cosmological role: provides the *intelligibility* of the manifest — sattva is the principle that makes the manifest *appear*, that gives it phenomenal availability. Phenomenologically: when sattva predominates, the medium becomes diaphanous and one notices the seen, not the seeing (resonance with Gebser's *diaphany*, McGilchrist's right-hemispheric direct contact, Aurobindo's "comprehending" poise).

The four sattvic *bhāvas* of SK 23: *dharma*, *jñāna*, *vairāgya*, *aiśvarya*. Soteriologically, sattva is propaedeutic — closer to *puruṣa* than the other two, since its luminous-clear character most resembles witness-consciousness — but still *prakṛti*, still a *bond*. *BG* 14.6: *sattvaṃ... sukha-saṅgena badhnāti jñāna-saṅgena cānagha* — "Sattva binds by attachment to pleasure and to knowledge." The final move is from sattva-predominance to *guṇātītatva* (going-beyond), which is *kaivalya*.

### Rajas

Etymology: from *rañj* (to color, to be passionate); cognate with Latin *regere*. Cosmological role: the energetic principle of all motion and change. Without rajas, sattva would be inert clarity, tamas inert weight; rajas makes their cooperation productive — in the lamp simile, the oil that sustains the flame. Phenomenologically: *intensity-without-stability* — the effort, the striving, the not-yet-done quality of every act.

*BG* 14.7: *rajo rāgātmakaṃ... karma-saṅgena dehinam* — rajas binds by attachment to action, generating new karmic seeds. The yogic discipline aims to *discipline* rajas under sattvic guidance, producing *niṣkāma karma* (action without attachment to fruits) — the *Gītā*'s central praxis. For the user's T7 (divine play through self-veiling): rajas is the energetic substrate of *līlā* — without it, divine play impossible; with it alone, entrapment.

### Tamas

Etymology: cognate with Latin *tenebrae*. The crucial nuance, often missed: *niyama* is not only "restraint" but *fixation* in the constitutive sense. Tamas *fixes* the form, gives a thing its *sthiti* (Patañjali's term in YS II.18). Without tamas, sattva's illumination has nothing to illuminate, rajas's activity no substrate. Tamas is the *constitutive density* of every actual structure — the brute facticity of what is the case (Heidegger's *Faktizität*).

This is precisely where Anirban's tamas = *bonding* meshes with the classical doctrine. Bonding is constitutive fixation; *niyama* in its constructive aspect. The dark/heavy connotation is preserved (the singularity-point is "undefined"), but the constructive role of tamas as that-which-fixes-form comes forward.

*BG* 14.8: *tamas tv ajñānajaṃ... pramādālasya-nidrābhis tan nibadhnāti* — tamas binds by delusion, negligence, lethargy, sleep. Even tamas is *necessary* for the manifest, and must be *integrated* (transmuted under sattvic illumination) rather than abolished.

### What Classical Sāṃkhya Does Not Do

The three guṇas in classical Sāṃkhya are characterized by nature (affective signature), purpose (functional role), operations (mutual relations), and manifestations (cosmological/phenomenological examples). They are *not* characterized mathematically, *not* derived from a more basic principle. The *Kārikā*'s structural precision *invites* a formalization it does not supply: what would it mean to say sattva is a particular *operation* on a particular *substrate*? For fifteen hundred years no one took up the question. Anirban Bandyopadhyay has now done so.

## Part IV — The Phase Prime Metric and the Primes-as-Architectural-Primitives Framework

The three-guṇa physics rests on a deeper claim. Anirban does not begin from the guṇas and reach for primes; he begins from primes and arrives at the guṇas. Without an understanding of the PPM, the guṇa physics is a disconnected mapping; with it, the mapping has a definite formal shape.

### The Core Claim

Primes — the integer prime numbers themselves, 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, … — are, for Anirban, the **architectural primitives of the universe**. Not "like" primitives, not "metaphorically." Literally: every dynamical structure that nature can self-assemble factors into a finite generating set of prime symmetries, and the *pattern of how primes can group and regroup* is a definite mathematical object he calls the **Phase Prime Metric (PPM)**.

The strongest formulations from the corpus:

> "We need **15 primes only to develop a self-operational mathematical universe** that could by itself process 99.99% dynamics of the universe." (Singh *et al.* 2020 Dodecanion paper, §2)

> "**Primes are the fundamental codes of the universe, and if a pair of primes is chosen, their multiples create a fractal of self-similar codes.**" (Medium, *"Why speaking in Sanskrit is an experience with creation rules of the universe"*, June 2024)

> "**Consciousness is the pattern of primes.**" (recurring formulation across his Medium essays and the *bsahely* secondary literature)

The conjunction of these is striking. (i) *Primes are the universal codes*. (ii) The pattern they form is a definite mathematical object, the PPM. (iii) Fifteen primes suffice to generate ~99.99% of all natural dynamics. (iv) Consciousness is the pattern of primes, instantiated in a physical substrate that operates on it. The PPM is the *bridge* between the abstract combinatorial fact (primes) and the physical instantiation (microtubules, time crystals, the brain).

### The Formal Construction

The PPM is, in Anirban's own words (Reddy *et al.* 2018, p. 11):

> "A phase prime metric is a plot (C–n) of the **number of ways** a given number of nodes can connect [under fixed periodicity], where compositions of a waveform are combined until it reaches prime number of nodes. Then, in a particular way, both time and space are integrated. … A group of primes determines C compositions. C–n plot shows … this is called a phase prime metric. **Twelve distinct ways one could connect the dots or events in the phase prime metric**. Therefore, there are **12 sub-metrics, or 12 distinct phase prime metrics**."

Operationally, the construction is:

1. Take *n* nodes arranged on a phase cycle (e.g., a clock with n positions).
2. Compute the number of distinct ways those nodes can group into composition-classes such that the group sizes are *prime*. (For n=6: groups of size 2+2+2 are allowed, 3+3 are allowed, 5+1 is not allowed because 1 is not prime, etc.)
3. Plot the *number of compositions C* against *n*.
4. Repeat for n = 1, 2, 3, … up to a desired maximum.

The resulting plot — the C(n) curve — exhibits a remarkable structure. It has self-similar ripples; it has a global *triplet-of-triplet* symmetry (groups of three ripples nested within groups of three ripples); it has twelve distinguishable sub-patterns when read along different scaling axes. The plot is *the* PPM; it is the canonical object Anirban means when he says "the pattern of primes." It contains, on his analysis, exactly the right symmetries to direct the spontaneous self-assembly of dielectric resonators (proteins, microtubules, organic gels) into operational time-crystal architectures.

The PPM splits into **12 sub-metrics**, each capturing a distinct way to read the C(n) curve. Anirban links these 12 sub-metrics to:

The dodecahedron is the most complex Platonic solid; the dodecanion is the smallest multinion algebra in which all rotations of the dodecahedron close; the Dodecanogram is the instrument designed to capture all twelve frequency bands of biological dynamics; the brain is the system in which all twelve are simultaneously active. ## Part V — Deleuze: The Seed, the Tree, the Rhizome, the Virtual

The user has flagged a question that this Part is designed to answer. Deleuze has views about the *seed* and the *tree* that seem to disagree with Sāṃkhya's tree-of-tattvas. Sāṃkhya's metaphysics is structurally *arborescent*: the orderly evolution from *prakṛti* through *mahat* through *ahaṃkāra* through the eleven *indriyas* and five *tanmātras* down to the five *mahābhūtas* is exactly the kind of root-trunk-branches structure that Deleuze polemicises against in the introduction to *Mille Plateaux* (1980). Yet the user's intuition — borne out by careful reading — is that the disagreement is more apparent than real, once the levels are properly distinguished. This Part lays out the relevant Deleuze, marks the genuine tensions, and shows how the apparent disagreement resolves.

### The Arborescent vs. the Rhizomatic

The introduction to *Mille Plateaux* opens with a methodological declaration: "Nous avons écrit l'Anti-Œdipe à deux. Comme chacun de nous était plusieurs, ça faisait déjà beaucoup de monde." ("We wrote Anti-Oedipus together. Since each of us was several, there was already quite a crowd.") The opening stages an ontology of multiplicity that the rest of the introduction will theorize: each subject is already an assemblage, not a unity; authorship proliferates from a network of intensities, not from a central Self.

Deleuze and Guattari then distinguish two ontologies through two book-types. The **livre-racine** (root-book) operates by "*la loi du livre, c'est la loi de la réflexion, le Un qui devient deux*" — the law of the book is the law of reflection, the One that becomes two. The classical book reflects a world; it has a root (an organizing principle), a trunk (sequential logic), branches (derived concepts), leaves (applications). This is the **arborescent** model: Western metaphysics has consistently figured reality as a tree (Porphyry's tree of genera and species, Hegel's *Logic*, Chomsky's tree-grammars). The root-book is the book of the One.

The **rhizomatic book** is different in kind. A rhizome — botanically, an underground stem from which roots and shoots emerge laterally — has neither beginning nor end, only a middle from which it grows and overspills. Deleuze and Guattari elaborate six principles:

> "Any point of a rhizome can be connected to any other, and must be."

This is the *connection-and-heterogeneity* principle (Principles 1° and 2°). Unlike the tree, which fixes a point and an order, the rhizome admits any-to-any connections; and it connects diverse orders (a wasp and an orchid; a musical score and a poem; mathematics and ritual). The principle of *multiplicité* (3°) follows: a multiplicity has neither subject nor object, only determinations, magnitudes, dimensions — and these cannot increase in number without the multiplicity changing in nature. The principle of *rupture asignifiante* (4°): a rhizome can be broken anywhere and resumes elsewhere; no break is privileged as the structurally founding cut. The principles of *cartographie* and *décalcomanie* (5° and 6°): the rhizome is a map (productive, oriented to experimentation) and not a tracing (which copies an already-given pattern).

The summary formula: "Le rhizome ne se laisse ramener ni à l'Un ni au Multiple" — the rhizome is not reducible to the One or the Multiple. Where Hegelian dialectic oscillates between the One and the Multiple and proposes synthesis (n+1 — building toward the whole), the rhizome operates by n−1 — subtracting the unifier, refusing the founding One. The injunction-mode of the principle: "*Faites des rhizomes et pas des racines, ne plantez pas! Ne semez pas, piquez!*" — "Make rhizomes, not roots, never plant! Don't sow, prick / graft!" The verb *piquer* is important: not the deep, hierarchical planting that establishes a tree, but the lateral, surface-level grafting that makes a new connection without claiming foundational status.

The deeper philosophical claim — and this is what the user's brief flagged as the apparent Deleuzean objection to Sāṃkhya — is that the arborescent model does not merely *describe* reality. It *produces* what it claims to describe. When thought is structured arborescently, certain connections become possible and others become impossible. The tree pre-selects what gets to count as "part of the tree." This is what Deleuze and Guattari call, polemically, *le fascisme* of the trunk: the tree's very form forecloses lateral, heterogeneous, unauthorised connections. Hegel's *Aufhebung* is the philosophical signature of this exclusionary logic; the rhizome's *et... et... et...* (and... and... and...) is its alternative.

### The Seed and the Tree in Différence et répétition

But there is a different Deleuzean treatment of the seed and the tree, in the earlier *Différence et répétition* (1968), and this one is more sympathetic to Sāṃkhya's structure. In Chapter IV ("Synthèse idéelle de la différence"), Deleuze develops the doctrine of the **virtual** and the **actual**, with the *germ* (the seed) playing a central role.

The virtual, for Deleuze, is *not* the merely possible (which is the standard Aristotelian-Leibnizian concept). The merely possible is "the same minus existence" — it is identical to the actual except that it lacks reality. The virtual is something else: it is *fully real* without being *actual*. The virtual is the structural-differential complex from which the actual is *produced* by a process Deleuze calls *différenciation* (with two meanings — *différentiation* with a "t" being the structural differentiation of the virtual itself, and *différenciation* with a "c" being the actualisation in the actual).

The germ — the seed — is for Deleuze the canonical example of the virtual. The seed contains, as virtual, the structural-differential pattern (the *Idea*, in Deleuze's technical sense) of the tree it will become. The seed *does not contain a small version of the tree*; that would be the merely possible. The seed contains the *problem* (the differential field) of which the tree is a *solution* (an actualisation). The actualised tree does not "look like" the seed; it cannot, because the seed contains a structural complex, not a miniature image. The tree is the *answer* to the seed's *question*; the tree is what the seed's structural-differential complex becomes when actualised in the conditions of soil, water, sunlight, time.

In Deleuze's French: *« la différenciation suppose toujours dans son acte une différentiation »* — actualisation always presupposes a prior differentiation in the virtual. The two-step process — first differentiation in the virtual, then actualisation as the actual — is what the germ-and-tree relation displays canonically.

This is, structurally, *exactly* Sāṃkhya's *prakṛti*-and-tattva relation. *Mūla-prakṛti*, the unmanifest root-Nature, contains the *avyakta* — the unmanifest — which is structurally close to Deleuze's virtual: real but not actual, a differential complex of the three guṇas in their pre-manifest equilibrium. The actualisation is the *pariṇāma* — real transformation — into the *vyakta*, the manifest tattvas. The actualised *mahat*, *ahaṃkāra*, the *indriyas*, the *tanmātras*, the *mahābhūtas* do not "look like" *avyakta prakṛti*; they cannot, because *avyakta prakṛti* is a tri-guṇa equilibrium, not a miniature world. The manifest tattvas are *answers* to the question that the unmanifest *prakṛti* poses; they are the actualisations of its differential field.

Read in this way, classical Sāṃkhya is not the kind of tree Deleuze polemicises against in *Mille Plateaux*. It is closer to the Deleuzean germ-tree relation of *Différence et répétition*. The hierarchical evolution from *prakṛti* through *mahat* through *ahaṃkāra* down to the *bhūtas* is *not* the hierarchical derivation of a Hegelian Logic, where each subsequent term *contains* its predecessor as a "moment." It is the actualisation of a virtual differential field — and the actualised tattvas are *real* in their own right, *irreducible* to a higher synthesis.

This is the user's instinct vindicated: Sāṃkhya's "tree" is not arborescent in the polemical sense. Each *tattva* is a real, irreducible articulation of *prakṛti*'s tri-guṇa structure; each is a "solution" to a different aspect of the virtual problem; none is reducible to or absorbable into a higher term. The whole twenty-five-tattva taxonomy is a *map*, not a *tracing*; a *carte*, not a *calque*. The *Kārikā*'s austerity — its refusal to derive everything from One in the Hegelian manner — is closer to Deleuze's plane of consistency than to Hegel's plane of organization.

### The Three-Guṇa Substructure as Anti-Arborescent

There is a further point. Each *tattva* in Sāṃkhya is, *internally*, a tri-guṇa composition. The *mahat* is sattva-rajas-tamas in a particular proportion (sattva-predominant); the *ahaṃkāra* in its three modes is sattva-predominant (vaikārika), rajas-predominant (taijasa), and tamas-predominant (bhūtādi); the *tanmātras* are tamas-predominant; the *bhūtas* are tamas-predominant in their gross aspect. Every node of the tree-of-tattvas is *itself* a knot of three differential intensities.

This is structurally exactly what Deleuze means by a *plane of consistency*: every "term" in the taxonomy is itself a multiplicity of differential intensities, not a simple identity. The three guṇas at every node make the tree internally rhizomatic. To traverse the tree is not to descend a hierarchy of identities but to move through a sequence of tri-guṇa compositions, each with its own internal lateral structure.

The connection to Anirban: the Phase Prime Metric is closer to a rhizome than to a tree. Primes are not hierarchically ordered; they are an unbounded sequence with no "first principle" beyond the bare 2. The geometric forms the PPM generates connect heterogeneously through the /Zhe operator's bonding-at-corners. There is no master node, no founding root. *But* the three operations — projection / transformation / bonding — impose the directionality that classical Sāṃkhya names *prakṛti*'s evolution toward *puruṣa*'s purpose. So Anirban's framework is *rhizomatic at the substrate* (primes, the PPM combinatorial pattern) and *arborescent in operation* (the three guṇa-modes, directed toward *puruṣārtha*). This is not a contradiction; it is the same structure that Sāṃkhya itself displays — internally rhizomatic at every node, externally directional in its evolutionary unfolding.

### The Fold

A third Deleuzean text bears on the question. *Le Pli: Leibniz et le baroque* (1988) develops the doctrine of the **fold** (*le pli*), which is Deleuze's most mature treatment of the relation between interiority and exteriority, soul and body, virtual and actual. The Baroque, for Deleuze, is "*une fonction opératoire*" — an operative function — that "endlessly produces folds" (*« il ne cesse de faire des plis »*). The fold "goes to infinity" (*« le pli qui va à l'infini »*); it does not stop at a determinate boundary. Where Cartesian space is rectilinear and finite, the Baroque space is folded, infinite, internal-to-its-own-elaboration.

The fold has two floors. The lower floor is matter — the body, the organic, the mechanical — folded upon itself into smaller and smaller portions, each communicating with the whole through the folds. The upper floor is the soul — the monad, the *raison* — folded upward into the comprehending unity. The two floors are not separate substances (against Descartes); they are two aspects of one and the same fold. Matter is folded from below; soul is folded from above. They communicate by *pre-established harmony* — co-expression of the same world from complementary aspects.

For the user's project, the fold is the bridge between Sāṃkhya's *puruṣa-prakṛti* dualism and the user's deeper non-dual position. Sāṃkhya, taken literally, has two substances — *puruṣa* (soul, witness) and *prakṛti* (matter, the active substrate). They cooperate via the *saṃyoga* (the lame and blind cooperation of *Kārikā* 21). But this dualism is not, on a careful reading, two separate substances in a Cartesian sense; the *saṃyoga* is more like Deleuze's fold: two aspects of one folding-relation, where *puruṣa* is the upper floor (the witnessing, the illuminating) and *prakṛti* is the lower floor (the substrate, the active). The Sāṃkhyic *saṃyoga* is *pre-established harmony* in Leibnizian-Deleuzean idiom.

This matters because Anirban's framework is structurally a fold-architecture. The PPM (Puruṣa) is the upper floor — the timeless prime-pattern, the structural substrate of all possible compositions. The phenomenal time-crystal architecture (the brain, the microtubules) is the lower floor — the material instantiation. The three guṇa-operations are *the folds themselves*: projection from infinity is the fold of the upper floor reaching down (sattva); transformation at singularity is the fold of the lower floor reaching up to meet the projection (rajas); bonding at the singularity-corner is the fold of the two floors being sutured into a single composite (tamas). On this reading, the three guṇas are the three modalities of the fold, and consciousness is the simultaneous co-presence of all three folds — the upper-floor pattern and the lower-floor substrate held together in a continuously self-elaborating fold.

### Where Sāṃkhya and Deleuze Genuinely Disagree

There is a real tension, and the user should hold it clearly. Deleuze, in *Mille Plateaux* especially, is *anti-personalist*. The rhizome is lived by *assemblages*, not by persons. There is no monad that expresses the whole (that is Leibniz, not Deleuze in his rhizomatic mode); no divine intelligence that maximizes compossibility (that is Leibniz's God); no individual locus of realization. The rhizome is impersonal.

Sāṃkhya, by contrast, is *radically personal*. The plurality of *puruṣas* (*Kārikā* 18) is irreducible. Each individual is a distinct, eternal *puruṣa*; the soteriological goal is the *kaivalya* of *each* such *puruṣa*. The whole metaphysics is built around the existence of plural witnesses, each of whom must be liberated individually. Aurobindo's *caitya puruṣa* (the psychic being) is the direct philosophical descendant of this Sāṃkhyic plurality: the indestructible individual soul-spark, eternally real.

This is where the user's thesis sides with Sāṃkhya against Deleuze. T11 (the ontology of phenomenology) requires a personal locus — the psychic being, the individual who undergoes *jñāna* by identity, the one who surrenders from fullness. Deleuze would dissolve this individual into a flow of intensities, a becoming-imperceptible, an assemblage that does not correspond to any face, body, or biography. The Aurobindonian-Sāṃkhyic insistence on the indestructible individual is irreconcilable with Deleuze-Guattari's impersonalism in the rhizomatic mode.

The resolution: as the user's earlier reader on Deleuze noted, Deleuze gives *conceptual solvents* against arborescent closure, not a *positive ontology* for the user's position. The rhizome prevents the user's framework from reterritorialising into Hegelian hierarchy. But Aurobindo's śakti-pluralism — the many real modes of the one Real, experienced personally, culminating in surrender from fullness — adds to the Deleuzean plane what Deleuze cannot provide: a self-manifesting Real, a divine freedom (*svātantrya*) that includes self-veiling, and an individual who is not dissolved but transformed. Anirban's "one hardware, three souls" sits comfortably here: one substrate (the PPM, Puruṣa), three operations (the guṇas), but also plural personal centers of consciousness (the individual *puruṣas* of *Kārikā* 18) each of which is a distinct locus of the simultaneous co-presence of the three guṇa-modes.

### Reading the Disagreement Correctly

So the apparent disagreement between Deleuze and Sāṃkhya — Deleuze's anti-arborescent rhizome versus Sāṃkhya's tree-of-tattvas — is real but misframed. The correct reading:

- Sāṃkhya's tree-of-tattvas is *not* a Hegelian-arborescent hierarchy; it is closer to a Deleuzean germ-tree-virtual-actual relation, where each *tattva* is a real solution to a region of the virtual problem posed by *prakṛti*.
- Each *tattva* is internally rhizomatic — a tri-guṇa composition, not a simple identity. The "tree" is, at every node, a knot of three differential intensities.
- The PPM (Anirban's substrate) is more rhizomatic than the *tattva*-tree — primes are not hierarchically ordered.
- The three guṇa-operations impose directionality (the *puruṣārtha*) on this rhizomatic substrate, producing the apparent arborescent structure of Sāṃkhya's evolutionary sequence.
- The fold (*Le Pli*) is the structurally most apt Deleuzean concept for understanding the *puruṣa-prakṛti saṃyoga*: two aspects of one folding-relation, in pre-established harmony.
- The genuine disagreement is on personalism: Sāṃkhya keeps the plural *puruṣas*, Deleuze dissolves them into impersonal assemblages. The user's position keeps the individual.

The seed-and-tree imagery, in the end, supports rather than undermines the user's project. Sāṃkhya's "tree" of tattvas is the actualisation of the virtual differential field of *prakṛti*; each branch is a real solution; the whole is a map of the manifest world. Anirban's three guṇa-operations are the modalities by which the actualisation occurs; the PPM is the structural-differential complex from which the modalities operate. Deleuze's *différence-actualisation* and *fold* concepts are the right philosophical idiom for understanding both. The polemical anti-arborescence of *Mille Plateaux* is reserved for the Hegelian and Chomskyan tree-grammars that Sāṃkhya, on careful reading, never was.

## Part VI — Where Sāṃkhya Sits in the User's Thesis

The user's thesis is articulated in twelve theses (T1–T12) plus auxiliary integrations (Aurobindo's triple-status supermind, Deleuze's univocity of being, eternal return as return of difference, Gebser's aperspectival-integral, McGilchrist's hemispheric thesis, Prigogine's irreversible becoming, Meillassoux's correlationism critique, Abhinavaguptan rasa-theory, the Aurobindonian secret-of-the-Veda, the overmind/supermind distinction). This Part shows where classical Sāṃkhya, the three-guṇa doctrine, and Anirban Bandyopadhyay's contemporary formalization intersect with this architecture.

### T1 — Darśana Register Thesis

T1 says that the six darśanas address different *registers*, not competing total theories. Sāṃkhya is the metaphysics of *form*. It does not compete with Advaita's metaphysics of *ground*; the two address structurally different questions. Sāṃkhya asks: what is the manifest world made of? What are its compositional principles? What determines its movements and its dispositions? Advaita asks: what is the ultimate substrate of which all this is a manifestation? Is the manifest finally real, or is it a sublatable appearance? The questions are different, and the answers do not contradict.

This is where Anirban's framework finds its register-home. The PPM-Puruṣa identification, the three guṇa-operations, the dodecanion algebra — all of these are *form-metaphysics*, in the user's classification. They specify *how the manifest is structured*; they do not address *what the ultimate substrate is*. Anirban's framework is, on a strict reading, neutral on the Advaitic question. Whether the PPM is itself a manifestation of Brahman in the Vivaraṇa sense, or whether it is the impersonal substrate Sāṃkhya posits, or whether it is something further — these are questions the formalism does not foreclose.

For the user, this is the right outcome. Anirban's framework gives Sāṃkhya the empirical traction it needed without absorbing or competing with the Advaitic register. The two readings can coexist: at the metaphysical-of-form level, the manifest is structured by the three guṇa-operations on the PPM; at the metaphysical-of-ground level, the whole structure is a manifestation of the one Real, said in many senses (T1's univocity-of-being clause, taken from Deleuze and from Aurobindo's *Realistic Advaita*).

### T2 — Self-Correcting Trajectory toward Realism

T2 traces the Vedāntic trajectory from Bhartṛprapañca through Bhāskara, Madhva, Caitanya to Aurobindo as a *recovery* of the realism the *Brahma-Sūtra* itself implies. Sāṃkhya is older than this Vedāntic trajectory and, in important ways, runs parallel to it. Sāṃkhya never had the Mayavada problem because it was never Advaita; *prakṛti* is real (*sat-kārya-vāda*); the *vyakta* is a real *pariṇāma* of the *avyakta*; *vivarta* (apparent transformation) is not Sāṃkhya's category, *pariṇāma* (real transformation) is.

This means Sāṃkhya's metaphysical realism *does not need* to be recovered against an Advaitic excess. It is realist from the start. Anirban's framework inherits this realism: the PPM is a real combinatorial pattern; the three guṇa-operations are real physical operations; the time-crystal architectures are real material substrates. The user's project benefits because it now has a *darśanic* register (Sāṃkhya) and a *contemporary scientific* register (Anirban) that both directly support metaphysical realism without requiring detour through the Advaitic-recovery argument.

### T7 — Divine Play through Willful Self-Ignorance

T7 is the user's most original move: Maya is a *manifestation* of the divine; ignorance is *positively existent* (*bhāvarūpa*); biases, perspective, and mind are *intentional expressions* of the divine. Sāṃkhya's *puruṣa* as witness watching *prakṛti*'s spectacle is structurally close to the user's hide-and-seek architecture, but classical Sāṃkhya stops short of the divine self-veiling doctrine because its *puruṣas* are plural and uninvolved — they are not a single divine that veils itself.

Where Sāṃkhya helps: the *kṣīra-vatsa* simile of *Kārikā* 57 — *prakṛti* acts unconsciously yet purposively for *puruṣa*'s release, like milk flowing for the calf. This is *teleology without a teleologist*, internal directionality without conscious intention. The user's T7 needs precisely this structure: ignorance must be *intrinsic* to the divine's self-manifestation (not foreign to it), yet must operate without the divine *intending* the ignorance as a separate act. Sāṃkhya's *kṣīra-vatsa* gives the formal shape: an unconscious-yet-purposive substrate operating for the witness's sake.

Where Anirban helps: the three guṇa-operations on the PPM are *unconscious* (the PPM is a mathematical pattern, not an agent) yet *teleological* (their operations are organized toward the conscious co-presence of all three modes — i.e., toward the recognizable phenomenology of consciousness). This is the structural analog of *prakṛti*'s *sva-kāryam* operating for *puruṣa*'s sake. Anirban's framework gives the user a contemporary physics-vocabulary for what T7 requires.

What the user adds, beyond Sāṃkhya and Anirban: the *single* Real. Aurobindonian *paramādvaita* — non-duality of duality and non-duality. Sāṃkhya's plural *puruṣas* are *aspects* of the one Real; the divine self-veiling occurs at the level where the plurality is itself a manifestation of the one. The *svātantrya* (sovereign freedom) of Kashmir Shaivism allows the one Real to manifest as many witnesses, each apparently independent, each subject to the *kaivalya*-trajectory of Sāṃkhya, each a poise of the same self-veiling-self-revealing Real.

### T9 — Unity-in-Apparent-Multiplicity

T9 says the divine purpose is unity-in-apparent-multiplicity. Pure non-dual meditative withdrawal alone *negates* the effulgent ongoing manifestation; therefore add divine play. *Prakṛti* is the engine of multiplicity in Sāṃkhya; the three guṇa-operations on Anirban's PPM are the operations by which multiplicity is generated and sustained.

This is where the user's Sāṃkhya engagement is most directly fruitful. The classical *guṇa-pariṇāma* — the differential transformation by which *prakṛti* generates the manifold tattvas — is *the* engine of the appearance of multiplicity. Anirban's three operations on the PPM are the contemporary specification of how this engine works: projection from infinity (sattva) gives multiplicity its luminous availability; transformation at singularity (rajas) gives multiplicity its dynamism; bonding at corner-singularities (tamas) gives multiplicity its substantial fixity. The single PPM substrate underwrites the unity; the three operations underwrite the apparent multiplicity. This is an extraordinarily clean formal structure for what T9 requires.

The user's seeking-of-unity-as-purpose maps onto Sāṃkhya's *puruṣārtha* — the purpose of *puruṣa* — which is the teleological clause that makes the entire evolution intelligible. *Prakṛti* operates *for puruṣa*; the three guṇas cooperate *for the single purpose* (the lamp simile of *Kārikā* 13); the recognizable unity is the *single artha* served by the manifest multiplicity. T9 is Sāṃkhyic in its structural form, with the Aurobindonian addition that the *artha* is not merely *kaivalya* (isolation, in Sāṃkhya) but the supramental transformation of the manifest itself.

### T11 — Ontology of Phenomenology

T11 says: wherever there is the person, there is always the divine perceived plus something seemingly outside / not making sense, prompting investigation, ending in sublimation back into understanding-this-as-divine. Hegelian *Aufhebung* recast as Aurobindonian involution-evolution-transformation. Phenomenology of an *individual* (psychic being / *caitya puruṣa*), not Hegelian-universal.

Sāṃkhya's account of *mahat* / *ahaṃkāra* / *manas* is a layered ontology of subjectivity that is structurally close to the Aurobindonian descent of supermind through overmind through intuitive mind through mental → vital → physical. *Mahat* is the discriminative-intellective layer; *ahaṃkāra* is the I-making layer; *manas* is the synthesizing layer. Each is a real *tattva*, irreducible to the others, with its own characteristic operations. The Sāṃkhyic *antaḥkaraṇa* (inner instrument) — *buddhi* + *ahaṃkāra* + *manas* — is the structural template for any layered ontology of subjectivity.

Anirban's framework adds that each of these layers is operationally a particular composition of the three guṇa-operations on the PPM. The *buddhi*'s discriminative function is sattva-predominant (projection from infinity, illumination); the *ahaṃkāra*'s I-making is rajas-predominant in its *taijasa* mode (transformation, the active appropriation of "this is mine"); the *manas*'s synthesizing operation involves all three. The whole inner instrument is a tri-guṇa composition operating on a single PPM-substrate.

For T11, what matters is that *each individual* has their own *antaḥkaraṇa*, their own composition of guṇa-operations, their own particular *bhāvas* (the dispositions of *Kārikā* 23: *dharma*, *jñāna*, *vairāgya*, *aiśvarya* and their opposites) that determine the trajectory of their evolution. The phenomenology is irreducibly *individual*. This is what Sāṃkhya's plurality of *puruṣas* enforces; what Anirban's framework instantiates physically (each brain has its own PPM-architecture, even if the underlying combinatorial pattern is universal); and what the user's psychic-being-doctrine inherits from both.

### The Big Open Question: Falsifiability

The single most important open question for the user's engagement with Anirban's framework is *falsifiability*. Is the framework, in its current form, a genuine scientific theory in the Popperian sense — one that makes predictions which could in principle be falsified by experiment — or is it a re-description, a translation between Sanskrit-philosophical and contemporary-physics vocabularies, dressed in the language of science?

The honest answer: it is both, in different proportions across different claims.

The **triplet-of-triplet observation** is genuinely falsifiable. If independent labs run frequency-response measurements on tubulin, microtubules, and cortical EEG, and they consistently fail to find the nine-fold ripple pattern Anirban reports, the framework's empirical anchor is in serious trouble. So far, the pattern has been reported across multiple substrates and multiple instruments by Anirban's group; the question is whether independent groups can replicate it. Komal Saxena's PhD work and the Pushpendra Singh / Pathik Sahoo papers form an internally consistent body of evidence; external replication is what is needed next.

The **DDG clinical observation** (full triplet awake → doublet-of-triplet in coma → singlet at death) is potentially falsifiable but methodologically harder. End-of-life DDG measurements on consenting subjects are difficult to obtain and require rigorous controls. If the observation replicates, it is genuinely striking — a measurable signature of consciousness-collapse. If it does not, the interpretation collapses to a within-group artefact.

The **PPM construction** itself, as a mathematical object, is not the kind of thing that *can* be falsified; it is a definite combinatorial pattern. What is in question is its *applicability* — the claim that the PPM is the correct organizing structure for natural dynamics, that 15 primes generate 99.99% of natural compositions. This claim is *operationalisable* — one could run extensive simulations to check the 99.99% figure independently — but it is not strictly falsifiable in the Popperian sense; it is a claim about coverage, not about prediction.

The **three guṇa identifications** (sattva = projection from infinity, etc.) are largely *interpretations* rather than falsifiable predictions. Anirban could revise the identifications without changing the underlying physics. If a different mapping (sattva = bonding, tamas = projection, etc.) turned out to be more elegant, the framework would survive. The Sāṃkhyic vocabulary is, in this sense, a *clothing* over the formal structure rather than a constitutive part of it.

This means the user should engage the framework with a layered epistemic stance. The empirical observations (triplet-of-triplet, DDG collapse) are genuinely scientific; treat them as data and watch for replication. The mathematical apparatus (PPM, /Zhe, dodecanion algebra) is a research program; treat it as a candidate formalism that may need refinement. The Sāṃkhyic identifications are a translation; treat them as illuminating but revisable.

The user's physicist collaborator should be especially careful here. The collaborator's natural instinct will be to demand strict falsifiability before taking the framework seriously. The right response is: take the empirical observations as data (the triplet-of-triplet is a real measurement with real instruments); attempt independent formalization of the PPM (the procedure is given, even if axiomatisation is incomplete); reserve judgment on the guṇa identifications (they are interpretive overlay, valuable for the user's project but not load-bearing for the underlying physics). The framework is best engaged as a serious research program proposing a quantitative reconstruction of Sāṃkhya plus a novel computer-science thesis — not as a finished theory ready for textbook deployment, and not as crank metaphysics to be dismissed.

### What Gets Formalized Next

The user has explicitly indicated wanting to formalize the framework further. Three concrete formalization targets are visible from the existing material:

(i) **The precise mathematical structure of the PPM.** The C(n) plot is given by procedure; what is the closed-form definition? Is C(n) the partition function restricted to prime parts, or some variant? What are the analytic properties (asymptotics, growth rate, recursion)? The user should be able to write down a formal definition that any number theorist could engage with. Anirban's own definitions are operational rather than analytic; a closed-form characterization would be a meaningful contribution.

(ii) **The precise operator-correspondence for each guṇa.** Sattva = stereographic projection from infinity is well-defined in geometry; the operator can be written down explicitly. Rajas = C_n rotation at a singularity is well-defined; the operator can be written down. Tamas = bonding at a corner-singularity, formalized by the /Zhe operator, is the least well-defined; the user (or collaborator) should attempt a complete axiomatisation of /Zhe. Once these three operators are explicit, their compositions and commutation relations can be studied — and the *anyonya-vṛtti* (mutual operation) of *Kārikā* 12 can be checked against the actual algebra.

(iii) **The precise experimental predictions.** What does the framework predict that conventional neuroscience does not? The triplet-of-triplet collapse pattern is one such prediction; what others are there? Are there predictions about the response of microtubules to specific frequency perturbations? About the dynamics of consciousness under specific anaesthetic regimes? About the time-crystal phase transitions during the wake-coma transition? The user and the collaborator should make these predictions explicit, so that they can be tested against existing or new data.

A successful formalization along these three lines would be a substantial contribution — not just to the user's thesis, but to the wider scientific question of whether classical Sāṃkhya has the formal resources to ground a contemporary theory of consciousness.

### Closing Note

Sāṃkhya is a viable metaphysics-of-form register; the three guṇas are robust ontological-aesthetic primitives; the *Kārikā*'s austerity invites formalization rather than foreclosing it. Anirban's framework is the first serious modern attempt: its empirical anchors are real (triplet-of-triplet), its mathematical apparatus tractable though incomplete, its Sāṃkhyic identifications illuminating where they need refinement. Deleuze's virtual-actual, rhizome, and fold give the philosophical idiom without forcing either into the Hegelian-arborescent framework.

T1, T7, T9, T11 gain contemporary scientific specification. *Vyaktāvyakta-jña-vijñānāt* — "from discriminating knowledge of the manifest, the unmanifest, and the knower" (SK 2): classical Sāṃkhya gave the threefold object; Anirban specifies what *vyakta* is made of and how it relates to *avyakta* and *jña*; the user's thesis situates this within the one Real said in many senses.
