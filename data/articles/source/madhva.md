## Reader's Orientation

Madhvācārya, also called Ānandatīrtha and Pūrṇaprajña, was born near Udupi around 1238 CE and composed, before his disappearance into the Himalayas around 1317, the densest single corpus of polemical Vedānta in classical Sanskrit: thirty-seven works enumerated as the *Sarvamūla-granthāḥ*. The corpus includes the *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya*; the metrical auto-commentary *Anuvyākhyāna* (philosophically the most rigorous); the *Viṣṇu-Tattva-Vinirṇaya* (the most concentrated independent statement of *bheda*); the *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna*; the *Tattvodyota*; the *Khaṇḍana-Traya* (*Māyāvāda-*, *Upādhi-*, and *Mithyātva-Anumāna-Khaṇḍana*); ten *Upaniṣad-Bhāṣyas*; the *Gītā-Bhāṣya*; and the *Mahābhārata-Tātparya-Nirṇaya*.

Cross-engagement: against Adi Sankara: `dependent-real` rather than `sublatable-not-null` for the manifestation status axis ([primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1)).

Within Vedānta historiography Madhva is traditionally called the founder of *Tattva-vāda* (often labeled Dvaita Vedānta). That label is misleading. Madhva's own technical name is *Tattva-vāda* — the doctrine of *tattvas*, of realities. "Dualism" carries Cartesian, Manichean, and Zoroastrian connotations that are inappropriate: none of those models is asymmetric in the way Madhva's is. Madhva's structure is *one* independent reality (*svatantra*), Viṣṇu, and a vast plurality of dependent realities (*asvatantra*) which are real in their own right but exist as continuously sustained modes of dependence. The structure is *aseity-and-participation-in-aseity*, closer in shape to Aquinas's *esse per se / esse per participationem* than to Sāṃkhya or Cartesianism.

Read in this light, the *viśeṣa* doctrine is not ad hoc patching but a serious technical contribution to the substance-attribute problem; the *māyā-vāda khaṇḍana* is not sectarian grumbling but a formal argument that identifies a real load-bearing problem in Śaṅkara's system, never answered on its own terms — only repositioned, by Madhusūdana, by Vivaraṇa-school authors, and (the user's reading) by Aurobindo.

The trajectory the user calls T2 — the self-correcting movement of Vedānta toward realism — runs Bhartṛprapañca (early *bhedābheda*) → Bhāskara → Madhva (maximally rigorous realist articulation) → Caitanya (*acintya-bhedābheda*) → Aurobindo (the *lived register* in which both *bheda* and *abheda* are simultaneously real). On this reading Madhva is the moment at which the realist intuition gets fully formal logical articulation. What comes after is not a *return* to Śaṅkara but a *completion* of the move Madhva started: the supramental simultaneity in which Madhva's rigorous *bheda* is preserved and a non-difference register is added without dissolving the differences he correctly named.

A glossary of preserved Sanskrit terms, used throughout:

- *bheda* — difference; ontological non-identity, not mere logical or perspectival distinction.
- *abheda* — non-difference; the contrary of *bheda*.
- *bhedābheda* — difference-and-non-difference (Bhāskara, Bhartṛprapañca, Nimbārka, Caitanya in qualified form).
- *acintya-bhedābheda* — inconceivable simultaneity of difference and non-difference (Caitanya).
- *tattva* — reality, that-which-is. Not "principle" or "category" in the abstract sense.
- *svatantra* — self-dependent, independent. Said of Viṣṇu alone.
- *asvatantra* / *paratantra* — other-dependent. Said of all entities other than Viṣṇu.
- *bhāva* — existent.
- *abhāva* — non-existent.
- *cetana* — sentient, conscious.
- *acetana* — insentient, non-conscious. Often = *jaḍa*.
- *jaḍa* — insentient matter.
- *jīva* — embodied individual conscious self; an *asvatantra cetana*.
- *Īśvara* / *Bhagavān* / *Hari* / *Viṣṇu* / *Nārāyaṇa* — names of the *svatantra*; in Madhva, all of these denote one being with one *svarūpa*.
- *prakṛti* — primordial materiality; under Viṣṇu's *adhyakṣa* (superintendence).
- *kāla* — time; *asvatantra* but eternal-with-modifications.
- *viśeṣa* — Madhva's technical term for an internal self-distinguishing capacity that is not itself a relation; the substance's own way of being non-synonymously characterisable. NOT "particular" in the Vaiśeṣika sense.
- *sākṣin* / *sākṣi-pratyakṣa* — witness; perception by the witness; direct, non-mediated apprehension.
- *vṛtti* — mental modification, a content-bearing state of the *antaḥkaraṇa* (inner organ).
- *antaḥkaraṇa* — inner organ (mind, intellect, ego, *citta*).
- *bimba-pratibimba* — original-and-reflection; Madhva's metaphor for the *jīva* as a real distinct dependent reflection of Viṣṇu.
- *pañcabheda* — the five irreducible differences; the structural core of Madhva's positive ontology.
- *māyā-vāda* — Madhva's name for Advaita; specifically the doctrine that the world is a *vivarta* of Brahman via *avidyā*.
- *vivarta* — apparent modification, as in rope-appearing-as-snake.
- *pariṇāma* — real transformation, as in milk becoming curd.
- *avidyā* / *ajñāna* — ignorance; in Advaita the explanans of phenomenal experience; Madhva's primary target.
- *anirvacanīya* — indescribable, neither *sat* nor *asat*. Advaita's technical status for *avidyā*.
- *bhāvarūpa* — positively existent, of the nature of being. Said of *avidyā* by Vivaraṇa-school Advaitins and by Madhusūdana once the Madhva-style critique forces the issue.
- *adhyāsa* — superimposition; Advaita's primary explanans, e.g. of the world on Brahman or the body on the self.
- *bādha* — sublation, the canceling of one cognition by a corrective one (rope-cognition sublating snake-cognition).
- *mukhyārtha* — primary meaning of a word or sentence.
- *gauṇārtha* — secondary, derived, or implied meaning.
- *bhāga-tyāga-lakṣaṇā* — the implication-by-rejection-of-part-meanings, Advaita's hermeneutic device on identity-passages.
- *anugraha* / *prasāda* — grace.
- *adhikāra* / *adhikārin* — qualification, qualified person; the principle that statements address particular kinds of hearer.
- *līlā* — divine play.
- *svātantrya* — sovereign freedom (especially Trika Śaiva).
- *acintya-śakti* — inconceivable power (Caitanya).

The thread running through everything Madhva wrote is one structural move: against Śaṅkara's *māyā-vāda*, *bheda* is ontologically real — not an artefact of *avidyā*, not a perspectival appearance, not subject to sublation. The world's plurality is as real as Brahman, and the *jīva*'s distinctness from Brahman is as real as the *jīva*. The positive ontology is structured by the *pañcabheda* (five irreducible real differences); the technical instrument for managing distinctions inside a single substance is *viśeṣa*. Together — *pañcabheda* outside, *viśeṣa* inside — they give an ontology that is neither monism, pluralism, nor symmetric dualism.

This reader is committed to Madhva's literal metaphysics: read his arguments as the formal arguments they are, with the Sanskrit alongside the English, since the positions of words and compounds are sometimes the entire argument. The user's reading takes Madhva as a *register correction inside the realist trajectory*. Three things hold together: (a) Madhva's *bheda*-claims are literally true at the register at which the spiritual path is intelligible — *jīva* is not Viṣṇu, *prakṛti* is not Viṣṇu, suffering is not illusion; (b) the *māyā-vāda khaṇḍana* is valid as critique of any pure-Advaita position; (c) Madhva's positive prescription, strict ontic plurality, is one possible answer to the dilemma he diagnoses, not the only one. The user's thesis takes the same diagnostic force and sends it through a different prescription: *avidyā* is *bhāvarūpa*, but it is Brahman's *own* self-veiling — divine *svātantrya* in the mode of self-concealment. This preserves Madhva's *bheda* at its register *and* recovers a non-difference register above it.

Within the user's theses, Madhva primarily serves T2 at structural-argumentative load; provides T8 with its epistemological instrument (*sākṣi-caitanya*); provides T7 with its *prosecutorial* arguments — those that force the Advaitin to concede *avidyā*'s positive reality, even though Madhva draws the wrong conclusion from the concession; and stands as counter-witness to any sentimental reading of T4 that would collapse the differences between schools.

## Part I — *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna*: The Dual Division

The shortest and clearest entry into Madhva's ontology is the opening verse of the *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna*. In that opening statement, reality is divided into the self-dependent and the other-dependent, and Viṣṇu alone is said to be self-dependent.

Cross-engagement: Anandabodha takes the identity relation axis to a different commitment — `non-otherness` at [primary text](cite://sarvajnatman/samksepa-sariraka/1.319-352) against `image-original-similarity` here.

Reality (*tattva*) is held to be of two kinds: the self-dependent (*svatantra*) and the other-dependent (*asvatantra*). The self-dependent is Lord Viṣṇu. The other-dependent further divides into the existent (*bhāva*) and the non-existent (*abhāva*).

Notice the structural asymmetry. There is *one* *svatantra* — Viṣṇu — and the entire rest of reality is *asvatantra*. This is not a symmetric two-substance ontology. It is an aseity ontology. *Svatantra* in Madhva's technical use means: independent in *svarūpa* (essential nature), in *jñāna* (cognitive function), and in *kṛtitva* (volitional efficacy). All three conditions must be jointly satisfied. No *jīva* satisfies any of them in the strict sense: every *jīva*'s *svarūpa* is what Viṣṇu has determined it to be (the *svabhāva-bheda* doctrine, below); every *jīva*'s cognition is conditioned by its *guṇa*-constitution and by Viṣṇu's *anugraha*; every *jīva*'s efficacy presupposes Viṣṇu's inner direction as *antaryāmin*. Lakṣmī, who is *nitya-mukta* and never enters *saṃsāra*, is closer to *svatantra* than any other being but still not *svatantra* in the strict sense — her aseity is participated, not original.

The *asvatantra* category subdivides as a tree. Following the *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna* and its companion *Tattva-Viveka*:

- *Bhāva* (existents) divides into *cetana* (sentient) and *acetana* (insentient).
- *Cetana* divides into *nitya* — the eternally untouched-by-suffering, of which Lakṣmī (also called Rāmā, Śrī) is the unique member — and *anitya*, all other *jīvas*, who undergo *saṃsāra*.
- *Acetana* divides into *nitya* (the Vedas as eternal sonic substance), *nitya-anitya* (the Purāṇas, *kāla* (time), and *prakṛti* (primordial matter)), and *anitya* (the 24 evolutes of *prakṛti* plus *brahmāṇḍa*, the cosmic egg).
- *Abhāva* (non-existents) divides into *prāgabhāva* (prior absence: the pot's not-yet-being before it is made), *pradhvaṃsābhāva* (post-destruction absence: the pot's no-longer-being after it is broken), *atyantābhāva* / *sadābhāva* (eternal non-being: the hare's horn, the son of the barren woman, the square circle), and (Madhva adds) *anyonyābhāva* (mutual absence: the cow is not the horse).

Several features of this taxonomy are worth flagging. First, *abhāva* is a real ontological category. Madhva is not the only realist to do this, the Naiyāyikas hold the same, but it is a strong commitment, and it has consequences. The non-being of the pot before it is made is not nothing; it is a specifiable absence with specifiable structure. Second, the category of *nitya-anitya* is sophisticated: *prakṛti* is eternal as substance and constantly modified in form. Time is eternal in its flow and momentary in its segments. This is closer to a process metaphysics than to substance metaphysics in the strict sense and — as the user's brief notes (Whitehead, Prigogine) — gives Madhva an unexpected resonance with twentieth-century process thought. Third, the *cetana*/*acetana* distinction is hard. There is no scale on which *acetana* arrangement converts to *cetana* by complexity. *Jīvas* are *cetana* by *svabhāva* and *jaḍa* is *acetana* by *svabhāva* and the difference is not bridgeable from below. This is structurally an anti-emergentist commitment, which the user's brief flags.

The closing of the *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna* gives the soteriological pay-off: the person who knows that all dependent reality always remains under Hari's governance is liberated from saṃsāra.

One who knows that this entire dependent reality is always subject to Hari — that person is liberated from the cycle of rebirths.

Liberation is *not* realization that one *is* Brahman. It is the recognition that everything other than Viṣṇu, including oneself, exists in continuous dependence on Viṣṇu. The recognition itself is dependent on Viṣṇu's grace. This is consistent realism all the way down: even the liberating cognition is not self-generated; it is given.

**Aseity vs participation-in-aseity.** *Asvatantra* entities are not a *second* reality alongside Viṣṇu; their reality is the reality of being dependent modes of Viṣṇu's manifestation. A pot is real, but its reality includes its having-been-made-by-the-potter as constitutive. Madhva's *jīvas* are real; their reality includes being-sustained-by-Viṣṇu as constitutive. The dependent is real precisely *as* dependent. The contrast with Viśiṣṭādvaita is sharp: for Rāmānuja, *jīvas* and *prakṛti* are *prakāras* of the one *prakārin* Nārāyaṇa, related by *aprthak-siddhi*. Madhva refuses this. *Jīvas* are not Brahman's body or modes; they are *real-distinct entities* that *depend* on Brahman. The "real-distinct" half blocks Viśiṣṭādvaita; the "depend on Brahman" half blocks symmetric dualism. Dependence is not identity, even of body-and-bearer.

The *Anuvyākhyāna* gives a dense parallel statement in causal-creational terms at AV 1.1.13: substance, action, time, intrinsic nature, and the *jīva* exist only through divine favor and fail to stand when that favor is withdrawn.

Substance, action, time, intrinsic-nature, and *jīva*: by-whose-favor they *are*; by-whose-disregard they *are not*. The *yad-* is left dangling: the verse never names the depender; the only being whose attention could constitute the being of these five is the *svatantra*. *Santi / na santi* are paired present indicatives — the implication is *moment-by-moment* dependence, continuous *creatio-conservatio*, not creation-and-then-autonomy. (Sharma's "exist by his grace, cease by his neglect" loses both the syntactic suspension and the temporal continuity.)

The pedagogical order of the *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna* matters: Madhva opens with *svatantra* / *asvatantra* and only then unfolds sub-divisions. He does not begin with *bheda* and ask what is differentiated. He begins with the *grade of reality* (independent vs dependent) and afterwards says *what* the dependents are. This is structurally an aseity ontology (Aquinas's *ens a se* / *ens ab alio*), not a Cartesian substance ontology. *Pañcabheda* falls out of the asymmetric framework rather than being stipulated independently. T2 needs both the realism *pañcabheda* secures and the asymmetry *svatantra/asvatantra* secures; a symmetric two-substance ontology would re-pluralise the ground. Caitanya and Aurobindo do not abandon this structure but add the supramental claim that the dependent is *also* non-different from the depender in a register the *jīva*'s logic cannot fully grasp.

## Part II — *Pañcabheda* via AV 1.4.110–112

The most cited single verse in Madhva's corpus is the *pañcabheda*-enumerating verse from the *Anuvyākhyāna*. It comes at the close of *adhyāya* 1, *pāda* 4, at the precise textual location where Madhva, having defeated *vivarta-vāda* in 1.4.109–110, gives the positive structure that replaces it. The placement matters. The *pañcabheda* is not announced as standalone doctrine; it is what falls out of the formal refutation of *vivarta*. AV 1.4.110 says that the world really arises and is structured as the fivefold difference.

Cross-engagement: on the finite cognition axis Anantakrishna Sastri reads `positive-ignorance` ([primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14)); the present section reads `real-dependent-veiling`.

[The root *vid*], in the sense of arising, already yields real origination; and this spread-out manifest world, though in general subject to eventual subsiding, is precisely the fivefold differentiation.

The literal force is: *prapañca* = *bheda-pañcaka*. The phenomenal world just is the fivefold difference. Plurality is not added to a prior unity; the world's *being-spread-out* is identical with its being structured by these five distinctions. Then AV 1.4.111 enumerates the five: difference between *jīva* and Īśvara, matter and Īśvara, one *jīva* and another, matter and *jīva*, and one material thing and another.

The sundering of *jīva* from Īśvara, likewise the sundering of the insentient from Īśvara; the mutual differentiation of *jīvas*; the sundering of the insentient from *jīva*; and the mutual differentiation of insentient things: that is the *prapañca*, the fivefold difference.

A philological note. The standard rendering "fivefold difference" is technically correct but understates the verb. Madhva uses forms of *bhid*, "to cleave" or "to split," in three of the five terms. The choice underlines that what is at stake is cleavage in being, not a merely logical distinction. The world, in its very spread-outness, is the sundering of these five. AV 1.4.112 confirms the modal force by insisting that this difference is real and beginningless, and that the denial of duality belongs to the ignorant.

This fivefold difference is real (*satyaḥ*) and beginningless (*anādiḥ*); if it had a beginning, it would also come to an end. "Difference does not exist" — that, accordingly, is the doctrine of the unknowing.

Each of the five is therefore a *nitya-bheda* — eternal, beginningless, real, non-perspectival, non-sublatable. None is produced by *avidyā*. None is dissolved at *mukti*. Each requires its own argument and its own treatment of anticipated objections. We take them in order.

### *Bheda* 1 — *Jīva–Īśvara-bheda*: Soul and God

**Claim.** Every *jīva*, in every state — bound, liberated, *deva*, *manuṣya*, *asura* — is eternally distinct from Viṣṇu. The distinctness is *svarūpa*-distinctness, not merely *upādhi*-distinctness.

Cross-engagement: a real divergence with Appayya Dikshita on the epistemic authority axis. The epistemological register is shared; the values are not — `plural-pramana-realism` here, `scripture-dominant` at [primary text](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/3.4).

**Argument.** The argument has three converging legs.

The first leg is a reductio. Suppose *jīva* and Brahman are numerically identical. Then either the *jīva*'s bondage is Brahman's bondage, or the *jīva*'s ignorance is Brahman's ignorance. Brahman is by all schools' admission *sarvajña* (omniscient) and *ananta-cit* (unlimited consciousness). Bondage and ignorance are inconsistent with these. The disjunction has no surviving disjunct. Therefore *jīva* and Brahman are not numerically identical.

The Advaitin's standard escape is: it is only the *jīva-as-bound* that suffers, and the *jīva-as-bound* is itself an artefact of *avidyā*; the *jīva-as-Brahman* (the *svarūpa* underneath the *upādhi*) does not suffer. Madhva's counter is that this move generates an explanatory regress. To say "the suffering belongs to the *upādhi*-conditioned *jīva*, not to Brahman as such" requires that the *upādhi*-conditioning be locatable. The *upādhi* must sit somewhere. The locus dilemma (Part V below) reappears here. The escape from the reductio is not free.

The second leg is *sākṣi-pratyakṣa*. Direct experience, perception by the witness-consciousness, testifies to limitation, finitude, suffering, the temporality of one's own *vṛttis*. These states are jointly incompatible with Brahman's nature. *Sākṣi-pratyakṣa* cannot be globally falsified, because to falsify it one would need another act of direct experience of the same epistemic kind, and the regress does not terminate. The testimony stands: the *jīva* is *not* Brahman in *svarūpa*. (The full argument for the *sākṣin*'s authority is in Part IV.)

The third leg is hermeneutical. The Advaitin appeals to the great identity-passages of *śruti* — *tat tvam asi* (Chāndogya 6.8.7), *ahaṃ brahmāsmi* (Bṛhadāraṇyaka 1.4.10), *ayam ātmā brahma* (Māṇḍūkya 2), *prajñānaṃ brahma* (Aitareya 3.1.3) — to read identity into the relation. To read these as *strict numerical identity* between *jīva* and Brahman, the Advaitin must invoke *bhāga-tyāga-lakṣaṇā*, the implication-by-rejection-of-part-meanings: from "you are that" we reject the parts of "you" (limited, suffering, embodied) and the parts of "that" (creator, sustainer of worlds) and keep only the residual *cit*. Madhva's hermeneutical principle is sharp: do not move from *mukhyārtha* (primary meaning) to *gauṇārtha* (secondary meaning) without warrant. The Advaita move is not warranted; *tat tvam asi* is perfectly intelligible at primary meaning if "you are that" is read as "you exist in continuous *aikya*-relation of being-grounded-in-and-belonging-to that," which is the natural reading once non-duality is not assumed in advance. The Advaitic insistence on *gauṇārtha* is question-begging — it presupposes the very non-duality the passage is supposed to establish. Madhva extends this hermeneutic principle systematically: throughout the *śruti* the contexts that frame the identity-passages distinguish *jīva* from Īśvara, the *bhāṣyakāra*'s task is to read the identity-passages in the tenor of those contexts, and the contexts force a non-strict-identity reading.

**Anticipated objection.** This is methodological resistance, not refutation. Madhva's reply: the burden of proof is on whoever moves from primary to secondary meaning. The Advaitin's demonstration — "primary meaning fails because *bheda* is incompatible with non-duality" — is circular: non-duality is the conclusion under dispute.

A second objection: if *jīva-Īśvara-bheda* is eternal, *mokṣa* is mere change of state. Madhva's reply: *mokṣa* is not loss of *svarūpa*-distinctness but achievement of *svarūpānanda* — the bliss native to the *jīva*'s own *svarūpa*, masked by *saṃsāra*. The *jīva* in *mukti* enjoys Viṣṇu's *sānnidhya* and participation in his *guṇas* (the four grades: *sālokya*, *sāmīpya*, *sārūpya*, *sāyujya*). What is not available is *svarūpaikya* — and Madhva regards this as a feature: it is what keeps *bhakti* real in *mukti*.

### *Bheda* 2 — *Jaḍa–Īśvara-bheda*: Matter and God

**Claim.** *Prakṛti*, *kāla*, the 24 evolutes, and the *brahmāṇḍa* are real, are eternally (or eternally-with-modification) distinct from Brahman, and are not phenomenal projections of *avidyā* on Brahman.

Cross-engagement: Bhatta Kallata works the determination axis in the logical-dialectical register too, but the value is `self-expression`, not `non-sublatable-difference` ([primary text](cite://bhatta-kallata/spanda-karika/1.1)).

**Argument.** Three legs.

First, *prakṛti* is *asvatantra* — fully dependent on Viṣṇu — but dependence is not identity. *Prakṛti* under Viṣṇu's *adhyakṣa* (superintendence: BG 9.10) is the material cause from which Viṣṇu, the efficient cause, produces the world. The two causes are *bheda*: *nimitta-upādāna-bheda*. This is a sharp formal commitment that distinguishes Madhva's *pariṇāma-vāda* from Śaṅkara's *vivarta-vāda*. For Śaṅkara, Brahman is both efficient and material cause and the world is *vivarta*. For Madhva, *prakṛti* is the material cause, Viṣṇu is the efficient cause, and the world is *pariṇāma* (real transformation of *prakṛti*). The clay-pot analogy from Chāndogya 6.1.4 is read by Madhva as straightforward *pariṇāma*: clay really becomes pot; pot is really clay-modified; clay is not pot in the sense of identity, but pot is not separable from clay either. Madhva will use this analogy decisively against Śaṅkara at [BSB 2.1.14](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14) (Part VI below).

Second leg, the pragmatic-transcendental argument. If the world were *mithyā* in the Advaita sense — sublatable upon *jñāna* — then Vedic injunctions prescribing real action for real results would be purposeless. The *kāryatā* (efficacy, prescriptiveness) of the Veda presupposes the reality of the world acted upon. Either the Veda is real (and so is its object, the world acted upon) or the Veda itself is *mithyā* (in which case no injunction binds, no *dharma* obligates, and the entire apparatus of *karma-kāṇḍa* is vacuous). The Advaitin's standard reply is the two-level scheme: the Veda is real *vyāvahārika* and *mithyā* *pāramārthika*. Madhva's counter (developed fully in Part V) is that the two-level scheme is itself incoherent at its joint. We cannot retreat from the *vyāvahārika* level to the *pāramārthika* level except by a cognitive act that is itself either real or *mithyā*; the regress does not terminate.

Third leg, scriptural. Several *śruti*-passages are decisive once one reads them at *mukhyārtha*. Taittirīya 2.1: *annaṃ brahmeti vyajānāt* — "he understood food to be Brahman" — does not mean food *is* Brahman in identity (this would make digestion a transformation of Brahman); it means food is Brahman's manifestation, Brahman's product, Brahman's creature. Bhagavad-Gītā 9.10: *mayādhyakṣeṇa prakṛtiḥ sūyate sa-carācaram* — "under my superintendence *prakṛti* gives birth to the moving and unmoving" — explicitly distinguishes the superintending Lord from the *prakṛti* that gives birth. The *adhyakṣa* is *not* what is superintended; the relation is *bheda*. This is the canonical Vaiṣṇava locus and it is not amenable to *gauṇārtha* without strain.

**Anticipated objection.** Is real eternal *prakṛti* not Sāṃkhya-style dualism? Reply: Madhva's *prakṛti* is *asvatantra* — its eternity is that of a continuously-sustained entity, not an independent ultimate. Sāṃkhya's *prakṛti* is *svatantra*; Madhva's is dependent. The categorial difference is in the *svatantra/asvatantra* divide.

### *Bheda* 3 — *Jīva–Jīva-bheda*: Soul and Soul

**Claim.** *Jīvas* are not interchangeable units of one consciousness covered by varying *upādhi*. Each *jīva* has its own *svarūpa* and its own *svabhāva*, and these differ across *jīvas*. *Jīvas* differ in intrinsic quality, not only in adventitious limiting condition.

Cross-engagement: the disagreement with Ananda K. Coomaraswamy is at the method axis itself: `formal-proof-or-inference` here, `comparative-reading` in [primary text](cite://coomaraswamy/the-transformation-of-nature-in-art/1).

**Argument.** This is among Madhva's more radical claims and requires its own apparatus. The Advaitic structure is: there is one Brahman, one *cit*; the apparent multiplicity of *jīvas* is the result of *upādhi*-individuation; the *upādhis* differ but the *cit* underneath is identical across *jīvas*. Madhva rejects every step. There is no underlying common *cit* across *jīvas*. Each *jīva* has its own *cit* as its own *svarūpa*. The differences across *jīvas* are not merely in *upādhi* (body, mind, *karma*-history) but in the *jīva*'s own *svabhāva* (intrinsic disposition).

The basis is multifold. First, scripture distinguishes kinds of beings — *deva*, *manuṣya*, *asura*, *daitya* — and this is read by Madhva as not merely a *karmic* ranking (this *jīva* has accumulated this *karma*) but a *svabhāvic* one (this *jīva* is *constituted* with these dispositions). Second, felt experiential reality: I know my own *jīva* and the next person's is not interchangeable with mine. *Sākṣi-pratyakṣa* certifies this. Third, Viṣṇu's free sovereign creation creates intrinsically varied entities; mass-produced identical *cit* covered by varying *māyā* would be a *workmanlike* creation, not a sovereign one.

Madhva goes further. He divides *jīvas* into three classes: *mukti-yogya* (constitutionally fit for liberation), *nitya-saṃsārin* (eternally caught in *saṃsāra*, never liberated), and *tamo-yogya* (constitutionally fit for *tamas* — eternal damnation, going to *andha-tamas* at the end of the cosmic cycle). This is the controversial *jīva-traividhya* (threefold *jīva*) doctrine, sometimes called the predestination thesis. It is the most commonly-attacked feature of Tattva-vāda from inside Vaiṣṇavism. Caitanya, accepting Madhva's *svarūpa*-distinctness of *jīvas*, will reject the strong *tamo-yogya* version: the Caitanya tradition holds that any *jīva* can in principle be drawn to Kṛṣṇa by grace, and the eternally-damned class is not metaphysically necessary.

**Anticipated objection.** Does *jīva-traividhya* not make grace arbitrary? Reply: grace is given in accordance with the *jīva*'s *svarūpa*. The trajectory T2 does not require this doctrine; Caitanya preserves Madhva's *svarūpa-bheda* without the strong *tamo-yogya*, which suffices for T2.

### *Bheda* 4 — *Jaḍa–Jīva-bheda*: Matter and Soul

**Claim.** *Jaḍa* and *jīva* are real, both *asvatantra*, but irreducibly different in kind. *Cit* is not a property that emerges from arrangements of *acetana*. Consciousness is ontologically primitive; matter is not.

Cross-engagement: Akalanka ([primary text](cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/1.34-35)) treats the truth-structure axis in the epistemological register with the value `standpoint-conditioned-realism`. The present section reads it as `single-absolute-truth`.

**Argument.** This is the structural anti-emergentism of Tattva-vāda, and it has surprisingly direct contemporary resonance. The argument: whatever is *acetana* in itself cannot, by composition, become *cetana*. The properties of a complex system depend on the properties of its parts and the structure of their composition. If the parts are *acetana* and the structure is itself *acetana* (a structural property is not a *cit*-property), then no degree of complexity yields *cetana*. *Cetana* must already be present at some level for it to appear at any level.

The contemporary resonance: this is the structural form of the "hard problem" argument as advanced by Chalmers, and the panpsychist response (Strawson, Goff) is structurally Madhvic — there must be *cetana* somewhere fundamental in the ontology. Madhva's version places the *cetana* at the level of individual *jīvas*, not at the level of fundamental physical particles, but the formal point is the same: *cetana* cannot be derived from *acetana*; it must be in the basement.

Within Madhva's system, *jaḍa* is what *prakṛti* and its evolutes are. *Jīvas* are the conscious individuals who are placed in *jaḍa* bodies for the duration of *saṃsāra*. The relation of *jīva* to body is intimate but not identity: the *jīva* uses the body as instrument; the body's modifications affect the *jīva* via the *antaḥkaraṇa*; the *jīva*'s actions modify the body. But the *jīva* is not the body, and the body is not conscious. The *bheda* is *real*.

**Anticipated objection.** The Advaitin argues that the *jīva*-body distinction is itself an *adhyāsa*: the *jīva* superimposes the body on itself and the body on the *jīva*. Madhva's reply: *adhyāsa* presupposes a real difference between superimposer and substrate. If *jīva* and body were really one, there would be no way to mistake the one for the other — there would be no two terms between which *adhyāsa* could obtain. The Advaitic appeal to *adhyāsa* presupposes the *bheda* it is supposed to explain away. (This is one of the most pointed structural arguments Madhva makes, and Jayatīrtha sharpens it considerably; see Part VIII.)

### *Bheda* 5 — *Jaḍa–Jaḍa-bheda*: Matter and Matter

**Claim.** Real numerical and qualitative differences obtain among material things. A pot is not a cloth. Gold is not silver. Earth is not water. The differences are real and not artefacts of cognition.

Cross-engagement: Edmund Husserl lands on `standpoint-conditioned-realism` for the perspectivism axis ([primary text](cite://husserl/ideas-i/31-32)); this section lands on `partial-perspectives-ranked`.

**Argument.** This is Madhva's commitment to the reality of differentiated phenomenal multiplicity — the most direct refutation of any "appearance-only" reading. Plurality among things is real; the world is not undifferentiated stuff appearing as differentiated under the action of *māyā*. The argument has two legs: a perceptual-evidentiary leg (we perceive distinct things; in the absence of countervailing evidence, perception is veridical; nothing in Advaita's framework provides countervailing evidence that does not itself presuppose a differentiated world) and a pragmatic leg (causation and use require differentiation: a pot holds water that a sieve cannot; gold buys what brass cannot; differentiation is causally efficacious, and what is causally efficacious cannot be appearance-only).

**Anticipated objection.** The Advaitin replies that perception itself is at the *vyāvahārika* level and so its testimony to differentiation is *vyāvahārika*-true and *pāramārthika*-false. Madhva's reply: this is the two-level scheme again, and the Advaitin owes us an account of the relation between the levels. Once that account is demanded, the Madhvic objections to the locus of *avidyā* (Part V) re-engage. *Bheda* 5 is, on its own, an argument from data; combined with the *māyā-vāda khaṇḍana*, it is an argument with no Advaitic counter-move that does not itself collapse.

### Synoptic notes

The five differences constitute the *minimum* set of distinctions required for the intelligibility of the spiritual path. Without *jīva-Īśvara-bheda*: no *bhakta*-Lord relation, no grace. Without *jaḍa-Īśvara-bheda*: no real creation. Without *jīva-jīva-bheda*: no real *adhikāra*. Without *jaḍa-jīva-bheda*: no real embodied agency. Without *jaḍa-jaḍa-bheda*: no real causation. Each *bheda* is load-bearing for practice; to collapse them is to vacate the apparatus.

Cross-engagement: the disagreement with Akshapada Gautama is at the practice-path axis itself: `devotion-and-grace` here, `knowledge-discipline` in [primary text](cite://aksapada/nyaya-sutra/1.1.1).

The five differ in evidential basis: *jīva-Īśvara-bheda* and *jaḍa-Īśvara-bheda* by *anumāna* and *śabda*; *jīva-jīva-bheda* by *sākṣi-pratyakṣa* and *śabda*; *jaḍa-jīva-bheda* by *anumāna* and *pratyakṣa*; *jaḍa-jaḍa-bheda* by *pratyakṣa*. The full *pramāṇa* apparatus is deployed.

Crucial for the user's reading: the five are right *at the level Madhva articulates* — formal conditions for the intelligibility of practice — but not unconditionally the only true description. The supramental correction does not deny them; it places them inside a frame in which *bheda* and *abheda* are simultaneously real. Madhva blocks this move; the trajectory takes it from outside.

## Part III — *Viśeṣa*: Real Distinction Without Pluralisation

Without *viśeṣa*, Tattva-vāda is incoherent. With it, Madhva has a technical instrument unique in classical Indian philosophy and the most direct precursor to Caitanya's *acintya-śakti* and Aurobindo's Real-Idea.

Cross-engagement: the disagreement with Alfred North Whitehead is at the soteric end axis itself: `service-with-distinction-preserved` here, `not-soteriological` in [primary text](cite://whitehead/process-and-reality/1.2).

### The problem *viśeṣa* solves

Viṣṇu possesses infinite *guṇas* — *sarvajñatva*, *sarvaśaktitva*, *ānanda*, *saundarya*, *kāruṇya*, *prabhutva*, and so on. Are these different from Viṣṇu, or identical with him?

Cross-engagement: same metaphysical register, different commitments at the causation model axis. Baladeva Vidyabhushana: `unchanged-ground-with-changing-power`, [primary text](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1). Here: `efficient-material-split`.

If different, Viṣṇu is composite, substance plus properties, and Brahman is *akhaṇḍa* by all schools' admission; composition is fatal to *svatantra* status. If strictly identical, "omniscient" and "omnipotent" are synonymous, "Viṣṇu is omniscient *and* omnipotent" is vacuous, and theological description goes inert.

This is the classical substance-attribute problem. Advaita's response is *nirviśeṣa* Brahman — but this generates its own problems (Part V): *nirviśeṣa* Brahman cannot be the object of inquiry, cannot ground sublating cognition, cannot be the locus of *avidyā*. Rāmānuja's response — *guṇas* and *jīvas* as *prakāras* of one *prakārin* under *aprthak-siddhi* — makes *jīvas* parts of Brahman's body, compromising *jīva-Brahman bheda*. Madhva's *viśeṣa* differs from both: it posits an intrinsic *non-relational* form of internal distinction.

### The doctrine

*Viśeṣa* is, in Madhva's technical formulation, "a substance's inherent capacity to display distinction between its attributes despite their inseparability from the substance and from each other." It is *not a relation*. It is *not a third term* between substance and attribute. It is an *intrinsic dharma* of the substance itself — the substance's *own way* of being non-synonymously characterisable.

Three positive characterizations are essential:

(i) *Viśeṣa* is *svayam-prakāśa* — self-luminous, self-disclosing. It does not require a further principle to disclose it; it is its own disclosure. Predicate-distinctness is not made evident by some additional act of inference or reflection on top of the substance; it is built into how the substance is.

(ii) *Viśeṣa* is *svanirvṛtta* — self-completing, self-sufficient. It does not require an external relation to ground its operation. There is no *sambandha* between the *viśeṣa* and the substance that needs further specification; the *viśeṣa* is *of* the substance intrinsically.

(iii) *Viśeṣa* "acts as the representative of *bheda* where *bheda* is absent." This is the most useful single formulation. It generates the *cognitive and logical effect* of difference (non-synonymy of terms, non-interchangeability of predications, non-vacuity of conjunctive descriptions) without requiring an actual gap between substance and property. It does the work *bheda* would do, in a register where *bheda* itself would be destructive.

This is not an evasion. The work *viśeṣa* is doing is real. Without it, theological discourse is either polytheistic (multiple deities, each with one attribute) or vacuous (one deity with one self-identical "nature" that no language picks out). With it, theological discourse can predicate "Viṣṇu is omniscient and omnipotent and merciful" non-redundantly while preserving Viṣṇu's *akhaṇḍa* status.

### The regress objection

The Advaitin's counter: *viśeṣa* is either identical with the substance (adds nothing) or different from it (a further *viśeṣa* is needed to ground the relation, ad infinitum). This is the third-man regress.

Madhva's reply: the regress threat applies only if *viśeṣa* is treated as a third-term relatum. *Viśeṣa* is not a relatum and not a relation; it is an intrinsic *power* of the substance — its own self-differentiating capacity. Asking "what relates *viśeṣa* to the substance?" is the category mistake of asking "what relates redness to the apple?" when redness is already the apple's intrinsic property. The regress objection presupposes that any distinction inside a single entity must be between two terms; *viśeṣa* refuses that presupposition. The substance is intrinsically capable of being terminus of multiple non-synonymous predicates without thereby being composite — a phenomenon Advaita itself cannot evade (Brahman is *sat-cit-ānanda*; what does Advaita mean by these three's distinction-yet-non-difference?). Every substance-attribute account requires a non-relational stop somewhere; *viśeṣa* is Madhva's name for that stop.

### Why this matters for the user's trajectory

**Caitanya's *acintya-śakti*** does the same logical work as *viśeṣa* at a higher register. Madhva applies *viśeṣa* only *inside* Viṣṇu; Caitanya extends an analogous structure to the *jīva-Brahman* relation, marking the simultaneity *acintya*. Madhva blocks this extension for theological reasons; Caitanya unblocks it.

**Aurobindo's Real-Idea.** At the supramental level, *viśeṣa* is not a special doctrine for one being but the ordinary structure of all real being: the unity *is* the multiplicity by *posing* the multiplicity as its own internal articulation. The Real-Idea is what *viśeṣa* looks like extended from within-Viṣṇu to all Saccidānanda manifestation.

A note on terminology. Vaiśeṣika's *viśeṣa* is a distinguisher *between* entities (atoms); Madhva's is a distinguisher *within* an entity. Translating Madhva's term as "particular" is a category mistake. Better: "self-distinguishing capacity," "intrinsic distinction-power." Madhva's solution is a form of primitivism: there is no further analysis of how the substance is internally distinguishable; that capacity is a basic fact about the substance.

## Part IV — *Sākṣi-Pratyakṣa*: The Witness as Authoritative *Pramāṇa*

Madhva accepts three *pramāṇas* and only three: *pratyakṣa* (perception), *anumāna* (inference), *śabda* (Āgama / reliable testimony). He rejects *arthāpatti* (postulation) and *upamāna* (comparison) as *independent* pramāṇas, treating them as sub-forms of inference. The interesting epistemological move, however, is not in the *pramāṇa* trio but in what Madhva places *underneath* it: the *sākṣin*, the witness-consciousness, which is the ultimate guarantor of the validity of any *pramāṇa*-cognition.

### The argument

Every *vṛtti-jñāna* — knowledge in the form of a mental modification — is *acetana* (non-conscious) in its character as a modification. A *citta-vṛtti* is a material event in the *antaḥkaraṇa* (inner organ); it is a configuration of subtle matter shaped by the contact of sense and object (perception), or by the recollection of *vyāpti* (inference), or by the reception of testimony (*śabda*). The *vṛtti* itself is not conscious; it is *content-bearing matter*. It cannot reveal itself.

If the *vṛtti* could reveal itself, infinite regress: every act of self-revelation needs another self-revealer, and the regress does not terminate. If the *vṛtti* is revealed by *another vṛtti*, the regress does not terminate either, since the second *vṛtti* faces the same problem. The series can only terminate at a non-*vṛtti* term. That term is the *sākṣin*: the *cit* (consciousness) of the *jīva*, which apprehends the *vṛtti* directly without itself being another *vṛtti*.

The crucial verse is in the *Anuvyākhyāna* at 2.2.68, in the chapter on the *aṇu*-magnitude of the *jīva*, where the witness is invoked precisely to terminate a regress about the unboundedness of cosmological magnitudes. Madhva says there that time and space are known as unbounded only by the witness, and that the same witness directly sees the non-terminating character of both the large and the minute.

By the witness alone, as instrument of knowing (*sākṣi-māna*), time and space are [known as] unlimited; and by that same witness, the *non-terminating* character of both [the macroscopic and the *aṇu*-microscopic] is seen.

The double force of this verse is what justifies its load-bearing role. Read closely. The first line says: time and space are known-as-without-limit (*nāntavān*) by *sākṣi-māna* alone — not "by perception together with the witness" but *kevalaṃ sākṣi-mānena*, "by the witness as instrument *exclusively*." The second line generalizes to the small as well as the large: the *non-coming-to-rest* (*aparyavasiti*) of the regress in either direction — outward to spatial unboundedness, inward to the partlessness of the *aṇu* — is *seen* (*dṛśyate*) by the *sākṣin*. The verb *dṛś* is decisive: this is *seeing*, not inferring. The *sākṣin* is a *pramāṇa*, not a meta-cognition supervening on inferences.

The point is not that time and space are *infinite*; it is that the *unboundedness* of time and space — the fact that no perceived moment is the last and no perceived location is the edge — is *not* known by *pratyakṣa* (which is bound to the present moment and the perceived locus), nor by *anumāna* (which would need a *vyāpti* not available here, since one cannot complete an inference whose conclusion is precisely *non-termination*), nor by *śabda* alone. It is known by the witness directly: the *sākṣin* apprehends the unbounded character of the temporal and spatial fields within which *vṛttis* arise.

The *sākṣin* directly apprehends, on Madhva's account:

- *Kāla* (time) and *deśa* (space) in their unbounded reality.
- The *jīva*'s own essential characteristics — what kind of being one is, in *svarūpa*.
- *Sukha* (pleasure) and *duḥkha* (pain) and their immediate phenomenal character.
- The *manas* (mind) and its modifications, as one's own.
- Certain supersensuous categories required by the spiritual path: bondage as bondage, the *jīva*'s dependence on Viṣṇu, the difference between bound and free states.

### Sharma's three functions

B.N.K. Sharma (*Philosophy of Sri Madhvacharya*, Ch. XVIII–XIX) lays out three functions performed by the *sākṣin* in Madhva's system, and the analysis is worth following carefully.

**Function 1: ultimate guarantor of the validity of all *pramāṇas*.** When perception delivers a *vṛtti* with content "this is a pot," that *vṛtti* is not self-validating. The validity of the *pramāṇa*-act is established by the *sākṣin*'s direct apprehension of the *vṛtti* as veridically corresponding to its object. Without the *sākṣin*, the *vṛtti* is just a configuration of *antaḥkaraṇa*-matter; with the *sākṣin*, it is a knowing. This is Madhva's answer to the foundational question of epistemology: what makes a *pramāṇa* a *pramāṇa*? Not the *pramāṇa* itself; the witness that cognises it.

**Function 2: logical fulfilment of any complete theory of knowledge.** Every reductionist theory that tries to reduce knowledge to *vṛttis* alone, without a witnessing term, must either smuggle in a non-*vṛtti* observer (in which case the theory has not actually reduced) or collapse into an absurd chain in which no knowledge is knowledge of anything (each *vṛtti* needs another, ad infinitum). Madhva's argument is that the *sākṣin* is not an *optional* posit; it is a *forced* posit. Any theory of knowledge that does not include it is incomplete.

**Function 3: only means of intuitive perception of supersensuous categories** — including, critically, the *jīva*'s own bondage. Bondage is not an Advaitic illusion. Bondage is a *real condition* directly known to the *sākṣin*. This third function is doing the heaviest anti-Advaita work. Once the *sākṣin* has authoritative direct access to bondage *as* bondage, the Advaitic move of declaring bondage *adhyāsa* (superimposition) cannot succeed.

### The anti-Advaita force

If the *sākṣin* directly and infallibly witnesses the *jīva*'s finitude, limitation, and suffering, then the Advaitic claim that these are *adhyāsa* — superimposed by *avidyā* — cannot be sustained without destroying the very epistemological foundation on which *jñāna* (the liberating cognition) itself rests. The Advaitin cannot appeal to higher knowledge (*pāramārthika-jñāna*) to cancel lower experience (*vyāvahārika*) without presupposing a *sākṣin* that witnesses both levels — and if the *sākṣin* witnesses both, it cannot be located exclusively at the *pāramārthika* level. The *sākṣin* sees bondage. The *sākṣin* is veridical. Therefore bondage is real.

This is the structural form of the argument. It is one of Madhva's sharpest moves against Advaita because it cannot be dodged by the two-level scheme. The two-level scheme presupposes a witness that has access to both levels. Once the witness is acknowledged, the witness's testimony to bondage is not erasable by a "higher" cognition that *also* requires the witness. The Advaitin is caught.

### Engagement note for the user's reading

T8 — right action as the lawful unfolding of *sākṣi-caitanya* — uses the *sākṣin* doctrine as instrument. In Madhva, the *sākṣin* is *jīva*-bound, permanently distinct from Viṣṇu. The trajectory reads the *sākṣin* both as the *jīva*'s witness *and* as the locus of the divine's self-presence in the individual — closer to the Pratyabhijñā / Trika *citiḥ svatantrā* than to Madhva's strictly individual *sākṣin*. The continuity is the formal argument that knowledge requires a non-*vṛtti* witness; the discontinuity is scope. Madhva confines the witness to the *jīva*'s own *cit*; the Trika identifies it with Śiva's self-cognising; Aurobindo places knowledge-by-identity at the supramental level. T8 invokes Madhva's formal point and extends its scope.

## Part V — *Māyā-Vāda Khaṇḍana*: The Philosophical Heart

This is the philosophical heart of the reader. Three preliminary points.

First: Madhva's critique is *formal*, not theological. The argument is that Advaita is internally incoherent — it cannot consistently state its own central doctrine, on three structural fronts: the locus (*āśraya*) of *avidyā*, the ontological status of the *anirvacanīya*, and the formal requirements for sublation (*bādha*).

Second: the target is not Advaita-as-such but specifically the doctrine that the world is a *vivarta* of Brahman through *avidyā*. Madhva's formal arguments leave open Bhāskara's *bhedābheda*, Caitanya's *acintya-bhedābheda*, and the user's *avidyā* as *bhāvarūpa* divine self-veiling.

Third, crucial for the user's reading, Madhva's diagnosis is correct but the prescription is too narrow. He sees that Advaita cannot locate *avidyā* and concludes that *avidyā* must be a real entity *other than* Brahman, hence Tattva-vāda. A third option he does not consider: *avidyā* is *bhāvarūpa* and *Brahman's own self-veiling*. The arguments are valid as critique; the positive prescription prematurely closes off the supramental option.

The argument runs in four stages: (V.1) the locus dilemma; (V.2) the *anirvacanīya* problem; (V.3) the Madhusūdana concession; (V.4) the *pratīti-bādha* problem.

### V.1 The locus dilemma

The question: where does *avidyā* sit? Two candidates exhaust the logical space — the *jīva* or Brahman — and Madhva argues each is fatal.

**Candidate A: Bhāmatī school — *jīva* is the locus** (Vācaspati Miśra). The problem: in Advaita, the *jīva* is itself a *product* of *avidyā*. So the *jīva*-as-locus is *constituted by* *avidyā* even as it is the *seat of* *avidyā*. This is *āśrayāsiddhi*: viciously circular. Further, if *jīva* exists *because* of *avidyā*, then *avidyā* is prior to its own locus — and an entity without a locus cannot exist.

**Candidate B: Vivaraṇa school — Brahman is the locus** (Prakāśātman). The problem: Brahman is *ananta-cit* — self-luminous, unlimited consciousness. For pure consciousness to be *covered* by ignorance is contradictory. The coverer must be real; but Advaita's *avidyā* is *anirvacanīya*. Further, if Brahman is ignorant, no individual act of *jñāna* removes Brahman's ignorance — the soteriology breaks.

Jayatīrtha sharpens (*Nyāya-Sudhā*): if Brahman is both *āśraya* and *viṣaya*, *avidyā* must be *bhāvarūpa* — positively real. But once *avidyā* is real, it is either *sat* (and Brahman includes ignorance — simplicity gone) or distinct from *sat* (and *dvaita* is vindicated). No third option.

AV 1.1.30 states the point compactly: even the world's supposed falsity depends on first establishing ignorance, and ignorance as a covering cannot operate on what is simply unreal.

Even the world's supposed *mithyātva* depends on the prior establishment of *ajñāna*; and *ajñāna*, precisely as a covering (*āvaraṇa*), is not admitted by the Advaitin himself to operate upon what is non-existent (*asat*). The argument turns on a reflexive bind: the *mithyātva*-claim presupposes (*apekṣate*) *ajñāna* as the producer of appearance, but *ajñāna* as coverer requires something real enough to be covered. If the world is *asat*, *ajñāna* veils nothing; if it veils something real, *mithyātva* collapses.

Formal locus dilemma: (P1) *avidyā* veils its locus; (P2) to veil is to stand in a real relation; (P3) real relation requires both relata to have determinate ontological status; (P4) therefore *avidyā*'s locus must be real; (P5) but for Advaita, *avidyā* is *anirvacanīya* — neither *sat* nor *asat*; (C) either *avidyā* is real (and is something besides Brahman; non-dual claim fails) or it is not real (and nothing is veiled; bondage is inexplicable).

### V.2 The *anirvacanīya* problem

Advaita's standard escape from the locus dilemma is to declare *avidyā* *anirvacanīya* — indescribable, neither *sat* nor *asat*. Madhva's argument at AV 1.1.29 is that this is not a solution but an evasion of the conditions on solutions.

AV 1.1.29 makes the companion point: if *ajñāna* is said to be *anirvacanīya*, instability follows, and if Brahman shines by itself as qualityless consciousness, nothing remains that ignorance could intelligibly veil.

If *ajñāna* is held to be *anirvacanīya*, non-settlement (*anavasthiti*) follows without remainder; and when Brahman shines of itself as *nirviśeṣa*, what is left there that ignorance could intelligibly veil? *Anavasthiti* is literally a failure to come to rest: at every level the question reopens whether the allegedly indescribable is itself *sat* or *asat*. And the second question is not rhetorical. Once Brahman is specified as *nirviśeṣa svayaṃ-bhāta*, no intelligible "what?" remains for ignorance to cover.

Two arguments interleave. First, the *anavasthiti*: if *anirvacanīya* is a third ontological status, every entity could be reclassified as *anirvacanīya* whenever the *sat*/*asat* dichotomy is inconvenient — a free-floating escape clause. Second, the *self-luminosity*: if Brahman is *nirviśeṣa* and *svayam-bhāta*, there is nothing in Brahman to veil. If featureless, what does *ajñāna* hide? If featured, Brahman is *saviśeṣa* and Advaita is refuted.

Extended formal argument: (i) any entity is either *sat* or *asat*; (ii) "indescribable" claims a third status, but this is a conceptual not ontological move — from "we cannot describe X as *sat* or *asat*" it does not follow that "X is in a third ontological status"; (iii) even granting it, the status itself must be real or unreal — back to the binary at one level up; (iv) the *anirvacanīya* entity must have *some* specifiable nature, which makes it at least nominally real. Therefore *anirvacanīya* collapses into *asat* (and *māyā* explains nothing) or into *sat* (and Advaita concedes realism). Jayatīrtha sharpens: *avidyā* is supposed to function causally; an entity with no describable nature cannot function causally; therefore *avidyā* is either real or causally inert.

### V.3 The Madhusūdana concession and Madhva's meta-objection

The most sophisticated Advaita response — Madhusūdana Sarasvatī's *Advaita-Siddhi* (sixteenth century, written against Vyāsatīrtha's *Nyāyāmṛta*) — *concedes* the *anirvacanīya*-collapses-into-real horn. *Avidyā* is *bhāvarūpa* — positively existent, of the nature of being, doing causal work, not mere absence.

The concession is structural. Three things follow. First, *avidyā* has a real positive ontology, breaking the strict reading of "Brahman alone is real." Second, it requires a real locus, since *bhāvarūpa* entities cannot be locus-less. Third, the *jñāna*–*ajñāna* relation is now between two positive terms, not a presence removing an absence. Madhusūdana places these in Brahman, holding the existence still *anirvacanīya* in being dissolved by *jñāna*. The user's reading takes this as a *forced* concession, what Madhva's pressure compelled the most rigorous Advaitin to give up, and reads its consequences toward T7.

Madhva's meta-objection: the two-level (*vyāvahārika* / *pāramārthika*) strategy is incoherent at its joint. The Advaitin asserts: at *pāramārthika*, *avidyā* is dissolved; at *vyāvahārika*, it operates. From which level is this assertion made? If *vyāvahārika*: the assertion is itself *vyāvahārika*-true and *pāramārthika*-false; the force of the scheme depends on *pāramārthika*-truth of the dissolution claim, which is not delivered. If *pāramārthika*: the speaker has access to a level at which there is no *vyāvahārika* world to speak from, no *jīva* speaking, no language. The two-level scheme fails to provide its own grounding — the *anavasthā* of AV 1.1.29.

AV 1.1.17 applies this to bondage:

AV 1.1.17 states the pressure succinctly: if suffering is said to be *mithyā*, that verdict must already be established at the moment of assertion, and the disputant has not shown how a *mithyā* item could establish anything.

If the *mithyātva* of suffering is what the Advaitin asserts, that verdict must already stand at the very point of asserting it; yet he has not shown how something *mithyā* could establish anything at all. To declare bondage false requires proof, and that proof is either real, in which case *bheda* is conceded, or itself *mithyā*, in which case it proves nothing. AV 1.1.18 makes the point nakedly: if real, *dvaita* is established; if otherwise, nothing is established.

### V.4 The *pratīti-bādha* problem

The fourth stage concerns the structure of *bādha* (sublation). Advaita's soteriology depends on the liberating cognition (*aham brahmāsmi*) *sublating* the cognition of plurality. For sublation to be genuine, the sublating cognition must have determinate content incompatible with the sublated content (rope-content sublates snake-content).

The argument: (i) if the sublating cognition is "Brahman alone is," it has determinate content; (ii) but *nirviśeṣa* Brahman has no determinate content; (iii) a contentless cognition cannot sublate anything; (iv) therefore either Brahman is *saviśeṣa* (vindicating Madhva) or the soteriological apparatus is void.

AV 1.1.16 attacks the analogous claim about bondage: liberation does not require bondage to be unreal, and bondage cannot be declared unreal in the face of direct experience.

Given [the earlier arguments], liberation does not require the *mithyātva* of bondage; and the *mithyātva* of bondage cannot be [established] because of its conflict with direct perception.

Bondage, pain, limitation are not optional features of experience that can be canceled by an interpretive move. They are *positively given* in *sākṣi-pratyakṣa*. If experience of bondage continues even in *jīvanmukti* (and Advaita admits it does — the *jīvanmukta* still has phenomenal experience, including pain), either bondage was not fully sublated, or the experience is now known *as illusory* — but then the *sākṣin* which knows it as illusory must have had genuine epistemic access to *both* bondage and non-bondage, which reinstates a real difference between bound state and free state.

### Madhva's positive alternative

AV 1.1.13 (Part I) is the *positive* alternative to *vivarta*: a *governed-dependence* account. The world is real; its reality is its continuous being-sustained by Viṣṇu's *anugraha*. Not *vivarta*, not Sāṃkhya-style self-sufficient *pariṇāma*, but *Viṣṇu-superintended pariṇāma* of *prakṛti*. *Avidyā* does no explanatory work; the world remains fully real.

Bondage is *real*, and liberation is its *real* removal — not recognition-as-illusory. Real removal requires a real cause: Viṣṇu's *anugraha*. *Jñāna* is instrumental — *jñāna* of Viṣṇu's nature pleases Viṣṇu — but grace is the efficient cause. The conjunction of *bhakti* and *jñāna* is an entailment of the ontology, not an ad hoc concession.

AV 1.4.122–123 close the *adhyāya* with the rejection of *vivarta* and its positive substitute. Note: the famous *uparamyate tato viṣṇuḥ* line belongs to 1.4.123, not 1.4.122 as commonly cited. AV 1.4.122 says that the *vivarta* doctrine cannot stand by any route and must be abandoned by those who seek the good.

AV 1.4.123 then adds that the enumeration of defects is suspended only because they are countless, and that Viṣṇu, full of perfect qualities, effortlessly and willfully makes the world as a father makes a son.

[Further] enumeration stops here only because the defects are countless and the text would swell without limit. Thereupon Viṣṇu — complete in the plenitude of all auspicious *guṇas* — brings forth the universe effortlessly (*aśramaḥ*) and by will (*icchā-pūrvakam*), as a father brings forth a son.

A philological correction. The standard translation (Padmanabhachar; even Sharma at points) reads 1.4.122–123 as a continuous quasi-soteriological remark — "this *vivarta-vāda* is to be rejected, and when it is rejected, Viṣṇu grants liberation." The Sanskrit does not say this. *Uparamyate* in 1.4.123 is impersonal-passive; its subject is *grantha-vistāra* (continued exposition) supplied from *granthādhikya-bhayāt*; the meaning is "the discussion is here laid to rest." What *follows* is not a reward for the ex-Advaitin but the *positive ontology* the chapter has been building toward: *karoti pitṛvad viśvaṃ pūrṇa-śeṣa-guṇātmakaḥ* — Viṣṇu, who *is* the totality of perfect *guṇas*, makes the world *as a father makes a son*. The *pitṛvat* simile is the decisive antithesis to *vivarta*: a son is *real*, *distinct from* the father, *dependent on* the father, *like* the father in nature, and produced *willfully* — every clause in the simile blocks a feature of the *vivarta-vāda*. This is Madhva's positive doctrine of creation as *willed real production by a maximal *guṇa*-bearer*, distilled into one verse.

### The user's reading of the *khaṇḍana*

Madhva's *māyā-vāda khaṇḍana* is *right* about the structural problem in pure Advaita. The locus of *avidyā* cannot be coherently assigned; *anirvacanīya* is an evasive non-answer; the two-level strategy generates explanatory circularity; the *pratīti-bādha* argument shows that Advaita's account of sublation is formally incomplete. Madhva names a load-bearing wall in Śaṅkara's system that cannot stand on its own.

But Madhva draws the wrong conclusion. He infers: therefore *avidyā* is something Brahman did not produce; therefore reality has irreducibly multiple grounds; therefore Tattva-vāda. This closes off a third option the formal arguments do not foreclose.

The third option, the user's T7, is that *avidyā* is *bhāvarūpa* because it is *Brahman's own self-expressive act of self-concealment*. This does not require a second real entity; it requires that Brahman's *svātantrya* include the freedom to self-veil. The Vivaraṇa move taken at its boldest, the Kashmir Śaiva *svātantrya* doctrine made structural, Madhusūdana's concession read as ontology rather than embarrassed retreat. The same instrument — *viśeṣa* — that Madhva uses internally to Viṣṇu can be extended (against Madhva's prohibition) to handle the relation between Brahman as veiled and Brahman as unveiled.

The harder claim — which Sharma, Padmanabhachar, Buchta, and Stoker do not explicitly make: Madhva's critique is valid *and* Aurobindo's *bhāvarūpa avidyā* doctrine answers it. Strict Advaita cannot reply to Madhva. Strict Tattva-vāda is the wrong positive answer to a real problem. The right answer: *avidyā* is real, *bhāvarūpa*, and Brahman's own.

T7 is *forced* by Madhva's arguments if one is unwilling to accept Tattva-vāda's pluralisation of grounds. Without Madhva's pressure, T7 looks like wishful reconciling theology; with it, T7 is the only consistent answer that preserves non-duality while accepting the *bhāvarūpa* status. Madhva's critique is, in this specific sense, T7's prosecutor.

## Part VI — *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya* Selections

Madhva's *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya* (BSB) reads Bādarāyaṇa's 564 sūtras as continuously affirming theistic realism. The *Bhāṣya* is shorter than Śaṅkara's and far more polemical; the auto-commentary in verse, the *Anuvyākhyāna*, expands the arguments at length and is the philosophically richer text. We treat four critical loci: [BSB 1.1.1](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1), [BSB 1.1.2](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.2), [BSB 1.4.21–22](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.4.21-22), and [BSB 2.1.14](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14). Each is a place where Madhva's reading of Bādarāyaṇa is structurally decisive against Śaṅkara's, and the disagreements turn on specific words and passages whose readings can be checked.

### [BSB 1.1.1](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1): *Athāto Brahma-Jijñāsā*

The opening sūtra of the *Brahma-Sūtras*: *athāto brahma-jijñāsā* — "Now, therefore, [begins] the inquiry into Brahman."

Śaṅkara reads *atha* (now) as marking the moment after the acquisition of the four-fold *sādhana-catuṣṭaya*: *nityānitya-vastu-viveka* (discrimination between the eternal and non-eternal), *ihāmutrārtha-phala-bhoga-virāga* (dispassion regarding fruits in this and other worlds), *śama-damādi-sādhana-sampatti* (the sixfold inner discipline), and *mumukṣutva* (desire for liberation). The qualified inquirer (*adhikārin*) is one who has these four; *atha* marks the moment they have been acquired.

Madhva reads *atha* as marking the moment of *prasāda*, the operation of Viṣṇu's grace that enables inquiry. The *Anuvyākhyāna* at 1.1.12 says that liberation comes only through divine grace, a grace connected to the knowledge that arises from inquiry.

By the strength of statements like "[Brahman] is attained only by the one whom [Brahman] chooses" [Kaṭha 1.2.23, Muṇḍaka 3.2.3], one is liberated only by [Viṣṇu's] grace, [a grace] arising from the knowledge born of inquiry.

The structural argument: the inquiry (*jijñāsā*) is not produced by the *adhikārin*'s effort alone. That would make liberation self-generated, would cut grace out of the picture, and would contradict the *vṛṇute yam* passages that explicitly make the relationship initiation-from-the-Lord's side. The very capacity and disposition for Vedāntic inquiry is Viṣṇu's gift. *Atha* marks the moment when the gift is operative.

On *brahma*: where Śaṅkara glosses Brahman as pure consciousness — qualityless and unlimited (*nirguṇa*, *nirviśeṣa*) — Madhva reads *brahma* etymologically and substantively. Etymologically: *brahma* < *bṛh* (to grow, to be great); *brahma* = *bṛhattva* (the supremely great), with *bṛhattva* understood not just as quantitative but as *qualitatively maximal* — the being whose every *guṇa* is *pūrṇa* (full, undiminished). Substantively: *brahma* = *pūrṇa-guṇatva* — fullness of all auspicious qualities. This is not a nominal disagreement with Śaṅkara. It forces the inquiry to be about *a being with a determinate nature*, not about the recognition of the inquirer's own already-existing nature.

The argument structure on *brahma*:

(i) If Brahman is *nirviśeṣa* (qualityless), *jijñāsā* (inquiry into Brahman) has no specifiable object. One cannot inquire into a purely contentless absolute; there is nothing about which questions could be raised, no content that could be discovered.

(ii) But the sūtra prescribes "inquiry" — implying that there is something to be discovered which is not already known. Therefore Brahman must have a nature to be discovered.

(iii) The disputed question — does Brahman have the full complement of qualities, some qualities, or none? — is precisely what generates the need for inquiry. This question is intelligible only if Brahman has a nature.

(iv) Vedic evidence, properly read, shows *Brahma = Viṣṇu = pūrṇa-guṇatva*: a real being with real qualities, eternally distinct from *jīvas*. The inquiry is therefore a real inquiry into a real being.

The *Anuvyākhyāna* gives a particularly dense line at 1.1.9: *oṃ* expresses all-pervading interwovenness and thereby declares the fullness of divine qualities, so that the word *brahma* points to the same being named by *Nārāyaṇa*.

The *oṅkāra* (Om), expressing *otatva* (interwovenness, all-pervading constitution), declares that being's *guṇa-otatā* (the interwoven fullness of his qualities); that very [being] is the meaning of the word "*brahma*," uttered by the term *Nārāyaṇa*.

The hermeneutic logic: *oṃ* = *bṛhattva* = *guṇa-otatā* = *brahma* = *Nārāyaṇa*. All four terms denote the same being, who is *pūrṇa-guṇa*. Inquiry into Brahman is inquiry into Nārāyaṇa as the bearer of all auspicious *guṇas*. This is not Śaṅkara's *nirguṇa*-Brahman. The textual evidence, Madhva claims, forces the *saguṇa* reading from sūtra one.

### [BSB 1.1.2](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.2): *Janmādyasya Yataḥ*

The sūtra: *janmādy asya yataḥ* — "From which [proceeds] the origin, etc., of this [world]."

Both Śaṅkara and Madhva read this as the *lakṣaṇa* (definition) of Brahman: that from which the world originates, is sustained, and into which it is dissolved. The disagreement is in the *ontological status* of the causal relation and in the *identity of cause and effect*.

Śaṅkara: Brahman is both material *and* efficient cause — the *abhinna-nimitta-upādāna*, the cause that is non-different from its effect. But the world is *mithyā*; the causation is *vivarta* (apparent), not *pariṇāma* (real). The snake does not really emerge from the rope; the world does not really emerge from Brahman.

Madhva: Brahman (Viṣṇu) is the *efficient* cause; *prakṛti* is the *material* cause under Viṣṇu's *adhyakṣa*. The two are *bheda* — *nimitta-upādāna-bheda*. Viṣṇu does not *become* the world; he creates it through *prakṛti* as instrument. The world is genuinely produced (*pariṇāma*).

The argument against *vivarta-vāda*:

(i) A genuine causal relation requires real *kāraṇa* (cause) and real *kārya* (effect).

(ii) If the world-effect is *mithyā*, then the causal relation is *mithyā*. But then Brahman's role as *janmādi-kāraṇa* is itself *mithyā* — the sūtra describes an appearance-relation, not a real one.

(iii) But then *the sūtra itself is not really a definition of Brahman* — it defines Brahman only "from the *vyāvahārika* level." A *mithyā* definition cannot ground inquiry into real Brahman; the *Brahma-Sūtras*' own project becomes, at best, a *vyāvahārika* exercise that does not deliver *pāramārthika* knowledge.

(iv) Therefore: either the sūtra is real (and the causal relation is real, vindicating Madhva's realism) or the sūtra is appearance-only (and Advaita has no sūtra-based evidence for its own position — it must appeal beyond the *Brahma-Sūtras* to support its central claim).

The argument is sharp. It puts the Advaitin in a dilemma about the very text on which Advaita is built. Either the sūtras are real (and they teach realism) or they are *mithyā* (and they cannot teach anything *pāramārthika*). Either way, the *Brahma-Sūtras* are not evidence *for* pure Advaita.

### [BSB 1.4.21–22](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.4.21-22): Against Sāṃkhya, with *Prakṛti* Preserved

The sūtras 1.4.21 (*prakṛtyaiva*) and surrounding adhikaraṇas concern the Sāṃkhya account of creation. For Bādarāyaṇa, Sāṃkhya is the primary rival to be defeated before Brahman-as-cause can be established. All three major Vedāntins — Śaṅkara, Rāmānuja, Madhva — defeat Sāṃkhya here. The disagreement is over what survives the defeat.

Madhva's argument:

(i) Sāṃkhya's claim is that *prakṛti* (unconscious primordial matter, in equilibrium of the three *guṇas*) causes the world through proximity to *puruṣa* (consciousness) but without *puruṣa*'s active agency.

(ii) An unconscious entity cannot self-organize *for a purpose* — particularly, for the purpose of *puruṣa*'s liberation, which is the teleological frame Sāṃkhya itself invokes — without an intelligent designer. The teleological structure of the universe (the ordered evolutionary sequence: *mahattattva* → *ahaṃkāra* → *tanmātras* → bhūtas, sense-organs, motor organs) is too determinate, too oriented, to be the product of unconscious matter alone.

(iii) Therefore there must be an intelligent efficient cause directing *prakṛti*. That cause is Viṣṇu.

So far, the argument is shared with Śaṅkara and Rāmānuja. The decisive difference: in Madhva's defeat of Sāṃkhya, *prakṛti* survives as a *real* eternal-with-modification material substrate. *Prakṛti* is not collapsed into *māyā* (Śaṅkara's move); it is not collapsed into Brahman's body (Rāmānuja's move); it is preserved as a real *asvatantra* substance under Viṣṇu's *adhyakṣa*. The *bheda* between Viṣṇu and *prakṛti* is preserved through the entire causal account.

The encoding verse, AV 1.1.13, repeated here in context, again says that substance, action, time, intrinsic nature, and *jīva* exist only by Viṣṇu's sustaining grace.

Substance, action, time, intrinsic nature, and *jīva* — these exist by [Viṣṇu's] grace and do not exist when he withdraws.

This is the positive theology that survives the defeat of Sāṃkhya. *Prakṛti* (substance), *karma* (action), *kāla* (time), *svabhāva* (intrinsic nature of each *jīva*), and *jīvas* are all real, eternal-with-modification, and continuously sustained by Viṣṇu. Not pantheism, not Advaita, not Sāṃkhya: a *governed-dependence* account where the world is real because and as Viṣṇu sustains it.

### [BSB 2.1.14](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14): *Tad-Ananyatva* — The Decisive Disagreement

The sūtra (Bādarāyaṇa 2.1.14): *tad-ananyatvam ārambhaṇa-śabdādibhyaḥ* — "The non-difference of [the effect from the cause is established] by the word [*ārambhaṇa*] and similar [terms]."

Context. The reference is to the *ārambhaṇa-adhikaraṇa* in Chāndogya Upaniṣad 6.1.4–6, in which Uddālaka instructs Śvetaketu about clay and pots: *yathā saumyaikena mṛt-piṇḍena sarvaṃ mṛn-mayaṃ vijñātaṃ syāt vācārambhaṇaṃ vikāro nāmadheyaṃ mṛttiketyeva satyam* — "Just as, dear one, by [knowing] one lump of clay everything made of clay is known; modification is a verbal undertaking, a name; clay alone is real." This is the *locus classicus* for the claim that effect is not different from cause — and therefore, on Śaṅkara's reading, that the world is not different from Brahman, that Brahman alone is real and the world is *vācārambhaṇam* (a verbal undertaking, mere name).

Madhva's counter-reading is structurally crucial and turns on the meaning of *ananyatva*. *Ananyatva* (non-difference, non-otherness) does *not* mean *abheda* (identity). It means *non-independence*, *ontological dependence*. The argument:

(i) A son is not "other" than his father in the sense of being causally disconnected — but son and father are not numerically identical. *Ananyatva* in the relational sense is compatible with numerical distinctness.

(ii) Similarly, the world is not causally disconnected from Brahman, but it is not Brahman.

(iii) If *ananyatva* meant strict numerical identity:

(a) Modifications of the effect would be modifications of the cause. Change in the world would be change in Brahman. But Brahman is *kūṭastha-nitya* (immutably eternal). Therefore the effect cannot be numerically identical with the cause.

(b) Destruction of the effect would be destruction of the cause. When a pot is broken, clay is not destroyed. But if pot = clay in identity, pot's destruction *would* be clay's destruction. The Chāndogya passage itself uses *clay-as-cause, pot-as-effect* as the model — and clay is *not* pot. The passage's own analogy rules out strict identity.

(iv) The correct reading: *ananyatva* = the effect's *dependence on* and *non-independence from* the cause. The pot is "not other" than clay in the sense that it cannot exist without clay, that it is *clay-modified*, that its existence is parasitic on clay's. But pot is not clay.

This reading is decisive against Śaṅkara at the textual level. Madhva is not proposing an extravagant interpretive scheme. He is reading the sūtra in line with its own analogical model (Chāndogya 6.1.4–6) and pointing out that the analogical model itself rules out strict identity. If clay-pot is not identity, *ārambhaṇa-śabda*'s testimony is not testimony to identity. The Advaitic reading either ignores the analogy or reads it as itself *mithyā* — which, again, undercuts the sūtra's evidential force. The dilemma is forced. Either *ārambhaṇa* teaches dependence (Madhva) or it teaches *mithyā* (Śaṅkara, but at the cost of removing the sūtra's evidential weight from the entire *vivarta-vāda*).

This is the textual move that most directly establishes Madhva's reading of *ananyatva* as *ontological dependence not identity* — the central interpretive claim of the Madhva *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya*. From this single hermeneutical decision, the rest follows: real-distinct effects, real causation, *pariṇāma* not *vivarta*, *jīvas* and *prakṛti* and Viṣṇu as three distinct ontological categories standing in real relations.

A final note on the Chāndogya passage's *vācārambhaṇam vikāro nāmadheyam* — "modification is a verbal undertaking, a name." Śaṅkara reads this as saying modification is *only* name, with no real referent — supporting *vivarta*. Madhva reads it differently: modification is *named* (it has a name as a modification), it is something we *speak about* (a verbal undertaking is a real linguistic act), but the underlying substance is *clay* — meaning that the name "pot" picks out a real configuration of clay, not an illusion. The passage on this reading is making a deflationary move about *names*, not about modifications. The pot is real; "pot" as a name is just the way we pick out a real configuration of clay. This is Madhva at his most analytically precise: the passage that Śaṅkara reads as teaching the unreality of modifications is read by Madhva as teaching the realness of modifications under the *aspect* of being clay-configurations. Both are real; what is "merely verbal" is not the modification but the *name*.

### Synoptic on the BSB selections

Across the four loci, Madhva's reading converges on a single structural claim: Bādarāyaṇa's *Brahma-Sūtras* teach a *real-distinction* metaphysics in which Brahman is the *svatantra* and the world (including *prakṛti* and *jīvas*) is the *asvatantra*, and the relations among them are real causal relations, not appearances. *Ananyatva* is dependence not identity. *Janmādi-kāraṇatva* is real causation not *vivarta*. *Brahma-jijñāsā* is inquiry into a *saguṇa* being not recognition of a contentless absolute. The *prakṛti*-discussion in 1.4 preserves *prakṛti* as a real material substrate.

The cumulative force is significant. Madhva's reading of the sūtras is not idiosyncratic in its hermeneutic ambitions — it claims to read *mukhyārtha* against Śaṅkara's *gauṇārtha* — and at several points (*ananyatva* against *abheda*, the clay-pot analogy against *vivarta*, the *anyaḥ* of BG 15.17 against identity) the case is strong. The trajectory T2's hermeneutical corollary is: a sustained realist reading of the *Brahma-Sūtras* is available, the *mukhyārtha* generally favors the realist reading, and Madhva is its most systematic articulator.

## Part VII — *Viṣṇu-Tattva-Vinirṇaya* and BG 15.17

The *Viṣṇu-Tattva-Vinirṇaya* (VTV) is a brief but rigorous independent treatise — perhaps the most concentrated single statement of Madhva's *bheda* thesis outside the *Anuvyākhyāna*. Its method is to establish *jīva-Viṣṇu-bheda* and *jaḍa-Viṣṇu-bheda* by all three *pramāṇas* (perception, inference, scriptural testimony), with each *pramāṇa*-line closing on an *anya*-passage from *śruti* or *smṛti* that uses the word "other" of the highest *Puruṣa*.

The structure of VTV:

(i) *Pratyakṣa* — direct experience certifies limitation, finitude, suffering. These states are positively given. They are incompatible with identification with Viṣṇu (who is *pūrṇa-guṇa*, *ānanda*, *sarvajña*). The witness directly apprehends them. The witness is veridical (Part IV's argument). Therefore the limitation is real and the *jīva* in that limitation is not Viṣṇu.

(ii) *Anumāna* — the teleological order of the universe implies an intelligent cause distinct from the material substrate. The cause cannot be identical with what it orders, because the ordering presupposes a perspective on the ordered. If Viṣṇu is the orderer, *prakṛti* is the ordered, and they are *bheda*.

(iii) *Āgama* / *Śabda* — *śruti* consistently speaks of Viṣṇu as *Puruṣottama*, distinct from *kṣara-puruṣa* (the mutable individual *jīva*) and from *akṣara-puruṣa* (the unchanging liberated soul). The canonical witness is BG 15.16–17.

The critical verse is BG 15.17, where the highest Puruṣa is called *anyaḥ*, another, the immutable Lord who enters and sustains the three worlds.

The highest *Puruṣa* is yet *another* (*anyaḥ*), called Paramātmā, the immutable Lord who, pervading the three worlds, sustains them.

Madhva's reading turns on a single word: *anyaḥ* — "yet another." The Gītā says explicitly that Paramātmā is *anyaḥ* — *other* than both *kṣara* and *akṣara*. The word cannot be read as identity. Paramātmā is irreducibly distinct from both classes of *jīva*. This is the scripturally certified *bheda*.

The argument structure of VTV: every Advaitic counter-reading of *anyaḥ* — "other" as nominal designation, "other" as perspectival, "other" as *upādhic* — violates *mukhyārtha*. The primary meaning of *anyaḥ* is "different." There is no philological warrant for moving to secondary meaning unless primary meaning fails. Primary meaning does not fail here; it gives a perfectly intelligible theistic reading. Therefore primary meaning is what the verse asserts. Therefore Paramātmā is *anyaḥ* from *jīvas* — really distinct.

A second key passage cited in VTV is from the *Mahābhārata* (which Madhva reads as theological-Vaiṣṇava throughout, and on which he wrote the *Mahābhārata-Tātparya-Nirṇaya*). The *Mahābhārata*-tradition repeatedly emphasizes Viṣṇu's transcendence: *yataḥ sarvāṇi bhūtāni bhavanty ādiyugāgame | yasmiṃś ca pralayaṃ yānti punar eva yugakṣaye ||* — "from whom all beings come at the dawn of the age, and into whom they go at the close of the age." The point is that the *yataḥ* and the *yasmin* are *one* being who is *not* the beings that come and go; the relation is asymmetric.

The condensed *bheda* argument that VTV mounts can be put in five lines:

(i) The witness directly apprehends limitation in itself.
(ii) Limitation is incompatible with Viṣṇu's nature.
(iii) Therefore the witness's *svarūpa* is not Viṣṇu's *svarūpa*.
(iv) The witness is the *jīva* itself, not a faculty of the *jīva*.
(v) Therefore the *jīva* is not Viṣṇu in *svarūpa* — *jīva-Viṣṇu-bheda* is established.

This is *pratyakṣa*-grounded *bheda*. It does not depend on hermeneutical disputes about identity-passages; it appeals directly to what the witness sees. The same argument applies, *mutatis mutandis*, to *jaḍa-Viṣṇu-bheda*: the witness directly apprehends material things as not having the marks of Viṣṇu's nature (consciousness, sovereignty, pūrṇatva); therefore the material world is not Viṣṇu in *svarūpa*.

The *anumāna*-line and the *śabda*-line then reinforce what *pratyakṣa* delivers. The result is a multiply-grounded *bheda* — established by all three *pramāṇas*, with no single line of argument bearing the entire weight.

VTV's polemical value lies precisely in this multiple grounding. The Advaitin who tries to unsettle one *pramāṇa*-line (by, say, declaring perception *vyāvahārika*-only) finds that the other two lines independently support the conclusion. The Advaitin who tries to undercut all three (by the global *vyāvahārika* / *pāramārthika* move) faces the locus dilemma and the *anirvacanīya* problem from Part V. There is no consistent escape route that does not pluralise Brahman or void the entire epistemic apparatus.

For the user's trajectory: VTV is the briefest entry into the formal architecture. If one had to read one Madhva-text as the synoptic statement, VTV is the candidate. Its argument structure is the same as the *Anuvyākhyāna*'s but compressed. Its central verse — BG 15.17 with its *anyaḥ* — is the canonical *śruti*-witness for the *bheda* doctrine, and the verse's force on *mukhyārtha* is hard to deflect.

## Part VIII — Jayatīrtha's *Nyāya-Sudhā*

Jayatīrtha (c. 1345–1388 CE), conventionally honored by the Mādhva tradition as *Tīkācārya* (master commentator), is the single most important secondary author in the school. The *Nyāya-Sudhā* — "the nectar of logical method" — is his sub-commentary on Madhva's *Anuvyākhyāna*, and it is the work that converts Madhva's compressed verses into a systematic philosophical apparatus. Where Madhva is often gnomic and allusive, Jayatīrtha is patient, expository, and rigorously formal. Subsequent Mādhva scholasticism (Vyāsatīrtha's *Nyāyāmṛta*, Rāghavendra's commentaries, the Vādirāja corpus) builds on the *Nyāya-Sudhā* as its second-level base text.

The *Nyāya-Sudhā*'s general method is to take each verse of the *Anuvyākhyāna*, isolate its argumentative components, identify implicit objections (*āśaṅkā*), and supply explicit replies (*samādhāna*). The result is a Madhvic *prakriyā* — a step-by-step philosophical procedure — that exposes the logical scaffolding under Madhva's compressed verses.

### Jayatīrtha on the maṅgala-verse: theology as definition

The very first analytical move in the *Nyāya-Sudhā* is on Madhva's opening *maṅgala* (auspicious salutation), AV 1.1.1, a dense praise of Nārāyaṇa as the all-perfect, faultless, world-causing Lord who is most attainable through the Vedic statements.

I bow to Nārāyaṇa, whose body is one with the entirety of his complete *guṇas*, who is faultless, who is most attainable through all good statements, who is at all times most beloved to me, who is to be adored even in respect of every distinction, [and] who is the [agent of] origination etc. of this [world].

Jayatīrtha's exegesis is meticulous. He takes the verse as a *lakṣaṇa* (definition) of Nārāyaṇa, with each compound functioning as a definitional clause. *Nikhila-pūrṇa-guṇaika-deha* — Nārāyaṇa's *deha* (body, in the sense of *svarūpa*) is one with the totality of his *pūrṇa-guṇas*. *Nirdoṣa* — free from *doṣas*, where *doṣa* is glossed as *pāratantrya* (dependence) and the like. *Āpyatama* — most-to-be-attained, by the *suvākyas* (good statements: Vedas, *Brahma-Sūtras*, etc.). *Asyodbhavādi* — the agent of origination etc. of this [world]. *Aśeṣa-viśeṣato 'pi vandyam* — to be adored even *aśeṣa-viśeṣataḥ*, which Jayatīrtha takes in two senses: (i) *aśeṣa* = exhaustive; *viśeṣa* = particular distinction; "to be adored in respect of every particular distinction" — i.e. each of his *guṇas* is independently adoration-worthy; (ii) *aśeṣa* = of all [other beings]; *viśeṣataḥ* = surpassingly; "more particularly to be adored than all [other beings]." The double reading is not a hermeneutic indulgence; it is Jayatīrtha showing that Madhva's compound is doing two argumentative jobs at once.

The structural point: Madhva's opening verse is *not* mere devotion. It is a compressed definition of the *svatantra*. Jayatīrtha unpacks it as such. The *guṇaika-deha* clause asserts that Nārāyaṇa is *not composite of substance plus attributes* — his body *is* the totality of his *guṇas*; this is the *viśeṣa* doctrine in seed form, before the term itself appears. The *nirdoṣa* clause asserts *aseity* — Nārāyaṇa is free from *pāratantrya*, which is what makes him *svatantra*. The *āpyatama* clause sets the epistemology: the way to Nārāyaṇa is through *suvākyas* (the *pramāṇas* of *śabda*-tradition). The *udbhavādi* clause asserts causal asymmetry: Nārāyaṇa is the cause of *this* (the world), not vice versa.

By Jayatīrtha's reading, the *maṅgala* alone contains the entire architecture of Tattva-vāda: aseity, internal non-composition, causal asymmetry, and epistemic accessibility through *śabda*. Madhva does not *announce* the system in a separate prose introduction; he encodes it in the *maṅgala* and lets Jayatīrtha decode it.

### Jayatīrtha on AV 1.1.13: from "exists by grace" to a full causal theology

The verse in question, repeated, is again AV 1.1.13, where the reality of substance, action, time, intrinsic nature, and *jīva* is made continuously dependent on divine favor.

Jayatīrtha's gloss begins by identifying the five enumerated *asvatantras*: *dravya* = *prakṛti* etc. as the five substances; *karma* = *dharma* and *adharma* (the moral-causal sequence); *kāla* = time; *svabhāva* = the *pariṇāma* (modification) of *prakṛti* and other *asvatantras*; *jīva* = the *abhimānin* (self-conceiving) sentient being. The *anugraha* / *upekṣā* contrast is glossed as *parameśvara-icchā* — the will of the supreme Lord — and the structure of the contrast is: positive will = sustaining (*santi*); withdrawn will (*upekṣā*) = ceasing-to-be (*na santi*). This is not destruction in the sense of an action by Viṣṇu *upon* the *asvatantras*; it is the absence of the sustaining will, after which the *asvatantras* simply do not continue.

The philosophical move Jayatīrtha makes here is significant. He takes the verse to assert *moment-by-moment continuous dependence*, not just causal origination. The *asvatantras* do not merely come into being by Viṣṇu's grace and then continue independently; they continue in being only insofar as Viṣṇu continues to will their being. This is closer to Aquinas's *creatio continua* than to the deistic picture in which God creates and then withdraws. The world's reality at every moment is a function of Viṣṇu's *anugraha* at that moment.

Jayatīrtha then makes the further move to *bandhasya īśvarādhīnatva* — the dependence of bondage on the Lord. If the *asvatantras* exist only by the Lord's continuing will, then bondage too — as a state of certain *asvatantras* (the *jīvas*) — exists only by the Lord's will. This has soteriological consequences: liberation from bondage requires the Lord's withdrawal of the bondage-sustaining will, which is to say the Lord's *anugraha* (favorable will toward liberation). This is the formal basis for Madhva's claim that *mokṣa* requires *prasāda*, not just *jñāna*. Jayatīrtha gives the formal argument.

He goes still further. Anticipating the Advaitin's objection ("if knowledge dissolves *avidyā* by its own nature, why is the Lord's grace needed?"), Jayatīrtha argues that *avidyā* itself, understood as the *āvarakatva* (covering-power) that hides the *jīva*'s nature, must be a real *dravya* (substance) under the Lord's control:

Jayatīrtha then says, in substance, that besides desire and karma one must accept some beginningless real stuff denoted by terms such as *māyā*, *avidyā*, and *prakṛti*.

[Therefore], distinct from desire, *karma*, etc., one must accept some beginningless substance, denominated by the words *māyā*, *avidyā*, *prakṛti*, etc.

This is a striking concession on the Advaitin's *bhāvarūpa* point, but in the opposite direction from Madhusūdana's. Where Madhusūdana places *bhāvarūpa avidyā* in Brahman as Brahman's *upādhi*, Jayatīrtha places it in the system as a real *dravya* under the Lord's *adhyakṣa*. *Avidyā* / *māyā* / *prakṛti* (Jayatīrtha treats them as functionally equivalent in this argument) is *real*, *beginningless*, and *Viṣṇu-controlled*. The Lord covers the *jīva*'s consciousness through this real *avidyā*; the Lord uncovers it (through *prasāda*) when the time is ripe. The structure is Vivaraṇa-school Advaita's own *bhāvarūpa* doctrine, but inverted: the locus is not Brahman but a real *asvatantra* substance which Viṣṇu controls.

The implications are sharp. Jayatīrtha is, in effect, articulating a position close to the user's T7 in mirror image. Where T7 says *avidyā* is *bhāvarūpa* and *Brahman's own self-veiling*, Jayatīrtha says *avidyā* is *bhāvarūpa* and *a real dravya distinct from Brahman that Viṣṇu wields*. Both moves preserve the *bhāvarūpa* status; they differ on the locus. The Madhva-Jayatīrtha line places the locus in a real *asvatantra* substance; the user's T7 places it in Brahman's own *svātantrya*. The formal move — *avidyā* must be *bhāvarūpa* and the *bhāvarūpa* status must be reconciled with the rest of the system — is the same move on both sides. The disagreement is over *whose* power *avidyā* is. Jayatīrtha's argument marks the formal slot into which T7 will later place divine self-veiling.

### Jayatīrtha on the *anirvacanīya*: the formal sharpening

On AV 1.1.29 (the *anirvacanīya* verse from Part V), Jayatīrtha's argument is the cleanest formal version of Madhva's critique. The structure:

(i) *Anirvacanīya* is supposed to mean: not *sat*, not *asat*, not both, not neither.

(ii) But the law of contradiction holds: *X* and *not-X* exhaust the logical space. There is no fifth option (the *catuṣkoṭi* gives four: *sat*, *asat*, *both*, *neither*; the *anirvacanīya* is supposed to be a *fifth* — *none of the above*).

(iii) The Advaitin replies: this is not a fifth status; it is a recognition of the limits of language. *Sat* and *asat* are predicates; the *anirvacanīya* refuses both because no predicate fits.

(iv) Jayatīrtha's counter: refusing both predicates is itself a description. To say "neither *sat* nor *asat* applies" is to specify a determinate ontological situation — the situation in which neither predicate applies. This situation must itself be either real or not-real. If real, *anirvacanīya* is *sat* in respect of *being-a-situation-in-which*; the dichotomy is reinstated at one level up. If not-real, the *anirvacanīya* claim is itself empty.

(v) Therefore *anirvacanīya* is not a stable ontological status; it is a name for the Advaitin's refusal to commit, and the refusal is unsustainable.

This is one of the most precise formulations of the *anirvacanīya* objection in classical Indian philosophy. It anticipates contemporary objections to "indeterminate" ontological categories: the indeterminacy must itself be either a property of the entity (in which case the entity has *some* property and is *sat* under that property) or a feature of our description of it (in which case the entity in itself has *some* determinate ontology, just one we cannot articulate). Either way, *anirvacanīya* fails to occupy the stable middle position the Advaitin needs.

### Jayatīrtha on the *brahma-jijñāsā* sūtra: the structure of inquiry

Jayatīrtha's commentary on Madhva's reading of the first *Brahma-Sūtra* extends the analysis with a detailed argument about the *upādhi* of inquiry — what makes inquiry possible. He develops a thesis worth quoting In brief, form: inquiry into Brahman presupposes (a) that Brahman has a nature to be inquired into, (b) that the inquirer has the capacity to inquire, (c) that the inquiry can succeed (i.e. there are *pramāṇas* that disclose Brahman's nature), and (d) that the success of inquiry has a soteriological consequence (i.e. *jñāna* leads to *mokṣa*).

For each of these four conditions, the Advaitic *nirviśeṣa* reading is in trouble. (a) *Nirviśeṣa* Brahman has no nature to be inquired into. (b) The *jīva*-inquirer is, on Advaita, an *avidyā*-product, so its capacity to inquire is itself parasitic on *avidyā*, generating the locus circularity. (c) The *pramāṇas* are *vyāvahārika* tools; their disclosing of Brahman is itself *vyāvahārika*; the *pāramārthika* nature of Brahman is, on Advaita's own admission, beyond the *pramāṇas*. (d) On the strict non-dual reading, *mokṣa* is not a "consequence" of *jñāna* in the sense of an effect; it is the recognition that Brahman *is* the *jīva*'s already-existing nature. But this recognition presupposes the very capacity (b) that is undermined.

Jayatīrtha's positive Mādhva reconstruction: Brahman is *Viṣṇu*, *saguṇa*, with a real determinate nature. The *jīva*-inquirer is real and has its own real *cit*. The *pramāṇas* disclose Viṣṇu's nature truly. *Jñāna* of Viṣṇu's nature *pleases* Viṣṇu (the soteriological link); pleased Viṣṇu confers *anugraha* (the efficient cause of liberation); liberated *jīvas* enjoy *svarūpānanda* in Viṣṇu's presence. Each of the four conditions has a determinate satisfaction in the Mādhva system. This is the formal argument that Jayatīrtha takes the *Anuvyākhyāna* to be making, when read with care.

### Why the *Nyāya-Sudhā* matters for the trajectory

For the user's reading of Madhva, three points stand out from Jayatīrtha.

First: Jayatīrtha makes Madhva's positive theology fully formal. Where Madhva's verses can be read as polemical assertion, Jayatīrtha shows they are formal arguments. The *bhāvarūpa* concession in Mādhva form, the *anirvacanīya* sharpening, the four-condition reconstruction of *brahma-jijñāsā* — all of these are Jayatīrtha's analytic moves, not Madhva's polemical moves, and they show that the system has formal teeth.

Second: Jayatīrtha's treatment of *avidyā* / *māyā* / *prakṛti* as a real *asvatantra dravya* under Viṣṇu's control parallels (in mirror-image) the user's T7. The formal slot — *avidyā* as *bhāvarūpa* — is established in the Mādhva tradition; the disagreement is over the locus. T7 takes the slot and relocates *avidyā* to Brahman's own *svātantrya*. Jayatīrtha is, in this very specific sense, the Mādhva commentator who most clearly *articulates the formal possibility* that T7 then re-purposes.

Third: Jayatīrtha's overall method — read each Madhva verse as a compressed argument with implicit objections and explicit replies — is the method by which Madhva's polemical force is transmitted to later Vedānta. Without the *Nyāya-Sudhā*, Madhva would be readable but not formally engageable; with it, the engagements are precise. The Vyāsatīrtha–Madhusūdana debate (sixteenth century) takes place at the formal level Jayatīrtha establishes. The trajectory T2's claim that Madhva's critique is *unanswered* by Advaita relies on the formal level Jayatīrtha makes explicit; without that level, Advaita could plausibly say it had answered (informally, theologically), but at the formal level the answers do not connect.

For the reading sequence (Part X below), the *Nyāya-Sudhā* is the second text after the *Anuvyākhyāna*. Read AV 1.1.1–50 (Madhva's compressed *māyā-vāda khaṇḍana*), then read *Nyāya-Sudhā* on the same verses. The pairing is essential: Madhva alone is too compressed; Jayatīrtha alone is too verbose; together they form the philosophical instrument the trajectory needs to register.

## Part IX — Reading Notes for the User's Trajectory

This part is the synthesis. It states explicitly where Madhva confirms the user's T2 trajectory, where he is one-sided in ways the trajectory must correct, what the user's own specific reading of Madhva is (against the secondary literature), and how to read Madhva in sequence.

### IX.1 Where Madhva confirms the trajectory

Madhva is the rigorous articulation of what the realist trajectory needs at the level of formal philosophy. Five points of confirmation.

**The reality of difference.** *Pañcabheda* names the formal conditions for the intelligibility of practice — devotional relation, real creation, individual *adhikāra*, embodied agency, real causation. Each *bheda* is load-bearing; to collapse them into appearance is to vacate the apparatus. T2 insists practice be intelligible; *pañcabheda* gives the formal articulation.

**The *māyā-vāda khaṇḍana* identifies real logical problems.** The locus dilemma, the *anirvacanīya* evasion, the two-level strategy's explanatory circularity, the *pratīti-bādha* incompleteness — these are genuine. T2 needs the prosecutorial brief to force the issue against pure Advaita; without it, Advaita drifts back into a *nirviśeṣa* claim that elides the problems.

**The *viśeṣa* doctrine is technical sophistication, not theological hand-waving.** It identifies non-relational internal distinction and solves the substance-attribute problem without pluralising the substance or making attribute-talk vacuous. It is the technical precursor to Caitanya's *acintya-śakti* and Aurobindo's Real-Idea — the move every realist Vedānta has to make under some name.

**The *sākṣin* doctrine names the epistemological instrument T8 deploys.** Right action as the lawful unfolding of *sākṣi-caitanya* presupposes a real, non-mediated, authoritative *pramāṇa* — exactly the position Madhva defends. T8's extension (the *sākṣin* as locus of the divine's self-presence) goes beyond Madhva, but the formal foundation is his.

**The *svatantra/asvatantra* divide preserves singular ground without symmetric dualism.** One independent, many dependents, real distinctions, real causation. The shape blocks both Sāṃkhya pluralism and Advaita's *nirviśeṣa* monism — the architecture the trajectory requires before the supramental simultaneity layer can be added.

### IX.2 Where Madhva is one-sided

Five points of one-sidedness.

**No admission of supramental non-difference.** The five *bhedas* are right *at the level Madhva articulates* but not unconditionally ultimate. Madhva treats them as exhaustively ultimate; the supramental register at which *bheda* and *abheda* are simultaneously real is not a refutation but a placement of *bheda* inside a larger frame. The trajectory takes the move he blocks.

**Rejects Caitanya's *acintya*.** Madhva's hermeneutic discipline makes identity-passages *gauṇārtha*; *acintya* would require both classes *mukhyārtha*, which Madhva refuses. The cost is that every identity-passage in *śruti* must be read as secondary — a position that strains natural reading.

**Restricts *viśeṣa* to within-Viṣṇu.** The same logic could handle the Viṣṇu-*jīva* relation; Madhva blocks the extension. Caitanya's *acintya-śakti* is that extension.

**Lacks the *adhikāra* discipline that reads Advaita as register-correct.** For Madhva, Advaita is flatly incoherent. The trajectory reads Advaita as register-correct for the comprehending pole of the supramental triple status; Madhva refuses this charitable reading.

**Rejects strong *bhakti-jñāna* simultaneity.** Madhva insists on a definite ordering: *jñāna* pleases Viṣṇu, who grants liberation. T5 takes different aims as co-valid at the level of register; Madhva's exclusive path is one path, not the only one.

### IX.3 The user's specific reading

This is the philosophically most important paragraph in the reading notes. It is a claim the standard secondary literature — B.N.K. Sharma, Padmanabhachar, Buchta, Stoker — does not explicitly make.

Madhva's *māyā-vāda khaṇḍana* exposes a *real* logical problem in pure Advaita. The locus of *avidyā* really cannot be coherently assigned within strict non-dualism. *Anirvacanīya* really is a category-evasion. The two-level strategy really does generate an explanatory regress.

Aurobindo's *bhāvarūpa* avidyā doctrine answers Madhva's critique by *repositioning what avidyā is*. *Avidyā* is not foreign error happening to Brahman from outside (which would either be Madhva's *dvaita* — a real second principle — or unintelligible). *Avidyā* is divine *svātantrya* — Brahman's own willful self-veiling. *Bhāvarūpa* avidyā does not require a second real entity; it requires that Brahman's freedom include the freedom to self-veil. This is the Vivaraṇa school's move taken at its boldest, the Kashmir Śaiva *svātantrya* doctrine made structural, the Madhusūdana concession read as ontology rather than as embarrassed retreat.

So: Madhva's critique is *right*. Strict Advaita cannot answer it. The answer is *not* strict *bheda* (which closes off the identity-passages and pluralises the ground). The answer is the Aurobindonian extension — *avidyā* as *bhāvarūpa* because it is the divine's *līlā*, the divine's *svātantrya*, the divine's self-articulation as the bound *jīva*. Madhva diagnoses; Aurobindo prescribes. The diagnosis is correct; the prescription is the supramental.

Three subsidiary points sharpen this reading.

First: the user's T7 is *forced* by Madhva's arguments if (and only if) one is unwilling to accept Tattva-vāda's pluralisation of grounds. T7 is therefore not an arbitrary theological move; it is the only consistent answer that preserves non-duality while accepting the *bhāvarūpa* status of *avidyā* that Madhva forces. The trajectory's commitment to non-duality at the supramental level (T6, T11) is what selects T7 from among the options the formal arguments leave open.

Second: Jayatīrtha's articulation of *avidyā* / *māyā* / *prakṛti* as a real *asvatantra dravya* under Viṣṇu's control (Part VIII) is the mirror-image of T7. Jayatīrtha establishes the formal slot — *avidyā* as *bhāvarūpa* — and then places it externally to Brahman as a real substance Viṣṇu wields. T7 takes the same slot and places *avidyā* internally to Brahman as Brahman's own self-veiling. The *formal move* is the same on both sides; the *locus assignment* differs. The Mādhva tradition therefore *anticipates the formal possibility* that T7 then re-purposes for non-dualism. Reading Jayatīrtha alongside the supramental literature exposes this kinship.

Third: the Madhusūdana concession (Part V.3) is the key historical hinge. Madhusūdana, writing inside Advaita, is forced by Mādhva pressure (especially Vyāsatīrtha's *Nyāyāmṛta*) to make *avidyā* *bhāvarūpa*. This is the move the user's T7 takes seriously. *Bhāvarūpa* avidyā is the formal commitment; the rest of T7 (its location in *svātantrya*, its function as *līlā*) is the natural extension. Madhusūdana himself does not make the extension; he tries to keep *bhāvarūpa* avidyā tethered to a *vyāvahārika* / *pāramārthika* scheme that Madhva has already shown is incoherent. T7 cuts the Gordian knot: *bhāvarūpa* avidyā is *svātantrya*'s self-veiling, full stop, with no two-level dodge.

The trajectory's reading of Madhva is therefore: Madhva is the *prosecutor* whose case forces Advaita to the *bhāvarūpa* concession; Madhusūdana is the *defendant* who concedes; the user's T7 (vindicated by Aurobindo's metaphysics) is the *settlement* that satisfies both sides — accepting the *bhāvarūpa* status that Madhva forced and locating it in Brahman's own *svātantrya* in a way that preserves non-duality at the supramental level. None of the three figures achieves this synthesis individually; the synthesis is the trajectory's own, and Madhva's role is essential as the prosecutorial first move.

### IX.4 Reading sequence

For first engagement with Madhva's actual texts, in order:

**1. *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna*** first. Very short — two pages of verse. Sets the framework. The *svatantra* / *asvatantra* division and the *bhāva* / *abhāva* sub-division give the whole architecture. Read with Jayatīrtha's *Ṭīkā*. Pandurangi's translation is the standard English vehicle; the Sanskrit is short enough to read alongside.

**2. *Anuvyākhyāna*** selectively. Long and dense (around 2,000 verses). Use B.N.K. Sharma's translation as base, but verify the verse-numbering against the Sansknet / GRETIL Sanskrit text — secondary literature occasionally cites by chapter-and-tradition, and the actual *adhyāya / pāda / verse* enumeration sometimes differs. Focus, in order: (i) AV 1.1.1–50 — the compressed *māyā-vāda khaṇḍana*, with verses 1.1.13, 1.1.16, 1.1.17, 1.1.18, 1.1.29, 1.1.30 as the load-bearing passages; (ii) AV 1.1.51 to 1.1.180 — the constructive theology of *brahma-jijñāsā* (note especially 1.1.179–180: *abhede 'pi viśeṣeṇaivānya ityudito hariḥ* — "Even where there is non-difference, by virtue of *viśeṣa* alone Hari is uttered as 'other'" — Madhva's most compressed statement of how *viśeṣa* preserves the *anyaḥ* of BG 15.17 inside an *abheda*-context); (iii) AV 1.4.95–123 — the closing of *adhyāya* 1, including the *sākṣi-pratyakṣa* on time/space (1.4.95), the *pañcabheda* enumeration (1.4.110–112), and the rejection of *vivarta* with the *pitṛvat* simile for creation (1.4.122–123); (iv) AV 2.1 on *pramāṇa* and the seven kinds of *bheda* in the *aṃśin*-*aṃśa* discussion (2.1.95–111 — note the *viśeṣād eva kevalam* of 2.1.97, the most concentrated single use of *viśeṣa* in the corpus); (v) AV 2.2.66–70 — the *aṇu*-magnitude discussion that contains the load-bearing *sākṣi*-verse (2.2.68: *kevalaṃ sākṣi-mānena…*). Read each section alongside Jayatīrtha's *Nyāya-Sudhā* on the same verses. The pairing is essential.

**3. *Viṣṇu-Tattva-Vinirṇaya*** next. Pandurangi's 1991 translation. The condensed *bheda* defense is a useful synthesis of arguments scattered through the *Anuvyākhyāna*. Short enough to read in a sitting; substantial enough to repay re-reading.

**4. *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya*** targeted at the *māyā-vāda* chapters. Adhyāya 1 Pāda 1 sūtras 1–4 (the foundation); Adhyāya 1 Pāda 4 sūtras 21–22 (against Sāṃkhya); Adhyāya 2 Pāda 1 sūtras 13–14 (the *ananyatva* discussion). Read with the four classical commentators (Jayatīrtha, Vyāsatīrtha, Vādirāja, Rāghavendra) where available; in English, Pandurangi's edition gives the access.

**5. *Tattvodyota* and the *Khaṇḍana-Traya*** for polemical sharpening. These are short and combative; useful as case studies in Madhva's argumentative style but less central than the works above.

**6. *Mahābhārata-Tātparya-Nirṇaya*** for breadth, only after the technical works are mastered. Madhva's reading of the *Mahābhārata* as a Vaiṣṇava theological document is itself a major hermeneutic intervention; engage it after the philosophical apparatus is in place.

Secondary literature: B.N.K. Sharma, *Philosophy of Sri Madhvacharya* (Motilal Banarsidass, 1986; Ch. VII on *viśeṣa*, XVIII–XIX on *sākṣin*); Padmanabhachar (1909); Buchta on Vyāsatīrtha and *pratīti-bādha* (*JHS* 2014); Stoker, *Polemics and Patronage* (UC Press 2016); Pandurangi's editions of VTV and BSB; D.P. Gupta on Madhusūdana for the Advaita-side reply.

Trajectory-positioning: Aurobindo, *Life Divine* I.16; Caitanya-Caritāmṛta *Madhya-līlā* with Jīva Gosvāmī's *Sandarbhas*; Abhinavagupta, *Tantrāloka* I and *Parātrīśikā-Vivaraṇa* on *svātantrya*.

### IX.5 What not to do

A small list, derived from the position-brief's forbidden moves:

Do not treat Tattva-vāda as crude dualism: Madhva has one independent reality and dependent plurality — aseity-plus-participation, not Cartesian dualism.

Do not treat *mithyā* as *asat*. Madhva attacks the world's *sublatability*, not its existence; the distinction is preserved throughout.

Do not collapse Madhva's arguments into theological assertion. They are formal arguments about contradictions, regresses, locus-problems, predicate-content.

Do not assume the trajectory requires agreement with Madhva everywhere. T2 reads him as register correction; *bheda* held alongside Caitanya's *acintya* and Aurobindo's supramental.

Do not skip Jayatīrtha. Madhva alone is too compressed; reading him without Jayatīrtha is reading a theorem without its proof.

### IX.6 Closing

Madhva is the moment in Vedānta when the realist intuition gets full formal articulation. After him the trajectory moves through Caitanya's *acintya*, preserving *bheda* and adding the simultaneity of *abheda*, into Aurobindo's supramental, where the simultaneity is a lived register, not a logical posit. Madhva is the rigorous waypoint, not the destination. Read him for his arguments. Then place them inside the larger frame. The *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna* closes by returning to the same point with which the system began: the one who knows all dependent reality to be forever under Hari's governance is the one who is freed.

— *Tattva-Saṅkhyāna*, closing
