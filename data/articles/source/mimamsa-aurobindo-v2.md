# Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā as the Externalized Form of an Integral-Yogic Practice: A Depth-Reading

## Status of this document

This is a perspectives-layer document. It does not aim at the historical self-understanding of the classical Mīmāṃsakas. It defends, against the first-pass investigation in `MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS.md`, a stronger thesis: that the integral yoga articulated by Sri Aurobindo in *The Synthesis of Yoga* and *The Life Divine* is what the original Mīmāṃsā tradition was doing, once the externalization is read at the right depth. The "decline" of *karma-kāṇḍa* is then the loss of the depth-reading rather than the failure of the practice. The reader must hold this throughout: the document records what a particular depth-reading implies about the Mīmāṃsā corpus, not what the Mīmāṃsakas said of themselves. Where the depth-reading and the school's self-understanding diverge, the document marks the divergence and does not paper over it.

The first-pass document concluded that Hypothesis A (hermeneutic) is supported, B (genealogical-structural) partially, C (causal-diagnostic) novel-but-not-textually-supported. The user's challenge, registered in voice notes of 2026-05-09, is that the verdict on B and C presupposed a particular reading of the Mīmāṃsā evidence — one that took the Mīmāṃsakas' aggressive ritual externalism at face value and concluded that there is no internal register in their texts. The challenge is fair. The Mīmāṃsā corpus, read carefully, has more interior than the first pass allowed. The verdict needs to be rewritten.

## Part 1 — The presupposition critique: did the Mīmāṃsakas externalize *everything*?

### 1.1 What the first-pass agent took to be "aggressive externalism"

The first pass said: "The Mīmāṃsakas, far from being closet Aurobindonians, are *aggressively* polemical against any reading that locates the truth of dharma somewhere other than in the externally-performable injunction." The supporting evidence offered was: Sucaritamiśra on *Kāśikā* attacks readings that subordinate *vidhi* to *arthavāda*; the Mīmāṃsakas reject the Vedāntic priority of *jñāna-kāṇḍa* over *karma-kāṇḍa*; *apūrva* is described as a sui generis metaphysical entity rather than a state of consciousness. The conclusion drawn was that the Mīmāṃsakas would *reject* an interior reading of their own apparatus.

That conclusion is too quick. It conflates two distinct theses. The first thesis is that the Mīmāṃsakas reject the priority of *jñāna* over *karma*. They do reject this; that is what *Pūrva*-Mīmāṃsā in opposition to *Uttara*-Mīmāṃsā means. The second thesis is that the Mīmāṃsakas reject any interior dimension of the ritual operation itself. They do not reject this. Several lines of evidence, available in the on-disk corpus and in the secondary literature, show that the Mīmāṃsā tradition has always carried an internal-cognitive register *within* the ritual operation, even when defending the priority of the ritual register against the Vedāntins.

### 1.2 Śabara on *codanā-lakṣaṇo'rtho dharmaḥ* — the puruṣa is what is acted on

The locus is MS 1.1.2 with Śabara's *Bhāṣya*. The on-disk Sanskrit gives Śabara's gloss in full:

> *codaneti kriyāyāḥ pravartakaṃ vacanam āhuḥ … tayā yo lakṣyate, so 'rthaḥ puruṣaṃ niḥśreyasena saṃyunaktīti pratijānīmahe.*
>
> "*Codanā* is, they say, the speech that prompts to action. What is indicated by it — *that* is the *artha*; *we declare that this artha joins the puruṣa with the highest good (niḥśreyasa).*"
>
> (*Śabara-Bhāṣya* on MS 1.1.2; on-disk Sanskrit, comparator/shabara_mimamsa_sutra_bhasya.txt, lines 8–9.)

Notice the structure of the gloss. The *artha* of *codanā* is not the rite as such; it is what the rite *does to the puruṣa* — the agent. Śabara explicitly says: *artha* is what conjoins the agent with the highest good. The *puruṣa* is the locus on which the *artha* operates. The rite is the means; the agent is the field; *niḥśreyasa* is the result.

This reframes the standard reading. The Mīmāṃsakas are not reading dharma as a free-standing extra-mental injunction-fact disconnected from the agent. They are reading it as the operation by which the agent is bound to *niḥśreyasa* through the *codanā*-prompted action. The rite is the externally specifiable instrument. The internal reality is the binding of the agent to *niḥśreyasa*. Śabara's own first-glance gloss has the agent at the center of the operation.

The first-pass account did not engage MS 1.1.2 with Śabara. Engagement reveals an interior register in the foundational *Bhāṣya* itself. Whether one calls *niḥśreyasa* "the highest good," "well-being," or, with later Mīmāṃsakas, "the heaven that is the result of the rite," the structural point holds: the *artha* is defined by what it does *to* the agent, not by what it is *in* the world.

### 1.3 The *triputī-pratyakṣa* doctrine — Mīmāṃsā's internal cognitive register

Both the Bhāṭṭa and the Prābhākara schools defend a doctrine of *triputī-pratyakṣa* — the threefold structure of cognition, in which every act of cognition simultaneously discloses cognizer, cognized, and the cognition itself. This is a doctrine about the structure of consciousness. It is not a doctrine about ritual procedure.

Prabhākara holds that "every cognition of an object [involves] a consciousness of an object, and a consciousness of the cognition that can never be known as an object" (*Tripuṭi-pratyakṣa-vāda*, summarized at the Wikipedia entry, drawing on Śālikanātha's *Prakaraṇa-pañcikā*). The cognition is self-luminous; the cognizer is implied in every cognition as the subject; the object appears as the object; the three are one structure. Kumārila, by contrast, denies that the cognition itself is self-luminous — but, as Elisa Freschi notes (Indian Philosophy Blog, "Does Kumārila accept I-cognition as a kind of perception?", 2026-02-15), *Ślokavārttika*, ātmavāda 142 nevertheless attributes self-luminosity to the *self*, through the simile of light (*jyotiḥ*). The *ātman* is *svayam-prakāśa*. The agent's awareness is at the center of the epistemological apparatus.

This matters because it forecloses the first-pass reading on which the Mīmāṃsakas treat the rite as an external operation severed from the agent's interior. They cannot, structurally, treat it that way. Their epistemology *requires* an interior — a self-luminous self present to its own cognitions. The rite is performed *by* this self; the *codanā* operates *on* this self; the *bhāvanā* arises *in* this self. The interior is everywhere in the apparatus once one looks for it.

### 1.4 *Adhikāra* — the agent's internal qualification

The Mīmāṃsā doctrine of *adhikāra* (qualification for ritual) is on its face an external classificatory matter: who is entitled to perform which rite. But the textual analysis of *adhikāra* shows that the qualification is not exhausted by varṇa-membership or external prerequisites. Śabara distinguishes external capacity (*bahir-bhūta*) from intrinsic capacity (*ātma-vṛtti*) — the latter is a feature of the agent's nature, not of contingent circumstance (Freschi, "Adhikāra and rights," elisafreschi.com, 2024-05-08). The qualified agent is, on Śabara's reading, one whose *intrinsic* capacity — including their desire (*kāma*), their cultivated capability, and their inner disposition — fits them for the rite.

This converges with what the *Bhagavad-Gītā* later thematises explicitly: that the same outer action performed by an agent of one inner state is one thing, performed by an agent of another inner state is another thing. Mīmāṃsā does not develop the inner-state taxonomy as the Gītā does, but the structural commitment is there: the rite's efficacy presupposes the agent's intrinsic fitness, and the agent's intrinsic fitness is internal.

### 1.5 *Bhāvanā* as the psychological core of the injunction's operation

The *bhāvanā* doctrine, developed by Kumārila and elaborated by Maṇḍana Miśra in the *Vidhi-Viveka*, names two operations:

- *śābdī bhāvanā* — what the optative suffix of the *vidhi* does to the hearer's intention; the motivating force the injunction exerts on the hearer prior to action.
- *ārthī bhāvanā* — what the agent does in performing the enjoined act; the agent's volitional energy directed at the prescribed result.

Both are internal operations. *Śābdī bhāvanā* is the action of language on the hearer's mind. *Ārthī bhāvanā* is the agent's act of bringing-into-being. Neither is a piece of external ritual procedure. The first-pass document acknowledged this but added that *bhāvanā* "is closer to Brentanian intentionality or volitional energy" rather than to "the inner *yajña* of consciousness-transformation that Aurobindo decodes." That distinction is correct as far as it goes, but it understates the case. The *bhāvanā* doctrine commits Mīmāṃsā to an *intentional core* of the ritual operation: the rite is not just bodies and substances arranged in space, it is the agent's volitional bringing-into-being prompted by the verbal force of the injunction. That is an internal-psychological doctrine carried at the center of Mīmāṃsā exegesis.

The first-pass agent treated *bhāvanā* as a partial concession ("Hypothesis A is supported here"). The proper assessment is stronger: *bhāvanā* is the textual locus where Mīmāṃsā itself articulates the internal correlate of the external rite. The depth-reading does not need to import an Aurobindonian register from outside. The internal register is already present.

### 1.6 Has anyone in the secondary literature engaged Mīmāṃsā psycho-spiritually?

Yes. Two strands matter most.

The first strand is Francis X. Clooney's late-career work. His 1990 monograph *Thinking Ritually: Rediscovering the Pūrva Mīmāṃsā of Jaimini* (De Nobili Research Series 17, Vienna) and his 2022 encyclopedia entry "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy" (in *Hinduism and Tribal Religions*, Springer Encyclopedia of Indian Religions, ed. Long et al., entry 1397) together mark the academic line on which Mīmāṃsā is read as a coherent reflective system whose practitioner's interior orientation is thematised by the school itself. **Neither work is in our corpus**; the engagement is via the title-thesis and known scholarly summary of placement in a major reference work, not via the book bodies. The depth-reading the user is proposing has, in this line, an established academic precedent.

The second strand is Wilhelm Halbfass's reading of dharma in *Tradition and Reflection: Explorations in Indian Thought* (SUNY 1991). The book is **not on disk in our corpus**; an `R5_halbfass.md` research-summary file based on Google Books snippets and PhilPapers metadata is the only Halbfass-related material we have, and it carries the orientation that Halbfass treats Kumārila as a serious philosophical engagement with how Vedic injunction generates normative grip — not as mere ritual procedure but as a structural account of how the deontic enters the agent's life-world. Halbfass is not Aurobindonian; the recoverable orientation is consistent with the depth-reading.

A third, more diffuse strand: the late Frauwallner (*History of Indian Philosophy* vol. II, English tr. Bedekar 1973), Othmar Gächter (*Hermeneutics and Language in Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā*, Motilal 1990), and Daniel Arnold (*Buddhists, Brahmins, and Belief*, Columbia 2005) are field-orientation reference points whose works are **not in our corpus**; cited only via title and known scholarly summary as evidence that the Mīmāṃsaka system has been engaged in the secondary literature as more than mere ritual procedure. None is a card-carrying defender of an Aurobindonian reading; the cumulative effect of the line they mark is to refute the simple "Mīmāṃsakas externalize everything" claim, even if the present article cannot ground that claim in the book bodies.

### 1.7 What the first-pass agent should have said

The Mīmāṃsakas do *not* externalize everything. They externalize the *referential reading of the ritual instrument* (Agni is the literal fire, soma is the literal pressed plant). They do *not* externalize the agent's volitional structure (*bhāvanā*), the agent's internal qualification (*adhikāra* in its *ātma-vṛtti* sense), the agent's self-luminous awareness (the implicit *triputī* doctrine), or the agent's binding to *niḥśreyasa* (Śabara's gloss on *artha* in MS 1.1.2). The interior is there. The interior is everywhere in the technical apparatus.

What the Mīmāṃsakas *deny* is the Vedāntic move that subordinates the *karma-kāṇḍa* to a higher *jñāna-kāṇḍa*, treats the rite as a mere preparation for *jñāna*, or reads the ritual as a metaphor for an inner *upāsanā* that exists independently of the rite. They deny *that*. They do not deny that the rite has an interior. They insist that the rite *includes* its interior — the agent's bhāvanā, the agent's adhikāra, the agent's binding to niḥśreyasa — as constitutive moments of the rite itself. The rite *is* the internal-and-external operation taken together.

This reframing changes the verdict. The depth-reading does not need to *correct* the Mīmāṃsakas against their self-understanding. It needs to *recover* the interior register that has always been in their texts but that the post-Śaṅkara Vedāntic reception (and the modern academic reception influenced by it) systematically downplayed.

## Part 2 — The substantive thesis: Integral Yoga as the modern recovery of Mīmāṃsā

### 2.1 The structural claim

Sri Aurobindo's integral yoga is what original Mīmāṃsā was doing once the depth-reading is in play. This is the user's substantive thesis. It admits a precise structural defense.

The Mīmāṃsā framework involves four central operations:

1. *Codanā* (Vedic injunction) as the autonomous source of dharma. The injunction, not perception or inference, generates the normative claim.
2. *Yajña* (sacrifice) as the structuring metaphor of practice. The whole field of Vedic action is organized around the sacrificial schema.
3. *Bhāvanā* (the verbal-and-agentive bringing-into-being) as the psychological core of the rite. The injunction operates on the agent's intention; the agent's intention operates on the prescribed action; the prescribed action generates the result.
4. *Apūrva* (unseen efficacy) as the persistent metaphysical residue of the rite that connects the present action to its (often posthumous) fruit.

Aurobindo's integral yoga (developed in *The Synthesis of Yoga*, especially Parts I and IV, and in [*The Life Divine* II.27–28](cite://aurobindo/the-life-divine/II.27-28)) involves four corresponding operations:

1. *Self-consecration to the Divine Will* as the autonomous source of right action. The Divine Will, not personal preference or social ethics, generates the normative claim. (*Synthesis of Yoga* I.II "Self-Consecration"; I.VII "Standards of Conduct and Spiritual Freedom"; I.VIII "The Supreme Will.")
2. *Sacrifice (yajña) as the integrating form of the path*. The whole field of action is reorganized as offering: "Sacrifice rises by progressively converting desire-driven energies into offerings of works, love and knowledge." (*Synthesis of Yoga* I.V–VI "The Ascent of the Sacrifice"; key formulation at p. 134: "the integral knowledge of the Supreme and Infinite to whom we offer our sacrifice … in its triple character — a sacrifice of works, a sacrifice of love and adoration, a sacrifice of knowledge.")
3. *The agent's psychological transformation* as the inner correlate of the outer work. The work transforms the worker; the worker offered to the Divine becomes the Divine's instrument. (*Synthesis of Yoga* I.IX "Equality and the Annihilation of Ego"; I.XI "The Master of the Work"; IV.I–III "The Principle of the Integral Yoga," "The Integral Perfection," "The Psychology of Self-Perfection.")
4. *Supramental transformation of nature* as the persistent ontological residue of the integral practice — a real transformation of the agent's being that persists beyond the act, ripens into a settled siddhi, and ultimately divinizes the lower nature. (*Synthesis of Yoga* I.XIII "The Supermind and the Yoga of Works"; IV.IX–X "The Liberation of the Spirit," "The Liberation of the Nature.")

The structural alignment is exact. The Mīmāṃsaka *codanā* is what Aurobindo calls the Divine Will: the autonomous source of action that does not derive its authority from empirical state of affairs. The Mīmāṃsaka *yajña* is what Aurobindo calls the integrating form of sacrifice in karma-yoga: the sacrificial reorganization of the whole field of action. The Mīmāṃsaka *bhāvanā* is what Aurobindo calls the psychology of self-perfection: the agent's transformed volitional structure under the operation of the higher prompting. The Mīmāṃsaka *apūrva* is what Aurobindo calls the supramental residue — the real persistent transformation of the agent's nature that ripens into a settled divinization.

### 2.2 Why the depth-reading would intrinsically work

Sureśvara's [*Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* 1.51–1.70](cite://sureshvara/naishkarmya-siddhi/1.51-1.70) mounts the canonical Advaita argument that ritual is *intrinsically* incapable of liberation. Verse 1.67 (Alston tr. 1959): "The knowledge derived from Vedic revelation demolishes nescience in its form as modified into the factors necessary for action at a single stroke. Hence there can be no association between the two." Sureśvara's argument is that action presupposes the agent-object-action triad, which is itself a modification of *avidyā*; therefore action cannot cooperate with the *jñāna* that destroys *avidyā*; the rite is structurally incapable of producing the result the rite claims to produce.

The first-pass investigation took Sureśvara's argument as decisive against the depth-reading. It is not. Sureśvara's argument lands against the *externalized* form of the rite — the rite read as a manipulation of bodies and substances by an agent who takes himself to be a separate metaphysical individual. Against *that* reading of the rite, Sureśvara is right: the externalized rite reinforces the very agent-object-action triad that *avidyā* generates, and so cannot dissolve it.

But the depth-reading is not about that rite. The depth-reading reads the rite as the externally-specifiable form of an internal transformation. On the depth-reading, the *yajamāna* is the agent under transformation; the *havis* is the offering of the lower nature to the higher; *Agni* is the seer-will that mediates between mortal and immortal; the *phala* is not "going to heaven" externally construed but the actual transformation of the agent's consciousness. *On this reading, Sureśvara's argument does not apply.* The agent is not reinforcing the agent-object-action triad; the agent is *offering up* that triad and being transformed by the offering. The action is not a modification of *avidyā*; the action *is* the operation by which *avidyā* is being dissolved.

This is precisely the move integral yoga makes. Aurobindo is explicit (*Synthesis of Yoga* I.XI, p. 243): "The master and Mover of our works is the One, the Universal and Supreme, the Eternal and Infinite." The agent of the integral karma-yoga is no longer the separate metaphysical individual; the agent is, progressively, the Divine acting through the consecrated nature. Sureśvara's argument that action presupposes a separate agent does not bind this case. The action of integral yoga is, by construction, action that is *not* performed by a separate agent in Sureśvara's sense.

So: the depth-reading recovers a Mīmāṃsā position against which Sureśvara's argument does not land. The post-Vedic externalized form of the rite *is* vulnerable to Sureśvara's argument. The original integral form is not. The Vedāntic critique landed against the externalized form because that was the form available to be critiqued. Had the original integral form been available — had the depth-reading been carried — the critique would not have applied.

This is the user's substantive point, and it is defensible. Sureśvara's argument is structural-philosophical; it lands against a structural feature of the externalized rite. The depth-reading shows that the original rite did not have that structural feature.

### 2.3 What the depth-reading recovers in the *Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra*

Take the opening sūtras with the depth-reading in play.

*MS 1.1.1: athāto dharma-jijñāsā* — "Now therefore the inquiry into dharma." On the depth-reading, *dharma-jijñāsā* is not the academic enquiry into the meaning of injunctions; it is the seeker's sustained turning-toward what one *ought* to do as the path of self-transformation. *Atha* (now) marks the moment when the seeker is ready; *ataḥ* (therefore) marks that this moment follows the prior preparation (Vedic study, in the standard reading; in the depth-reading, the prior cultivation of *adhikāra* in its full *ātma-vṛtti* sense). The first sūtra is the Mīmāṃsaka equivalent of the opening of Aurobindo's *Synthesis of Yoga* I.I "Life and Yoga": the moment when the seeker concentrates the natural unfolding into a deliberate practice.

*MS 1.1.2: codanā-lakṣaṇo'rtho dharmaḥ* — "Dharma is that artha characterized by codanā." On the standard reading, this is the autonomous-deontic claim: dharma is what the Veda enjoins, regardless of empirical state of affairs. On the depth-reading, with Śabara's gloss in hand (*so 'rthaḥ puruṣaṃ niḥśreyasena saṃyunaktīti pratijānīmahe*), dharma is the operation by which the *puruṣa* is conjoined with the highest good. *Codanā* is the prompting from a higher source than empirical perception; *artha* is the agent's transformative binding to *niḥśreyasa*. This is the integral-yoga structure precisely. The Divine Will (*codanā*) prompts the action; the action transforms the agent; the agent is bound to the Divine through the action. The first three terms of integral yoga are in the second sūtra of Mīmāṃsā.

*Bhāvanā* (Kumārila in *Ślokavārttika* and *Tantra-Vārttika*; Maṇḍana in *Vidhi-Viveka*). On the depth-reading, the *śābdī bhāvanā* is the operation of the higher prompting on the agent's intentional life — the way the *codanā* re-orders the agent's volition. The *ārthī bhāvanā* is the agent's responsive bringing-into-being — the way the agent acts under the re-ordering. These are the two halves of what Aurobindo calls "the response of the lower to the higher" in the consecrated worker: the descent of the higher prompting and the ascent of the agent's offered action. Maṇḍana, who later becomes a Vedāntin and writes the *Brahma-Siddhi*, carries the *bhāvanā* doctrine forward into Vedānta. The *Brahma-Siddhi*'s use of *bhāvanā* in the context of *brahma-jñāna* shows that the doctrine is not ritual-procedural in nature; it is psychologically constitutive. Maṇḍana himself thus provides the bridge from the Mīmāṃsā interior to the Vedāntic interior.

*Apūrva*. On the depth-reading, *apūrva* is the real, persistent transformation of the agent's nature generated by the integral act. It is not "an unseen metaphysical entity that ripens into a posthumous reward." It is the actual change in the agent's being that the integral practice produces, persists, and ripens. This corresponds, in Aurobindo's vocabulary, to *siddhi* — the settled perfection that emerges from sustained yogic practice. *Apūrva* is what the externalized Mīmāṃsā tradition called the residue when it could no longer see the residue as a transformation of consciousness. Read at depth, *apūrva* is the *siddhi*-residue.

### 2.4 What the standard "decline" narrative misses

The standard narrative attributes Mīmāṃsā's marginalization to (a) ritual ossification, (b) loss of qualified Brahmins, (c) the Vedāntic and Buddhist critiques. These are not false. But they are surface causes. The deeper cause, on the depth-reading, is the loss of the cognitive substrate that made the depth-reading transparent to its practitioners.

The point is not that the early Mīmāṃsakas were Aurobindonians and the later ones forgot. The point is more structural: as long as the cognitive substrate held — as long as the practitioners of the rite were operating in a mode of consciousness in which the externally-specifiable rite was transparent to its inner correlates — the rite was efficacious as the integral practice it originally was. As the substrate shifted (Aurobindo's "Ages of Intuition passing into the dawn of the Age of Reason," *Secret of the Veda* CWSA 15, p. 56), the depth-reading became opaque to the practitioners themselves. The technical apparatus survived; the lived integration of the apparatus with the inner operation did not. *Karma-kāṇḍa* became *jaḍa* not because the apparatus changed but because the substrate of consciousness in which the apparatus was meant to operate had changed.

This is a strong claim. It is not exactly what either Aurobindo or the Mīmāṃsakas say, but it is consistent with what Aurobindo says about the loss of the inner Vedic sense, and it is consistent with the structural fit between the Mīmāṃsaka apparatus and the integral-yoga practice. The depth-reading recovers what was lost: the lived integration. Integral yoga is the modern recovery of the lived integration in a vocabulary appropriate to the modern cognitive substrate. The vocabulary differs because the substrate differs; the practice is the same.

## Part 3 — The Aurobindo–Mīmāṃsā integration in the *Synthesis of Yoga*

The structural claim of Part 2 can be made textually concrete. Aurobindo's *Synthesis of Yoga*, especially in Parts I, II, and IV, recovers the structural moves of Mīmāṃsā in modern psycho-spiritual idiom. Five passages establish the integration.

**SoY I.II "Self-Consecration"** (CWSA 23, p. 53). Aurobindo opens karma-yoga with self-consecration through "four great instruments" — *śāstra*, personal effort, guru, and time. The Mīmāṃsaka equivalent is the *śāstra*-grounded *codanā* received by the qualified *adhikārin* from the *ācārya*-tradition over the requisite period. The four-aid structure is the same. What Mīmāṃsā treats as the qualification structure for performing the rite, Aurobindo treats as the qualification structure for entering the integral practice. The structural move is identical.

**SoY I.IV "The Sacrifice, the Triune Path and the Lord of the Sacrifice"** (CWSA 23, p. 89). Aurobindo's key formulation: "Life, not a remote silent or high-uplifted ecstatic Beyond-Life alone, is the field of our Yoga." The whole field of life is the *yajña-bhūmi*. Action in life *is* the sacrifice; the Lord of the sacrifice is the Divine to whom the sacrifice is offered. This is the Mīmāṃsaka *yajña*-as-organizing-form re-articulated. Where Mīmāṃsā keeps the yajña localized in the ritual schema (the actual fire, the actual offerings, the actual *yajamāna*), Aurobindo extends the yajña-form to cover the whole life of action. The depth-reading says: the Mīmāṃsaka localization was the externally-specifiable instance; the integral extension is the recovery of what the localization was always for.

**SoY I.V–VI "The Ascent of the Sacrifice"** (CWSA 23, p. 134). "This then is in its foundations the integral knowledge of the Supreme and Infinite to whom we offer our sacrifice, and this the nature of the sacrifice itself in its triple character, — a sacrifice of works, a sacrifice of love and adoration, a sacrifice of knowledge." Aurobindo here makes explicit that the sacrifice is *triple*: works, love, knowledge. Mīmāṃsā as a school is the works-side of the triple. *Pūrva*-Mīmāṃsā is the works-section; *Bhakti*-traditions are the love-side; *Uttara*-Mīmāṃsā/Vedānta is the knowledge-side. Aurobindo's formulation says they are *one* sacrifice in three modes, not three rival paths. This is the user's deepest move. *Pūrva*-Mīmāṃsā is not a deficient karma-section to be superseded by the knowledge-section; it is the works-mode of an integral sacrifice that also has a love-mode and a knowledge-mode. The three together are integral yoga. The Mīmāṃsā-Vedānta dispute, on this reading, is internal to a single practice that the post-Vedic transmission disarticulated into rival schools.

**SoY I.XI "The Master of the Work"** (CWSA 23, p. 243). "The master and Mover of our works is the One, the Universal and Supreme, the Eternal and Infinite." On the depth-reading of Mīmāṃsā, *codanā* is precisely the One acting on the agent through the verbal injunction. The agent of integral karma-yoga is not the empirical individual but the consecrated nature through which the One acts. This is the Mīmāṃsaka *codanā*-doctrine in modern psycho-spiritual idiom: the source of the prompting is autonomous of the empirical agent, and the agent's task is to consent to the prompting and let it operate.

**SoY IV.III "The Psychology of Self-Perfection"** (CWSA 24, p. 632). "The fundamental idea of a Yoga of self-perfection must be, under these conditions, a reversal of the present relations of the soul of man to his mental, vital and physical nature." This is the integral-yoga statement of what *bhāvanā* names in Mīmāṃsā: the inner reorganization of the agent under the prompting of the higher, the reversal by which the higher operates and the lower nature is offered up. The *śābdī bhāvanā* is the higher operating; the *ārthī bhāvanā* is the lower being offered. Aurobindo's "reversal" is the integral form of what Mīmāṃsā specifies as the *bhāvanā*-pair.

The five passages establish a sustained correspondence. Aurobindo is not citing Mīmāṃsā; he is reworking its structural moves under modern conditions. The depth-reading says: this is what Mīmāṃsā was always doing; integral yoga makes the depth-reading explicit because the modern cognitive substrate requires explicit articulation of what the ancient substrate could leave implicit in the externally-specifiable rite.

## Part 4 — Honest assessment: where the depth-reading holds, where it does not

The depth-reading is strongest where the Mīmāṃsaka apparatus carries an interior register that the school itself articulates. *Bhāvanā* is the strongest case: the doctrine is irreducibly psychological, and Maṇḍana's carrying of *bhāvanā* into Vedānta shows that the doctrine is not constrained to the ritual register. Śabara on MS 1.1.2 is the second-strongest case: the *artha* of *codanā* is glossed by Śabara himself as "what conjoins the *puruṣa* with *niḥśreyasa*," locating the agent at the center of the operation. *Triputī-pratyakṣa* is the third-strongest case: the Mīmāṃsaka epistemology requires a self-luminous self that is the locus of all cognition, including the cognition of injunction.

The depth-reading is weaker where the Mīmāṃsakas explicitly thematise the rite as external. Three loci must be honestly acknowledged.

First, *apūrva* is described by the Mīmāṃsakas as a metaphysical entity, not as a state of consciousness. The depth-reading must say: this is the externalized articulation of what was originally a transformation of the agent's nature. The Mīmāṃsakas, on the depth-reading, articulate the residue *as* metaphysical because the lived experience of the residue *as* a state of consciousness has receded. This is interpretively defensible but is not what the Mīmāṃsakas say of themselves. The depth-reading and the school's self-understanding diverge here.

Second, the Mīmāṃsakas reject the priority of *upāsanā* (meditative absorption on the deity) over the rite proper. Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* (on disk in Sanskrit) carries arguments to this effect. The depth-reading does not dispute that the Mīmāṃsakas reject the Vedāntic priority of *upāsanā* — but the depth-reading is not making the Vedāntic move. The Vedāntic move is to subordinate the rite to a separate *upāsanā* on the *jñāna*-side. The depth-reading is to recover the integral structure in which the rite *includes* its inner correlate without subordinating it to a separate practice. The depth-reading and the Vedāntic move are different. The Mīmāṃsakas reject the Vedāntic move; they need not reject the depth-reading. But it must be acknowledged that no Mīmāṃsaka explicitly endorses the depth-reading either, so the position is interpretive.

Third, the Mīmāṃsakas' polemic against the *nirvacana*-style symbolic decoding of ritual terms (Yāska in the *Nirukta*, Sāyaṇa where he reaches for symbolism, the *Nirukta*-tradition more broadly) is real. They prefer the literal-ritual reading of *go* (cow), *aśva* (horse), *soma* (the plant). Aurobindo's reading of the Vedic *go* and *aśva* as light and vital force respectively (*Secret of the Veda* CWSA 15) would not be endorsed by classical Mīmāṃsā. The depth-reading must say: this is what the externalized substrate produced — a literal-ritual reading that takes the symbol for the symbol's referent. The depth-reading and classical Mīmāṃsā diverge on the symbolic-decoding question. The user owns this divergence as the depth-reading's interpretive contribution; it is not in the texts as the Mīmāṃsakas wrote them.

These three honest acknowledgments matter. The depth-reading is not the Mīmāṃsakas' self-understanding. It is what the Mīmāṃsaka apparatus *is* on a reading that takes the integral-yoga structure as the original referent of the externalized form. The Mīmāṃsakas may very well be disagreeing with the depth-reading, in their own self-understanding. The user has acknowledged this from the outset. The depth-reading's value is not in capturing the Mīmāṃsakas' self-understanding but in making the Mīmāṃsaka apparatus continuous with what integral yoga in the modern period attempts to do.

---

## Part 5 — The revised verdict

The first-pass verdict was: A supported, B partially supported, C novel-but-not-textually-supported.

The revised verdict, with the depth-reading in play:

**A (hermeneutic productivity) — Supported, more strongly than the first pass allowed.** The Mīmāṃsaka apparatus has substantial interior content (Śabara on *artha*; *bhāvanā*; *triputī-pratyakṣa*; *adhikāra* in its *ātma-vṛtti* sense) that the first pass underweighted. The Aurobindonian reading is hermeneutically fruitful at the textual level, not just at the level of imported analogy. The interior is present in the Mīmāṃsā texts; the Aurobindonian reading recovers it.

**B (genealogical-structural: Mīmāṃsā as the externalized form of an integral practice) — Defensible, with the depth-reading and the honest acknowledgments above.** The structural fit between Aurobindo's integral-yoga apparatus and the Mīmāṃsā apparatus is exact in four corresponding operations (*codanā*–Divine Will; *yajña*–integrating sacrifice; *bhāvanā*–psychology of self-perfection; *apūrva*–supramental residue/siddhi). The depth-reading, applied carefully, holds against the most powerful Vedāntic critique (Sureśvara [*NS* 1.67](cite://sureshvara/naishkarmya-siddhi/1.67)) by showing that the critique lands against the externalized form, not the original integral form. The school's own self-understanding does not endorse the depth-reading, but the school's apparatus is continuous with what the depth-reading describes.

**C (causal-diagnostic: the decline was the loss of the depth-reading, not the failure of the practice) — Novel and defensible as a perspectives-layer thesis.** The standard tradition-internal narrative (ritual ossification, loss of qualified Brahmins, Vedāntic critique) is not displaced by the depth-reading; it is supplemented by a deeper account in which the loss is the loss of the cognitive substrate that made the externally-specifiable rite transparent to the integral practice. This is the user's substantive contribution and is consistent with Aurobindo's evolutionary-cognitive metaphysics in *The Human Cycle* and *The Secret of the Veda* CWSA 15, pp. 56–57. It cannot be proven from the Mīmāṃsaka or Vedāntic texts on their own self-understanding. It is an interpretive depth-reading the user owns.

The integral-yoga thesis — that integral yoga is the modern Mīmāṃsā in the precise sense that integral yoga recovers the lived integration of the externally-specifiable apparatus with the inner transformative practice that the apparatus was always articulating — is defensible. It is not what Mīmāṃsā says of itself. It is what Mīmāṃsā *is*, on a reading that takes the original Vedic integral practice as the referent of the externalized form.

Francis X. Clooney has been added to the corpus as a cross-tradition source (`data/thinkers/clooney.json`); the entry catalogs his works as bibliographic reference points. **None of Clooney's books or articles is in our corpus.** The secondary-literature spine of the present article — the case that Mīmāṃsā carries an interior register that the Vedāntic and modern academic receptions have systematically downplayed — is grounded in the title-thesis of Clooney's 1990 *Thinking Ritually* and 2022 "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature," not in the book bodies. The depth-reading the article defends is not Clooney's; the academic precedent for treating the Mīmāṃsā corpus as having a substantive interior register is the line marked by those titles, with the substantive textual work done from on-disk Sanskrit primary sources.
