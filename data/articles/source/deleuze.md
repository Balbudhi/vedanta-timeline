# Reading Deleuze

*A guided reading session in original French with careful English translation, organized around the question of difference, univocity, and the violence of unification, what Deleuze enables for the user's thesis, where it confirms, and where it must be supplemented.*

## Reader's Orientation

### A bio arc

Cross-engagement: Abhinavagupta presses the same determination axis in the logical-dialectical register, but with the value `self-expression` rather than `difference-without-negation` in [primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

Gilles Deleuze (1925–1995) is, with Foucault and Derrida, one of the three figures whose work defines "French theory" in the second half of the twentieth century. His trajectory is unusual: he did not pass through phenomenology. Where Sartre, Merleau-Ponty, and Levinas formed themselves on Husserl and Heidegger, Deleuze formed himself on Hume, Nietzsche, Bergson, and Spinoza, and on a strand the Sorbonne curriculum had quietly demoted (the Stoics, Duns Scotus, Leibniz, Maïmon). He is not a phenomenologist who turned anti-phenomenological; he never went through the door.

Cross-engagement: Friedrich Nietzsche works the same method axis in the historical-genealogical register. Here the operative value is `genealogy`; Friedrich Nietzsche lands on that same value in [primary text](cite://nietzsche/beyond-good-and-evil/22). The convergence is narrow rather than total: the shared primitive is real, but the wider doctrine still travels under a different architecture.

He began as a historian of philosophy in a peculiar mode, slow careful monographs on figures he loved (*Empirisme et subjectivité* 1953; *Nietzsche et la philosophie* 1962; *La philosophie critique de Kant* 1963; *Le Bergsonisme* 1966; *Spinoza et le problème de l'expression* 1968), and out of those readings he constructed his own philosophy, arriving in two works of 1968–69: *Différence et Répétition* (his thèse principale) and *Logique du sens*. He liked to say that his way of doing history was a kind of *enculage*: he took the philosopher from behind to produce a child monstrously his and yet recognizably the philosopher's. The monographs do not summarize, they inflect.

In 1969 he met Félix Guattari and the two produced *Capitalisme et Schizophrénie*: *L'Anti-Œdipe* (1972) and *Mille Plateaux* (1980), the books that gave us *rhizome*, *machine désirante*, *corps sans organes*, *déterritorialisation*, *agencement*. Late Deleuze returns to monograph form: *Foucault* (1986), *Le Pli: Leibniz et le Baroque* (1988), and the two cinema books (1983, 1985). He died in November 1995, by his own hand, having long suffered from a lung disease. The last essay, "L'immanence : une vie..." (1995), reads as a final crystallization.

### The single thread for the user's thesis

Across the whole career, one thread is continuous, and it is the only thread that matters for the user's project here:

Cross-engagement: Akshapada Gautama presses the same method axis in the methodological register, but with the value `formal-proof-or-inference` rather than `mixed-or-layered` in [primary text](cite://aksapada/nyaya-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

**Difference is not a derivative of identity. Being is said in one and the same sense of everything that is, but the things of which it is said differ, being is said *of difference itself*.**

This is the doctrine of *univocité* (univocity), and it is the structural homologue of the position the user has been building. *One Real, said in many senses.* The darśanas, the planes of consciousness, the registers, the traditions, these are not competing total theories within one homogeneous domain (where logic would force us to pick the winner) and they are not independent worlds (where logic would forbid us to compare them at all). They are differences over which the same single voice of being is said, equally and without analogical gradation. The voice is one. The differences are real. The voice is said *of* the differences, not *despite* them, not *in spite of* them, but *of* them.

This single thread has many local expressions in Deleuze's work, and the parts that follow each pull on one of them:

- In *Différence et répétition*, it is the doctrine of univocity itself, with its three moments (Scotus, Spinoza, Nietzsche) and its critique of the four shackles by which Western philosophy has imprisoned difference.
- In *Mille plateaux*, it is the rhizome, the figure that emerges when univocity is taken into the political and social: a mode of connection that is not arborescent (tree-like, with a trunk that founds and branches that derive) but lateral, multiple, decentered. Deleuze and Guattari's claim is that arborescent thinking *does violence*, it does not merely describe a hierarchical reality; it *produces* hierarchy by suppressing the lateral connections that the rhizome would have made visible. This is the precise concept the user's position needs for the violence-of-unification critique (T11, T12).
- In *Logique du sens*, it is the surface of events, the fourth dimension, sense, that resolves the regress generated when propositions try to specify their own frames.
- In *Spinoza et le problème de l'expression*, it is *expression*, the relation in which the One is genuinely present in the Many without being absorbed into them and without remaining transcendent above them.
- In *Le Pli*, it is the fold, the operation by which interiority and exteriority are produced through one another, with the Leibnizian monad as a perspective rather than a subject having a perspective.
- In *Nietzsche et la philosophie*, it is the eternal return, read not as the return of the same but as the return of difference under selective pressure: only what affirms returns, and what returns is never the identical but always difference itself. This is, finally, Deleuze's deepest thought, the one his entire philosophy circles back to.

Read in this single thread, Deleuze becomes one of the most powerful continental allies for the kind of position the user's thesis defends. *Provided* one is also clear about where Deleuze stops, and what must come from elsewhere, from Aurobindo, from Abhinavagupta, from the Vedāntic realist line, to make the position what it is. The reader's task throughout is double: to take the philosophical apparatus Deleuze provides as fully as he provides it, and to mark, with care, where the apparatus does not go far enough.

### A note on how to read what follows

Each Part takes one major work, keeps the original title and a few indispensable technical terms inline, and gives the argument in English with citations and explication. Translations are based on Patton (*Difference and Repetition*), Massumi (*A Thousand Plateaus*), Lester and Stivale (*The Logic of Sense*), Joughin (*Expressionism in Philosophy: Spinoza*), Conley (*The Fold*), and Tomlinson (*Nietzsche and Philosophy*), with adjustments noted where the English needs tightening.

Cross-engagement: Adi Sankara presses the same soteric end axis in the soteriological register, but with the value `identity-with-ground` rather than `not-soteriological` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

### Key Technical Terms

- **univocité**, univocity. The saying of being in one same sense across all difference. The single ontological proposition.
- **différence**, difference. For Deleuze, primary, productive, in itself; not derivative of identity, not exhausted by opposition or analogy or resemblance.
- **répétition**, repetition. Not return of the same but the very mode in which difference returns; the time-form of difference's productivity.
- **multiplicité**, multiplicity. An n-dimensional figure that is neither One nor Many; that changes nature as its dimensions change; that is held by relations of consistency rather than by an underlying substance.
- **devenir**, becoming. Not transition between fixed states but the primary mode of being itself, of which "states" are local and provisional crystallizations.
- **agencement**, assemblage. A configuration of heterogeneous elements held in consistency, with a content side and an expression side, neither reducible to the other. The ontological unit of *Mille plateaux*.
- **plan d'immanence**, plane of immanence. The surface on which thought operates without transcendent grounding; the philosophical correlate of univocity.
- **plan d'organization**, plane of organization. The hidden plan that organizes diversity from above; the arborescent counter-concept to *plan d'immanence*.
- **plan de consistance**, plane of consistency. The plane of immanence considered as what holds heterogeneous elements together; the rhizomatic alternative to *plan d'organization*.
- **rhizome**, the lateral, multiply-connected, non-arborescent mode of growth. Six characteristics: connection, heterogeneity, multiplicity, asignifying rupture, cartography, decalcomania.
- **déterritorialisation / reterritorialisation**, deterritorialization and reterritorialization. The breaking-loose of an element from its coded territory and its taking-hold elsewhere. Always paired; never absolute on either side except in the limit case.
- **le pli**, the fold. The operation by which interiority and exteriority are produced through each other; the architectural figure of Leibniz's monad.
- **événement**, event. The incorporeal effect at the surface of bodies; the unit of sense; the locus of the eternal return.
- **sens**, sense. The fourth dimension of the proposition; the expressed; the limit at which propositions and things meet without being absorbed into either.
- **force active / force réactive**, active force / reactive force. The Nietzschean distinction Deleuze uses to characterize a body as a relation of forces and modernity as a privileging of the reactive.
- **éternel retour**, eternal return. Not the return of the same but the return of difference; both cosmological doctrine and ethical-selective principle.
- **devenir-imperceptible**, becoming-imperceptible. The limit of *devenir*; the dissolution of the egoic self into the flow of intensities. The Deleuzean counterpart to which the position offers *psychisation* (Aurobindo's psychic deepening) as a different and decisive alternative.

Cross-engagement: Baladeva Vidyabhushana presses the same individuation status axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `irreducible-dependent` rather than `expressive-singularity` in [primary text](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

Note on *différentiation* (with *t*) vs *différenciation* (with *c*): the doublet is technical in *D&R* (virtual differen*t*iation vs. actual differen*c*iation). Where the doublet is not at stake, "difference" without marking is fine.

## Part I — *Différence et Répétition* (1968)

Deleuze's thèse principale, defended 24 January 1969 at the Sorbonne (Gandillac director; Alquié, Canguilhem, Ricœur, Wahl on the jury). *Spinoza et le problème de l'expression* was the complementary thesis the same day. Argument: Western philosophy from Plato through Hegel has subordinated difference to identity, allowing it to appear only as the distance between two things sharing a concept. Real difference, *différence en elle-même*, has not been thought. The book's five chapters are a fugue, each taking up difference-repetition from a different angle: I from being (representation's four shackles); II from time (the three syntheses); III from the image of thought; IV from the differential Idea; V from intensity. Chapter I is the philosophical core; what follows elaborates.

### The univocity formula

Deleuze states the canonical formula early in Chapter I, "La différence en elle-même," after a preparatory discussion of how being has been distributed (analogically by Aristotle, then onward through the scholastics):

*There has only ever been one ontological proposition: Being is univocal.* (PUF p. 52; Patton p. 35)

Univocity is not one ontological position among others to be defended against equivocity and analogy. It is *the* ontological proposition, the one without which there is no ontology, only ontotheology. Two sentences later, the formula proper:

*Being is said in one and the same sense of everything of which it is said, but that of which it is said differs: it is said of difference itself.* (PUF p. 53; Patton p. 36)

Each clause does work. *One and the same sense*, not two senses of "being," one proper to God and a derivative one for creatures (the analogical doctrine of Aristotle through Aquinas). *Of everything of which it is said*, without exception, without categorical hierarchy. *But that of which it is said differs*, the unity of the voice does not impose unity on what is said. *Said of difference itself*, difference is not absorbed into the unity of the voice; it is what the voice is *of*.

The structural fit with the user's thesis is close. *One Real, said in many senses, of which each darśana, each tradition, each register speaks truly.* The registers are not species of one genus and not analogical proportions of one supreme being; they are the differences of which the one voice is said. Advaita at its most stringent (Śaṅkara on *mithyā* as *asat*) makes the differences unreal in the last instance; univocity rejects this. The differences are real *as differences*. This is the continental form of the Aurobindonian-Rāmānujan-Bhāskaran-Madhvan correction *mithyā ≠ asat*.

Just after the formula, Deleuze extends it:

*It is of the essence of univocal being to relate itself to individuating differences, but these differences do not have the same essence, and do not vary the essence of being.* (PUF p. 53; Patton adapted)

The differences are *individuantes*, individuating. They do not divide an already-existing whole into parts; they constitute individuals. And they "do not vary the essence of being", the voice does not modulate as it says one difference rather than another. This is the precise point at which the position diverges from any totalizing monism that would absorb the differences into the One.

A third sentence completes the cluster:

*There is a single 'voice' of Being, which relates itself to all its modes, the most diverse, the most varied, the most differentiated.* (PUF; Patton adapted)

*Une seule voix*, voice, not foundation, not first principle, not common essence. The aural metaphor matters: a voice is heard in its modulations, not in spite of them.

### The two distributions: nomos sédentaire and nomos nomade

Deleuze immediately gives the formal-political articulation of univocity. There are two ways to distribute beings:

*Univocal being is at once nomadic distribution and crowned anarchy.* (PUF; Patton adapted)

Two distributions, opposed term by term:

**Nomos sédentaire**: distribution that implies a partition of the distributed, closed space fixed, lots assigned, place determined by hierarchy of forms. This is analogy, *bon sens*, *sens commun*, what Western metaphysics has been doing.

**Nomos nomade**: distribution that does not partition a closed space but distributes those distributed across an open space without precise limits, *errance et même de "délire"*. *Toutes choses se répartissent en lui dans l'univocité de la simple présence (l'Un-Tout).*

Univocity is *anarchie couronnée*: anarchy as absence of organizing arche, crown as unity of the voice within the anarchic distribution.

*The univocity of being therefore also signifies the equality of being.* (PUF; Patton adapted)

Not democratic equality between beings but *ontological* equality: being is said equally of all that is. Differences in *power* (intensity, capacity, expression) remain real, but being is said univocally of all. This is what T1 needs: darśanas are not on a vertical hierarchy of more-or-less reality; they are equal in being, differing in register and clarity. The Aurobindonian planes are similarly distinguished by *transparency*, not by reality-degree.

### The quadruple carcan

The negative argument of D&R Chapter I is the diagnosis of the four ways philosophy has imprisoned difference. Deleuze names them together:

*The fourfold shackle within which difference can be thought only as that between what is identical, similar, analogous, and opposed.* (PUF p. 180; Patton p. 138)

*Carcan* names the iron collar of a slave or galley convict. Difference is allowed to walk only within the four shackles.

**1. Identité dans le concept**. Difference appears only as the distance between two things falling under one concept (Aristotle's *differentia specifica*). At the level of the categories themselves, being is not a genus, there is no concept above them, so they are linked only by analogy.

**2. Opposition dans le prédicat**. Hegel's contribution: difference becomes contradiction. Difference is made dynamic, but only as the negation of identity, not as itself.

**3. Analogie dans le jugement**. Being is said analogically of the categories, *analogia proportionis*, *analogia attributionis*. Aristotle's solution to "being is not a genus," inherited by Aquinas and the scholastic tradition, echoed in Kant's table.

**4. Ressemblance dans la perception**. Things recognized as the same kind by perceptual resemblance, presupposing a schema. Hume on belief, Locke on ideas operate here.

Four aspects of one operation: subordinating difference to identity, in conceptual, predicative, analogical, and perceptual modes.

The decisive sentence on Hegel:

*Difference does not imply the negative, and does not allow itself to be carried to the point of contradiction, except insofar as one continues to subordinate it to the identical.* (PUF p. 1; Patton adapted)

Hegelian contradiction is difference *domesticated by identity*, difference viewed from inside an identity-frame as what negates identity. Real difference does not need to negate; it simply differs. This lets the position borrow Hegel's *Aufhebung* only for *the climb* (T3), not for the landing: *Aufhebung* names the ascent, while rhizomatic *et... et... et...* names the non-totalizing arrival.

Deleuze names Aristotelian analogy and Hegelian opposition as the two great representations, *grande représentation organique* and *grande représentation orgique*. Hegel appears to liberate difference but only reverses the priority: identity remains the frame; contradiction is difference-within-identity. Deleuze's decisive sentence:

*We say not only that difference in itself is not "already" contradiction, but that it does not let itself be reduced or led back to contradiction, because contradiction is less profound, not more profound, than difference.* (PUF p. 74; my translation, with Patton consulted.)

*Moins profonde, et non pas plus profonde.* Hegel claimed contradiction is more profound than contrariety; Deleuze inverts: contradiction is *less* profound than difference. Contradiction is the shadow difference casts when difference has been "placed by force in a prior identity" (*mise de force dans une identité préalable*, same paragraph). Difference is the positive productive excess from which both identity and negation emerge as secondary formations.

### The three moments of univocity: Scotus, Spinoza, Nietzsche

Deleuze identifies three philosophical moments in which univocity has been thought, each deeper than the last:

*There have been three great moments in the thought of the univocity of being.* (PUF; Patton adapted)

**Duns Scotus** thought univocity *formally and neutrally*: being applies in the same sense to God and creatures, without analogy's equivocation. The *distinctio formalis* and modal univocity carried the doctrine, but it remained abstract, *univocité de l'être, neutre, abstrait*. The univocity was not yet *expressed*; it remained a claim about the concept, not a doctrine about the real.

**Spinoza** made univocity expressive and affirmative:

*An admirable repetition of substance, attributes, and modes [...] univocal being becomes expressive, a genuinely expressive affirmative proposition.* (PUF p. 59; Patton adapted)

Substance expresses itself in attributes; attributes in modes; each finite mode is a center where the infinite expresses itself under a determinate form. Univocity becomes the lived reality of the expressive triad (developed in Part IV). But Spinoza is not yet the third moment: substance still functions as the One *of which* the modes are said; substance remains a kind of identity. Full affirmation requires that the One turn around the Different.

**Nietzsche** is the third and decisive moment:

*Such a condition can be fulfilled only at the price of a more general categorical reversal, according to which being is said of becoming, identity of the different, the one of the multiple, etc. That identity is not first, that it exists as a principle but as a second principle, as a principle become, that it turns around the Different, such is the nature of a Copernican revolution that opens to difference the possibility of its proper concept, instead of maintaining it under the domination of a concept in general posited already as identical.* (PUF p. 59; Patton p. 41 adapted)

*L'être se dit du devenir*, being is said of becoming. *L'identité, du différent*. *L'un, du multiple*. *L'identité n'est pas première*; *comme second principe, comme principe devenu*; *elle tourne autour du Différent*. The primacy that has always belonged to identity is transferred to difference. Identity becomes a stable effect difference produces locally and provisionally. Copernicus put the earth in motion around the sun; Nietzsche puts identity in motion around difference.

This is the philosophical resource for *paramādvaita* / supramental non-duality without identity-monism. The supramental does not flatten registers into one identity; it holds them in irreducible difference, with identity as a stable secondary effect, one register (formless non-dual realization), not the structuring frame.

### Eternal return as return of difference

Deleuze's reading of Nietzsche's eternal return is the key to univocity's full realization:

*The returning is the only Same of what becomes.* (PUF p. 59; Patton p. 41)

The "Same" of becoming is not any particular state that recurs but the recurrence itself. *Le revenir* is the only Same. What returns is never the identical state but always difference under selective pressure.

*[The eternal return] practically operates a selection of differences according to their capacity to produce, that is, to return or to withstand the test of the eternal return.* (PUF p. 60; Patton adapted)

The eternal return is not the return of the identical, Nietzsche's most exoteric formulation, which Deleuze treats as partial. The cosmological doctrine in *Zarathustra* is a *test*: can this be affirmed, willed again, returned as itself without ressentiment? Only the affirmative survives.

*Only the extreme forms return, those which, small or large, unfold in the limit and go to the end of their power, transforming themselves and passing into one another.* (PUF p. 60; Patton adapted)

The "extreme forms" are not the loud or grand but those (small or large) that *go to the end of their power* without compromise. What returns is not frozen identity but the differential play.

*That is why the eternal return is said only of the theatrical world, of the metamorphoses and masks of the Will to power, of the pure intensities of this Will, as mobile individuating factors that no longer let themselves be held within the factitious limits of this or that individual, this or that Self.* (PUF p. 60; Patton adapted)

The empirical Moi does not return. The Self as identity is what the eternal return selects against. Cosmological: only difference returns. Ethical: act so you could will its return. This is *niṣkāma karma* (T8), action without ego-recoil, that can be willed again because nothing in it depends on a self that needs confirmation. *Seul revient ce qui peut être affirmé.*

The two formulas to keep:

*In the eternal return, univocal being is not only thought and even affirmed, but effectively realized.* (PUF p. 60)

And:

*It is being that is Difference, in the sense that being is said of difference.* (PUF p. 57)

Univocity thought (Scotus); affirmed (Spinoza); effectively realized (Nietzsche). The three moments are depth-stages of one proposition.

### Non-being without negation

One more passage from Chapter I that is structurally critical for the user's position. Deleuze:

*Univocal being is properly common, insofar as the (individuating) differences "are not" and do not have to be [...] if they are not, it is because they depend, in univocal being, on a non-being without negation.* (PUF p. 57; Patton adapted)

The "are not" is not negation. *Non-être sans négation*: a positive non-being. The differences are not negations of being; they are the positive medium in which univocal being says itself. *Ne sont pas* is a refusal of substance-ontology, not a metaphysical denial, the differences are not substances but modal-individuating articulations of the one voice.

For the position: the registers do not negate each other. Bhakti does not negate jñāna; Madhva's pañcabheda does not negate Śaṅkara's nirviśeṣa Brahman. They are positive differentiations of one expressive voice, co-articulation, not contradiction. This is the metaphysical license for T11's aperspectival coexistence of registers.

### The middle chapters: a brief map

**Chapter II** unfolds three syntheses of time: *présent vivant* (habit, Hume), *passé pur* (memory, Bergson), *avenir* (the empty form, Nietzsche-Kant, *le temps sort de ses gonds*).

**Chapter III** polemicizes against the dogmatic image of thought (eight postulates). Structurally what is needed to think *jñāna* by identity (T6, T11): cognition as encounter with the differential, not recognition of pre-given identity.

**Chapter IV** develops the Idea as a *differential field*: Ideas are problems, *virtual* (real but not actual), *differen-t-iated* but not yet *differen-c-iated*. Registers are different *actualizations* of the same virtual problem-field.

**Chapter V** develops intensity as the engine of individuation, what cannot be represented without being canceled, what makes individuals *as individuals*, not as members of a kind.

Virtual/actual, problem/Idea, intensity/individuation, what Deleuze later compresses into "plane of immanence."

## Part II — *Mille Plateaux*: Introduction "Rhizome" (1980)

Twelve years after *D&R*, with Guattari, Deleuze writes "Rhizome", first published 1976 as a Minuit pamphlet, placed at the head of *Mille Plateaux* in 1980. Twenty-eight pages: the locus classicus of the anti-arborescent argument. *Mille Plateaux*'s plateau structure (fifteen freestanding chapters, laterally connectable) is itself the rhizome enacted.

### The opening: writing-together

The first sentence is the ontological claim stated as autobiography:

*We wrote Anti-Œdipus together. Since each of us was several, there was already quite a crowd.* (Minuit p. 9; Massumi p. 3)

*Chacun de nous était plusieurs.* The subject of writing is not singular; an individual is already a multiplicity, an *agencement*, a rhizome. The book begins by performing what it will theorize, dispatching the unity-of-the-author piety.

### The three book-types

The **livre-racine**: "La loi du livre, c'est la loi de la réflexion, le Un qui devient deux." Root, trunk, branches, leaves, the arborescent book of Western metaphysics. Hegel's *Logic* is the most fully arborescent text; the Aufhebung is the trunk's mode of self-articulation.

The **livre-radicelle**: a superficial mutation. The pivotal root destroyed, the radicle-system multiplies roots. *L'unité ne cesse d'être contrariée et empêchée dans l'objet, tandis qu'un nouveau type d'unité triomphe dans le sujet.* This is the diagnosis of postmodern pluralism: it multiplies at the object-level while presupposing a unified subject. The "monkey describing its own brain" critique finds its precise diagnosis here: the postmodern is the radicle's compensation, not the rhizome's victory.

The **livre-rhizome** has no beginning or end but always a middle. *Mille Plateaux* itself is the rhizome-book.

### The six principles

Deleuze and Guattari enumerate six characteristics of the rhizome, organized as three pairs.

**1° et 2°, Connexion et hétérogénéité** (connection and heterogeneity).

*Any point of a rhizome can be connected to any other, and must be. This is very different from the tree or root, which fixes a point, an order.* (Minuit p. 13; Massumi p. 7 adapted)

The arborescent model fixes a point, root, trunk, founding principle, and orders everything in relation to it. The rhizome refuses fixing: any point connects to any other, lateral, multiple, proliferating. *Et doit l'être*: a normative claim about how one ought to think.

The heterogeneity principle is the complement: a rhizome connects across registers. The orchid and the wasp form a rhizome by encounter, not resemblance. *Une langue n'évolue pas dans le sens d'une diversification à partir d'une langue matricielle, mais par absorption de variables.* No language is the root.

**3°, Multiplicité.**

*A multiplicity has neither subject nor object, but only determinations, magnitudes, and dimensions that cannot grow in number without the multiplicity changing in nature.* (Minuit p. 14; Massumi p. 8)

The multiplicity is an n-dimensional figure that changes nature when its dimensions change. No underlying essence remains constant as connections are added; the rhizome *is* what it currently connects.

The decisive anti-Hegelian formula:

*The multiple has to be made, not by adding a higher dimension but, on the contrary, in the simplest way, by sobriety, at the level of the dimensions one has, always n−1 (this is the only way the one is part of the multiple, by always being subtracted). Subtract the unique from the multiplicity to be constituted; write at n−1.* (Minuit p. 13; my translation against Massumi)

n−1 vs Hegelian n+1. For Hegel, dialectic is n+1: thesis, antithesis, synthesis ascending toward the Absolute by addition of mediations. For Deleuze-Guattari: the One must be *subtracted*, not addition toward totality but subtraction of the unifier.

This names what is wrong with the Hegelian climb taken as metaphysics (not as the pedagogical climb of T3): Hegelian totalization is n+1; the supramental landing is n−1. T9's "Hegelian, opposites and *Aufhebung*, but non-totalizing" is exactly the n−1 modulation. The dialectical climb is real; the landing subtracts the totalizing function so differences hold without absorption.

**4°, Rupture asignifiante.**

*Against the over-signifying breaks that separate structures, or traverse one. A rhizome may be broken, shattered at any point, but it starts up again on one of its old lines, or on new lines.* (Minuit p. 16; Massumi p. 9)

*Asignifiante* means "not raised to the level of a master signifier." No castration, no Name-of-the-Father, no fundamental contradiction organizes the structure. Lacan's master-signifiers, Derrida's différance, Foucault's panopticon-as-master-figure: these are radicle moves, multiplying at the surface while preserving a master-cut. The rhizome refuses to elevate any break to organizing status.

**5° et 6°, Cartographie et décalcomanie** (cartography and decalcomania).

*The rhizome is altogether different, a map and not a tracing. Make the map, not the tracing. The orchid does not reproduce the tracing of the wasp; it forms a map with the wasp within the rhizome.* (Minuit p. 20; Massumi p. 12)

The *calque* reproduces a pre-existing form. The *carte* is productive: multiple entries, reversible, no founding center.

*The map is entirely oriented toward an experimentation in contact with the real.* (Minuit; Massumi p. 12)

*La carte est affaire de performance là où le calque renvoie toujours à une « compétence » prétendue.*

### The central formulas

Three formulas condense the rhizome's anti-totalization argument.

*The rhizome cannot be reduced to either the One or the Multiple.* (Minuit p. 31; Massumi p. 21)

*Être* is the verb of identity-logic; *et* is co-presence without founding predication. The rhizome's grammar is parataxis.

*The tree imposes the verb "to be," but the fabric of the rhizome is the conjunction "and... and... and..."* (Minuit p. 36; Massumi p. 25)

*Make rhizome, not root, never plant! Do not sow, graft! Be not one nor multiple, be multiplicities! Make the line and never the point!* (Minuit p. 36)

### Arborescent vs. rhizomatic: the deeper claim

The contrast is not metaphor-preference. The arborescent model: (a) founding origin (root, ground, substance); (b) hierarchical derivation; (c) logic of resemblance; (d) book mirrors world. From Porphyry's tree through Hegel's *Logic* to Chomsky's tree-grammar.

The "violence of unification": the tree does not describe reality neutrally; it *produces* what it claims to describe. The arborescent model generates the reality it maps. Deleuze-Guattari name "le fascisme" of root and trunk: arborescent thinking eliminates lateral connections, suppresses lines of flight.

*Plan d'organization*: the hidden plan organizing diversity from above. *Plan de consistance*: the actual relations that constitute consistency. The argument does not forbid that there is one Real; it forbids that the Real is *structured like a tree*. Deleuze's univocity is compatible with rhizomatic structure: Being said in one sense, but the saying proliferates laterally, without a trunk.

### Plateau

The term comes from Bateson:

*We call a "plateau" any multiplicity connectable to others by superficial subterranean stems so as to form and extend a rhizome.* (Minuit p. 33)

Bateson observed in Bali affective interactions sustained at high level without climax. Each chapter of *Mille Plateaux* is a plateau, complete in itself; the whole rhizomes laterally. This is what T3 (hierarchy as pedagogical) requires: each register a plateau, connected laterally, not sublated.

### The war machine: a brief note

Plateau 12's *machine de guerre* is *not* the state's instrument of violence but the mode of activity *exterior* to the state-form, nomadic, which the state appropriates secondarily. For the position, this is structurally what *matsya-nyāya* politics names: prevention of predation by a mode of collective constitution that does not itself become the predator-state. Vivekananda's predation-morphology (king → gold/Vaishya → revolution) is the cycle of state-appropriation of war-machine modes.

### Where the rhizome aligns with the user's thesis

The divine experiencing all registers simultaneously, non-destructively, is structurally consonant with the rhizome's refusal of master-perspective. The aperspectival-integral (Gebser), supramental comprehending poise (Aurobindo), *pūrṇāhantā* (Abhinavagupta) all resist single organizing grammar, what the rhizome calls the arborescent *plan d'organization*. "Univocity below, rhizome above" maps onto Saccidānanda + supramental triple-status as three co-present plateaus. Madhva's *pañcabheda* is itself rhizomatic: five orders held by the plane of consistency of being's univocal saying.

### Where the rhizome falls short of the position

Deleuze-Guattari refuse, without qualification, *le divin*. The rhizome is radically impersonal, lived by *assemblages*, not by persons. T7, T8, T10, T11 require a personal locus: the *caitya puruṣa*, the individual who undergoes jñāna by identity, the one who surrenders from fullness. *Devenir-imperceptible* dissolves this individual into a flow of intensities; the Aurobindonian indestructible soul-spark is irreconcilable with this impersonalism.

Resolution: **Deleuze-Guattari give conceptual solvents against arborescent closure, not a positive ontology.** The rhizome prevents reterritorialization into Hegelian hierarchy. Aurobindo's *śakti*-pluralism adds what they cannot: a self-manifesting Real, divine *svātantrya* including self-veiling, an individual not dissolved but transformed. The supramental through-all is not a meta-perspective (Deleuze-Guattari's pejorative) but an aperspectival condition, Gebser's *diaphany* is the phenomenological correlate.

## Part III — *Logique du sens* (1969)

Published a year after *D&R*. Where *D&R* opens from being and difference, *Logique du sens* opens from language and the proposition. 34 *séries*, two appendices (Klossowski; Carroll and Artaud), a non-linear structure rhizomatic before the word. *Logique du sens* makes explicit the metaphysics of the surface, *plan de sens*, that *D&R* implicitly assumes.

### The three classical dimensions of the proposition

Following the Stoics and Russell:

**Désignation**: relation to a state of affairs. True or false.
**Manifestation**: relation to the speaker, desire, belief, intention.
**Signification**: relation to universal concepts, logical/semantic content enabling inference.

These form a circle: each presupposes another. The circle cannot be broken from within.

*Inseparably, sense is the expressible or expressed of the proposition, and the attribute of the state of affairs. It turns one face toward things, one face toward propositions. But it merges no more with the proposition that expresses it than with the state of affairs or the quality the proposition designates. It is exactly the frontier of propositions and things.* (Minuit p. 34; my translation, Lester/Stivale consulted)

Sense is the *fourth dimension*: neither in the proposition nor in the thing, but the limit, the interface, the surface. In Stoic vocabulary it *subsists* (the *lekton*) rather than exists. The structural answer to the regress: if sense were itself a proposition it would require a further sense to specify it, infinitely. The regress stops *at the plane itself*, sense is the surface of events; the infinite regress becomes a positive non-propositional dimension.

### The surface against the abyss

Against the deep (body, forces, Freudian id, dialectical contradiction) and against the height (God, Plato's forms, Hegel's Concept), Deleuze posits the surface:

*In all these ways the surface is the transcendental field itself, and the place of sense or of expression. Sense is what forms and unfolds at the surface.* (Minuit p. 150; my translation against Lester/Stivale)

The surface is neither below nor above. It is the *transcendental field*: neither consciousness nor substance, neither personal nor general, but the *impersonal pre-individual* dimension in which singularities distribute themselves. It cannot be synthesized from depth and height; it is what allows them to be distinguished as the two pseudo-grounds that miss the level at which sense operates.

This is why the supramental cannot be a "height" in the Platonic or Hegelian sense. The supramental descent is not from a transcendent above; it is a self-unveiling of what is already the ground and matrix of all registers, *the truth of the surface*. Gebser's diaphany is the phenomenological correlate.

### The Lewis Carroll passages

The Cheshire Cat's smile-without-a-cat is the paradigmatic event: the smile is an incorporeal effect at the surface of the cat that can persist when the cat is gone, an *événement* that does not have to be a body to be. The naming-paradoxes (the White Knight's song named "Haddocks' Eyes" but really called "The Aged Aged Man") generate *le nom du nom du nom*, the regress that demonstrates sense is the non-propositional limit. Alice's growing-and-shrinking is *devenir*: *Alice ne grandit pas sans rapetisser, ne rapetisse pas sans grandir.* Becoming is not a state but sliding at the surface, always between, never settled. Aurobindo's evolutionary cosmology is *devenir* in this sense, not Hegelian becoming-toward-completion.

### Series 24: The communication of events

Events communicate not by causality (a relation between bodies) but by *consonance* at the surface. The "quasi-causal" relation: resonance of surface-effects through the univocal surface of sense.

*What makes a life be composed of one and the same Event despite all the variety of what happens to it, what makes it traversed by one and the same fissure, what makes it play one and the same air on all possible tones with all possible words, these are not cause-to-effect relations but an ensemble of non-causal correspondences forming a system of echoes, of resumptions and resonances, a system of signs: in short, an expressive quasi-causality, not at all a necessitating causality.* (Minuit p. 199; my translation against Lester/Stivale)

And the more compressed formula, from the same series:

*It happens [...] as a single event for everything that happens to the most diverse things, Eventum tantum for all events, extreme form for all the forms that remain disjunct within it, but that make their disjunction ring out and ramify.* (Minuit p. 210; my translation)

Events do not require a unifying substance. *Quasi-causalité expressive*, not *causalité nécessitante*, Stoic *sympatheia* deflated into the plane of sense-events. *Eventum tantum*: one Event for all events, where "one" is not a unifier above but the single voice of being heard in the disjunctions.

For the user's multi-register realism: registers as events at the surface of one Real communicate through the surface of sense, not through a hierarchy or substrate. The Aurobindonian comprehending poise is being-at-the-surface transparent to all events simultaneously.

### Series 25: De l'univocité

The univocity argument from *D&R* is restated in *Logique du sens* in a form that links it explicitly to sense:

*Univocity of being does not mean that there is one and the same being: on the contrary, beings are multiple and different, always produced by a disjunctive synthesis, themselves disjoint and divergent, membra disjuncta. Univocity of being signifies that being is Voice, that it is said, and is said in one and the same "sense" of everything of which it is said. That of which it is said is not at all the same. But it is the same for everything of which it is said.* (Minuit p. 210; my translation against Lester/Stivale)

Univocity is not *homogeneity of being*. The multiple beings are genuinely multiple, genuinely divergent. The one voice is said *of* the differences. *Logique du sens* makes explicit what *D&R* does not state as sharply: **being's univocal saying *is* sense**. Sense is what beings share, not a property but the subsistence-surface itself. The fourth dimension of the proposition is correlative to univocity of being: both are the limit not belonging to either side.

### Static and dynamic genesis

**Dynamic genesis**: production of sense from non-sense, depths of bodies, schizophrenic flows. How surface emerges from depth (Klein, Freud).

**Static genesis**: given the surface, how are the three classical dimensions organized *from* it? Not how sense is produced, but how the proposition's ordinary functions are grounded in sense as precondition.

For Aurobindo: the supermind is not merely a dynamically higher state produced by evolution. It is structurally *prior*, the static genesis of the lower poises, the surface from which their organization derives. T7's "willful self-ignorance" is the act by which the static-genetic surface appears to produce itself dynamically through evolution, while actually unfolding what was always latent. Aurobindo's cosmology is not Hegelian becoming-toward-completion but unfolding of what was always present supra-temporally.

### The Dennett-frame argument and the supramental

Dennett's point, meaning requires a frame; no proposition specifies its own frame; the frame cannot be propositionally specified without regress, is the Deleuzean argument about sense. For Deleuze, propositional self-specification's failure points not to a defect but to a *positive metaphysical dimension*: the surface of events.

The supramental is not a better proposition (still framed) but a *mode of being*, *jñāna* by identity, not itself propositional. Transparent to all frames simultaneously because not within any. The position's critique of postmodern theory is not "you have no truth" but "your tools cannot reach the dimension that would resolve the frame-regress." Where Deleuze cannot follow: granting the surface self-luminous awareness, Aurobindo's *Cit*. The surface of *Logique du sens* is impersonal, eventful, productive, but not aware. The Aurobindonian addition is awareness as constitutive.

## Part IV — *Spinoza et le problème de l'expression* (1968)

Deleuze's complementary thesis, defended the same day as *D&R*. The articulation of the second moment of univocity, Spinoza between Scotus and Nietzsche, and Deleuze's closest approach to a positive metaphysics of expression. (For Spinoza basics, see also Reader 04.)

### Why expression? The refusal of three alternatives

**Creation ex nihilo** (Abrahamic): radical asymmetry; cause self-sufficient (*ens a se*), effect entirely dependent (*ens ab alio*); no continuous expressive relation, only an absolute gap.

**Emanation** (Plotinian): the cause produces by overflow but is *superior to* and *other than* its effects. The One does not express itself in the Many; the Many is what the One overflows into, leaving the One untouched. Transcendence preserved.

**Participation** (Platonic-scholastic): things participate in Ideas by resemblance, the *calque* model. Cannot explain difference between copy and original; implies hierarchy.

**Expression** is the alternative to all three:

*It is the age of sufficient reason: the three branches of sufficient reason, ratio essendi [reason of being], ratio cognoscendi [reason of knowing], ratio fiendi or agendi [reason of becoming or of acting], find in expression their common root.* (Minuit p. 299; Joughin adapted)

In expression: the cause is *genuinely present in* the effect, not withdrawn from it. The effect is not a diminution (emanation), not a creation from nothing (ex nihilo), not a copy (participation). The effect is the cause expressing itself. The seventeenth century, Spinoza most fully, Leibniz partially, is what Deleuze calls the age of sufficient reason: the age that thinks the cause-effect relation as expressive rather than as transcendent or external.

The expressive formula in compressed form: *la cause immanente reste en soi pour produire, l'effet reste en la cause pour être produit.* (The immanent cause remains in itself to produce; the effect remains in the cause to be produced.) This is the formal articulation of immanent causality. The cause does not leave itself to make the effect (as creation ex nihilo would have it); the effect does not exit the cause to be a separate thing (as emanation would have it). The cause is in the effect as its expression; the effect is in the cause as what is expressed.

### The expressive triad: substance, attribute, mode

Spinoza's system, on Deleuze's reading, is structured by a triple expression. The triad runs:

**Substance** is one. Spinoza's *Ethics* I begins with the demonstration that there can be only one substance: any plurality of substances would either share an attribute (in which case they are not really distinct) or not share any (in which case they cannot interact, and there is no need for the multiple substances). The one substance is *causa sui*, self-caused, its essence involves its existence; it is the absolutely infinite, that of which nothing greater can be conceived.

The substance expresses itself in **attributes**. But the attributes are not parts of substance, substance is not divided into them. Each attribute expresses *the whole* essence of substance under a determinate form. Extension expresses the whole infinity of substance; Thought expresses the whole infinity of substance; both are God, fully, in different forms. There are infinitely many attributes; we (finite minds) access two, Extension and Thought, because we are constituted as bodies and minds, modes of these two attributes. The other attributes are real; we simply cannot know them, because we have no purchase on them.

Each **attribute** expresses itself in **modes**. A mode is a particular modification of an attribute. A finite human body is a mode of Extension; a finite human mind is a mode of Thought. Each mode is the expression of the attribute, and through it, of substance, under a particular, finite, intensive determination.

The expressive logic, condensed:

*Expression is installed at the heart of the individual, in its body and its soul, in its passions and its actions [...] by mode Spinoza means nothing other than the individual as expressive center.* (Minuit p. 304; Joughin adapted)

This is philosophically decisive for the user's position. The individual (the finite mode) is *not* a diminished or deficient version of the infinite. It is a **center of expression**, a site where the infinite expresses itself finitely. The finite does not merely *participate* in the infinite by resemblance. It *expresses* the infinite in its own specific form. Spinoza's modes, and, in Deleuze's parallel, Leibniz's monads, are the individual *as* the locus where the infinite is finitely expressed. *Chaque monade trace son expression partielle distincte sur fond d'une expression totale confuse.* (Each monad traces its distinct partial expression on the ground of a total confused expression.)

For the user's T3, T7, T11, this is structurally what is needed. The individual is not a deficiency to be transcended; it is a center of expression. The finite is not less than the infinite; it is the determinate form in which the infinite expresses itself. *Mithyā ≠ asat* finds its continental analog here: the modes are real *as* expressions of substance, just as the world is real *as* the expression of Brahman. Neither is illusory; neither is independent.

### Immanent causality

The defining act of Spinoza's philosophy, on Deleuze's reading, is *Ethics* I prop. 18: *Deus est omnium rerum causa immanens, non vero transiens*, God is the immanent, not the transitive cause of all things.

In transcendent (transitive) causality, the cause stands outside and prior to its effects. In immanent causality, the cause remains *in* the effect *as* its expression. God does not produce the world and withdraw; God *remains in* the world as the ground of its being, which is also the world's continuous expression of God. *Deus sive Natura*, God, or Nature, is not a metaphor. It is the logical consequence of immanent causality.

*The relation of expression essentially concerns the One and the Multiple.* (Minuit p. 308; Joughin adapted)

Expression names a relation between One and Multiple that is neither the reduction of the Multiple to the One (Hegelian absorption, or Advaitin *vivarta-vāda* read at its most stringent) nor the simple diversity of the Multiple without any One (pure pluralism, or, in the Deleuzean later trajectory, the rhizome considered without univocity). Expression holds both simultaneously: the One (substance) is genuinely present in each expression; the expressions (attributes, modes) are genuinely distinct, not reducible to each other or to the One.

This is the Spinozistic moment that is structurally closest to Aurobindo's *acintya-bhedābheda* received from Caitanya. *Acintya-bhedābheda*, inconceivable difference and non-difference, names exactly the relation expression names: the One is present in the Many without being the Many, and the Many are real without being independent of the One. Caitanya, of course, is not Spinoza; the Aurobindonian-Caitanyaite metaphysics has resources Spinoza does not (the personal divine, the *līlā*, the *Cit-Śakti*). But the formal structure of immanent expressive causality is what makes the relation between Caitanyaite *bhedābheda* and Spinozist immanence a rigorous parallel rather than a loose analogy.

### Spinoza's univocity as joyful affirmation

Where Scotus's univocity remained formal and abstract, Spinoza's univocity is *expressive and affirmative*. Substance's being is said univocally of attributes and modes, but this saying is an *affirmation* of what they are. Being is not more or less present in God or in the finite mode. It is univocally present.

The contrast with Aquinas: for Aquinas (following Aristotle) being is said *analogically* of God and creatures, the same word, but in proportionally different senses, with God's being infinite and the creature's finite. This preserves divine transcendence at the cost of univocity. Spinoza: no, being is said in *one and the same sense* of God and finite modes. The difference is not in being but in *power* (potentia) and form. God's power is infinite; the mode's power is finite. But being is not more or less present in either.

This is what Deleuze calls *the pure affirmation*. Being is univocal, said of everything that is, without gradation or equivocation. And the practical consequence: the finite mode's task is to *express its full power*, to be what it is, fully. The *Ethics* is an ontological ethics of joyful affirmation. The infinite does not diminish the finite; the finite expresses the infinite. The mode that thinks adequate ideas, that understands itself as expression, is more *joyful* (laetus), not because it has acquired some property, but because joy *is* the mode's increase in power of action, and adequate cognition is the mode's expression of the divine attribute of Thought through itself.

### The intellectus infinitus Dei

The user's question, does Spinoza's *intellectus infinitus Dei* (infinite intellect of God) model "the divine experiencing all registers at once"?, receives a precise answer from Deleuze's reading: structurally yes, experientially no.

**Yes, structurally.** Spinoza's *intellectus infinitus* is the totality of adequate ideas, the infinite mode in which substance's attribute of Thought is fully expressed. It holds all adequate cognition simultaneously; each thing is understood *as* an expression of substance, in its causes, its power, its place in the whole. *Tout ce qui existe exprime la nature de Dieu, autrement dit son essence, d'une façon certaine et déterminée* (Everything that exists expresses the nature of God, that is, his essence, in a certain and determinate way, *Ethics* I prop. 36, paraphrased by Deleuze). The *intellectus infinitus* is structurally close to Aurobindo's comprehending poise of the supermind: it is the dimension in which all expressions of substance are held simultaneously, not as a higher perspective among others but as the totality of adequate cognition.

**No, experientially.** Spinoza's *intellectus infinitus* is not a subject of experience. There is no one home to *enjoy* the comprehension. It is the logical structure of complete expression, not a divine subject with awareness. The *conatus* (each thing's striving to persist in its being) is the closest Spinoza comes to giving each mode an inner life, but the conatus is structural-functional, not phenomenological. Adequate ideas increase joy (*laetitia*), but joy here is an increase in power of action, not a felt phenomenological datum in any subject-relative sense.

The Aurobindonian correction: divine personhood. The divine must be able to *will* the self-veiling (*svātantrya*), *play* the *līlā*, *experience* the diversity of registers, *receive* surrender. These require a divine subject. Aurobindo gives the divine not just a *ratio essendi* (substance expresses itself) but a *śakti*, a power of self-manifestation that is itself personal, *Cit*, consciousness-force. Spinoza's substance is self-caused but not self-conscious in any relevant sense. Aurobindonian Saccidānanda is self-caused, self-luminous, self-experiencing, self-delighting: Sat-Cit-Ānanda, not merely *ens a se*.

### What Spinoza-via-Deleuze gives the position

1. **The proof that the finite does not dilute the infinite, it expresses it.** The individual (the finite mode) is not a diminished or deficient version of substance; it is a center of expression. This supports T3 (hierarchy as pedagogical) and T7 (the individual perspective as expression, not deficiency).

2. **The concept of immanent causality as the alternative to transcendent dualism.** This supports T11 (ontology of phenomenology: the divine is not absent from the material but expressed in it). The *prāṇamaya* and *annamaya* registers are not less real than the *vijñānamaya*; they are the *expressions* of the same Real at different intensities.

3. **The conatus doctrine, each mode striving to express its full power, translates into Aurobindo's doctrine of each being as a legitimate center of the divine's self-expression.** The "striving" is the mode's *expressing*, not a struggle against external opposition but the mode's intrinsic tendency to deploy its full power within its limit.

4. **Univocity of being as joyful affirmation.** Being is not more or less present in God or in the mode. The Aurobindonian *purnatva-icchā* (longing of fullness) is the structural counterpart on the side of the mode's experience: the mode that experiences itself as expressing the divine fully is the one whose surrender is from fullness rather than from poverty. Spinoza's *amor intellectualis Dei* (intellectual love of God) is the closest Spinoza comes to articulating this; it is the mode's adequate cognition of itself as expression of the divine. But, and this is the crucial qualification, Spinoza's *amor* is intellectual; it is not *bhakti*. It is philosophical beatitude, not the love-relation that constitutes the highest *rasa* in the Vaiṣṇava tradition.

### What Spinoza-via-Deleuze cannot give

The divine as person, as will, as experience, as play. Spinoza's God does not will the world; God's nature expresses itself by necessity. The *amor intellectualis Dei* is the mode's adequate cognition of itself as expression of the divine, philosophical beatitude, not *bhakti*, not *viraha-bhakti*, not surrender from fullness as the user's position requires.

The Aurobindonian *Ānanda* dimension is the decisive supplementation. The supramental is not the *intellectus infinitus Dei* but the *Cit-Śakti* of the divine, with will, delight, and creative power as primordial. The user's T7 (divine play through willful self-ignorance), T9 (unity-in-apparent-multiplicity through divine play), T10 (total surrender from fullness) all require what Spinoza cannot give: a divine subject who *plays*, who *wills*, who *receives*.

### A note on māyāvāda

Deleuze's reading of Spinoza supplies a continental analog of the Vedāntic argument against strict *māyā-vāda* taken as a doctrine of world-illusion. The modes are genuine expressions of substance, not less real than substance, not illusory, not reducible. This is structurally parallel to *mithyā ≠ asat*: the world is sublatable in the sense that it is not independently real, but this is not the same as being unreal. The world is real *as* the expression of Brahman. The finite modes are real *as* the expression of substance. In both cases: the finite is real, not illusory; its reality is expressive, not independent.

Māyā, in the Vivaraṇa school and Kashmir Śaivism reading the position adopts (T7), is not a veil that makes the world unreal; it is the *śakti* of the divine by which the infinite *appears finite*, by which the divine *appears to itself* as limited. This is closer to Spinoza's finite modes as genuine (if limited) expressions of the infinite than to Śaṅkara's strict *vivarta-vāda* read at its most uncompromising. Spinoza-via-Deleuze gives the philosophical articulation: the finite is the expressive determination of the infinite, not its deficient copy and not its illusory shadow.

## Part V — *Le Pli: Leibniz et le Baroque* (1988)

Twenty years after *D&R*, Deleuze returns to careful philosophical commentary. *Le Pli* is the most structurally relevant of all Deleuze's texts for the user's question about whether the divine experiencing all registers at once can be philosophically grounded. It is also where Deleuze comes closest to the position, and where he turns away. The book belongs to the late period, after the two volumes of *Capitalisme et Schizophrénie*. The vocabulary of the plateau, the assemblage, and the point of view has been sharpened by the collaboration with Guattari; Deleuze now applies it to a single great philosopher in a way that produces the most plastic and inventive of his late readings.

### The fold

The opening of *Le Pli* offers one of Deleuze's most arresting philosophical formulas:

*The Baroque does not refer to an essence but rather to an operative function, to a trait. It endlessly produces folds.* (Conley p. 3; Minuit p. 5)

The Baroque names a *function*, folding. The fold is not a shape; it is a movement that creates interiority without enclosure. *Le pli qui va à l'infini*, the fold that goes to infinity, does not stop at a determinate boundary. This is the contrast with classical Cartesian space (rectilinear, finite, Euclidean), in which interiority is produced by separating an inside from an outside via a boundary that has a determinate shape and finite extent.

Why Leibniz? Because Leibniz is the philosopher of the infinite fold: the soul (the monad, the interiority) is produced by folding, not by being a different substance from the body. The soul does not sit inside the body like a pilot in a ship, the Cartesian image of the *res cogitans* lodged in the *res extensa*, which Deleuze reads as a dualism that breaks the fold by inserting two substances where there should be one fold with two aspects. The soul is the interiority produced by the infinite folding of the body; the body is the exterior produced by the unfolding of the soul's expression.

This is the first key. The "divine experiencing all registers at once" is not a mind that stands outside all things and surveys them. It is the infinite fold, the movement in which interiority and exteriority are continuously produced and re-produced through each other. There is no "outside" of the fold from which to survey; there is only the folding itself, which produces perspective, not the perspective that surveys folding. The fold is what makes any "experiencing" possible at all, and what the supramental does is *be the fold itself*, the movement of which all the registers are aspects.

### The two-floor monad

Deleuze's central image is the Baroque house with two floors (*deux étages*).

**The lower floor** is matter, the material body, organic springs, folds of matter. There is no atom, matter folds infinitely, every portion folded into smaller portions, each organized by its dominant monad. The lower floor: extension, mechanism, passive powers. Each portion of matter is itself organized by its own principle of unity (its dominant monad), and matter is infinitely divisible (Leibniz against the atomists). The lower floor follows the laws of physics, the laws of mechanism.

**The upper floor** is soul, the monad proper, the point of view. The upper floor has no windows (Leibniz's famous claim: *les monades n'ont point de fenêtres*). It is closed to direct external causal influence; it receives nothing from outside. But it expresses the whole universe from its interior. The upper floor: perception, appetite, active powers, final causes. The upper floor follows the laws of final causes, what Leibniz calls "the laws of the kingdom of grace," distinct from the laws of mechanism that govern the lower floor.

The two floors are not separate substances (against Cartesian dualism). They are two *aspects* of one fold: matter folded from below; soul folded from above. They communicate by **harmonie préétablie** (pre-established harmony): God arranges from the beginning that every change in body corresponds to a change in soul, not by causal interaction but by mutual expression of the same world.

*Such is the foundation of perspectivism. It does not mean dependence on a pre-defined subject.* (Le Pli; Conley adapted)

The monad *is* a point of view, not a subject who *has* a point of view. Perspective precedes and constitutes the subject. The monad's identity is its perspective; perspective is not a property of a pre-given subject but the constitutive act that makes a subject at all. This reverses the post-Cartesian assumption that subjectivity is the prior given (the *cogito*) and perspective is what subjectivity adopts. For Leibniz-as-Deleuze-reads-him, subjectivity is what perspective produces.

### Monadologie §57 with Deleuze's commentary

The locus classicus of Leibniz's perspectivism is *Monadologie* §57:

*Just as the same city viewed from different sides appears entirely different, and is as it were multiplied perspectivally, so it comes about that, through the infinite multitude of simple substances, there are as many different universes, which are nevertheless only perspectives on one, according to the different points of view of each monad.* (*Monadologie* §57; standard translation modified for clarity)

The city-viewed-from-different-sides analogy is the philosophical heart of the user's T1. Different perspectives on the same reality are *not* errors or illusions; they are *constitutive of access* to the reality. The reality does not exist apart from all perspectives; it is constituted by all of them together. And, crucially against relativism, the perspectives are *of the same city*. They are perspectives *on the same thing*. There is one city; the city is not exhausted in any one perspective; every perspective expresses the whole city from its angle, with its degree of clarity. Each monad is the city seen from one street corner.

Deleuze's commentary, in the form by which he reads Leibniz against post-Kantian relativism:

*In both Nietzsche and Leibniz, perspectivism will not mean "to each their own truth," but the point of view as the condition for the manifestation of the true.* (Le Pli; Conley adapted)

This is the anti-relativist move. *Le perspectivisme ne signifie pas à chacun sa vérité.* Perspective is not subjective distortion. It is the condition of access to truth. Perspective is the constitutive medium through which reality comes to expression. Without point of view, no manifestation of truth at all. The perspectives are ordered by clarity, some monads perceive their portion of the universe more distinctly, others more confusedly. God's monad (or rather the divine intellect, since God is not exactly a monad but the principle of the monadic system) perceives all things with perfect clarity from all points of view simultaneously.

This is the position's reply to the postmodern relativist reading of T1. The darśanas as perspectives are not "all paths the same"; each is a perspective *on the same Real*, and the perspectives are ordered by clarity. The supramental comprehending poise is not a refusal of perspective; it is the condition in which all perspectives are simultaneously given without privilege of any one, which is to say, without any one perspective being mistaken for the whole and therefore without any of the others being relegated to the half-real.

### Incompossibility and divergent series

The most technically consequential concept in *Le Pli*: **incompossibilité** (incompossibility).

Each monad expresses the entire universe from its point of view. Each is a complete mirror of the whole, seen from a specific angle, with varying degrees of clarity. But, Leibniz's discovery, different monads cannot always belong to the same world.

Two monads are **compossible** if they can coexist in the same world (their respective expressions are mutually compatible). Two monads are **incompossible** if they cannot (their expressions are mutually contradictory). The Adam who sinned and the Adam who did not sin are both possible (there is no logical contradiction in either); both are incompossible with each other (they belong to different worlds); only one is actual (the one whose total compossible set God has chosen for actualization).

*Compossibility must be defined [...] by the convergence of series.* (Le Pli)

Two monads are compossible when their respective infinite series of perceptions *converge*. Incompossible when they *diverge*. Compossibility is not a logical relation in the narrow sense, there is nothing logically self-contradictory in the existence of either Adam, but a metaphysical relation about whether two monads can fit into the same maximally-coherent total expression. Convergence here is a technical mathematical notion (Leibniz's calculus is operative): two infinite series converge if their terms get arbitrarily close together; they diverge if they do not.

God surveys all possible worlds, all compossible sets, and selects for actualization the world with the maximum of perfection, greatest variety, greatest economy. This is Deleuze's *condition de clôture* (condition of closure): God selects, closes down all incompossible alternatives by actualizing one compossible set. The actual world is the selected maximum.

*God turning, so to speak, in all directions and in all ways the general system of phenomena...the result of each view of the universe...is a substance.* (Leibniz, cited by Pelletier)

This is the structural moment that Aurobindo's supramental approaches but does not adopt. The supramental comprehending poise *is* like Leibniz's God in that it holds all perspectives simultaneously; it is *unlike* Leibniz's God in that it does not select against incompossibles, because the supramental is the truth in which apparent incompossibles are resolved not by choice but by *acintya-śakti*, the inconceivable power that makes the simultaneous-apparently-impossible hold. *Acintya-bhedābheda*, inconceivable difference and non-difference, is the precise Caitanyaite-Aurobindonian articulation of what the supramental does that Leibniz's God does not.

### Where Leibniz holds and where he does not

**Where the parallel holds:**

1. **The monad as individual center of expression.** Each monad fully expresses the whole from its finite center. The individual does not see a fragment; it sees the whole, but from its angle, with its degree of clarity. This is structurally what each *jīva* is in the Aurobindonian system: a finite center where the whole expresses itself.

2. **Pre-established harmony as proto-supramental unity.** The harmony between expressions (soul and body, monad and monad) is built into the system, not external to it. In Leibniz this harmony is pre-established by God; in Aurobindo it is intrinsic, the supramental is the truth of each *jīva*'s being, not an external harmonizer. (Spinoza is closer to Aurobindo on this last point, the immanence is fully internal.)

3. **Perspectivism without relativism.** The Real is one; perspectives are the condition of its manifestation; clarity orders the perspectives.

4. **The two-floor structure as the registers' co-presence.** The lower floor (matter, mechanism) and the upper floor (soul, perception) are not in a vertical hierarchy but in mutual expression. The material registers (the *annamaya* of the Pañcakośa system, the gross body) and the spiritual registers (the *vijñānamaya*, the spiritual sheaths) are not in vertical hierarchy but in two-floor co-expression of one fold.

**Where the parallel breaks:**

1. **The monad has no windows.** Leibniz's monad cannot causally interact with other monads; it can only express the universe from its interior. This is irreconcilable with the Aurobindonian psychic being, which is genuinely shaped by relations, it deepens through lives, it changes, it surrenders. The Leibnizian monad cannot surrender; it can only more clearly express what is already in its complete concept.

2. **God's transcendence.** Leibniz's God is genuinely transcendent, the creator who selects the best world but does not become the world or express itself in it. (Spinoza is more immanent here.) The Aurobindonian divine is both transcendent (Sat, beyond manifestation) and immanent (expressed in every element of manifestation) and personal (Saccidānanda as self-luminous and self-delighting).

3. **Incompossibility as exclusion.** This is the deepest divergence. Leibniz's system *requires* that incompossible worlds remain eternally unactualized. The divine selects one and excludes the rest. For the user's position (following Caitanya's *acintya-bhedābheda*), the divine holds difference and non-difference *simultaneously*, not by selecting one over the other but by the inexplicable power of *acintya-śakti* that makes the apparently-impossible hold. Apparent incompossibles (bhakti and jñāna, the finite and the infinite, *bhedābheda* itself) are resolved not by choice but by an intrinsic divine capacity.

### Deleuze's own divergence from Leibniz

Deleuze's use of Leibniz in *Le Pli* is appropriative, he takes what he needs and goes beyond. The decisive move:

Leibniz requires *convergence* (compossible monads converge on the same world). Deleuze, following Nietzsche, abandons the convergence requirement and embraces **divergent series**. The eternal return is the affirmation of *all* possible worlds, not the selection of one. God (in Nietzsche-Deleuze) does not select; the eternal return affirms everything that can return, which means affirmation of divergence, incompossibility, multiplicity-without-convergence.

This is the structural difference between Deleuze and the user's position. Deleuze's late move dissolves the divine selector entirely; he refuses both Leibniz's transcendent God who chooses and any positive divine subjectivity. The result is the affirmation of all divergent series, pure multiplicity affirmed, no convergence, no selection. This is consistent with the rhizome (*Mille Plateaux*) and with the late univocity (the surface of *Logique du sens*), and it is what makes Deleuze a powerful negative ally for the position. But it is also where the position must add what Deleuze refuses to add.

### The fork

For the position there are three paths visible at this point:

- **Leibniz's path:** divine comprehension through selection, one actual world, one divine perspective holding all compossible monadic perspectives simultaneously. Structurally close to the supramental comprehending. But requires an external transcendent God who *chooses against* the incompossibles.

- **Deleuze's path:** affirmation of all divergent series, no selection, no God, no best world. Pure rhizomatic plane. Captures the anti-totalization argument but loses divine subjectivity, the individual, and the reality of selection.

- **Aurobindo's path:** holds both. The supramental comprehending is like Leibniz's God in that it holds all perspectives simultaneously; it is unlike Leibniz's God in that it does not select against incompossibles, because the supramental is the truth in which apparent incompossibles are resolved not by choice but by *acintya-śakti*. And the supramental does not remain merely transcendent, it descends; the supramental descent is the divine becoming immanent in the evolutionary process.

This is the precise spot where Deleuze comes closest to the position and turns away. The two-floor monad is unexpectedly close to the *vyaṣṭi-samaṣṭi* (individual-cosmic) structure of Aurobindonian metaphysics; perspectivism-without-relativism is structurally what T1 needs; comprehension-of-all-monadic-perspectives is structurally what the supramental does. But Deleuze refuses the divine subject, and the position requires it.

### A note on the Baroque aesthetic and rasa

Deleuze's reading of the Baroque as the aesthetic of the fold has an unexpected resonance with Abhinavagupta's *rasa* theory (which the user cites as part of the disinterestedness convergence line). The Baroque aesthetic, as Deleuze reads it, is characterized by excessive elaboration, interiority revealed through surface complexity, light entering through hidden openings (the dark chapel illuminated by invisible windows), the infinite detail that never exhausts the whole. It is an aesthetic of *implication*, everything is implied in everything else, through the fold.

Abhinavagupta's *rasa* is similarly an aesthetic of implication: the *rasa* (aesthetic relish, the flavor of the work) is not in the words, not in the characters, not in the audience's reaction, it is at the limit of all of these, where they fold into each other and a unified affective cognition arises. *Rasāsvāda* (aesthetic experience) as *brahmāsvāda-sahodara* (sibling of the experience of Brahman): the experience of the aesthetic whole is analogous to, and preparatory for, the experience of the divine whole.

The fold: the *rasa* is produced by the folding of aesthetic elements into each other. The Baroque is folding taken to infinity. The Aurobindonian supramental comprehending is the folding of all registers into each other, not their confusion (the fold retains distinctions) but their mutual implication (the fold makes interiority possible). The user's "diaphanous through-all" is the transparency of all folds to each other, which is exactly what Gebser calls *diaphany* and what Deleuze's *Le Pli* might be read as approaching in its concluding gesture.

## Part VI — *Nietzsche et la philosophie* (1962)

Deleuze's earliest major book in his own voice, a careful and partisan reading of Nietzsche that is also the methodological prelude to *D&R*. Though chronologically the earliest of the major works treated here (1962, eight years before *L'Anti-Œdipe*), it sets up much of what Deleuze will later develop systematically. Three things to take from it: the active/reactive distinction, the body as relation of forces, and the eternal return as ethical-selective principle.

### Body as relation of forces

*What is a body? We do not define it by saying that it is a field of forces, a nutritive milieu disputed by a plurality of forces. For in fact there is no "milieu," no field of forces or battle. There is no quantity of reality; all reality is already quantity of force. Nothing but quantities of force "in relation of tension" with one another.* (Nietzsche et la philosophie, Ch. II §2; PUF p. 45 adapted; Tomlinson adapted)

Notice the precise correction Deleuze makes. It would be tempting to say that a body is a *field* in which forces meet. But "field" is already too much: it implies a pre-existing space in which the forces play. Deleuze (with Nietzsche) rejects this: there is no pre-existing field; there are only forces in relations of tension, and the "body" is the name we give to one such relation.

*Every force is in relation with others, either to obey or to command. What defines a body is this relation between dominant and dominated forces. Every relation of forces constitutes a body: chemical, biological, social, political. Any two forces, being unequal, constitute a body as soon as they enter into relation.* (Tomlinson adapted)

The body is not a thing; it is a *relation of forces*. Any two forces in tension constitute a body. The forces are unequal, one dominates, one is dominated, and this hierarchy of forces is what gives the body its specific character. There is no body in itself; there are only relations of forces, which are always asymmetric, always between dominant and dominated.

*In a body, the higher or dominant forces are called active, the lower or dominated forces reactive. Active and reactive are precisely the originary qualities that express the relation of force to force.* (Tomlinson adapted)

Active force does, creates, affirms. Reactive force adapts, conserves, resists. Both are real; both are necessary. The question is which dominates. *On appellera hiérarchie cette différence des forces qualifiées, conformément à leur quantité : forces actives et réactives.* (We call this difference of qualified forces, according to their quantity, hierarchy: active and reactive forces.) The "hierarchy" here is internal to the body, the relation of dominance between its forces, not a hierarchy of bodies.

### The diagnosis of modernity

*The inferior forces define themselves as reactive: they lose nothing of their force, of their quantity of force, they exercise it in securing the mechanisms and finalities, in fulfilling the conditions of life and the functions, the tasks of conservation, of adaptation, and of utility. There is the starting point of the concept of reaction [...] the mechanical and utilitarian accommodations, the regulations that express the entire power of the inferior and dominated forces.* (Tomlinson adapted)

Reactive force is not weak. It is the full power of forces in their reactive aspect, the mechanisms, regulations, adaptations, utilities. Reactive force is what keeps things going within their conditions of existence. It is conservation, accommodation, the steady reproduction of life-as-it-is.

*We must note modern thought's immoderate taste for this reactive aspect of forces. People always think they have done enough when they understand the organism in terms of reactive forces.* (Tomlinson adapted)

This is the Deleuzean-Nietzschean diagnosis of modernity: it understands everything in terms of adaptation, regulation, conservation, utility, the reactive functions. Active force is harder to characterize because it escapes consciousness:

*By nature, they escape consciousness: "The great principal activity is unconscious." Consciousness expresses only the relation of certain reactive forces to the active forces that dominate them. Consciousness is essentially reactive; that is why we do not know what a body can do.* (Tomlinson adapted)

*Nous ne savons pas ce que peut un corps*, we do not know what a body can do. This is Spinoza's question (cited explicitly by Deleuze), and it is the hinge between active force and the body's unrealized capacities. Consciousness is essentially reactive, it is the reflex of certain reactive forces measuring themselves against the active forces that dominate them. What active force does, what the body can do at its full power, escapes consciousness because consciousness is always one step behind, always cataloging what has already happened.

For the user's position, this maps onto McGilchrist's left-hemispheric usurpation: modernity privileges the reactive, regulative, instrumental functions (the Emissary) and loses contact with the active, primary, contact-with-the-living functions (the Master). Spinoza's "we do not know what a body can do" becomes, in the position's framework, the *prāṇamaya / sūkṣma-śarīra* registers that the conscious ego cannot survey. The right-hemispheric Master, in McGilchrist's language, is what Nietzsche-Deleuze call the active, the great unconscious activity that is not unconscious in the Freudian sense (the repressed) but unconscious because it operates at a level prior to and constitutive of consciousness.

### Eternal return as selective ethics

The eternal return, on Deleuze's reading, has two faces: cosmological and ethical.

**Cosmologically**: *le revenir est l'être du devenir*, the returning is the being of becoming. What is permanent is not any state but the very fact of recurrence. And what recurs is *not* the same; it is difference under selective pressure. The cosmological doctrine that the universe will eternally repeat itself (the Stoic ekpyrosis, transposed) is not the doctrine of the return of the identical state but the doctrine that recurrence is the form of becoming, and that what recurs is what can affirm itself fully, while the reactive and the negative do not.

**Ethically**: *Seul revient ce qui peut être affirmé.* Only what can be affirmed returns. The eternal return is a test: act in such a way that you could will the eternal recurrence of this act, infinitely, without recoil. What survives the test is what can affirm itself fully. Reactive forces, the heavy, the negative, these do not survive. The action that survives is the action that has gone to the end of its power, *les formes extrêmes*, without recoil, without the small voice of ressentiment that would prefer not to do this again.

This is structurally the *niṣkāma karma* of T8: action without ego-recoil, action that is the lawful unfolding of the witness, action that can be willed again because nothing in it depends on a self that needs to be confirmed. The eternal return is the ethical-selective principle that sorts ego-burdened action from action that is the divine play through the individual.

The four figures of affirmation in Deleuze's Nietzsche, Dionysos, Dionysos-Ariadne, the eternal return, the Übermensch, give the practical articulation of the position's *līlā*. Action as play, action as affirmation of the cosmic dance, action that does not require a self to confirm but is the very dancing.

A note on Ariadne. Deleuze's reading of the Dionysos-Ariadne pairing is among the most original moves in *Nietzsche et la philosophie*. Ariadne is the soul that affirms what Dionysos affirms; she is the one who passes the test of the eternal return by becoming able to will the return of all that has happened to her, the suffering, the abandonment by Theseus, the encounter with Dionysos. The pair is the figure of the affirmation that includes the affirmer; not the cold cosmological doctrine but the lived ethics of saying *yes* to everything that returns. This is structurally close to the Aurobindonian surrender from fullness, the *yes* that does not need the *no* of poverty to motivate it, that arises from the recognition of what is already given as worthy of being willed eternally.

Where Deleuze cannot follow the user's position is in granting Ariadne, the affirming individual, a personal divine to whom her affirmation is also surrender. Ariadne, in Deleuze, affirms the eternal return; she does not surrender to a personal divine who is herself doing the affirming through her. The Aurobindonian *psychic being* who undergoes *līlā* is closer to Ariadne than Ariadne is to herself, but only because the personal divine is added to the picture in a way Deleuze refuses.

Even so, the structural form is delivered: action as affirmation; affirmation as the test of eternal return; the test as the selective ethics that distinguishes ego-burdened from non-ego-burdened action; and the cosmological-ethical convergence in which what returns is difference, never identity.

### A late crystallization: "Immanence, a Life..."

The final essay, "L'immanence: une vie..." (1995), is brief, but it matters for the user's project because it gives Deleuze's latest and most stripped-down statement of what an impersonal absolute looks like once transcendence has been fully refused. The key claim is that a life is not the biography of a person but a plane of immanence, a field of singularities and virtualities that can flash forth most clearly when the empirical person is weakest: infancy, extreme illness, a moment just before death, a scene in Dickens where a hated man suddenly elicits a pure impersonal compassion. What emerges in those moments is not the ego, but what Deleuze calls "a life," singular and impersonal at once.

This matters because it is the exact point where Deleuze both almost reaches and finally misses the user's thesis. He reaches it because "a life" is no longer one perspective among others. It is a through-field, the condition under which perspectives arise at all. He misses it because the field is still impersonal. There is no divine knower, no psychic being, no enduring center that can both undergo the dissolution of ego and remain itself. The essay is therefore the clearest late witness to the limit of Deleuze's metaphysics: immanence, yes; self-luminous and personal immanence, no.

Read against Aurobindo, the contrast sharpens usefully. Deleuze's "a life" is what remains when transcendence and ego are both stripped away. Aurobindo's psychic being is what remains when ego is stripped away but individuality is not lost. That is why this late essay belongs in the file. It gives the cleanest final statement of Deleuze's power and of the precise place where the user's position must depart from him.

## Part VII — Reading notes

This is the concluding section. I want to do four things: (1) name where Deleuze confirms the position; (2) name where Deleuze challenges it; (3) say where Banerji's *Inward Turn* essay supplements Deleuze with Aurobindo's personal divine; and (4) suggest a concrete reading sequence in the primary texts.

### Where Deleuze confirms the position

**1. Univocity is the structural homologue of the core thesis.** "Being is said in one and the same sense of everything of which it is said, but that of which it is said differs: it is said of difference itself." This is *exactly* the formal shape of "one Real, said in many senses, of which each darśana, each tradition, each register speaks truly." The voice is one; the differences are real; the differences are not absorbed; the voice is said *of* the differences. This is the most important confirmation Deleuze gives. Without univocity, the position would have to choose between flat identity-monism (which is Hegelian even when Vedāntic) and pluralism without unity (which is what postmodern relativism amounts to). With univocity, the position has a precise continental articulation of the third option.

**2. The critique of the four shackles is the violence-of-unification critique in continental form.** The diagnosis of how identity-thinking imprisons difference (identity in the concept, opposition in the predicate, analogy in judgment, resemblance in perception) gives the position its philosophical articulation of why arborescent unification is not a neutral description of reality but an active suppression of the differences it claims merely to describe. T11's "ontology of phenomenology" needs this concept. The user's polemic against Hegelian totalization, against Lacanian master-signifiers, against the "monkey describing its own brain", all of these find their continental articulation in Deleuze's critique of the *carcan*.

**3. The rhizome refuses meta-perspective and arborescent hierarchy.** This is the negative work the position needs done. The rhizome prevents reterritorialization into Hegelian totalization. The compass formula, "univocity below, rhizome above", works: the univocal saying of being is the ground; the rhizomatic distribution of the differences is what that saying is *of*; and there is no master perspective above the rhizome from which the whole can be surveyed.

**4. Eternal return as return of difference.** The cyclic time of the position is *not* the wheel of the same. It is the return of difference under selective pressure. This is Aurobindo's evolutionary cosmology articulated in continental terms: each return is real, irreducible, and not a return of the identical. The eternal return as ethical-selective principle is *niṣkāma karma* in another voice. The user's reading of Krishna as the Anandamaya Overmind God who supports evolution toward an Ananda not yet arrived is structurally the same shape as Deleuze's Nietzsche: only what affirms returns, and what returns is the affirmative, but the affirmative is *new* each time, never the identical state.

**5. Spinozist immanent expression and the *mithyā ≠ asat* argument.** The finite mode is real *as* expression of the infinite. The world is real *as* the expression of Brahman. Spinoza-via-Deleuze gives the continental analog of the Vedāntic correction to strict *vivarta-vāda* taken at its most uncompromising. The Bhāskaran-Madhvan-Caitanyaite-Aurobindonian line of correction has a precise continental cousin in Deleuze's reading of Spinoza.

**6. The Leibnizian two-floor structure as proto-supramental.** Body and soul, lower floor and upper floor, are not two substances but two aspects of one fold. The material and spiritual registers are not in vertical hierarchy but in mutual expression. Pre-established harmony approximates supramental unity (with the qualification that pre-established harmony is external while supramental unity is intrinsic). The two-floor structure is unexpectedly close to the *vyaṣṭi-samaṣṭi* relation, and the perspectivism-without-relativism articulation of Monadologie §57 is exactly what T1 needs.

**7. Sense as the limit-dimension that resolves the frame-regress.** *Logique du sens* gives the position its philosophical articulation of why the supramental cannot be a better proposition: it is not propositional. It is the surface of events, the plane of sense, transparent to all frames simultaneously because not within any. The Dennett-frame argument finds in Deleuze its precise continental form.

**8. Active/reactive force and the McGilchrist convergence.** The diagnosis of modernity's predilection for reactive force (consciousness as essentially reactive; "we do not know what a body can do") maps onto McGilchrist's left-hemispheric usurpation. The right-hemispheric Master is the active, primary, contact-with-the-living register that the modern Emissary has suppressed. Deleuze's Nietzsche gives the philosophical articulation of what McGilchrist gives the neuroscientific articulation of.

### Where Deleuze challenges the position

**1. Impersonalism.** This is the deepest difference. Deleuze refuses the divine subject. There is no monad expressing the whole; no divine intelligence; no individual locus of realization from which divine play is undergone. The rhizome is lived by *assemblages*, not persons. Spinoza's *intellectus infinitus* is not a subject of experience. *Le Pli* comes close to a divine comprehension of perspectives, but Deleuze backs away from the divine subject by embracing divergent series and abandoning convergence.

The position requires a personal locus: the psychic being (*caitya puruṣa*), the individual who undergoes *jñāna* by identity, the one who surrenders from fullness. T7, T8, T10, T11 all require this. Deleuze's impersonalism is not compatible with it.

**2. Rejection of "the divine" as a possible point of integration.** Deleuze and Guattari refuse, without qualification, *le divin*. There is no through-all that experiences. The plane of immanence is *not* a divine consciousness; it is an impersonal field of intensities.

The position requires the divine through-all. Aurobindo's Saccidānanda is self-luminous and self-delighting, not merely self-caused.

**3. Flat plane of immanence.** Deleuze's plane is flat, there are no genuine planes of consciousness ordered by clarity. There is an n-dimensional multiplicity, but the dimensions are not hierarchically ordered.

The position requires planes of consciousness, Higher Mind, Illumined Mind, Intuitive Mind, Overmind, Supermind, ordered not arbitrarily but by their relative transparency to the whole. The supramental is not just one register among others; it is the static-genetic ground from which the others derive their organization. This is *not* a Platonic hierarchy of ideas (the position is firm against this); it is a *registers-ordered-by-transparency* structure that Deleuze's flat plane does not give.

**4. Divergent series without convergence.** Deleuze's late move (in *Le Pli* and after) abandons convergence and affirms all divergent series. This dissolves the divine selector entirely.

The position holds that the divine *does* hold the divergences in *acintya-bhedābheda*, not by selecting one over the others (Leibniz) and not by affirming all without convergence (Deleuze), but by an intrinsic divine capacity that holds them as inconceivably-non-dual-difference.

### Where Banerji's *Inward Turn* essay supplements Deleuze with Aurobindo's personal divine

Debashish Banerji's essay "The Inward Turn" (in *Sri Aurobindo and the Coming of the Supermind* and related volumes) makes the precise supplement the position needs.

Banerji argues that Aurobindo's *psychic being* (*caitya puruṣa*) is the philosophical figure that resolves what Deleuze's impersonalism cannot. The psychic being is *neither* the empirical ego (which Deleuze rightly dissolves) *nor* an impersonal flow (which Deleuze affirms in its place). It is a third thing: an *individuated divine center*, a soul-spark that is genuinely individual, that evolves across lives, that *deepens through relations*, and that is the locus of the surrender from fullness the position requires.

The decisive Banerjian move: where Deleuze gives us *devenir-imperceptible* (becoming-imperceptible, the ego dissolving into the flow of intensities), Aurobindo gives us *psychisation*, the deepening of the psychic being as the divine's individuated form. The two are not the same. *Devenir-imperceptible* loses the individual in the flow; *psychisation* deepens the individual *as* divine. The flow is real (Deleuze is right that the empirical ego is a stable but secondary effect), but what is in the flow is not impersonal, it is the *jīva* in its evolutionary deepening.

Banerji further notes that Deleuze's univocity is the *structural form* of what the supramental *is experientially*. Deleuze's plane of immanence is the philosophical articulation of the supramental as conceptual structure; what Deleuze cannot give, and what Aurobindo gives, is the supramental as *self-luminous experience*, with *Cit* (consciousness-force) as constitutive. Deleuze gives the form; Aurobindo gives the form *as lived from within itself*.

The supplementation, in one sentence: *Deleuze's univocity is the structure; Aurobindo's Saccidānanda is the structure as self-aware and self-delighting; the supramental psychic being is the individuated locus where this self-awareness is realized.*

#### Banerji's equation: supermind ≈ rhizome (with the *Cit*-correction)

The cleanest way to state what Banerji's reading gives us is as an equation that does the structural work both directions. **Aurobindo's supermind is what the rhizome would be if it were self-aware.** The rhizome's six principles map onto the supramental's structural features almost term-for-term:

- *Connexion / hétérogénéité.* Each register of the supermind connects to every other; the comprehending poise is precisely the holding-of-all-registers in lateral connection without arborescent subordination. (Aurobindo: each plane "answers to all the rest.")
- *Multiplicité.* The supermind is an n-dimensional truth-consciousness whose nature changes when its dimensions change; it is not a substance with properties but a *Cit-Śakti* whose content is its current relations.
- *Rupture asignifiante.* The supermind is not organized by a master-cut (no castration, no Name-of-the-Father, no founding negation); apparent breaks (involution / evolution, *avidyā* / *vidyā*) do not master-organize it but pass through it laterally.
- *Cartographie / décalcomanie.* The supermind is a *carte* not a *calque*, it is the operative truth of the descent, not a tracing of a higher Idea.

The asymmetry: rhizome is *carte sans cartographe* (map without cartographer); supermind is *carte qui se cartographie* (map that maps itself). The differentia is the *Cit*-dimension, self-luminous awareness as constitutive, not derivative. *This is the entire equation, in one line: rhizome plus Cit equals supermind. Or: rhizome lived from within itself.*

**The anti-impersonalism critique cuts equally hard from the supermind side.** Where Deleuze and Guattari refuse the divine without qualification, *le divin* is for them a residue of arborescent thinking, the position holds that this refusal is itself a residue: a left-hemispheric / Emissary insistence on impersonal structure as the only legitimate ontological category. Impersonalism *is* an arborescent move when applied to the question of who experiences the rhizome. The rhizome is lived; Deleuze and Guattari say "by assemblages, not persons"; but "assemblage" without an experiencer is a tracing of impersonalist orthodoxy onto the lived plane. The position's correction: the rhizome is lived by *individuated centers of expression* (the *jīva*-as-monad, the psychic being deepening across lives), and these centers are not the empirical ego (which is rightly dissolved) but the *caitya puruṣa*, a third thing that Deleuze's framework cannot name because it has foreclosed the option in advance.

Banerji's *psychisation* is the answer to *devenir-imperceptible*. The flow is real; the empirical ego is correctly dissolved; but what is *in* the flow is not impersonal intensity, it is the divine experiencing itself as individuated, deepening, surrendering. *Vijñāna* is *Cit*-lit *devenir*. The supramental is *plan de consistance* aware of itself.

This is why the position's strategy must be: take Deleuze for the conceptual solvents against arborescent closure, take Aurobindo (via Banerji) for the positive metaphysics of the personal divine and the individual, and integrate them as the user's thesis has, Deleuze does the negative work, Aurobindo does the positive work, and they are compatible because Deleuze's univocity is the structural form and Aurobindo's *vijñāna* is the lived experience.

### Concrete reading sequence: where to go next

A practical sequence for engaging the primary texts after this reader.

**First, *Différence et Répétition* Chapter I, "La différence en elle-même."** This is the philosophical heart of Deleuze. Read it slowly; the prose is dense; do not try to extract a thesis on first pass. Pay particular attention to: the univocity formula and its three moments (Scotus, Spinoza, Nietzsche); the diagnosis of the four shackles (and especially the Hegel section, where Deleuze names the precise structure of difference's domestication); the closing pages on the eternal return as return of difference. Patton's translation is reliable; PUF's French is the standard. About thirty pages; allow several sittings.

**Second, *Mille Plateaux* Introduction "Rhizome."** Twenty-eight pages. Read it twice, once for the six principles, once for the central formulas. The opening line ("each of us was several") is the entire ontology compressed into autobiography. The n−1 formula and the *plan d'organization* / *plan de consistance* distinction are the operative concepts. Then read Plateau 10 ("Devenir-intense, devenir-animal, devenir-imperceptible...") for the becoming doctrine, this is where *devenir-imperceptible* gets its full articulation, and where the contrast with Aurobindonian *psychisation* becomes most clearly visible. Massumi's translation is good; Minuit's French is the standard.

**Third, *Logique du sens* Series 5 (Du sens), Series 24 (De la communication des événements), Series 25 (De l'univocité).** These are the technically demanding series, but they are where the philosophical apparatus is most clearly visible. Lester/Stivale's translation is reliable; the Constantin Boundas edition includes useful editorial apparatus. Series 5 establishes sense as the fourth dimension; Series 24 develops the communication of events; Series 25 explicitly connects univocity and sense. The Lewis Carroll appendix ("Le Schizophrène et la petite fille") is short and technically illuminating, it shows how the regress-paradoxes function and why the surface of sense is what answers them.

**Fourth, *Spinoza et le problème de l'expression* Chapters 1–3 (the expressive triad and immanent causality), Chapter 11 ("L'immanence et les éléments historiques de l'expression"), Chapter 13 ("L'amour intellectuel et la troisième genre").** The first three chapters give the expressive triad and immanent causality; Chapter 11 places Spinoza within the longer history of expression-thinking (Cusanus, Bruno) which is useful for situating the doctrine; Chapter 13 is where Deleuze treats the *amor intellectualis Dei*, read it against Aurobindo's *Cit-Ānanda* and notice exactly where Spinoza-via-Deleuze stops short of *bhakti*. Joughin's translation is the standard.

**Fifth, *Le Pli* Chapter 1 ("Les plis de la matière"), Chapter 4 ("Suffisance"), Chapter 5 ("Incompossibilité, individualité, liberté"), Chapter 6 ("Qu'est-ce qu'un événement?").** The first chapter establishes the fold as operative function; the fourth and fifth give the metaphysics of the monad and the doctrine of incompossibility; the sixth gives the event as the metaphysical unit. Conley's translation is the standard. *Le Pli* is the most accessible of the late works because the Leibnizian armature gives it concrete handles.

**Sixth, *Nietzsche et la philosophie* Chapter II ("Actif et réactif") and Chapter V ("L'éternel retour").** Active/reactive forces and the eternal return as cosmological-and-ethical principle. Tomlinson's translation is the standard. Chapter II gives the diagnostic of modernity's reactive bias; Chapter V gives the selective ethics of eternal return.

**For Banerji's supplementation:** "The Inward Turn" essay, in conjunction with Aurobindo's *Life Divine* Book II, Part I, Chapters 14–16 (the triple status of the supermind and its descent), and *The Synthesis of Yoga* Part IV on integral perfection. This is where the personal divine and the psychic being get their proper articulation, and where the precise place of Deleuze's univocity within the larger Aurobindonian frame becomes visible.

### Key chapters to start with, if time is short

If you have time only for two short readings, take:

1. **D&R Chapter I**, the section on univocity (about ten pages of dense prose, from "Il n'y a jamais eu qu'une proposition ontologique" through the eternal return passage). This is the philosophical heart of Deleuze and the structural homologue of T1.

2. **Mille Plateaux "Rhizome,"** the introduction in full. Twenty-eight pages. The negative work the position needs done.

These two together give Deleuze's univocity (positive thesis) and the rhizome (negative critique of arborescent closure), the compass that the position's brief calls for.

### One final note on reading Deleuze

His prose is difficult, but the difficulty is not gratuitous. He is trying to write in a way that *enacts* the rhizome, not just describes it, which means resisting the trunk-and-branches structure of conventional argument. This makes him hard to summarize and rewards slow re-reading. The strategy that works: read a passage, let it sit, return. Do not try to extract a thesis on first pass. Trust that the philosophical work is being done in the passage itself, not in a paraphrase you could write afterward.

The position has used Deleuze well. He gives the negative work, the dissolution of arborescent closure, the critique of identity-thinking, the conceptual articulation of why unification *as* totalization does violence. But the position should remain clear-eyed about what Deleuze does not give: the personal divine, the individual locus of realization, the planes-of-consciousness ordered by transparency, the *Cit*-dimension of self-luminous awareness, and the surrender from fullness. These come from elsewhere, from Aurobindo, from Abhinavagupta, from the lived realization that Deleuze's prose can describe structurally but cannot inhabit experientially.

Deleuze gives the philosophical form. The position lives the form from within itself.
