## Reader's Orientation

Ādi Śaṅkara (traditional dating c. 788–820 CE; some scholars push the dates earlier, into the 7th century, on the basis of citations in early Pratyabhijñā literature and the absence of references to figures one would expect a 9th-century author to know) is the most influential commentator in the Vedānta tradition and arguably in Indian philosophy as a whole. In a life conventionally given as thirty-two years he produced the three foundational *bhāṣyas* of *Prasthāna-trayī*, the *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya* (henceforth BSB), commentaries on the ten principal *Upaniṣads* (Īśa, Kena, Kaṭha, Praśna, Muṇḍaka, Māṇḍūkya with Gauḍapāda's *Kārikās*, Aitareya, Taittirīya, Bṛhadāraṇyaka, Chāndogya), and the *Bhagavad-Gītā-Bhāṣya*. Across this body of work runs one philosophical thread: *advaita*, non-duality. The Self (*ātman*) and Brahman are *non-different*; the world of names and forms is *mithyā*, sublatable and dependent on Brahman for its being, but **not** *asat* (non-existent). The two-syllable summary slogan most often quoted, *brahma satyaṁ jagan mithyā jīvo brahmaiva nāparaḥ* (a verse from the *Brahma-Jñānāvalī-Mālā* tradition), does not translate as "Brahman is real, the world is unreal." It translates as "Brahman is real; the world is sublatable-dependent; the *jīva* is none other than Brahman." The lexicographic precision matters. *Mithyā* is not *asat*. ### The User's Crucial Corrective

This reader is built around a corrective the textual record itself demands. There is a textbook Śaṅkara, the world-denier, the magician's-illusion theorist, and there is the actual Śaṅkara of the *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya*. They are different.

Cross-engagement: against Abhinavagupta: `sublatable-not-null` rather than `expressive-manifestation` for the manifestation status axis ([primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16)). [register: M / scope: philosophically trained reader; manifestation-status of the world; cross-school comparison staged by this article; sorting convergence from doctrinal difference / language-game: reconstruction across traditions / status: [REAL-DISAGREEMENT] on the manifestation-status axis, with a later partial register-translation only at the user's Shaiva refinement / source: Śaṅkara, BSB 2.1.14, 2.1.33; Abhinavagupta, *Tantrāloka* 1.16]

The actual Śaṅkara:

- Distinguishes *mithyā* (sublatable, dependent) from *asat* (non-existent). A son of a barren woman is *asat*; a pot is *mithyā* from the standpoint of clay, empirically real, holding water and breaking when dropped.
- Calls divine creation *līlā-kaivalyam*, "sheer play", at [BSB 2.1.33](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.33). Creation flows from Brahman's *plenitude* (*āpta-kāmatva*), not deficiency.
- Operates with a three-tier ontology, *pāramārthika* (ultimate), *vyāvahārika* (empirical), *prātibhāsika* (apparent-illusory). The empirical world is not collapsed into the third tier.
- Does not actually use *vivarta* as a developed technical term. *Vivartavāda* is a sub-school crystallisation due primarily to Padmapāda's *Pañcapādikā* and Prakāśātman's *Pañcapādikā-Vivaraṇa*. Sureśvara, Śaṅkara's most theologically conservative direct disciple, avoids the term. Śaṅkara himself uses *brahma-pariṇāma*-language qualified by *Chāndogya* 6.1.4's *vācārambhaṇa* argument.
- Takes *bheda-śrutis* seriously. He does not always reduce them to *artha-vāda*; portions of the BSB treat difference-language as applicable at the *vyāvahārika* level.

**Why this corrective matters.** The user's thesis (T1, T2, T3, T7) requires a Śaṅkara who is not a world-denier. T3 (the climb-up through negation) needs him as the *jñāna-register* baseline. But T7 (divine play through willful self-ignorance) requires that *māyā* not be foreign to Brahman but Brahman's own self-veiling activity. A Śaṅkara who literally denied the world would block the synthesis. The actual Śaṅkara, with the *līlā* sūtras at the center, the *vivarta*-as-sub-school correction in place, and the three-tier ontology kept distinct, is integrable as the *jñāna*-register base on which the realist line (Bhartṛprapañca, Bhāskara, Madhva, Caitanya, Aurobindo) builds. [register: T + M / scope: reader of the present reconstruction; the bearing of Śaṅkara on the user's thesis; retrospective synthesis of multiple Śaṅkara loci; determine whether later realism is blocked or permitted / language-game: meta-methodological framing / status: reconstruction; the continuity claim is the user's move and survives only where later traditions remain outside Śaṅkara's original scope / source: BSB 2.1.14, 2.1.33-36, 1.1.4]

## Part I: *Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya*: The Foundational Argument

The *Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya* (henceforth AB) is Śaṅkara's introduction to the BSB as a whole. It precedes the commentary on 1.1.1; it is not a commentary on any specific sūtra. It is a philosophical prolegomenon, Śaṅkara's argument for *why* Vedāntic inquiry is needed in the first place, what the basic cognitive predicament of the embodied being is, and what the structure of that predicament reveals about the Self. The AB is the most cited and, in our submission, the most misread portion of Śaṅkara's corpus. Read carefully, it establishes that *adhyāsa requires a real substratum*, the rope-snake illusion presupposes a real rope, and uses that requirement to argue for the reality of the witness-consciousness which is *Brahman*. ### The Opening Argument: Subject and Object as Light and Darkness

Śaṅkara opens the *Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya* by saying that subject and object, like light and darkness, cannot be mutually identified, and therefore their attributes cannot be mutually identified either.

[register: P1-E + P / scope: modern comparative theologian versus bhāṣya-reader; source of authority for interpreting Śaṅkara; modern scholarly reconstruction against primary-text exegesis; sort method from doctrine / language-game: modern secondary framing versus classical commentary / status: [REAL-DISAGREEMENT] about method, not about the Sanskrit passage itself / source: Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya opening; Maharaj, *Infinite Paths to Infinite Reality* ch.1]

**Word-for-word.** *yuṣmad*, "you" (object-pole marker); *asmat*, "I" (subject-pole); *pratyaya-gocarayoḥ*, "of the two whose spheres are these cognitions"; *viṣaya-viṣayiṇoḥ*, "of object and subject"; *tamaḥ-prakāśavat*, "like darkness and light"; *viruddha-svabhāvayoḥ*, "having mutually opposed natures"; *itaretara-bhāvānupapattau* (locative absolute), "the mutual identification being impossible"; *siddhāyām*, "this being established"; *tad-dharmāṇām api sutarām*, "even more so of their attributes"; *āsīt*, "it would be the case" (imperfect marking the conclusion as already-given).

**My literal English (primary).** "Of the two whose spheres are the 'you'-cognition and the 'I'-cognition, the object and the subject, opposed-in-nature like darkness and light, once their mutual-identification has been established as impossible, *a fortiori* the mutual-identification of their attributes is impossible too."

**For comparison.** Gambhirananda: "Since the object, the content referred to by the 'you'-cognition, and the subject, the content referred to by the 'I'-cognition, being respectively the seen and the seer, are by nature mutually opposed as light and darkness, and their mutual identification is impossible; it follows even more strongly that the attributes of the one cannot be identified with those of the other." Thibaut (SBE 34, pp. 3–4): "The objects of the conception of 'you' (the not-Self) and the conception of 'I' (the Self), pertaining to the spheres of subject and object respectively, being opposed to each other as much as darkness and light, it follows that the one cannot be the other."

The standard translations gloss over the locative-absolute *itaretara-bhāvānupapattau siddhāyām*, the conditional-priority operator: *given* substance-non-identity, attribute-non-identity follows *a fortiori* (*sutarām*). Śaṅkara is not asserting the substance-opposition; he treats it as already-given (the imperfect *āsīt*), and squeezes the attribute-conclusion from it.

The opening does two things. First, it sets up the *oppositional structure* of subject and object, the 'I'-pole (*cit*) and the 'you'-pole (*jaḍa*, inert) as categorically distinct, like *tamas* and *prakāśa* (technical terms, *prakāśa* for self-luminous consciousness, *tamas* for inert obscurity). Second, it poses the puzzle: given this opposition, *why do we constantly identify them?* "I am tall," "I am angry," "I know," "I exist as this body," the body's, mind's, *antaḥkaraṇa*'s features taken as the Self's. This pervasive cross-identification, *adhyāsa*, is the structure of every ordinary moment of self-experience. ### The Definition of *Adhyāsa*

Śaṅkara defines *adhyāsa* as the appearing, in another locus, of something previously seen, in the form of memory.

**Word-for-word.**
- *smṛti-rūpaḥ*, "of the form of memory." *Smṛti* is the technical term for *memory*. *Adhyāsa* is not fresh perception, not imagination, not inference, it is a *recognition-overlay*: a memory-trace taking on the appearance of present perception.
- *paratra*, "in another [locus]." The thing appears *somewhere else* than where originally cognized.
- *pūrva-dṛṣṭa*, "previously seen." What now appears was at some prior point genuinely cognized; *adhyāsa* is not *ex nihilo*.
- *avabhāsaḥ*, "appearance, shining-forth." Not nothing, a real shining-forth, with real effects (fear, flight). What it is *not* is what it claims to be: an independently existing snake.

**My literal English.** "*Adhyāsa* is the shining-forth, elsewhere, of something previously seen, in the form of memory."

**For comparison.** Gambhirananda: "Superimposition is the apparent presentation, in the form of remembrance, to consciousness of something previously observed in some other thing." Thibaut (SBE 34, p. 4) similar. The literal force of *paratra*, "in another [locus]", licenses the substratum-requirement; both translators dilute it to "in some other thing," losing the locative structure.

*Adhyāsa* is a *structured cognitive event* with three components: (1) *something previously cognized* (the rope-snake illusion presupposes prior experience of a snake); (2) a *substratum* on which the appearance now occurs; (3) the *misidentification*. The structure repeats in body-as-Self illusion: prior body-experience, witness-consciousness as substratum, misidentification of body-content with witness. Take away the substratum, and the appearance has nowhere to occur. *There is no adhyāsa in a void.* [register: Φ + M / scope: mumukṣu; phenomenology of illusion and its ontological presupposition; bhāṣya definition of superimposition; show why witness-consciousness must be real / language-game: doctrinal phenomenology / status: source-claim / source: Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya definition and examples]

These are the three classical examples that will recur throughout the bhāṣya and throughout the entirety of post-Śaṅkaran Advaita literature. Note carefully what they all share: *the substratum is real in every case*. Mother-of-pearl, post, rope, all real, all empirically present, all causally efficacious in the situation. The error is the *overlay*, not the substratum. The substratum is what makes the overlay possible at all. There can be no silver-illusion floating in mid-air; it has to be silver-illusion *on something*. There can be no snake-fear without something to fear it *on*. This is why the AB is, in our submission, the *opposite* of a world-denial argument. The argument for the existence of the witness-consciousness is built precisely on the requirement that *adhyāsa* presupposes a real substratum. The world-as-superimposed presupposes the witness-as-substratum. To deny the substratum is to deny the very logic by which *adhyāsa* is identified.

### The Four Traditional Accounts of *Adhyāsa*

Śaṅkara surveys four accounts of the *mechanism* of *adhyāsa*. His own preferred account selects and recombines elements from (2), (3), and (4) while rejecting (1).

Cross-engagement: same soteriological register, different commitments at the soteric end axis. Alfred North Whitehead: `not-soteriological`, [primary text](cite://whitehead/process-and-reality/1.2). Here: `identity-with-ground`. [register: S / scope: philosophically trained comparative reader; final end of inquiry; cross-tradition comparison staged by this article; prevent smuggling metaphysical resemblance into soteriological equivalence / language-game: comparative philosophy / status: [REAL-DISAGREEMENT] / source: Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya telos; Whitehead, *Process and Reality* I.2]

**(1) The Sāṃkhya *tādātmya* account.** Mutual identification of two real entities, Puruṣa and Prakṛti, both ontologically equal. Śaṅkara rejects this: Brahman alone is *pāramārthika-sat*. The Sāṃkhya phenomenology of distinguishable poles is right; its dual ontology is wrong.

**(2) The "two-things-appearing-as-one" account.** *Adhyāsa* is seeing one thing where there is only the other, a confusion of substrate. The substrate is real; the appearance overlays it. Śaṅkara accepts the structural insight (substrate real, overlay borrowed from prior cognition) but not the implication that the overlay is a fully independent real entity. The snake-overlay is *avabhāsa*, appearance.

**(3) The "attribute-transfer" account.** *Adhyāsa* is misattribution of properties across the ontological divide, body's pain experienced as Self's pain, *antaḥkaraṇa*'s change as witness's change. This correctly identifies the *ego-structure* as an *adhyāsa*-structure: "I am heavy," "I am happy" are attribute-transfers from non-witness to witness.

**(4) The "one-thing-appearing-as-another" account.** Pure *ābhāsa*, the appearing content borrows reality from the substrate without itself being independently real. There is no independent snake; only a rope appearing-as snake. This is closest to Śaṅkara's *anirvacanīya* doctrine: the appearing snake is neither *sat* (no independent existence) nor *asat* (it appears, has effects).

**Śaṅkara's own resolution** synthesizes (2), (3), (4): *adhyāsa* is the appearance of attributes across the subject-object divide, *real-as-appearance* but *unreal as identification*. The structure is already that of *mithyātva*: dependent on a real substrate, sublatable when the substrate is correctly seen. This lets Śaṅkara say at once: (i) ordinary experience genuinely occurs; (ii) the witness-substrate is real; (iii) the cross-divide identification is illegitimate; (iv) the cure is correct understanding, not abolition of experience. ### The *Sākṣi-Cetana* / *Aham-Pratyaya* Connection

Generalizing the substratum-requirement: the mutual superimposition of subject and object *requires* a real, unchanging, luminous *sākṣi-cetana* that is itself not subject to *adhyāsa*. The Vedāntins hold that there is an innermost *pratyak-cetana* / *sākṣin* serving as the *adhiṣṭhāna* for all superimposition, never itself superimposed upon.

The decisive textual nexus locking *sākṣin* and *aham-pratyaya* together is the bhāṣya on [BSB 1.1.4](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.4), the *aupaniṣada-puruṣa* passage:

At [BSB 1.1.4](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.4), Śaṅkara says that the Upaniṣadic Puruṣa is the true content of the "I"-cognition.

**Word-for-word.** *aham-pratyaya-viṣayaḥ*, "having the 'I'-cognition as its content"; *aupaniṣadaḥ puruṣaḥ*, "the Puruṣa of the Upaniṣads."

**My literal English.** "The Puruṣa-of-the-Upaniṣads is the [true] content of the 'I'-cognition."

This is the load-bearing identification. The *aupaniṣada-puruṣa*, the witness-consciousness, *kūṭastha*, *sarva-bhūtastha*, *sama*, *eka*, is what the 'I'-cognition is actually about, even when the empirical *aham-pratyaya* misidentifies body or *antaḥkaraṇa* as its referent. The ego-*aham* is the *adhyāsa*-overlay; the *sākṣi-cetana* is the real referent, reached *through* the I-cognition (not behind it). Śaṅkara's *vyatirekeṇa*-phrasing in the same passage makes the point exact: the *sākṣin* is *not accessed separately from* the agent-pole of the *aham-pratyaya*. The cure for *adhyāsa* is not abolition of the 'I'-cognition but its correct deciphering.

**Logical structure of the AB's central argument.**

- P1: All *adhyāsa* requires a real, non-superimposed substratum.
- P2: The mutual superimposition of subject and object, ordinary cognitive life, is *adhyāsa*.
- C: Therefore there is a real, non-superimposed substratum, the *sākṣi-cetana*.
- Corollary: This substratum is Brahman / Ātman, as the *abheda-śrutis* teach (*tat tvam asi*, *aham brahmāsmi*, *ayam ātmā brahma*, *prajñānaṁ brahma*).

The *aham-pratyaya* is the experiential index of this witness. The ordinary *aham* is the ego, body-, role-, mind-attributes assimilated to the witness ("I am tall," "I am sad"). Beneath it is the witness-*aham*, the very light by which superimposition is seen, the *I* that notices the sadness without being sad.

**For T7, the consequential reading.** The argument does *not* say the world-appearance is nothing. The rope-snake is a real appearance with real effects. The snake is *mithyā*, not *asat*. *Eliminating the snake-error does not eliminate the rope*; it reveals the rope. Analogously, *jñāna* does not eliminate the world; it reveals the world *as Brahman*, not elimination but transparent recognition. The Vivaraṇa school will exploit this structure: Brahman is *both āśraya and viṣaya* of avidyā, substrate and content, with no second locus required (Part VIII). The user's thesis adopts this and, via Kashmir Shaivism's *svātantrya*, inverts the evaluation: the snake-appearance is not a defect to flee but the divine's willed self-veiling, *līlā* at the cognitive register. ## Part II: [BSB 2.1.14](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14): *Tad-ananyatva* and the Clay-Pot Argument

We move now from the prolegomenon (the AB) to the most ontologically central sūtra of the BSB for the question of cause-effect non-difference. [BSB 2.1.14](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14) is the sūtra that establishes, in dialogue with *Chāndogya* 6.1.4, the ontological structure of *ananyatva* (non-difference) of effect from cause. This is the sūtra at which the textbook caricature most often crystallizes into the world-as-illusion picture, and it is also the sūtra at which the careful reader sees most clearly that the caricature is wrong.

### The Sūtra

The sūtra at [BSB 2.1.14](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14) states, in English, that the effect is non-other than the cause, as shown by the "basis" passage and similar scriptural texts.

**Word-for-word.** *tat*, "that" (= the effect); *ananyatvam*, "non-otherness, non-difference" (literally, *an-anya-tvam*, the abstract noun of "not-other-ness"); *ārambhaṇa-śabdādibhyaḥ* (ablative plural), "from the words used for creation/beginning, etc."

**My literal English (primary).** "Its not-being-other-than that [cause] is established from the 'ārambhaṇa' wording and the like."

**For comparison.** Gambhirananda: "The non-difference [of the effect] from that [cause is established] from the words used for creation and so on." Thibaut (SBE 34, p. 320): "The non-difference of them (i.e. of cause and effect) results from such terms as 'origin' and the like."

The literal force of *ananyatvam*, "not-being-other-than-ness," is the corrective. *Ananyatva* is *negative*: the absence-of-otherness. *Abheda* would be *positive*: the presence-of-non-difference, i.e., identity. To say the effect is *non-other* than the cause is *not* to say it is identical with the cause; it is to deny it the status of a self-established second-something. Both Gambhirananda and Thibaut translate as if Bādarāyaṇa had written *abheda*; he did not. He wrote *ananyatva*. [register: M + SL / scope: trained Vedāntin; cause-effect relation in BSB 2.1.14; sūtra exegesis governing ontology; prevent lexical flattening of non-otherness into identity / language-game: sūtra gloss with semantic discrimination / status: source-claim / source: BSB 2.1.14]

Bādarāyaṇa is responding to the *pūrva-pakṣa*: if the world is *different* from Brahman, Brahman is not the sole cause; if *non-different*, the variety collapses. The sūtra cuts the knot with *ananyatva*: the effect does not have being independent of the cause; it is not "another" thing standing in self-established existence. The pot is *ananya* from clay, not identical with the lump (you can't carry water in a lump), but non-other than clay in its very being.

### The *Vācārambhaṇa* Argument (Chāndogya 6.1.4)

Śaṅkara's bhāṣya appeals at length to *Chāndogya Upaniṣad* 6.1.4, the famous passage in Uddālaka Āruṇi's instruction to Śvetaketu. The relevant clause:

At *Chāndogya Upaniṣad* 6.1.4, Uddālaka says that by knowing one lump of clay, all things made of clay are known, because the modification is only a matter of verbal designation and clay alone is real.

**Word-for-word.** *yathā*, "just as"; *saumya*, "dear one"; *ekena mṛt-piṇḍena*, "by one lump of clay"; *sarvaṁ mṛṇmayam*, "everything made of clay"; *vijñātaṁ syāt*, "would be known"; *vācārambhaṇam*, "[that which has] verbal-designation as its grasping" (*vāc* + *ārambhaṇa*, "speech" + "grasping"); *vikāraḥ*, "modification"; *nāmadheyam*, "name-designation"; *mṛttikā iti eva satyam*, "clay alone is real."

**My literal English.** "Just as, dear one, by a single lump of clay all things made of clay would become known: the modification is something that begins in speech, a mere name; 'clay' alone is what is real."

The hinge phrase *vācārambhaṇaṁ vikāro nāmadheyam* attributes to the modification the predicate "held only by being spoken-of." The modification is real-as-articulated; its means of cognitive grasp is verbal designation, not independent self-establishment. *Mṛttikā ity eva satyam* then says: clay, the substance, is *satyam* (real) in the unconditional sense. This is the lexical seed of the three-tier ontology: empirical *nāma-rūpa* is real as *nāma-rūpa*; the substratum is real as substance; they are not in competition, just register-distinct.

Śaṅkara takes the *vikāra* (pot, bowl, jug) to lack independent being. Its being is the clay's being articulated in *nāma-rūpa*. But *vācārambhaṇam* does not mean the pot is non-existent. The pot holds water, breaks, is bought and sold. What it lacks is *self-established* being. Its being is *para-sattā*, the being-of-another. This is precisely *mithyātva*: ontological dependence, *vyāvahārikally* operative, *pāramārthikally* not self-established.

### The Critical Distinction: *Ananyatva* ≠ *Abhāva*

> *ananyatva* (non-otherness, ontological dependence) ≠ *abhāva* (absence, non-existence)

*Abhāva* is non-occurrence: hare's horns, barren woman's son. *Ananyatva* is the ontological structure of a really-occurring entity: the pot, the world, the body, these occur, have effects, are *ananya* from their cause. Confusing these, translating *mithyā* as "unreal" in the *abhāva*-sense, produces the world-denier caricature. The pot is *vyāvahārika-satya*; the merchant who sold it did not sell you nothing. The discovery that the pot's being is clay's being alters not the empirical functioning but the ontological understanding.

### Application to the World-Brahman Relation

Generalizing: the world is *ananya* from Brahman, Brahman's being articulated in name-and-form. *Brahma-jñāna* delivers not the world's disappearance but the recognition that what seemed an independent secondness is Brahman expressing itself. *Idaṁ sarvaṁ brahma* and *neha nānāsti kiñcana* (BU 4.4.19) say the same thing: the apparent independent secondness is not ultimate, while the apparent world as appearance or articulation is precisely Brahman's being. [register: M + S / scope: mumukṣu moving toward jñāna; world-Brahman relation; bhāṣya inference from BSB 2.1.14 and allied śruti; liberation through right apprehension of dependence / language-game: doctrinal generalization from sūtra to śruti / status: source-claim / source: BSB 2.1.14; *Chāndogya* 6.1.4; BU 4.4.19]

**For T2 and T7.** The variety of *nāma-rūpa* is not error but Brahman's expressive mode. What is removed is not the variety but the misperception of it as independent self-established secondnesses. Bhāskara's *bhedābheda*, Madhva's emphasis on *bheda*, Caitanya's *acintya-bhedābheda*, and Aurobindo's later realist Advaita are not excluded by [BSB 2.1.14](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14) carefully read; they are excluded only by the caricature in which *ananyatva* is collapsed into *abhāva*. [register: M + T / scope: reader of the present reconstruction; downstream Vedāntic options opened by BSB 2.1.14; retrospective synthesis across traditions; test whether Śaṅkara blocks or permits later realist developments / language-game: cross-school reconstruction / status: reconstruction; Bhāskara direct witness remains [NOT YET RETRIEVED], Madhva/Caitanya/Aurobindo comparisons preserve later disagreements on the same metaphysical axis / source: BSB 2.1.14; Bhāskara [NOT YET RETRIEVED direct witness]; Madhva, *Tattvodyota* opening and *Mithyātvānumāna-Khaṇḍana*; Jīva, *Paramātma-Sandarbha* §§42-45; Aurobindo, *Life Divine* I.4-6]

## Part III: [BSB 2.1.33–36](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.33-36): *Lokavat Tu Līlā-Kaivalyam*, the Līlā Sūtras

If the AB is the foundation of Śaṅkara's argument and [BSB 2.1.14](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14) is its ontological center, then [BSB 2.1.33](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.33) is its theological-cosmological heart. This is the sūtra at which the textbook caricature most catastrophically fails: the same Śaṅkara who is supposed to have denied the world here gives that world its most explicit and most jubilant theological license. The world is Brahman's *līlā-kaivalyam*, sheer play, and play arises from the *plenitude* of one whose desires are fulfilled.

This is the textual anchor for the user's T7 (divine play through willful self-ignorance), T9 (divine purpose as unity-in-apparent-multiplicity), T10 (surrender from fullness, not poverty), and T12 (decentralized philosophy, with *līlā* as the modality of the divine's freedom). Without these sūtras, the user's thesis would have to be argued against Śaṅkara; with them, it is argued in continuity with Śaṅkara, as the natural extension of his own scriptural commentary.

### The Problem the Sūtras Address

The *pūrva-pakṣa*: if Brahman is *āpta-kāma* (one whose desires are fulfilled), why create at all? Creation presupposes need or external purpose; Brahman has neither. Therefore either (a) Brahman cannot create (*nirīśvaravāda*); or (b) some purpose drives creation, contradicting *āpta-kāmatva*; or (c) creation is a defect from a perfect being. The same dilemma appears in later Platonist, Hegelian, and Christian discussions of plenitude and manifestation. Bādarāyaṇa and Śaṅkara's answer is *līlā*. [register: T + M / scope: philosophically reflective reader; purpose of creation; sūtra-level objection and reply; clear conceptual space for non-needful manifestation / language-game: pūrva-pakṣa and siddhānta within bhāṣya argument / status: source-claim for Śaṅkara's side, reconstruction for the Plotinus-Hegel-Christian comparison / source: BSB 2.1.32-33]

### [BSB 2.1.32](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.32): The Objection Stated

[BSB 2.1.32](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.32) states the objection in English: Brahman cannot create because action would require some purpose.

**Word-for-word.** *na*, "not"; *prayojanavattvāt*, "because of [requiring] purpose."

**English (Gambhirananda).** "[Brahman cannot create] because of [the requirement of] having some purpose." (The *pūrva-pakṣa*.) Thibaut similar.

The sūtra states the objection in sharpest form: a *kṛta-kṛtya* has no motive; without motive, no action. Śaṅkara accepts that on the standard *kāma-prayojana* analysis of action, Brahman's creating is impossible. The resolution requires a different analysis, one that does not presuppose external purpose. That is what 2.1.33 supplies.

### [BSB 2.1.33](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.33): The Decisive Sūtra

[BSB 2.1.33](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.33) gives the reply in English: as in ordinary life, creation is sheer play and nothing else.

**Word-for-word.**
- *lokavat*, "as in the world"; the *-vat* indicates analogy with empirical observation.
- *tu*, "but" (adversative, marking the *siddhānta* against 2.1.32's *pūrva-pakṣa*).
- *līlā*, "play, sport; effortless activity without external motive." Used of children's play, a king at leisure, dance, the gestures of love.
- *kaivalyam*, "alone-ness, completeness-in-itself"; built on *kevala* (alone, only). In Yoga-Sāṃkhya it is the technical term for the Puruṣa's *summum bonum* at liberation. Here as *karmadhāraya* with *līlā*: "play alone, nothing but play," with exclusive force.

**My literal English (primary).** "But it is nothing but play, as in the world."

**For comparison.** Gambhirananda: "But [Brahman's creative activity] is mere sport (*līlā-kaivalyam*), as [is seen] in the world." Thibaut (SBE 34, p. 362): "mere sport, such as we see in ordinary life."

Both render *līlā-kaivalyam* as "mere sport," which loses the *kaivalyam* charge. "Mere" in English carries a deflationary force that *kaivalya* does not. *Kaivalya* is the *summum bonum*, the highest, complete-in-itself. The literal "play-and-nothing-else" preserves the exclusivity without smuggling in deflation. The play is exhaustively play, not merely play.

The activity is *līlā-kaivalyam*, sheer play, no surplus motive. *Lokavat*: as a king who lacks for nothing plays chess; as a child plays; as a dancer dances; as a singer sings, so Brahman creates. Not from need or compulsion. Out of fullness, as expression.

### Śaṅkara's Bhāṣya on 2.1.33: The Full Argument

Śaṅkara's commentary unfolds in four moves (Gambhirananda pp. 359–363; Thibaut SBE 34 pp. 362–364).

**Move 1, Human play as analog.** Even among humans we observe activity not driven by external purpose: a king playing chess, a wealthy man building gardens, a dancer dancing, *svataḥ-siddha-sukha*, self-sufficing in delight.

Śaṅkara's supporting point is that even one whose desires are fulfilled is still seen to act, and such action can be purposeless except for play itself.

"It is not the case that one whose ends are fulfilled never acts. Activity is in fact observed that has no purpose beyond play itself."

**Move 2, *Līlā-kaivalyam*.** The *-kaivalyam* echoes Yoga's *summum bonum*: this play is the final state, not a means to anything beyond. Creation is not a mistake to be undone, not a fall to recover from. It is Brahman's ultimate state expressed dynamically.

**Move 3, No need, no compulsion, no lack.**

Śaṅkara then makes the point explicitly: Brahman's activity is not undertaken with any fruit in view.

"Brahman's activity is not undertaken with any fruit in view." Creation is Brahman's *svabhāva*, not an external project. Brahman is not first still and then made to act; Brahman is stillness and self-expression in one. This is what Aurobindo's *Saccidānanda* will later recover and what Prigogine's "from being to becoming" will re-open from within physics: static and dynamic are two poises of one Real.

**Move 4, The thesis.** *Līlā-kaivalyam* is not concession but positive thesis. Brahman creates because it is Brahman's nature to express, manifest, play, not from need. The world is the natural expression of Brahman's fullness, *brahmaṇaḥ līlā-mātraṁ*. This is the anchor for T7 and T9: *māyā* is not foreign defect but the modality of the divine's play-activity. The textbook caricature suppresses this by glossing past 2.1.33 or treating *līlā* as mere figure. *Līlā-kaivalyam* is the *siddhānta*. [register: M + T / scope: philosophically reflective reader; creation as divine activity; BSB creation adhikaraṇa; establish plenitude rather than lack as the purpose-grammar / language-game: bhāṣya-register doctrinal exposition / status: source-claim / source: BSB 2.1.33-34]

### [BSB 2.1.34](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.34): Brahman's Plenitude

[BSB 2.1.34](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.34) adds that Brahman is endowed with every power, as scripture shows.

**Word-for-word.** *sarvopetā*, "endowed with everything"; *tad-darśanāt*, "from scripture's seeing of that."

**English (Gambhirananda).** "And [Brahman is] endowed with all [powers], as is seen [in scripture]."

The sūtra establishes the *plenitude* from which the *līlā* flows. Brahman is *sarva-śaktimat*, *sarva-jña*, *sarva-kāraka*. The *līlā* is the sport of plenitude, a being that lacks nothing plays not to gain but because play expresses fullness. This anchors T10 (*surrender from fullness, not poverty*); Abhinavagupta's *pūrṇatva-icchā* is structurally present in Śaṅkara already.

### [BSB 2.1.35–36](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.35-36): Variety, Suffering, and the *Karma* Account

[BSB 2.1.35](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.35) handles the charge of *vaiṣamya* (partiality) and *nairghṛṇya* (cruelty); 2.1.36 the charge that Brahman destroys its creation-children.

[BSB 2.1.35](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.35) says that the charges of partiality and cruelty do not apply, because Brahman's dispensation is conditioned by karma, as scripture shows.

**English (Gambhirananda).** "[The charges of] partiality and cruelty do not apply, because of [Brahman's] dependence [on karma]; so scripture declares."

At the *vyāvahārika* level, world-variety is explained by *karma*-variety. Brahman as *jagat-kāraṇa* is *sāpekṣa* on *jīva-karma* when allotting empirical conditions; differences are grounded in karmic precedents the *jīvas* themselves established.

[BSB 2.1.36](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.36) adds that the absence of a first differentiated karma is no objection, because the cycle is beginningless.

**English (Gambhirananda).** "If it be objected that [karma cannot ground variety because] there is no karma-distinction at the original creation, no, because of beginninglessness."

The *anāditva* move blocks the regress: each cycle of creation-dissolution inherits the previous; the cycle as a whole has no first member.

**The two-level framing.** Śaṅkara operates simultaneously at both levels. *Vyāvahārikally*, variety is explained by *karma*, suffering by past actions, ethics has real grip. *Pāramārthikally*, the whole cycle is Brahman's *līlā*. Not in contradiction: the empirical *karma* mechanism is what *līlā* looks like from inside; the transcendent *līlā* is what *karma* causally is from the source. This is structurally T8: at the empirical level the *jīva* must act lawfully, not arrogate the cosmic perspective; but at the transcendent level the whole is divine expressive activity. Empirical lawful unfolding *is* the divine's mode of expression.

### Why the Līlā Sūtras are Decisive (T7 Anchored)

T7, *Maya is itself a manifestation of the divine*, is sometimes presented as a Kashmir-Shaiva or Aurobindonian importation argued *against* Śaṅkara. It is not. T7 is anchored in Śaṅkara's own *līlā-kaivalyam* sūtras, read non-deflationarily.

The deflationary reading: *līlā* is metaphor; creation is metaphysically gratuitous, irrelevant to the *jñāni*. The non-deflationary reading: *līlā* is the precise theological term for activity-of-fullness; Brahman's creating is the expressive activity of *pūrṇatva*. Textual grounds favor the non-deflationary: the *-kaivalyam* exclusivist marker, *sarvopetā*'s plenitude, and *lokavat*'s analogy with actual joyful human play. Śaṅkara makes *līlā* the *siddhānta* of an entire *adhikaraṇa*. Once 2.1.33 is read non-deflationarily, the alleged opposition between Śaṅkara and the bhakti/Vaiṣṇava traditions shrinks. The AB establishes that *adhyāsa* requires a real substratum; the *līlā* sūtras tell us what the substratum is doing. Manifestation is the dynamic side of the same *sat-cit* the AB established as witness-substrate. The realist trajectory (Bhartṛprapañca → Bhāskara → Madhva → Caitanya → Aurobindo) is the patient unfolding of what was already implicit in *līlā-kaivalyam*.

## Part IV: [BSB 1.1.4](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.4): *Tat Tu Samanvayāt*, the Harmonisation Sūtra

We turn back from *adhyāya* 2 to the opening of the bhāṣya, to the sūtra that establishes Śaṅkara's *method* of reading scripture. [BSB 1.1.4](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.4) is the *samanvaya-adhikaraṇa*, the section establishing that all the apparently divergent statements of the Upaniṣads converge upon a single purport: the knowledge of Brahman. The methodological doctrine here developed is structurally the foundation of the user's T1 (the darśana register thesis): different textual registers are harmonised under a single *tātparya* without eliminating any.

### The Sūtra

[BSB 1.1.4](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.4) states that Brahman is established from the convergence of the Upaniṣadic texts.

**Word-for-word.** *tat*, "that" (= Brahman, set up by 1.1.1); *tu*, "but" (adversative, marking *siddhānta*); *samanvayāt*, "from convergence-of-meaning."

**My literal English (primary).** "But That [— Brahman —] [is established] from [the texts'] convergence-of-meaning."

**For comparison.** Gambhirananda: "But that [Brahman is the purport of all Vedāntic texts] because of the harmony [of all texts pointing to one meaning]." Thibaut (SBE 34, p. 18): "because it is connected [with Scripture] as the purport [thereof]."

*Samanvaya* = *sam* (joint) + *anvaya* (syntactic-semantic linkage by which words cohere into meaning, technical in Mīmāṃsaka semantics). Generalized from sentence to corpus: the Upaniṣadic corpus is *anvaya*-linked toward a single *tātparya*. The literal "convergence-of-meaning" preserves the philological exactness Gambhirananda's "harmony" and Thibaut's "purport" expand interpretively. This is not merely scriptural exegesis; it is a *register-discipline*, the model for harmonising divergent darśanas (T1).

### What the Sūtra Does

The Upaniṣads on a flat reading say many things: some passages describe Brahman *nirviśeṣa* (*neti neti*, *avāṅmanasagocaram*, *aprāṇaṁ amanasaṁ śubhraṁ*); some *saviśeṣa* (*satya-jñāna-ananta*, *sarva-śaktimat*, *īśvara*); some describe creation; some affirm plurality (*bheda-śrutis* like *dvā suparṇā sayujā sakhāyā*, MU 3.1.1). Śaṅkara's *samanvaya* principle: all Vedāntic texts have Brahman as their *eka-tātparya*. Apparent contradictions resolve by distinguishing textual registers.

### *Mukhya-śruti* vs. *Gauṇa* / *Ārtha-vāda*

**(1) *Mukhya-śruti*.** Direct literal affirmations of non-dual Brahman, the *mahāvākyas*: *prajñānaṁ brahma*, *aham brahmāsmi*, *tat tvam asi*, *ayam ātmā brahma*, *sarvaṁ khalv idaṁ brahma*. Taken in *mukhya-vṛtti* without indirection.

**(2) *Gauṇa-śruti* / *upacāra*.** Secondary/metaphorical, where literal meaning conflicts with *mukhya-vṛtti* (e.g., calling Brahman *nara-siṁha*).

**(3) *Ārtha-vāda*.** Laudatory amplification, true as praise, not as independent metaphysics.

**(4) *Upāsanā-artha*.** Passages describing Brahman with attributes as meditative supports, register-correct for the *upāsaka* needing a focused object.

The *mahāvākyas* are *mukhya*; descriptive passages often *upāsanā-artha*; some are *ārtha-vāda*; the single *tātparya* is non-dual Brahman.

### Śaṅkara Does NOT Always Reduce *Bheda-Śrutis* to *Ārtha-Vāda*

The textbook account, that Śaṅkara reduces *bheda-śrutis* to *ārtha-vāda*, is a half-truth. He uses several strategies:

**(a) *Ārtha-vāda* reduction.** Some *bheda-śrutis* are read as praise-passages.

**(b) *Upāsanā-artha* recontextualisation.** Many are read as *upāsanā-vidhi*, meditative supports register-true for the *upāsaka*.

**(c) *Vyāvahārika* affirmation.** This is what the textbook account misses. In *adhyāya* 3, Śaṅkara *affirms* the *bheda-śruti*'s teaching at the *vyāvahārika* level. Difference is *vyāvahārika-satya*; non-difference is *pāramārthika-satya*. He does not eliminate difference; he registers it.

**(d) *Nāma-rūpa-viveka*.** Empirical *bheda* is *nāma-rūpa*: real-as-name-and-form, not real-as-independent-substance. This is *vācārambhaṇa* applied to inter-entity difference.

Śaṅkara's method does *not* require eliminating all difference as illusion; it requires registering it. This is *register-discipline*, exactly what T1 articulates.

### Why This Matters for T1 and T4

T1 and T4 generalize *samanvaya* from text-registers to *darśana*-registers: Advaita is register-correct for the comprehending pole; Viśiṣṭādvaita for the qualified-non-dual; Madhva for the difference-affirming; Sāṃkhya for the metaphysics-of-form; Nyāya for categorial realism. Each is register-true within its own properly delimited scope; none is simple error. Śaṅkara's *samanvaya*, generalized across the Indian philosophical landscape, is T1. ## Part V: [BSB 3.2.21](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/3.2.21): *Neha Nānāsti Kiñcana*, *Bādhita* vs *Abhāva*

The third major BSB locus we examine is the area around 3.2.21, where Śaṅkara handles the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* 4.4.19 statement *neha nānāsti kiñcana*, "here there is no manyness whatsoever." This is the passage that the realist critics (Madhva, Rāmānuja) seize on as evidence that Advaita collapses the world into nothing. It is also the passage at which the *bādhita*-vs-*abhāva* distinction is most consequential.

### The Scriptural Background

At *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* 4.4.19, the text says that whoever knows Brahman becomes Brahman, and that here there is no plurality whatsoever.

Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.4.19. **My literal English.** "He who knows That as Brahman becomes Brahman alone. Here, not-anything as manyness exists." (For comparison: Mādhavānanda, "Whoever truly knows that Brahman becomes Brahman . Here there is no multiplicity whatsoever.")

The literal *neha nānāsti kiñcana* unpacks word-for-word: *na* (not) + *iha* (here) + *nānā* (manyness, plurality) + *asti* (is) + *kiñcana* (anything-whatsoever). The *kiñcana* is the existential quantifier, "anything-whatsoever", and what is denied is *nānā*-being, *manyness-being*, of any token. The negation is over the *manyness-claim*, not over *being-as-such*. "Here" (*iha*) is the *jñāna-dṛṣṭi*-context: from the standpoint of the one who has just been told *brahmaiva bhavati*. The translation "no multiplicity whatsoever" reads correctly; the negation is *of the manyness-predicate*, not *of being*. The pots remain; their independent-secondness does not.

The line *neha nānāsti kiñcana*, "here there is no manyness whatsoever," is the *locus classicus* of Advaita's apparent world-denial. Read flat, it appears to say: there is nothing other than Brahman, no distinctions, no plurality. Madhva and Rāmānuja take this as evidence that Śaṅkara's reading collapses the world into Brahman *tout court*, eliminating the plurality of *jīvas* and forms that the rest of scripture and ordinary experience attest. ### Śaṅkara's Reading

Śaṅkara's commentary on the related BSB passages (3.2.21 and surrounding) and on the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka* itself (in his BU-Bhāṣya) handles this with a precise distinction between the two *dṛṣṭis* (perspectives, vantage-points):

**Pāramārthika-dṛṣṭi.** From the ultimate vantage, Brahman alone *is*, and all difference is *adhyasita*, superimposed. At this level, "there is no multiplicity here." But this is a statement *made from within* the *pāramārthika* perspective, not a metaphysical claim about what literally exists in the world of names and forms. It is what Śaṅkara calls *jñāna-dṛṣṭyā* (from the vantage of knowledge): from the perspective of one who has realized non-duality, there is no manyness.

**Vyāvahārika-dṛṣṭi.** From the empirical vantage, the world of names and forms is real and operative. Empirical distinctions are *vyāvahārika-satya*, true in their register. Pots and persons are distinct from each other, *jīvas* are distinct from *Īśvara*, here from there, now from then. None of this is denied at the empirical level.

The *neha nānāsti* statement does not abolish the *vyāvahārika*; it relativizes it. It says: the manyness, real-at-its-own-level, does not have ultimate ontological independence. At the level of *being-as-such*, there is only Brahman; the manyness is Brahman's own being articulated. The statement is not "there are no pots" but "there is nothing-other-than-Brahman; the pots' being is Brahman's being expressed in pot-form."

### The Crucial Distinction: *Bādhita* vs *Abhāva*

Here is where the precision of Sanskrit ontological vocabulary becomes load-bearing. Śaṅkara distinguishes between two ways something can fail to have ultimate ontological standing:

**(1) *Bādhita* (sublatable, sublated).** That which, when subjected to higher inquiry, is *revealed* as not having the independent being it seemed to have. The snake-in-the-rope is *bādhita*: when the rope is seen, the snake-cognition cannot sustain its claim to independent being. But the rope remains. The snake-error is sublated; the substratum is revealed.

Crucially: *bādhita* does not mean "non-existent." It means "not-having-independent-being-when-viewed-from-the-correct-vantage." The snake-as-independent-snake is sublated; but the snake-as-appearance-on-the-rope was a real cognitive event, with real causal effects (fear, behavior). What is *bādhita* is the *claim of independent being*, not the *occurrence of the appearance*.

**(2) *Abhāva* (absence, non-existence).** Non-existence proper. The horns of a hare. The son of a barren woman. A square circle. These are *abhāva* / *asat*: they never were, never can be, never appeared. The category is reserved for what is *logically* or *categorically* impossible.

The world is *bādhita*, not *abhāva*. The Upaniṣadic *neha nānāsti kiñcana* asserts the *bādhya*-character of the manyness, its sublatability at the level of ultimate inquiry, not its *abhāva*. The manyness is real-as-appearance, real-at-the-vyāvahārika-level, sublatable when one views it from the *pāramārthika* vantage and sees that its independent being is borrowed from Brahman's being.

This distinction is what defines *mithyātva* in Śaṅkara's technical usage. *Mithyā* = *bādhya*, sublatable. Not *abhāva*, not *asat*. The world is *mithyā* in the precise sense that its claim to independent being does not survive ultimate inquiry, but it has *vyāvahārika-sattā*, empirical being, all the way down to the moment of *brahma-jñāna*, and even afterward (the *jīvanmukta* continues to live, eat, walk, teach).

### *Bhajagovindam*'s Lurk

Even at this height of the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka*, the bhāṣya does not abolish the world. The *jñāni* who realizes *neha nānāsti kiñcana* does not vanish from the world; the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka* itself continues with extensive narrative about Yājñavalkya's life, his two wives, his teaching activities, his eventual departure. The realization does not abolish the empirical scaffolding; it transforms its meaning. The same Yājñavalkya who teaches *neha nānāsti kiñcana* takes leave of his wife and goes forth to *parivrāj*, a continued, embodied, ethical action.

This is the Śaṅkara who can also write *Bhaja Govindam*. The realization of non-duality is not the end of action; it is the transformation of the action's meaning. The *jīvanmukta* acts, but the action is *līlā*, sport, expression of fullness, no longer ego-driven striving. We will return to this in Part X.

## Part VI: *Mithyā* ≠ *Asat*: The Three-Tier Ontology

We now consolidate the lexicographic and ontological precision that the corrective requires. This part is the most technically precise of the reader, because the entire interpretive contest between the textbook caricature and the actual Śaṅkara turns on exactly these distinctions. *Mithyā* is not *asat*. *Anirvacanīya* is not "vague" or "incoherent." The three-tier ontology is not a metaphysical multiplication of entities but a register-discrimination structurally identical to the one Śaṅkara's *samanvaya* method already deploys at the textual level.

### The Three Tiers

Śaṅkara's BSB operates with a three-tier structure of being. The terminology is fully developed in later Advaita (most explicitly in Dharmarāja Adhvarīndra's *Vedānta-Paribhāṣā*, c. 17th c.), but the *substantive* tripartition is implicit throughout the BSB and the Upaniṣad-bhāṣyas.

**(1) *Pāramārthika-sattā*, ultimate-level being.** Brahman alone, *nirviśeṣa* (without differentiating qualities), *nirvikāra* (without modification), *nityā* (eternal), *advitīyam* (without-a-second). This alone is *sat* in the absolute, unconditional sense, being that depends on nothing, being that is not modified by anything, being that is identical with consciousness and bliss.

**(2) *Vyāvahārika-sattā*, empirical/conventional being.** The world of names, forms, causes, effects, persons, transactions, knowledge, error. Pots and stars, governments and grammars, perceptions and inferences. This is *sat* at its own level, functionally operative, causally efficacious, subject to its own internal lawfulness. Not dismissed as nothing. *Vyāvahārika-sattā* is the level at which all empirical Indian philosophy, Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Mīmāṃsā, Sāṃkhya, does its work, and Śaṅkara *grants* this work its register-validity.

**(3) *Prātibhāsika-sattā*, apparent/illusory being.** The snake in the rope, silver in shell, water in mirage, dream-objects. These are real-as-appearances within a particular cognitive episode but are *canceled even at the empirical level* once the substratum is correctly perceived. The snake is sublated when you see the rope (you don't need to attain *brahma-jñāna* to dispel the rope-snake illusion; ordinary daylight suffices).

The crucial point: *mithyā* in Śaṅkara's technical usage refers *primarily* to the **second** level when viewed in light of the first. The world is *vyāvahārika-satya* but *pāramārthika-mithyā*. It is not collapsed to *prātibhāsika* (mere illusion within an empirical episode) or to *abhāva* (non-existence). The world has its own ontological dignity at its own level, operatively real, causally efficacious, ethically engaged-with. Only at the level of *ultimate* inquiry does its claim to *independent self-established being* fail.

### *Anirvacanīya*: Lexicographic Precision

The most important technical term in Śaṅkara's ontology of the world is *anirvacanīya*, literally, *an-* (not) + *nirvacanīya* (declarable, definable). Built on the verbal root *vac* (to speak) with the prefix *nir-* (out, definitively): *nirvacana* is "definitive declaration"; *anirvacanīya* is "indeclarable, indeterminable."

The standard formulaic usage: *sadasad-anirvacanīyam*, "indeterminable as either *sat* or *asat*." This is the defining predicate of *māyā* / *avidyā* / *jagat* in the Advaita technical vocabulary.

The *world*:
- Is not *sat* (fully, real), it does not have the unconditional, independent being of Brahman; its being depends on Brahman.
- Is not *asat* (non-existent), it is not like a square circle or a barren woman's son; it appears, has effects, sustains transactions, supports ethical action.
- Is *anirvacanīya*, it escapes both predicates because it has *dependent being* (*para-sattā*): its being is Brahman's being articulated in name-and-form.

**Thibaut's English (SBE 34, p. 23, footnote).** "Neither real nor unreal (*sadasad-anirvacanīya*)", rightly capturing the refusal of the simple dichotomy.

**Gambhirananda (BSB Introduction, p. lv).** "[Māyā is] indescribable in terms of either existence or non-existence... it is not a sheer nothing, because it produces effects; and it is not a reality of the same order as Brahman, because it is sublatable."

The objection often raised against *anirvacanīya*, most pointedly by Rāmānuja in his *saptavidhānupapatti*, is that it violates the law of excluded middle. Every entity must be either *sat* or *asat*; there is no third. To posit a third (*anirvacanīya*) is to commit a logical contradiction.

Śaṅkara's response, implicit in the BSB, explicit in later Advaita, is that the *sat*/*asat* dichotomy is itself a *vyāvahārika* category. The categories of Nyāya logic apply at the level where they were developed: the level of empirical analysis. They do not necessarily apply *absolutely*. The challenge to the dichotomy is the philosophical work; the dichotomy cannot be presupposed to refute the challenge.

This is, we suggest, the genuine philosophical situation: Rāmānuja's critique presupposes what Śaṅkara is challenging. It is not a clean refutation; it is a dialectical standoff in which one side insists on a logical principle the other is questioning. Whether that principle is absolute or *vyāvahārika* is the disputed question. ### *Mithyātvāt* Arguments: Ontological Dependence, Not Annihilation

The standard *mithyātvāt* arguments in Advaita (most rigorously in Madhusūdana's *Advaita-Siddhi*) have the form:

> *X is mithyā because X is bādhya*, sublatable under conditions of complete knowledge.

This is a claim about *what X is*, not about *whether X exists or operates*. The pot is *mithyā* not because you cannot use it, but because its claim to *independent being* (being-other-than-clay) does not survive ultimate inquiry. When you see the pot-as-clay throughout, you do not stop using the pot, but you no longer mistake the pot's being for *anything other than* clay's being.

Applying this to the world: the world is *mithyā* not because it disappears or becomes hallucinatory, but because its claim to *independent being* (being-other-than-Brahman) does not survive *jñāna*. Under *jñāna*, the world is seen as Brahman's own being appearing in the mode of *nāma-rūpa*, and that seeing does not annihilate the appearance, it *sees through* it to its substance.

**The crucial corollary:** *mithyātvāt* arguments are *ontological-dependence claims*, not *annihilation claims*. They establish that the world's being is *para-sattā* (the being-of-another), not *svata-siddhā* (self-established). They do not establish that the world ceases to occur, that experience is hallucinatory, or that ethics has no grip. The *jīvanmukta* continues to live in the world, but lives in it as the artist sees the painting she has painted, no longer mistaking the painted-figures for independent agents.

This is exactly what the user's T3 (*vijñāna* returns to the stairs after reaching the roof) and T7 (Maya as divine self-expression) require. The *mithyātvāt* arguments do not threaten the realist trajectory; they ground it. The world's *mithyātva* is precisely its *Brahman-articulated-ness*, its being-as-divine-self-expression. Once *mithyā* is correctly understood (not as *asat* but as *bādhya*-with-*vyāvahārika-sattā*), Śaṅkara's argument *for* mithyātva is *itself* an argument for the world as the divine's self-expression.

### Where Śaṅkara Explicitly Affirms Empirical Reality

A few texual anchors where Śaṅkara's affirmation of *vyāvahārika-sattā* is unmistakable:

**[BSB 1.1](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1).2** (*janmādy asya yataḥ*): Śaṅkara's bhāṣya on the sūtra defining Brahman as the source of the world's *janma* (birth), *sthiti* (sustenance), *laya* (dissolution). Śaṅkara does not *deny* these events. He affirms them and asks: what is the source of this real threefold process? The source is Brahman.

**[BSB 2.1](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1).14** (the clay-pot argument, examined in Part II): the world is *vyāvahārika-satya* in the cause-effect analysis. The pot made of clay is real at the empirical level; the clay's being is what the pot's being *is*.

**[BSB 2.1](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1).33** (*līlā-kaivalyam*, examined in Part III): the world is Brahman's *līlā*, play is *real activity* of a *real agent*. Non-real play is not play. The *līlā*-framing presupposes the world is operatively real for the play to occur.

**Standard Śaṅkara move in dialectical objections:** when an opponent presses "if the world is unreal, how do you eat, walk, speak?", Śaṅkara's response (well-attested across the BSB) is *vyāvahārika-sattāyāṁ na kaścid doṣaḥ*, "at the level of empirical being, there is no defect [in the world's reality]." Practical transactions (language, cognition, perception, action) are all *vyāvahārika-satya* and the Advaitin does not deny them. What is denied is *only* their *pāramārthika* independence, their claim to being-other-than-Brahman at the ultimate level.

### Why the Three-Tier Scheme is Not Metaphysical Multiplication

A reader unfamiliar with this register-thinking may ask: are you not multiplying ontological levels? Have you not committed to three real beings, *pāramārthika*, *vyāvahārika*, *prātibhāsika*, when one would do?

The answer is: the three tiers are not three *beings*. They are three *registers* of being-talk, three *vantage-points* from which the same being can be analyzed. Brahman is the only ultimate reality. The empirical world is Brahman articulated; its *vyāvahārika-sattā* is just Brahman's being viewed as expressed-in-name-and-form. The illusory snake is Brahman articulated through the rope and through the mistaken perception; its *prātibhāsika-sattā* is just Brahman's being viewed as articulated-in-cognitive-error. There is no multiplication of ontological substances. There is precision about the levels of analysis at which different claims are correct.

This precision is what allows Śaṅkara to say all the following at once without contradiction: (i) Brahman alone is; (ii) the world is real at its own level; (iii) the rope-snake is real-as-appearance even though sublated within the empirical level itself; (iv) *brahma-jñāna* delivers not the *abolition* of the world but the *recognition* of the world's true ontological structure; (v) ethics has grip at the *vyāvahārika* level; (vi) *līlā* describes the *pāramārthika* truth of the activity.

The user's thesis takes this register-discipline and extends it across the whole map of Indian philosophy. T1, T3, T4 are register-claims structurally identical to Śaṅkara's three-tier scheme; T7 reinterprets the *vyāvahārika*-level world as the divine's *līlā*-articulation, with the locus question handed to the Vivaraṇa-school resolution refined by Kashmir Shaivism's *svātantrya*. The whole architecture rides on the precision of *mithyā* ≠ *asat*, *bādhya* ≠ *abhāva*, and *anirvacanīya* read non-deflationarily as a refusal of the *sat*/*asat* dichotomy. Once this precision is in place, the textbook caricature falls apart and the realist trajectory becomes one possible extension of Śaṅkara rather than a forced contradiction. ## Part VII: *Vivarta* vs *Pariṇāma*: The Decisive Sub-School Correction

This part contains the philologically decisive corrective on which much of the realist re-reading hinges. The standard textbook account of Advaita identifies it with *vivartavāda*, the doctrine that Brahman undergoes no real transformation in producing the world; the world is *vivarta* (apparent modification) of Brahman, not *pariṇāma* (real transformation). On this account, the world is like the rope-as-snake, pure appearance, with no genuine change in the substratum.

This is the doctrine at which Śaṅkara is most often charged with world-denial. *If* this is Śaṅkara's settled position, then his Brahman is a static, immutable Real with the world as a mere illusory overlay, and the realist criticisms (Bhāskara's, Madhva's, the Vaiṣṇavas') gain enormous purchase.

But this is not Śaṅkara's settled position. *Vivartavāda* as a developed technical doctrine is a *sub-school crystallisation*, not Śaṅkara's own dominant vocabulary. This is one of the most consequential interpretive points in modern Advaita scholarship, and the user's thesis depends on getting it right.

### The Classical Distinction

In later Advaita systematisation, the central metaphysical distinction in causation-doctrine is:

**Pariṇāma** (real transformation, modification). The cause genuinely *becomes* the effect, modifying itself in the process. Milk genuinely becomes yogurt, the milk is *transformed*, not merely "appearing as" yogurt. The pre-yogurt milk is gone; the post-yogurt yogurt is genuinely a new state of the same substance. This is the Sāṃkhya causation-doctrine (Prakṛti *pariṇāma*-s into the manifest world) and Bhāskara's Vedānta-doctrine (Brahman genuinely transforms into the world; the *bheda* and *abheda* aspects are both real, *bhedābheda*-style).

**Vivarta** (apparent transformation, illusory modification). The substratum remains *unchanged*; only an *appearance* arises on it, without any genuine change in the substratum. The rope "appears as" snake without the rope becoming a snake. The substratum is uninvolved in the appearance except as its *adhiṣṭhāna*. In later Advaita, this is the doctrine of Brahman's relation to the world: Brahman is the *vivarta-upādāna* (the cause-in-a-vivarta-relation); the world is the *vivarta* (the appearance).

The two doctrines are not equivalent. *Pariṇāmavāda* gives the world *real ontological standing* as a transformation of Brahman; *vivartavāda* gives the world only *apparent* ontological standing as an illusory overlay. The realist trajectory (Bhāskara, Madhva, Caitanya, Aurobindo) sits with *pariṇāma* and its variants; the deflationary Advaita sits with *vivarta* and reduces the world's standing accordingly. ### What Śaṅkara's Own BSB Says

This is the philologically decisive point. Śaṅkara himself in the BSB *does not use the term vivarta as a developed technical metaphysical category*. The word *vivarta* occurs in the BSB; the verbal root *vṛt* with the prefix *vi-* gives "to turn around, to revolve, to appear as something else." But the noun *vivarta* in the developed technical sense, the contrast-term to *pariṇāma*, the doctrine that Brahman undergoes no real change, is *not* Śaṅkara's settled vocabulary.

In [BSB 2.1.14](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14) (the cause-effect *adhikaraṇa*) and surrounding passages, Śaṅkara's primary vocabulary is the *pariṇāma-vāda* framework, *qualified* by the *vācārambhaṇa* argument from *Chāndogya* 6.1.4. That is: Brahman's creative activity is described in transformation-language, and then immediately the transformation is qualified as *vācārambhaṇam vikāro nāmadheyam*, the modification is "verbal-designation," only a name. The qualification of *pariṇāma* by *vācārambhaṇa* is *not yet vivartavāda*; it is a careful middle position.

What does this middle position say? It says: Brahman is the *upādāna-kāraṇa* (material cause) of the world; the world is a *vikāra* (modification) of Brahman; *but* the *vikāra* has no *independent self-established being*, its being is Brahman's being expressed in *nāma-rūpa*. This is *not* the same as saying the world is unreal; it is saying the world is real-as-Brahman-articulated, which is the *ananyatva* doctrine of 2.1.14.

**Śaṅkara's actual vocabulary in BSB on causation:**

- *brahma-pariṇāma-vāda*, used in some passages, but qualified by the *vācārambhaṇa* argument
- *upādāna-kāraṇatva*, Brahman as the material cause from which the world arises
- *nāma-rūpa-vikāra*, the modification as only name-and-form
- *sadasad-anirvacanīya*, the world's ontological status as indeterminable

What Śaṅkara does *not* use as his settled technical category:

- *vivartavāda* as a contrastive doctrine to *pariṇāmavāda*
- *vivarta* as the technical name for Brahman's relation to the world

### What Came From the Sub-School, Not From Śaṅkara

The developed *vivartavāda* doctrine emerges in stages after Śaṅkara, in the work of his successors:

**Sureśvara** (Śaṅkara's direct disciple, c. late 8th c.): Sureśvara's *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* and the massive *Bṛhadāraṇyaka-Bhāṣya-Vārtika* are the most theologically conservative works of the early Advaita tradition. Sureśvara is strikingly close to Śaṅkara's own idiom, and *he avoids the developed technical vocabulary of vivartavāda*. This is significant: the disciple who is most faithful to the master's idiom does not use the term that later commentators will treat as definitive of "Advaita." Sureśvara's preferred causation-vocabulary is closer to *nāma-rūpa-vikāra* and the *vācārambhaṇa* qualification of *pariṇāma*, not the explicit *vivarta* contrast.

**Padmapāda** (Śaṅkara's direct disciple): the *Pañcapādikā* (extant only on the first four *pādas* of BSB) is where the *vivarta* vocabulary begins to crystallize as a technical category. Padmapāda introduces the term in a developed sense; his successors will systematise it.

**Vācaspati Miśra** (c. 950 CE): the *Bhāmatī* sub-commentary uses *pariṇāma* at the level of *avidyā* (which transforms into the world) but *vivarta* at the level of Brahman (which does not transform). This is the *Bhāmatī*-school compromise: the substratum (Brahman) is *vivarta*-related to the world, but the proximate transformer (avidyā) is *pariṇāma*-related. Brahman is preserved unchanged; the changing is the avidyā's.

**Prakāśātman** (c. 13th c.): the *Pañcapādikā-Vivaraṇa* fully systematises *vivartavāda* as the correct characterization of Brahman's relation to the world. The Vivaraṇa-school after Prakāśātman becomes the main carrier of the developed *vivartavāda* doctrine.

So when one speaks of "Advaita's *vivartavāda*," one is speaking of a doctrinal position that crystallized in the centuries *after* Śaṅkara, primarily in the Padmapāda to Prakāśātman line, with the *Bhāmatī* school offering an alternative compromise. Śaṅkara's own BSB is more guarded and more middle-positioned than the developed *vivartavāda* of the sub-school. ## Part VIII: The Locus Problem and Madhusūdana's *Advaita-Siddhi*

We turn now to the most genuine philosophical problem in the Advaita system, the *locus problem* of avidyā, and to the most rigorous late defense of Advaita against this problem, Madhusūdana Sarasvatī's *Advaita-Siddhi*. This is where Madhva's and Rāmānuja's critiques have their most genuine bite, and where the user's resolution (following Aurobindo and the Vivaraṇa-school refined by Kashmir Shaivism) does its most distinctive work.

### The Problem

Avidyā (ignorance) is said by Advaita to be the cause of *saṁsāra* (the transmigrating cycle), of *adhyāsa* (superimposition), of all worldly suffering. But: *whose avidyā?* Where does it reside (*āśraya*)? What does it conceal (*viṣaya*)?

The classical dilemma:
- If avidyā resides in Brahman, then Brahman is ignorant, contradicting Brahman's omniscience (*sarva-jñatva*).
- If avidyā resides in the *jīva*, then the *jīva* is itself a product of avidyā, circular (avidyā produces its own locus, presupposing what it is supposed to ground).

This is *Rāmānuja's saptavidhānupapatti* (seven-fold impossibility, [*Śrī-Bhāṣya* 1.1.1](cite://ramanuja/shri-bhasya/1.1.1)) compressed into its most compact form. Rāmānuja's full seven-fold critique unpacks the dilemma along several axes, locus, content, concealment-mechanism, removal-mechanism, sublatability, and so on, but its philosophical core is this: the Advaita posit of avidyā with both *āśraya* and *viṣaya* in Brahman generates structural incoherences that Advaita has no formal solution for. ### Śaṅkara's Own Handling

Śaṅkara in the BSB does not give a fully systematised answer to this dilemma. He handles specific objections contextually and deploys several characteristic moves.

**(1) *Anāditva* (beginninglessness) as deflection.** In the area of [BSB 2.1.35–36](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.35-36) and the avidyā-passages of *adhyāya* 2 generally, Śaṅkara's standard move is: avidyā is *anādi*, beginningless. Therefore asking "when did avidyā begin?" or "what produced the locus before avidyā began?" is unanswerable because the question presupposes a temporal starting point that *anādi*-avidyā does not have.

The standard formulation here is that the world's material basis is name-and-form projected by beginningless *avidyā*. This is a widely used Advaita formula, even where the exact BSB locus for the wording remains to be pinned down in the project's verification workflow.

The *anāditva* move is a *deflection*, not a *solution*. It blocks the regress by denying its presupposition (that avidyā has a temporal beginning) but does not formally solve the *locus* problem. The *jīva*-as-locus circularity remains; *anāditva* just says the circularity is *beginningless* and so does not require external initiation.

**(2) Pragmatic deflection to *Sākṣin*.** For practical purposes, for the *sādhaka* engaged in *sādhana*, the *jīva* is the functional locus of avidyā. The goal of *sādhana* is the removal of the *jīva*'s avidyā through *jñāna*. Asking for the *ultimate* locus of avidyā is a question that arises only in the *pāramārthika* register, but in the *pāramārthika* register, avidyā does not exist (Brahman alone is, and Brahman is never ignorant). So the dilemma is *vyāvahārikally* formulated but *pāramārthikally* dissolved.

This is a register-move: the locus-question is *vyāvahārikally* answered by saying "the *jīva* is the functional locus," and *pāramārthikally* dissolved by saying "from the ultimate vantage there is no avidyā at all." But the move is dialectically uncomfortable, it leaves the *vyāvahārika* answer (*jīva*-as-locus) circular, and uses *pāramārthika* dissolution to relieve the pressure rather than to resolve the structural incoherence.

**(3) *Avidyā-pratyaya* analysis.** In the *Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya*, avidyā is described through the structure of *pratyaya* (cognition/content): what is superimposed is not independently real but is a *pratyaya* arising in the *antaḥkaraṇa* (internal organ), using the *sākṣi-caitanya* (witness-consciousness) as its *prakāśa* (illuminating light). The *sākṣin* is not ignorant, it is the light by which ignorance is illuminated. The *antaḥkaraṇa* is the functional locus of avidyā for all practical purposes.

This is the cleanest of Śaṅkara's moves, and it gestures toward the resolution that the Vivaraṇa-school will systematise: the locus is the *antaḥkaraṇa*-with-Brahman-as-its-illuminating-substrate. The avidyā-content arises *in* the *antaḥkaraṇa* but is *illuminated by* the witness; the witness is not ignorant in any contaminating sense, but is the medium *in which* the ignorance is structured.

### The Two Sub-Schools' Answers

Post-Śaṅkara, the two great sub-schools of Advaita give two formal answers to the locus problem.

**The *Bhāmatī* position (Vācaspati Miśra, c. 950 CE):**
- *Āśraya* (locus): the *jīva*.
- *Viṣaya* (content/concealment-target): Brahman.

The individual self is the seat of avidyā; what is concealed *by* avidyā is Brahman itself. Brahman *as Brahman* is never the locus of ignorance.

The regress problem (the *jīva* is itself a product of avidyā, yet is its locus, circular) is met by Vācaspati's acceptance of an *infinite series of beginningless jīvas and avidyās*. The regress is *anavasthā-doṣa-rahita*, not a defective regress, since both *jīva* and avidyā are *anādi* (beginningless), and neither needs prior cause. *Mukti*: when the individual *jīva* attains direct knowledge of Brahman, *that jīva*'s avidyā is destroyed, but the world (as substrate of other *jīvas*' avidyās) continues. **The *Vivaraṇa* position (Prakāśātman, c. 13th c.):**
- *Āśraya*: Brahman.
- *Viṣaya*: Brahman.

Brahman itself is *both* the substratum of avidyā *and* what avidyā conceals. There is no second locus needed.

The omniscience-problem is met by Prakāśātman's distinction between *śuddha-caitanya* (pure consciousness, untouched) and *pramā-jñāna* (positive cognitive states arising as modifications). ### Why the User's Thesis Adopts the Vivaraṇa, with Kashmir-Shaiva Refinement

The user's T7, "Maya is itself a manifestation of the divine; ignorance is not foreign, it is, ontologically, part of Brahman's own self-manifestation," is *structurally* the Vivaraṇa thesis. Brahman is the locus of avidyā: avidyā is therefore not *outside* the divine; it is *of* the divine, *in* the divine, *enacted by* the divine. But the Vivaraṇa school's standard reading takes this in a *deflationary* direction: avidyā is something to be *removed* so that pure Brahman shines unmodified. The user's thesis *inverts* the evaluation. Avidyā is not a defect to be removed; it is the divine's *own self-veiling activity*, to be *recognized as such* and transparently understood. The Brahman-as-locus thesis is preserved; the deflationary reading is replaced by Kashmir Shaivism's *svātantrya* (the *Pratyabhijñā-Hṛdayam*, the *Spanda-Kārikā*): Brahman / Śiva, in sovereign freedom, expresses itself through voluntary self-limitation. Here *spanda* should be understood correctly as the dynamic mode of *svātantrya* itself, the pulsation of self-expression of Brahman / Śiva, not as the *rajas* operation alone.

In Kashmir Shaivism's terms: *Cit-Śakti, by her own free will (svātantrya), unfolds the universe upon her own canvas* (Pratyabhijñā-Hṛdayam, sūtra 2). The "canvas" *is* the locus (Brahman / Śiva); the "unfolding" *is* the avidyā-modification. The Vivaraṇa structure is preserved; the evaluation is non-deflationary: the unfolding is itself the divine's joyful expressive activity, not a defect.

This converts the metaphysical achievement of the Vivaraṇa school (locating avidyā in Brahman without contaminating Brahman's purity) into the user's thesis (Maya as the divine's own willed self-veiling, structurally identical to the *līlā-kaivalyam* of [BSB 2.1.33](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.33) read non-deflationarily).

### Madhusūdana's *Advaita-Siddhi*: The Bhāvarūpa Argument

The most rigorous defense of avidyā's metaphysical structure in late Advaita is Madhusūdana Sarasvatī's *Advaita-Siddhi* (c. 16th c.), a line-by-line refutation of Vyāsatīrtha's Madhva-school *Nyāyāmṛta*. Madhusūdana's central thesis is that avidyā is *bhāvarūpa*, *positively existent*, not mere *abhāva* (absence) of knowledge.

This is the move that converts Madhva's polemical pressure (which argued that Advaita's avidyā is incoherent because it must be either *bhāva*, but then it conflicts with non-duality, or *abhāva*, but then it cannot have causal effects) into the user's thesis (avidyā as positively existent and therefore as a real *modality* of the divine, not a mere absence). [register: M + P1-LD / scope: trained dialectician; status of avidyā as positive or privative; late Advaita reply to Madhva-school pressure; secure causal efficacy for ignorance / language-game: polemical defense / status: [REAL-DISAGREEMENT] with Madhva, reconstruction in the final move to the user's thesis / source: Madhusūdana, *Advaita-Siddhi* opening and bhāvarūpa argument cluster; Madhva, *Mithyātvānumāna-Khaṇḍana* and *Tattvodyota* opening]

**The five pramāṇas for *bhāvarūpa* avidyā** (following Citsukha and Vidyāraṇya, systematised by Madhusūdana):

**(1) *Pratyakṣa*, direct witnessing of ignorance.** In the experience *aham ajñaḥ* / *aham eva ajānāmi* ("I am ignorant" / "I do not know"), what is witnessed is not an *absence* of cognition but a *positive cognitive content*, the cognition of one's own ignorance. We don't infer ignorance; we witness it. An absence cannot be the *content* of a present cognition; only an existent (*bhāva*) can. The "three perceptions" formulation (Citsukha → Madhusūdana): perception of ignorance prior to corrective cognition; perception of *one's having been ignorant* immediately after; perception of *general* ignorance unattached to any specific object. Each requires ignorance to be a *positive content of awareness*.

**(2) *Anumāna*, inferential argument.** Knowledge (*jñāna*) is the destroyer (*nivartaka*) of ignorance. An act of destruction requires a real (positive) *destruendum*. *Abhāva* (absence) cannot itself be destroyed (since it is already nothing); only a *bhāva* can be the object of destruction. Therefore the destructible-by-knowledge must be a *bhāva*.

**(3) *Arthāpatti*, argument from postulation.** The phenomenal world appears. Brahman is non-dual reality. There must be some *positive* principle accounting for the *appearance* of difference, since absence-of-knowledge cannot itself produce *appearance* (a positive event). Hence avidyā as *bhāvarūpa*.

**(4) *Āgama*, scripture.**

At *Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad* 4.10, the text says to know *māyā* as *prakṛti* and the wielder of *māyā* as the Great Lord.

Śvetāśvatara IV.10. **English.** "Know māyā as *prakṛti* (productive nature), and the wielder of māyā as the Great Lord."

Māyā here is treated as a *substance* (*prakṛti*), not as absence. The Bhagavad-Gītā 7.14 reinforces:

At *Bhagavad-Gītā* 7.14, Kṛṣṇa says that His divine *māyā*, made of the *guṇas*, is difficult to cross.

**English.** "This my divine māyā is composed of the *guṇas* and difficult to overcome."

*Guṇa-composition* implies positive substantial reality: that which is composed of *sattva*, *rajas*, *tamas* is not absence but a configured positive entity.

**(5) *Causal efficacy*.** Avidyā produces effects: *adhyāsa* (superimposition), *saṁśaya* (doubt), *viparyaya* (misperception), *bhrama* (delusion), and the entire phenomenal world-cognition. A non-existent cannot produce effects (a barren woman's son cannot cause anything). Causal efficacy entails *bhāva-vastutva*, positive thing-hood.

### Madhusūdana's Destructive Dilemma against the Absence-Theory

Madhusūdana's polemical move against Vyāsatīrtha's claim that avidyā is mere *jñāna-abhāva* (absence of knowledge) is sharp:

**(a)** If avidyā = absence of knowledge, then where there is *some* knowledge, avidyā cannot subsist, but the Vedāntin needs avidyā to coexist with partial knowledge (this is why the rope is *partially* seen even when mistaken for snake; you see the elongated shape, you just misidentify it).

**(b)** If avidyā is removed merely by the arising of knowledge (not by destruction), then the *removal* is not a real action, but śruti and śāstra repeatedly speak of *jñāna-nāśya avidyā* (avidyā destroyed by knowledge), which presupposes a real entity to be destroyed.

**(c)** If avidyā is mere absence, it cannot be the *upādāna-kāraṇa* (material cause) of the apparent world, but Advaita requires it to be the material cause (or at least the *vivarta-upādāna*) of the world-appearance. From mere absence, no *vikāra* can arise.

Therefore avidyā must be *bhāva-rūpa* (positively existent), *anādi* (beginningless), and *jñāna-nivartya* (removable only by knowledge).

### The Resulting Ontology and Its Implication for T7

Madhusūdana's avidyā is:
- *bhāva-rūpa*, positively existent
- *anādi*, without beginning (so not produced; it is *anādi* in the same sense Brahman is *anādi*)
- *jñāna-nivartya*, removable only by direct knowledge of Brahman
- *anirvacanīya*, neither *sat* nor *asat*, escaping the Nyāya dichotomy
- *Brahman-āśraya* (Vivaraṇa) or *jīva-āśraya* (Bhāmatī), the locus dispute is internal to Advaita

**The user's thesis (T7) anchored.** Once avidyā is forced to be a *positive entity* (Madhusūdana's polemical achievement against Madhva), the question becomes: *whose entity?* The user's thesis adopts the Vivaraṇa answer (Brahman is both *āśraya* and *viṣaya*) and refines it via Kashmir Shaivism's *svātantrya*: Brahman / Śiva, in sovereign freedom, *chooses* to manifest as self-veiled. This converts Madhusūdana's polemical achievement (forcing avidyā to be positive) into the user's metaphysical thesis (Maya is the divine's own willed self-veiling).

The *bhāvarūpa avidyā* of Madhusūdana is *structurally identical* to Aurobindo's *vidyā-avidyā-mayī māyā*, the māyā that is composed of both knowledge and apparent ignorance, the divine's own active power of self-articulation. What Madhusūdana defends polemically (against Madhva) the user's thesis adopts ontologically: avidyā is not foreign defect but divine activity, the modality of the supermind's self-projection into name-and-form.

### Note on Madhusūdana Himself: The Same Author Who Wrote *Bhakti-Rasāyana*

One philosopher, both registers, no contradiction. Madhusūdana is the historical bridge from Advaita to the Caitanya / Vaiṣṇava synthesis the user's T2 traces.

This is not a biographical curiosity. It is structural evidence for the user's thesis. The same intellectual capacity that produces the most rigorous polemical defense of non-dualism also produces ecstatic devotion to a personal form of the divine. The two registers are not opposed; they are register-distinguished. The same Real, said in different senses; the same author, in different *adhikāras*. Madhusūdana models the register-multiplicity at the level of *one author within the Advaita tradition itself*. The user's T2 generalizes this from one author to a trajectory: Bhartṛprapañca, Bhāskara, Madhva, Caitanya, Aurobindo each working different registers of the same Real, each *register-true*, none reducible to any other.

One further major work should stay in view here: the *Upadeśa-Sāhasrī*. It is the most secure non-commentarial work in Śaṅkara's corpus and shows that his project is pedagogical as well as polemical. There the stress falls on hearing, reflection, disciplined discernment, and the teacher-student relation as the path by which non-dual knowledge is awakened. That matters for the user's thesis because it keeps Śaṅkara from collapsing into a mere bundle of metaphysical theses: his non-duality is a lived discipline of clarification, not just a doctrinal endpoint.

## Part IX: Selected Upaniṣad-Bhāṣyas

The BSB is the systematic text; the Upaniṣad-bhāṣyas are where Śaṅkara works at close reach with the source-texts of Vedānta themselves. We sample four, Bṛhadāraṇyaka, Chāndogya, Māṇḍūkya (with Gauḍapāda's *Kārikās*), and Taittirīya, to convey both the texture of his method and the doctrinal weight he assigns to specific passages central to the user's thesis.

### Bṛhadāraṇyaka-Bhāṣya: *Yājñavalkya's Maitreyī-Brāhmaṇa*

The *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* is the longest of the principal Upaniṣads and the one Śaṅkara comments on at greatest length. The bhāṣya runs to several hundred pages in the Ānandāśrama edition. We focus on the *Maitreyī-Brāhmaṇa* (BU 2.4 and 4.5), the dialogue between Yājñavalkya and his wife Maitreyī, which contains some of the most-quoted Upaniṣadic passages on the Self.

The famous formulation:

At *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* 2.4.5, Yājñavalkya says that the husband is dear not for the husband's sake but for the sake of the Self, and that the Self must be seen, heard, reflected on, and deeply contemplated.

BU 2.4.5. **English.** "Verily, dear, not for the sake of the husband is the husband dear, but for the sake of the Self the husband is dear. ... The Self, is to be seen, to be heard about, to be reflected on, to be deeply meditated on."

Śaṅkara's bhāṣya on this passage establishes the *fourfold sādhana*, *śravaṇa* (hearing), *manana* (reflection), *nididhyāsana* (meditative deepening), and the implicit fourth, *darśana* (direct seeing). This is the canonical Advaita pedagogy of *jñāna-mārga*. Notice that it is *not* a method of world-denial; it is a method of *sustained inquiry*. The Self is to be *sought*, not *fled-into*. The seeking is itself an active engagement.

The bhāṣya's most consequential move on 4.5 (the second Maitreyī-passage, parallel to 2.4): Yājñavalkya teaches that everything is dear *for the sake of the Self*. Śaṅkara reads this not as a reductive psychological egoism (everything is dear because it serves the empirical ego) but as an *ontological* claim about the structure of love: the Self is *intrinsically* lovable because it is *ānanda*, bliss, and all the empirical objects we love are loved through the reflection of the Self's *ānanda* in them. Love is not a defect to be transcended; it is the empirical mode of the Self's self-recognition.

This is significant for the user's thesis. It means even at the deepest *jñāna* register, love is not eliminated, it is *traced to its source*. The bhakti-tradition's emphasis on *prema* (love) is not in opposition to Śaṅkara's *jñāna* register; it is what *jñāna* discovers love to * be*. The same Maitreyī-Brāhmaṇa that teaches *ātmā vā are draṣṭavyaḥ* also teaches that love is the empirical signature of the Self's *ānanda*. The two are continuous.

The *neha nānāsti kiñcana* line we examined in Part V comes from BU 4.4.19, the *Yājñavalkya-Brāhmaṇa* shortly after the Maitreyī-passage. In the bhāṣya, Śaṅkara handles this with the *bādhita*-not-*abhāva* precision we have already established. The realized one becomes Brahman; from that vantage there is no manyness; the manyness is *vyāvahārikally* operative but *pāramārthikally* sublated. The *jīvanmukta*-Yājñavalkya continues to act, teach, depart from the world, but the action is now *līlā* in its register.

### Chāndogya-Bhāṣya: The *Sad-Vidyā* of Uddālaka

The *Chāndogya Upaniṣad*'s sixth *prapāṭhaka* is the *sad-vidyā*, the teaching of *sat* (Being), Uddālaka Āruṇi's instruction to his son Śvetaketu. This is the locus of *tat tvam asi* (CU 6.8.7), the *mahāvākya* most central to Śaṅkara's *jñāna-mārga*, and of the *vācārambhaṇa* argument we examined in Part II.

The *sad-vidyā* opens with the famous question:

At *Chāndogya Upaniṣad* 6.1.3, Uddālaka asks for that teaching by which the unheard becomes heard, the unthought thought, and the unknown known.

CU 6.1.3. **English.** "Have you asked for that teaching by which the unheard becomes heard, the unthought thought, the unknown known? How, sir, is that teaching?"

Uddālaka's question presupposes that there is *one teaching* by which *all* is known, *one* substance, *one* knowing, on grasping which *every* particular is grasped. This is the *ekena vijñātena sarva-vijñānam* doctrine, by knowing the One, all is known.

Śaṅkara's bhāṣya makes this the foundational principle of *jñāna-mārga*. Brahman is the *sat* by knowing which all is known, just as by knowing clay all clay-products are known. The *vācārambhaṇa* argument follows immediately:

At *Chāndogya Upaniṣad* 6.1.4, the clay example returns: by knowing one lump of clay, all clay-objects are known, because modification is only verbal designation and clay alone is real.

(*Quoted in full in Part II.*) Śaṅkara's gloss in the Chāndogya-bhāṣya is more extended than in the BSB's quotation: he traces the *vikāra* through three further analogies (gold-and-ornaments, iron-and-tools, and the salt-in-water analogy that follows in CU 6.13). Each analogy makes the same point: knowing the substance is knowing all its modifications; the modifications have their being in the substance, not separately.

The *tat tvam asi* declaration (CU 6.8.7 onwards, repeated nine times across the *prapāṭhaka*) is then read by Śaṅkara as the *mahāvākya*'s direct cognition: the same *sat* that is the substance of all *vikāra*s is the substance of *you*. Self and World are non-different from the same Brahman-substance.

The Chāndogya-bhāṣya is also where Śaṅkara works out his most sustained engagement with the *prāṇa-saṁvāda* (the dialogue of the *prāṇas*) and the *udgītha-vidyā*, meditative practices on cosmic correspondences. These are *upāsanā-vidhis*, register-true for the *upāsaka*, in Śaṅkara's hierarchical hermeneutic. The *jñāni* does not need them, but they are not "false", they are register-correct for those whose *adhikāra* is *upāsanā* rather than direct *jñāna*. This is the register-discipline of *samanvaya* in actual exegetical practice.

### Māṇḍūkya-Bhāṣya with Gauḍapāda's *Kārikās*

The *Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad* is the shortest of the principal Upaniṣads, twelve mantras only, and the most ontologically dense. It treats *Om* as the symbol of all reality, analyzes the four states of the Self (*jāgrat*, *svapna*, *suṣupti*, *turīya*, waking, dreaming, deep sleep, the fourth), and identifies the fourth state with Brahman.

The *Māṇḍūkya-Kārikā* by Gauḍapāda, Śaṅkara's *parama-guru* (teacher's teacher), through Govindapāda, is the philosophical expansion of the Upaniṣad in 215 verses. Gauḍapāda's *Kārikā* is the most *vivartavāda*-leaning of the early Advaita texts; it is also where the engagement with Mahāyāna Buddhism (especially the Madhyamaka) is most visible, and where the doctrine of *ajāti-vāda* (non-origination) is given its strongest formulation.

Śaṅkara's bhāṣya on the Upaniṣad-plus-*Kārikā* navigates this. He accepts Gauḍapāda's *ajāti-vāda*, the doctrine that nothing has truly come into being, that *creation* is *adhyasita* on the unborn Brahman, but with characteristic guardedness about the developed *vivarta* technical vocabulary (compare Part VII: *vivarta*-as-technical-term is sub-school crystallisation; Gauḍapāda's text uses it but in a less developed sense than later Vivaraṇa-school).

The most-cited mantra, *Māṇḍūkya* 7:

At *Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad* 7, the fourth state is described by a long series of negations and then as peaceful, auspicious, non-dual, and the Self to be known.

**English (Gambhirananda).** "[The fourth state is] not inwardly cognitive, not outwardly cognitive, not cognitive both ways, not a mass of cognition, not cognitive, not non-cognitive. Unseen, beyond empirical dealings, ungraspable, undefinable, unthinkable, indescribable, its sole essence being the consciousness of one's own Self, the negation of all phenomena, peaceful, auspicious, non-dual, the fourth they consider; that is the Self, that is to be known."

Śaṅkara's bhāṣya on this mantra is a tour-de-force of the *neti neti* method. The Self is reached *only* by negation of every available cognitive category. This is the *nirviśeṣa* register at its most concentrated. But notice the positive predicates that follow the negative ones: *śāntam* (peaceful), *śivam* (auspicious, the good), *advaitam* (non-dual). The negation is in service of an *affirmation*, the affirmation of *śiva* as the auspicious, the *advaita* as the non-dual unity.

For the user's thesis, this is the textual root of the *jñāna-register* base: the *neti neti* method is the necessary cognitive discipline by which the ego-superimposition is recognized and de-identified. *Without* this discipline, the climb-up does not happen. T3 of the user's thesis explicitly preserves the necessity of the *jñāna* climb. The Māṇḍūkya-bhāṣya is its textual home.

### Taittirīya-Bhāṣya: The *Bhṛguvallī* and the Five-*Kośa* Analysis

The *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* is divided into three chapters (*vallīs*): the *Śikṣā-vallī* (instruction in pronunciation and basic ethics), the *Brahmānanda-vallī* (the joy of Brahman, with the famous five-*kośa* analysis), and the *Bhṛgu-vallī* (Bhṛgu's progressive ascent through five definitions of Brahman).

We focus on the *Bhṛguvallī*, because it is structurally one of the most important Upaniṣadic passages for the user's thesis. The *Bhṛguvallī* enacts a *register-progression*: Bhṛgu is taught Brahman by his father Varuṇa, and through *tapas* (concentrated inquiry) progressively ascends through five definitions, each *register-true* but superseded by the next. [register: P + S + M / scope: brahmacārin or advancing seeker; graded cognition of Brahman in TU 3.1-3.6; Upaniṣadic pedagogy with Śaṅkara's commentary in view; move the student from gross to subtle and then beyond / language-game: teacher-student instruction / status: source-claim for the pedagogy, reconstruction in the phrase "register-progression" / source: *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 3.1-3.6; Śaṅkara on TU 3.1-3.6]

The structure (TU 3.1–3.6):

1. **Bhṛgu first thinks Brahman is *anna*, food.** "From food creatures are born; by food they live; into food they pass." This is the *anna-maya kośa*, the food-sheath, the gross body. *Register-true* at the empirical-biological level.

2. **Returning to *tapas*, Bhṛgu thinks Brahman is *prāṇa*, life-force.** "From *prāṇa* creatures are born; by *prāṇa* they live; into *prāṇa* they pass." This is the *prāṇa-maya kośa*, the vital sheath. *Register-true* at the level of biological vitality.

3. **Then Brahman is *manas*, mind.** The *mano-maya kośa*, mental sheath. *Register-true* at the level of mental life.

4. **Then Brahman is *vijñāna*, discriminative intelligence.** The *vijñāna-maya kośa*, intellectual sheath. *Register-true* at the level of discriminative knowledge.

5. **Finally Brahman is *ānanda*, bliss.** The *ānanda-maya kośa*, bliss-sheath. This is the deepest *kośa*, often identified with the causal body.

But the *Bhṛguvallī* does not stop here. The five-*kośa* analysis is the empirical layering; *beyond* the *ānanda-maya kośa* is the *Ātman* / *Brahman* itself, the *kūṭastha* (immovable) substance underlying all five. The *Bhṛguvallī*'s progression is not a ladder of *abandonment*. Bhṛgu does not say "food is wrong, life is wrong, mind is wrong, intellect is wrong, bliss is wrong." He says "Brahman is *more than* food, *more than* life, *more than* mind, *more than* intellect, *more than* the *ānanda-maya kośa*." Each prior definition is preserved as register-true at its own level; the next definition is reached as a *deeper* register.

This is structurally the user's T3 (*vijñāna* returns to the stairs, recognizing them as the same substance) and T1 (the darśana register thesis). The *Bhṛguvallī* is the textual paradigm of register-progression: each *kośa* is register-true, none is abolished, all are seen through to the deeper register that contains them. Aurobindo's reading of the Veda (in *The Secret of the Veda*) operates on a comparable model: the literal sacrificial ritual is not simply wrong; it is register-true at the karma-kāṇḍa level, and the inward yogic meaning is what the same words mean at a deeper register. [register: P + T / scope: reader of the present reconstruction; use of TU 3.1-3.6 for the user's broader method; retrospective comparison between Śaṅkara and Aurobindo; test whether graded pedagogy can be generalized / language-game: reconstruction across traditions / status: reconstruction; the Aurobindo comparison is the user's move, not the Upaniṣad's / source: *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 3.1-3.6; Śaṅkara on TU 3.1-3.6; Aurobindo, *The Secret of the Veda*]

Śaṅkara's Taittirīya-bhāṣya does not put the *Bhṛguvallī* in quite these terms. Śaṅkara reads it as a progressive *teaching-method* by which Bhṛgu's understanding is brought from gross to subtle to the unconditioned Brahman. But the structural logic is the same: each level is taken seriously, *engaged*, *meditated upon*; only when fully grasped is the next reached. The progression is not iconoclastic but cumulative, register-true all the way, ending in the *ānanda* that is Brahman's intrinsic nature.

The *Brahmānanda-vallī*'s famous declaration:

At *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.1.1, Brahman is described as real, knowing, and infinite.

TU 2.1.1. **English.** "Brahman is real, knowing, infinite."

This is one of the most-cited *positive* characterizations of Brahman in the Upaniṣads, *positive*, not *neti neti*. Brahman *is* truth, *is* knowledge, *is* infinity. Śaṅkara's bhāṣya accepts the positive characterization but reads each predicate as *exclusionary*: *satyam* excludes the unreal (*asatya*), *jñānam* excludes the inert (*jaḍa*), *anantam* excludes the finite (*anta-vat*). The positive predicates work by exclusion of their opposites; they are not attributes of a substantialised Brahman, they are determinations of what Brahman is *not by exclusion of opposites*.

But, and this is consequential for our reading, the predicates are positive in form. Brahman is not *only* described by *neti neti*. *Satyaṁ jñānam anantam* is one of Śaṅkara's own most-quoted formulae for Brahman, and it is *positive*. The negative method (*neti neti*) and the positive predication (*satyaṁ jñānam anantam*) are both register-correct moves; neither alone is the whole.

This already begins to push back against the purely negative-theological reading of Śaṅkara. Brahman is real, is consciousness, is infinite; these are positive claims. The *jñāna*-register is not exhausted by negation. It includes both negation of false attribution and positive recognition of the Self's own *sat-cit-ānanda* nature. ## Part X: *Bhaja Govindam* Verse 1: The Devotional Śaṅkara

We close the textual examination with a register-shift. The same Śaṅkara who wrote the rigorous *Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya* and the *Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya* also wrote, or, on a more conservative attribution, transmitted within his immediate circle, the *Bhaja Govindam* (also called the *Mohamuḍgara*, "the hammer that breaks delusion"), one of the most ecstatic devotional stotras in the Sanskrit tradition.

Verse 1 says, in English: worship Govinda, worship Govinda, worship Govinda, O fool; when the time of death draws near, grammatical learning will not save you.

**Word-for-word.**
- *bhaja*, "worship, adore, devote yourself to" (imperative, second person singular). Repeated three times: *bhaja, bhaja, bhaja*. The repetition is not redundant; it is the form of urgency.
- *govindam*, "Govinda", Krishna, Viṣṇu, the personal divine. Accusative singular: the object of devotion.
- *mūḍha-mate*, "O fool!", vocative; *mūḍha* (deluded, foolish) + *mati* (mind, intellect). The addressee is the *foolish-minded one*.
- *samprāpte sannihite kāle*, "when the time has arrived nearby"; *sam-prāpta* (arrived) + *san-nihita* (drawn near) + *kāle* (locative, "in the time"). A locative absolute construction: "when the time of death has come near."
- *na hi na hi*, "not at all, not at all"; emphatic double negation.
- *rakṣati*, "will save" (verb, present indicative used in the future-imminent sense).
- *ḍukṛñ-karaṇe*, *ḍukṛñ* is the technical Pāṇinian grammatical formula naming the verbal root *kṛ* (to do); *karaṇe* is locative of *karaṇa* (instrument, doing). Together: "the [grammatical knowledge of] the rule for the verbal root *ḍukṛñ*." The reference is to memorized Pāṇinian grammar, symbolic of all empty academic learning.

**English.** "Worship Govinda! Worship Govinda! Worship Govinda, O fool! When the time of death draws near, your memorisation of grammatical rules will not save you, will not save you."

The verse is *pointed*. It is addressed to the *paṇḍita*, the learned man, who has accumulated grammatical and ritual learning but who has not turned the heart toward the divine. *Bhaja*, "worship, devote yourself", three times. *Govinda*, the personal divine, the Krishna-form, the *bhakti*-object, three times. The addressee is *mūḍha-mate*, "fool." The argument is *adhikāra*-driven: when death arrives, your accumulated learning will not save you; only devotion will.

### Why This Matters: The Same Author, Different Register

The same author who in the BSB writes austere argument about *adhyāsa* and *sākṣi-cetana* and *līlā-kaivalyam* writes here, with no apparent contradiction, a stotra exhorting personal devotion to Krishna. The textbook caricature struggles to accommodate this. The bloodless world-denier of the textbook should not be writing *bhaja govindam* with passionate urgency.

The actual Śaṅkara has no such struggle. The two registers are distinct, not contradictory. The BSB is the *jñāna*-register, the systematic argument for the structure of non-dual cognition. The *Bhaja Govindam* is the *bhakti*-register, the call to personal devotion for those whose *adhikāra* is bhakti. They are not in tension. They are the same Real, said in different registers and addressed to different audiences in different conditions. [register: P + AD + S / scope: different adhikārins, from dialectician to bhakta; relation between bhāṣya and stotra corpora; corpus-wide genre discrimination; prevent flattening of all Śaṅkara material into one speech-act / language-game: cross-register corpus reading / status: register-resolved; the apparent contradiction dissolves once bhāṣya and stotra are not forced into one language-game / source: BSB 1.1.4, 2.1.14, 2.1.33; *Bhaja Govindam* v.1; *Upadeśa-Sāhasrī* I.11.14-16, I.17.4-9]

This is the methodological principle of *adhikāra*, audience-fitness, that runs throughout the Indian philosophical tradition and that the user's methodological principles preserve. Persons are not interchangeable. A teaching is true *for whom*? A teaching is *fit for whom*? The same teacher can address the *paṇḍita* with *jñāna*-arguments and the *bhakta* with *bhakti*-stotras and the *karma-yogī* with *karma-yoga* exhortations, without contradiction, because the registers are matched to the audiences.

### Register-Fit and the *Bhaja Govindam*'s Power

The traditional account of the *Bhaja Govindam*'s composition: Śaṅkara, walking in Banaras, encountered an old paṇḍita reciting Pāṇinian grammatical rules to himself, preparing for an examination, and was moved to compose this verse on the spot. Twelve more verses followed, and the work is also called the *Dvādaśa-Mañjarikā-Stotra*, the "garland of twelve flowers." A circle of disciples added further verses, and the full text (the *Mohamuḍgara*) circulates in two recensions.

What matters philosophically is not the historicity of the encounter-anecdote but the *register-fit* it embodies. The verse is *exactly* what is needed by *that* listener, the paṇḍita who has accumulated rules but missed the heart. The verse does not argue with him about Advaita metaphysics. It does not lecture him on *anirvacanīya* or the three-tier ontology. It says: worship. Three times: worship. Death is coming; your accumulated learning will not save you; turn the heart now.

This is *adhikāra* in action. The teaching is matched to the listener. The same Śaṅkara who writes the rigorous BSB for the trained dialectician can write the urgent stotra for the heart-untouched paṇḍita. There is no contradiction here once the addressee, viṣaya, sambandha, and prayojana are kept in view. [register: P / scope: distinct adhikārins; pedagogy fitted to audience; stotra and bhāṣya as different communicative acts; right hearing of apparently divergent teachings / language-game: pedagogical discrimination / status: register-resolved / source: *Bhaja Govindam* v.1; *Upadeśa-Sāhasrī* I.17.4-9]

For the user's thesis, this is the structural validation of T1 (different darśanas address different registers) at the level of *one author's own corpus*. Śaṅkara *himself* operates across registers. The textbook reduction of Śaṅkara to one register (the austere *jñāna* of the BSB) is bad reading even of Śaṅkara, let alone of the broader Vedānta corpus. The actual Śaṅkara is *register-multiple*, *jñāna* in the BSB, *upāsanā* in the Upaniṣad-bhāṣyas' meditative passages, *bhakti* in *Bhaja Govindam*, *praise of the goddess* in *Saundarya-laharī*. The user's T1 is just the generalization of what Śaṅkara himself models in his own corpus.

### What Śaṅkara Actually Claims

We can now state the actual Śaṅkara cleanly, in eight propositions.

**(1) Brahman alone is *pāramārthika-sat*.** At the ultimate level of being, Brahman is the only self-established reality. *Sad eva, somya, idam agra āsīt, ekam evādvitīyam* (CU 6.2.1, "in the beginning, dear, this was *sat* alone, one without a second"). This is the *sad-vidyā* foundation. [register: M / scope: mumukṣu and trained Vedāntin; ultimate being; Chāndogya teaching as read by Śaṅkara; ground non-dual inquiry / language-game: Upaniṣadic doctrinal affirmation / status: source-claim / source: *Chāndogya Upaniṣad* 6.2.1; Śaṅkara on CU 6.2.1]

People act and suffer. *Karma* operates lawfully. The world is not nothing. ### Where the Locus Problem Genuinely Bites

We must, however, be intellectually honest. There *is* a genuine philosophical problem that Śaṅkara does not formally solve: the *locus problem* of avidyā. It is the problem on which Madhva's and Rāmānuja's critiques have their sharpest bite.

**The problem stated cleanly.** Avidyā has effects (it produces the world-appearance, the *adhyāsa* of subject-object, the *saṁsāra*-cycle). What has effects must have a real locus. Brahman cannot be the locus (this would make Brahman ignorant, contradicting *sarva-jñatva*). The *jīva* cannot be the locus (the *jīva* is *itself* a product of avidyā, generating circularity). The *anāditva* deflection blocks the *temporal* regress but does not solve the *structural* circularity.

This is a *real* problem. Śaṅkara's BSB does not formally solve it. The *Bhāmatī* school accepts an infinite regress of beginningless *jīvas*-with-avidyās; the *Vivaraṇa* school distinguishes *śuddha-caitanya* from *pramā-jñāna* and locates avidyā in the latter. Both moves are clever, but both have philosophical costs.

Madhva's critique, in particular, has force here. Madhva argues that the Advaita appeal to *anāditva* and to *anirvacanīya* is philosophically unstable: it relies on suspending Nyāya-style logical principles (excluded middle, the requirement that effects have determinate causes) without adequately motivating the suspension. Madhva's own move, to take the difference of Brahman, *jīvas*, and the world as ontologically real (the *pañca-bheda* doctrine), and to refuse the avidyā-mediated structure altogether, solves the locus problem by denying its presupposition. There is no avidyā-locus problem if there is no avidyā. Rāmānuja's *viśiṣṭādvaita* takes a softer version of the same move: the world is real as Brahman's *śarīra* (body), the differences are real-internal-to-the-substance, and the avidyā-mediated appearance-structure is replaced by a real qualified-non-dual ontology. [register: M + P1-LD / scope: trained dialectician; force of anti-Advaita critiques; cross-school pressure on the locus problem; preserve disagreement that survives register-translation / language-game: polemical diagnosis / status: [REAL-DISAGREEMENT] / source: Madhva, *Mithyātvānumāna-Khaṇḍana*, *Tattvodyota*, *Māyāvāda-Khaṇḍana*; Rāmānuja, *Śrī-Bhāṣya* 1.1.1]

The user's thesis (T2) takes Madhva and Rāmānuja seriously here. They are not merely confused dualists who failed to grasp Śaṅkara's *advaita*. They are *register-correct* on the *vyāvahārika*-affirmation pole, and they correctly identify a genuine structural problem in the locus-of-avidyā doctrine. Their critiques are *register-corrections*, not refutations of the *jñāna* register, but they *do* correctly diagnose where the Advaita system has unfinished philosophical work.

### The User's Resolution: Aurobindo's *Bhāvarūpa Avidyā* as Divine *Svātantrya*

The user's resolution of the locus problem follows Aurobindo and the Vivaraṇa school refined by Kashmir Shaivism. The structural moves:

**(1) Adopt the Vivaraṇa locus-thesis.** Brahman is both *āśraya* and *viṣaya* of avidyā. There is no second locus. This avoids the *jīva*-circularity of the *Bhāmatī* and avoids the *anāditva*-deflection by accepting Brahman-as-locus directly.

**(2) Use Madhusūdana's *bhāvarūpa* concession.** Avidyā is positively existent, not mere absence. This avoids the absence-theory incoherences (Madhusūdana's destructive dilemma) and gives avidyā the ontological standing needed for it to have effects.

**(3) Refine via Kashmir-Shaiva *svātantrya*.** The Vivaraṇa thesis (Brahman-as-locus) is read non-deflationarily. Brahman / Śiva, in *sovereign freedom* (*svātantrya*), expresses freedom *as* voluntary self-limitation. Avidyā is not a defect imposed *on* Brahman; avidyā is *Brahman's own free expression of self-veiling*. The *omniscience-objection* is met not by distinguishing levels of consciousness within Brahman but by recognizing that omniscience *includes* the freedom to self-veil. A being with *full svātantrya* can choose to play at being limited; that is what *sovereignty* includes.

**(4) Tie this to *līlā-kaivalyam*.** The self-veiling activity is itself the *līlā* of [BSB 2.1.33](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.33). *Māyā* as the divine's willed self-veiling is *līlā* read at the cognitive register; *līlā* as Brahman's expressive activity is *māyā* read at the cosmological register. They are the same divine activity from different angles. The *līlā-kaivalyam* of [BSB 2.1.33](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.33) and the *bhāvarūpa avidyā* of Madhusūdana, joined by Kashmir-Shaiva *svātantrya*, form a single coherent doctrine.

**(5) Ground in Aurobindo's supermind.** The *vidyā-avidyā-mayī māyā* of Aurobindo's *Life Divine*, māyā composed of both knowledge-mode and apparent-ignorance-mode, the supermind's self-projection in name-and-form, is the *Saccidānanda*-level resolution. Avidyā at the human-cognitive level is the *involuted form* of the supermind's *self-articulating-as-many*. The same divine activity that at the supramental level is *vidyā-māyā* (knowledge-māyā, perfect self-articulation) appears at the human-mental level as *avidyā* (apparent ignorance, partial self-veiling). The two are not in opposition; they are degrees of one self-veiling-and-self-revealing activity.

This resolution preserves what is correct in the Bhāmatī (the *jīva* as functional locus of avidyā at the *vyāvahārika* level, relevant to *sādhana* and the individual's psychic-being development), the Vivaraṇa (the ontological locus in Brahman, no second substance required), Madhusūdana (avidyā as *bhāvarūpa*), Kashmir Shaivism (Brahman's freedom *includes* self-veiling), and Aurobindo (the supermind as the level where vidyā and avidyā are non-different).

It also addresses the genuine bite of Madhva's critique: there *is* a real ontological positive, *bhāvarūpa avidyā* / divine *svātantrya* / supramental *vidyā-avidyā-mayī māyā*, that does the work of accounting for appearance. It is not a mere absence; it is not unstable; it does not require suspending logic. Madhva's own *pañca-bheda* is preserved at the *vyāvahārika* register, the *jīvas* really are distinct from each other and from *Īśvara* at *that* register; the differences are *vyāvahārika-real*, but at the *pāramārthika* register, the differences are recognized as the supermind's own self-articulating activity, not as ontologically independent secondnesses.

This is the user's T7 in its fullest form: *Maya is itself a manifestation of the divine; ignorance is not foreign, it is, ontologically, part of Brahman's own self-manifestation*. The locus problem is solved by recognizing the locus-question as itself an artifact of *vyāvahārika* logic projected onto *pāramārthika* ontology; at the *pāramārthika* level, there is one Real, expressing itself freely as many, with both the unity and the multiplicity *register-true*.

### Closing Note: Śaṅkara as the *Jñāna*-Register Base

Without Śaṅkara, there is no *jñāna* register; without it, no climb-up; without the climb-up, the *vijñāna* return has nothing to return from. T3 preserves the necessity of *jñāna*; Śaṅkara is its architect. The *Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya*'s analysis of ordinary ego-cognition is the most rigorous account of self-deception in the Indian tradition.

The actual Śaṅkara distinguishes *mithyā* from *asat*, makes *līlā-kaivalyam* the center of creation-doctrine, operates with three-tier register-discrimination, takes *bheda-śrutis* seriously at *vyāvahārika*, and writes *Bhaja Govindam* without contradicting his BSB. He is the *jñāna*-register base on which Bhartṛprapañca, Bhāskara, Madhva, Caitanya, and Aurobindo build successive register-recoveries.

*Brahma satyaṁ jagan mithyā jīvo brahmaiva nāparaḥ*, "Brahman is real; the world is sublatable-dependent; the *jīva* is none other than Brahman." The same Govinda is the *līlā-kaivalyam* of [BSB 2.1.33](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.33) and the *Brahman* of the *Adhyāsa-Bhāṣya*'s witness-substrate. Different names, different registers, same Real.
