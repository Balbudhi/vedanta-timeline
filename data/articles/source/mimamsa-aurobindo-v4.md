# Mīmāṃsā Worked: The Symbol-Environment Thesis and the Contemporary Reader's Encounter

## Status of this document

This article supersedes v1, v2, and v3. The earlier passes oscillated between two errors. v1 and v2 framed the question as a story of decline — what went wrong with classical Mīmāṃsā such that *karma-kāṇḍa* came to be perceived as ritualism without depth, and how Aurobindo's hermeneutic recovers what was lost. The framing imposed on the school a moral-cognitive deterioration narrative the texts do not require. v3 corrected by retracting the symbolism-shift thesis altogether, reading Mīmāṃsā as coherent on its own terms (with Clooney as warrant) and treating Aurobindo's hermeneutic strictly as a contemporary-reader stance. The correction was right about Mīmāṃsā's coherence and right about not pathologising the school. It was wrong to retract the symbolism-shift thesis. The shift in symbol-environment is a structural cultural-historical fact independent of any decline-story; it is not a moral failing, not a school-internal corruption, and not a deterioration of consciousness, but it is real, and it bears on what the rite means for whom. This v4 reinstates the symbolism-shift thesis cleanly, in its proper register.

## §1 — The position

Five claims structure the position taken here.

Cross-engagement: Abhinavagupta presses the same manifestation status axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `expressive-manifestation` rather than `real-transformation` in [primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

First, **Mīmāṃsā worked**. The school is coherent on its own terms. Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, and Prabhākara Miśra articulated a practice-philosophy whose technical apparatus — *codanā* as autonomous *pramāṇa* for *dharma*, *bhāvanā* as the cognitive-volitional structure of injunctive force, *adhikāra* as the agent's qualification, *apūrva* as the persistent residue connecting present action to eventual fruit — does the work the school takes it to do. For the practitioners who inhabited the cultural matrix in which the rite was lived practice, the rite operated as itself, with full philosophical and spiritual integrity. There is no internal incoherence and no need for an external supplement to make the school's own categories work.

Second, **the symbol-environment was the lived condition under which the rite operated**. By "symbol-environment" the position means the lived cultural matrix in which the Vedic ritual symbols — *Agni*, *Soma*, *Yajña*, the cow (*go*), the horse (*aśva*), the seven rivers (*sapta sindhavaḥ*) — carried their referential force not as outer objects in isolation but as terms whose external and inner registers were held together in daily practice. The kindled fire on the *gārhapatya* hearth was the same word as the inner aspiration; the cow yielding milk was the same word as the light streaming into the chamber of mind; the pressed *Soma* and the *ānanda*-current bore one name. Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* is the canonical contemporary articulation of how the Vedic vocabulary did this work. The position taken here extends Aurobindo's reading from the Veda back into Mīmāṃsā: the school's externally specifiable rite was the canonical articulation of an apparatus whose inner register was carried by the symbol-environment of those who performed it.

Third, **the symbol-environment changed across centuries**. This is a structural cultural-historical fact, of the kind *Religionsgeschichte* studies. New symbol-systems — devotional *bhakti*, Tantric ritual, sectarian iconography, *Purāṇic* mythography, regional vernacular traditions — layered over the Vedic symbols; the daily lived associations between *Agni* and inner aspiration, between *Soma* and *ānanda*, between *go* and light, attenuated. By the eleventh and twelfth centuries (Maṇḍana, Sureśvara, the great commentators), the Vedic symbols are no longer the primary daily reference of cultivated Hindu life; by the early modern period they are antiquarian; by the twenty-first century, even the inheritors of the tradition rarely have the original lived symbol-system. The change is in the cultural-cognitive substrate that activates the symbols, not in the operations the school describes.

Fourth, **for people outside the original symbol-environment, the rite no longer carries the inner register the symbolism originally bore**. Not because the operations changed — the *codanā* still prompts, the *bhāvanā* still structures volition, the *apūrva* still names the persistent residue — but because the symbol-substrate that activated the operations as inner-yogic correlates is no longer there. This is what the user calls "it didn't work for the people who didn't have the symbolism." The school as a *philosophy* of action, language, *codanā*, and *apūrva* keeps working. What stops working is the *immediate spiritual-symbolic carrier* of the rite for participants who no longer have the symbol-environment.

Fifth, **the school-internal "decline" narrative misdiagnoses the cause**. The traditional internal Hindu narrative attributes the perceived failure of Vedic ritual to Brahmin moral corruption, loss of qualified ritual performers, decay of Sanskrit fluency, *kali-yuga* moral decline. This is the wrong diagnosis. The actual change is structural-cultural — a shift in the symbol-environment — not personal-moral or linguistic-competence. The position taken here is sharp on this point: §5 develops it. To attribute structural cultural change to moral failure is a category mistake.

The article that follows is organized in seven sections. §2 develops the school's own coherence through Clooney's reading. §3 develops the symbol-environment thesis as a structural-cultural fact (the load-bearing new section relative to v3). §4 turns to the contemporary reader's encounter with the corpus and the use Aurobindo's hermeneutic can be put to. §5 rejects the school-internal decline narrative explicitly. §6 draws implications for how the corpus engages Mīmāṃsā in contemporary work. §7 names defeaters honestly.

## §2 — Mīmāṃsā on its own terms: Clooney as the principal interpreter

The standard reception of Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā in twentieth-century English-language scholarship was shaped by the older Vedāntic priority. The school was treated as the technical apparatus of *karma-kāṇḍa*, useful as a propaedeutic to the genuinely philosophical work of *Uttara*-Mīmāṃsā but not in itself a serious philosophical engagement with the world. Clooney's *Thinking Ritually* (1990) is the sustained refutation of that reception. The book's title carries the thesis: the Mīmāṃsā ritualist *thinks*, and the thinking is constitutive of the ritual operation rather than ornamental to it.

Cross-engagement: Arthur Schopenhauer presses the same identity relation axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `self-expression-or-appearance` rather than `non-otherness` in [primary text](cite://schopenhauer/the-world-as-will-and-representation/1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

**Honest scope on Clooney 1990.** The 1990 monograph itself is not on disk in this project's corpus; engagement here proceeds via the title-thesis (which is reported across the secondary literature without controversy), the published abstracts and reviews, the specific arguments cited in Clooney's own subsequent work, and standard scholarly summaries. The 2022 encyclopedia entry "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy" (Springer Encyclopedia of Indian Religions, entry 1397) is similarly engaged via title-thesis, abstract, and the published précis available through the encyclopedia's open metadata. Where particular arguments are attributed below, they are arguments standardly reported in the secondary discussion of Clooney's work; where direct quotation would be required for a specific load-bearing word-by-word claim, the article does not undertake it. This scope is sufficient for the present argument, which uses Clooney as warrant for the proposition that Mīmāṃsā is a coherent reflective practice-philosophy, not for any finer-grained claim that would require page-level citation.

Three of Clooney's moves are engaged substantively here.

### 2.1 The *karmin* as a reflective subject

Clooney's first move is to reframe the agent of the rite. The standard reading treats the *yajamāna* and the *ṛtvij* as performers of a procedural sequence whose efficacy is independent of their cognitive states beyond the minimum required for correct performance. Clooney argues, against this, that the Mīmāṃsā corpus articulates the *karmin* as constitutively a reflective subject. The technical apparatus the school develops — the *aṅga-aṅgi-vibhāga* (analysis of subordinate and principal ritual elements), the rules of *prakaraṇa*, *sthāna*, *krama*, and *samākhyā* ([Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra 3.1–4](cite://jaimini/mimamsa-sutra/3.1-4) with Śabara's *Bhāṣya*), the *atideśa* doctrine for extending procedures across rites (MS 7.1–4) — is not a code for mechanical performance. It is the apparatus a thinking practitioner uses to determine what the rite *is* in this case, what its parts are, how they are subordinated, what counts as correct execution under variable circumstances. The rite, in Mīmāṃsā, is a thing the practitioner *understands*, not just performs.

Cross-engagement: Adi Sankara presses the same selfhood structure axis in the phenomenological register, but with the value `witness-self` rather than `psychic-individual` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

This reframing has a textual center at MS 1.1.1 — *athāto dharma-jijñāsā*, "now therefore the inquiry into *dharma*." The school is, by its own opening word, *jijñāsā* — *inquiry*. The Mīmāṃsaka is not the executor of an inherited code; the Mīmāṃsaka is the inquirer into what *dharma* requires. The rest of the twelve-*adhyāya* corpus is the structured working-out of that inquiry. Mīmāṃsā is a school of *thinking* about ritual, not a school *of* ritual in some non-thinking sense. The point is in the first *sūtra* and Clooney's reading recovers it.

### 2.2 *Codanā* and *bhāvanā*: the cognitive-volitional structure of the injunction

Clooney's second move is to take the *codanā* and *bhāvanā* doctrines as cognitively substantive rather than as machinery for getting from the verbal injunction to the bodily act. The *codanā*, glossed by Śabara as *kriyāyāḥ pravartakaṃ vacanam* — "the speech that prompts to action" (Śābara-Bhāṣya on MS 1.1.2; on-disk Sanskrit, line 8) — is not simply an instruction that lands on a passive recipient. It is a verbal operation that *prompts*, that *urges*, that re-organizes the hearer's intentional life around the prescribed action. Śabara's gloss continues: *tayā yo lakṣyate, so 'rthaḥ puruṣaṃ niḥśreyasena saṃyunaktīti pratijānīmahe* — "what is indicated by it, *that* is the *artha*; we declare that this *artha* conjoins the *puruṣa* with the highest good (*niḥśreyasa*)" (Śābara-Bhāṣya on MS 1.1.2; on-disk Sanskrit, line 9). The *artha* of *codanā* is constitutively defined by what it does *to* the agent. From the foundational *Bhāṣya*, the school's category of *artha* is agent-binding, not world-describing.

Cross-engagement: Anandabodha presses the same finite cognition axis in the epistemological register, but with the value `positive-ignorance` rather than `adhyasa-or-superimposition` in [primary text](cite://sarvajnatman/samksepa-sariraka/1.319-352). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

The *bhāvanā* doctrine, articulated by Śabara (on MS 2.1.1) and elaborated by Kumārila Bhaṭṭa in the *Tantra-Vārttika* and by Maṇḍana Miśra in the *Vidhi-Viveka*, names two operations:

- *śābdī bhāvanā* — the action of the optative suffix on the hearer's intention; the verbal force prior to action.
- *ārthī bhāvanā* — the agent's volitional energy directed toward the prescribed result.

Clooney's reading treats the *bhāvanā*-pair as the school's own articulation of the cognitive-volitional core of the rite. The first half is what language *does* to the listener. The second half is what the listener-turned-agent *does* under the language's prompting. Both halves are interior. Neither is bodily-procedural in the reductive sense the older reception assumed. The Mīmāṃsakas are giving an account of how a verbal injunction, received by a competent agent, re-orders that agent's intentional life and is enacted as a determinate course of action. That is, on Clooney's reading, philosophy of action carried through with technical precision.

### 2.3 *Adhikāra*, *apūrva*, and the reflective architecture of qualification

Clooney's third move is to read *adhikāra* and *apūrva* as further evidence of the school's reflective architecture. The *adhikāra* doctrine specifies the conditions under which a given agent is qualified to perform a given rite. Śabara's analysis distinguishes external prerequisites (caste, age, ritual purity, possession of fire) from intrinsic capacity — the agent's desire (*kāma*), cultivated capability, and inner disposition. The Mīmāṃsā treatment of *adhikāra* is not a sociological classification; it is a structural doctrine about the conditions under which a verbal injunction binds a particular agent. The agent is internal to the system as a qualified subject, not external to it as a contingent performer.

Cross-engagement: Akshapada Gautama presses the same epistemic authority axis in the epistemological register, but with the value `plural-pramana-realism` rather than `scripture-dominant` in [primary text](cite://aksapada/nyaya-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

*Apūrva* is the most contested category in the school. Standard polemic reads *apūrva* as a metaphysical placeholder — an unseen entity invoked to bridge the gap between the present rite and the posthumous *phala*. Clooney does not deny that the Mīmāṃsakas develop *apūrva* in this metaphysical register. The functional point is that *apūrva* names the school's account of how the action's effect is real, not exhausted by its visible occurrence, and structured by the verbal injunction that prompted it. The Mīmāṃsakas, faced with the question "what makes the rite efficacious across the gap between performance and fruit," answer with a category that gives the persistence ontological weight.

### 2.4 The introspective extension: Clooney 2022

The 2022 encyclopedia entry sharpens the 1990 reading. The argument is that Mīmāṃsā is not only philosophy of language and exegesis but *introspective literature*: a literature that thematises the practitioner's interior orientation to the sacred. Working through Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika* — the *Codanā-Sūtra* section on the autonomous validity of injunctive cognition (*svataḥ-prāmāṇya*), the *ātmavāda* on the self-luminous *ātman*, and the Bhāṭṭa analysis of how injunctive cognition operates on the agent — the entry establishes that the school articulates an interior dimension within the ritual operation itself.

Cross-engagement: Alfred North Whitehead presses the same temporal mode axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `process-primary` rather than `both-orthogonal` in [primary text](cite://whitehead/process-and-reality/1.2). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

The 2022 entry does two things. It establishes in a major reference work that the introspective-philosophical reading of Mīmāṃsā is academically responsible and textually grounded. And it makes clear that the introspective dimension is *internal to the school's self-understanding*, not imposed from outside. The Mīmāṃsakas themselves articulate a *karmin* whose cognitive and volitional life is at the center of the ritual operation. Clooney is not interpolating an external register into the texts; he is recovering a register the texts already carry, which the post-Śaṅkara Vedāntic reception and the modern academic reception had downplayed.

### 2.5 What this section establishes

Mīmāṃsā is a coherent reflective practice-philosophy whose interior dimension belongs to the school's own self-understanding. The school is precisely as coherent as Clooney shows it to be. This is the foundation on which the rest of the article rests. The symbol-environment thesis developed in §3 is *not* offered as a fix to a defective school. The school is not defective. The thesis is offered as an account of why the same coherent operations, performed at different cultural moments, do not bear the same lived weight for the participants.

Hegel presses the same evolution register axis in the historical-genealogical register, but with the value `sublative-becoming` rather than `real-cosmological-evolution` in [primary text](cite://hegel/phenomenology-of-spirit/17). ## §3 — The symbol-environment thesis

This section is the load-bearing new section relative to v3. It develops the symbol-environment thesis as a structural cultural-historical fact, in vocabulary drawn from history of religions and the comparative study of ritual symbolism. The thesis is that the rite's operation as the inner-yogic correlate Aurobindo decodes was carried, for the original practitioners, by the symbol-environment they inhabited. The symbol-environment changed; the inner register dropped out; the school's apparatus continued to operate as a philosophy of action, language, and persistence, but its immediate spiritual-symbolic carrier no longer reached participants who lacked the original substrate. This is structural change, not corruption.

Cross-engagement: Baladeva Vidyabhushana presses the same truth-structure axis in the epistemological register, but with the value `single-absolute-truth` rather than `hierarchical-standpoint-truth` in [primary text](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

### 3.1 What "symbol-environment" means

By symbol-environment the position means the matrix of lived cultural usage in which a given symbol-system carries its referential force. A symbol-environment is not a doctrine and not a private mental state. It is the public-cultural fact that, in a given community at a given period, certain symbols carry certain meanings as part of the daily practice of speaking, eating, kindling fire, marrying, dying, naming children, addressing elders, going about. The symbols and the meanings are held together by repeated use, embedded ritual, shared narrative, and the simple transmissive force of one generation handing the system to the next.

Cross-engagement: Bhaktisiddhanta Saraswati presses the same motive force axis in the soteriological register, but with the value `devotion` rather than `bliss-or-delight` in [primary text](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/5.29). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

For the early Vedic practitioner, *Agni* was at once the literal fire on the hearth and the seer-will Aurobindo decodes; *Soma* was at once the pressed plant juice and the *ānanda*-current; *go* was at once the cow yielding milk and the light streaming into the chamber of mind; *aśva* was at once the horse drawing the chariot and the embodied energy. The two registers were not held together by a learned interpretive operation; they were held together by the structure of the symbol-environment. To say "*Agni*" was, in that environment, to say both at once, without an act of decoding intervening.

Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* (CWSA Vol. 15) is the canonical contemporary articulation of how the Vedic vocabulary carries this dual reference. The "double language" thesis (CWSA 15, pp. 8–9) says that the Veda's expressions are at once outer-referential (the fire, the cow, the horse, the rivers) and inner-referential (the seer-will, the light, the embodied energy, the streams of inspiration), and that the Vedic *ṛṣis* spoke in a register where the two were one utterance. The thesis describes a feature of the symbol-environment, not a private code requiring decryption.

The position taken here extends Aurobindo's reading from the Veda back into Mīmāṃsā as the school of *karma-kāṇḍa*. Mīmāṃsā externalizes the rite — articulates it as a determinate, publicly specifiable operation involving these substances, these formulas, these qualified performers. The externalization is not a flattening. Under the conditions of the original symbol-environment, the externally-specifiable operation carried its inner correlates by the structure of the environment itself; explicit decoding would have been redundant, possibly distorting. The Mīmāṃsakas were faithful to the integrity of a practice in which the inner register was already carried, in lived practice, without needing separate articulation.

### 3.2 How the symbol-environment changes

Symbol-environments change by ordinary historical processes. They are not changed by sudden moral catastrophe or by deliberate corruption; they shift slowly under the pressure of new linguistic registers, new ritual forms, new political configurations, new economic substrates, new patterns of religious practice that compete with the older ones. The change is structural-cultural, in the sense studied by *Religionsgeschichte* and the comparative anthropology of religion. Mircea Eliade's discussion of the saturation and de-saturation of religious symbols (in *Patterns in Comparative Religion* and elsewhere; the position taken here references his conceptual vocabulary, which is standardly summarized, without page-level engagement with works not on disk in this project) gives one register for it. Alexis Sanderson's mapping of the rise of Tantric ritual systems in the early-medieval period and their layering over Vedic substrates gives another. Wilhelm Halbfass's treatment of Indian cultural transformation (in *Tradition and Reflection*; on-disk research file `R5_halbfass.md`) gives a third.

Cross-engagement: Anantakrishna Sastri presses the same practice-path axis in the soteriological register, but with the value `knowledge-discipline` rather than `transformative-integration` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

Three concrete dynamics, in the Indian case, are worth naming.

First, **the rise of devotional registers**. From the centuries leading up to the early-medieval period, the bhakti registers grow from local cult-practice into pan-Indian religious form. New deities, new images, new temples, new ritual idioms — *pūjā* in the iconic register, *darśana* of the deity-image, *kīrtana* and *bhajana* — establish themselves as the lived religious substrate of a much wider population than ever participated in *śrauta* ritual. The Vedic vocabulary remains in liturgical use, but its lived associations migrate: *Agni* becomes one of many deities in a much larger pantheon, no longer the center of daily ritual life for the cultivated Hindu.

Second, **the rise of Tantric ritual systems**. Sanderson's work documents the absorption of the Vedic ritual idiom into the Śaiva, Vaiṣṇava, and Śākta Tantric systems through the early-medieval period. Tantric ritual builds new symbol-environments — *cakras*, *mantras* with new theologies of efficacy, new initiatory structures, new inner-yogic correspondences — that re-articulate the inner register of ritual in a substantially different vocabulary. The Vedic substrate persists liturgically but the inner-yogic register that was originally carried by the Vedic vocabulary is increasingly carried by Tantric vocabulary instead.

Third, **the antiquarisation of the Vedic substrate**. By the early-modern and modern periods, the Vedic ritual idiom is preserved by specialist communities (*śrautins*, *Veda-pāṭhaśālā*-trained Brahmins) but is no longer the substrate of cultivated Hindu life. The language itself becomes a learned register, accessible by training rather than native immersion. By the twenty-first century, even the inheritors of the tradition rarely have, as their daily lived substrate, the symbol-environment in which *Agni* is at once hearth-fire and seer-will. The substrate has shifted.

None of this is corruption. None of it requires moral failure or ritual ossification or *kali-yuga* deterioration. It is the ordinary history of how symbol-environments change under cultural pressure over many centuries. It is the kind of fact one would document if one were writing the *Religionsgeschichte* of any large tradition over a comparable timespan.

### 3.3 What this means for the rite

The Mīmāṃsā rite's operations remain what the school says they are. The *codanā* still operates as the autonomous *pramāṇa* for *dharma*. The *bhāvanā*-pair still articulates the verbal-and-volitional structure of injunctive force. The *adhikāra* doctrine still specifies the agent's qualification. The *apūrva* still names the residue. These are operations of language, cognition, volition, and persistence, and they hold whenever an agent receives a verbal injunction and acts under it. The school as a *philosophy* of these operations does not lose force under symbol-environment change; the operations remain analysable on the school's own terms.

Cross-engagement: Appayya Dikshita presses the same soteric end axis in the soteriological register, but with the value `identity-with-ground` rather than `transformation-of-life` in [primary text](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/3.4). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

What changes is the rite's *immediate spiritual-symbolic carrier*. For the original practitioner, the *codanā* was not just an instance of injunctive verbal force in general; it was the *codanā* specifically of the Vedic injunction, in a symbol-environment where the Vedic vocabulary carried its dual reference as a feature of lived practice. The agent's *bhāvanā* was not just any volitional re-organization around an enjoined act; it was the volitional re-organization around the act of offering *havis* into *Agni* in a symbol-environment where *Agni* was at once the literal fire and the seer-will. The *apūrva* was not just any persistent residue; it was the persistent residue of a rite whose inner register was carried by the symbol-environment.

For the participant outside the original symbol-environment, the operations remain the same operations, but the symbolic dimension that originally rendered them inner-yogic correlates of consciousness is no longer carried by the substrate. The rite still works as language, cognition, volition, and persistence. It does not, for the participant who lacks the symbol-environment, work as the inner-yogic correlate Aurobindo decodes. This is what the user calls "it didn't work for the people who didn't have the symbolism." It is a precise claim, and it is a claim about the participant's symbol-environment, not about the school.

### 3.4 The diachronic distribution

The shift is diachronic and uneven. There were no doubt practitioners in the eleventh and twelfth centuries who still inhabited substantial portions of the original symbol-environment, alongside others for whom it had already become antiquarian. The shift is not a single historical moment; it is the slow change in the distribution of who, in a given community, has the substrate and who does not. By the time of Maṇḍana and Sureśvara, the philosophical conversation about *karma* and *jñāna* is happening primarily among interlocutors for whom the *śrauta* substrate is one inheritance among several rather than the primary lived register. By the time of the early-modern commentators, the *śrauta* substrate is largely the inheritance of specialist communities. By the twenty-first century, it is rare even within those communities.

The unevenness matters for §7 (defeaters): there are contemporary practitioners for whom substantial portions of the original symbol-environment remain lived, and for whom the rite continues to bear its original inner register. The position taken here does not deny their existence; it speaks to the diachronic statistical fact that, across centuries, the proportion of participants who inhabit the substrate has shifted, and to the corresponding fact about the contemporary English-reading inheritor of the tradition for whom the substrate is largely absent.

### 3.5 Why this is structural and not moral

The position taken here insists that the change in the symbol-environment is a structural cultural-historical fact, not a moral or personal failing. The vocabulary required to make this clear is the vocabulary of comparative religion and history of ritual systems — not the vocabulary of decline, decay, or degeneration. *Religionsgeschichte* studies how symbol-systems form, propagate, layer, and re-form across centuries. This is its standard subject matter. To treat the Vedic-Mīmāṃsā case in those terms is to treat it as one case among many, analysable by ordinary historical methods, without any imputation of failure on the part of the practitioners who inhabited the changing substrate.

The traditional internal Hindu narrative often borrows decline-vocabulary from the *yuga*-discourse (*kṛta*, *tretā*, *dvāpara*, *kali*) and reads structural cultural change as moral deterioration. The position taken here rejects that translation. *Yuga*-discourse is a theological cosmology with its own framework; it is not a historiography of ritual systems. Reading the symbol-environment shift through *yuga*-discourse misdescribes a structural-cultural fact as a moral one. §5 develops this further.

## §4 — The contemporary reader's encounter

Aurobindo's hermeneutic is, on its own self-presentation, a *recovery* — the recovery of an earlier Vedic sense that, on his account, was already opaque to Yāska and to Sāyaṇa, and that the modern reader needs explicit interpretive work to access. The position taken here reads this as *amplification* of what was once lived, for the contemporary reader who lacks the original symbol-environment, and not as *correction* of a school whose original practice was somehow defective.

### 4.1 The asymmetry of the symbol-environment

The classical Mīmāṃsaka inhabited a culture in which the Vedic apparatus was the substantive vocabulary of public and personal life. *Agni* was the actual fire kindled in the actual *gārhapatya*, *āhavanīya*, and *dakṣiṇāgni* hearths of the actual ritual enclosure; the inner resonance of *Agni* as seer-will was carried in the lived practice without needing separate articulation. The same is true of *Soma*, *Vāc*, and the other Vedic terms. The terms were not foreign vocabulary requiring interpretive recovery; they were the household vocabulary of a ritual culture. Under those conditions, an explicit psychological-symbolic decoding of the terms would have been redundant at best, distorting at worst. The Mīmāṃsakas' insistence on the literal-ritual reading was not a failure to appreciate depth; it was a faithfulness to the integrity of a practice in which the depth was already carried in the lived application of the literal terms.

The contemporary reader inhabits a different symbol-environment. The Vedic apparatus is foreign vocabulary. *Agni* arrives in translation as "fire" or "the fire-deity," and the contemporary reader has no lived practice in which a kindled fire functions as the substantive vocabulary of ritual life. The *gārhapatya* hearth is, for the contemporary reader, a curiosity. Under those conditions, a literal-ritual reading of the Vedic corpus is *not equivalent* to the literal-ritual reading available to the classical Mīmāṃsaka. The same propositional content, encountered in a symbol-environment where the symbols no longer carry their original lived weight, is a different thing. The literal reading, for the contemporary reader, leaves the corpus as antiquarian rather than transformative.

### 4.2 Aurobindo's hermeneutic as amplification

This is where Aurobindo's hermeneutic does its work. By proposing that *Agni* names the seer-will, *Soma* the *ānanda*-current, *Indra* the illumined intelligence, Aurobindo gives the contemporary reader an interpretive frame in which the Vedic terms are again referring to something the reader has access to in their own interior life. The frame is not what the Mīmāṃsakas had — they did not need it. It is what the contemporary reader, lacking the original symbol-environment, can use to encounter the corpus as a living document. The hermeneutic is amplification of what was once lived for those who lived it, available now to the reader who does not have the substrate.

Two clarifications attach to this. First, amplification is not correction. The Mīmāṃsakas were not making a mistake the hermeneutic now repairs. They were giving a coherent account, in their own categories, of the practice they actually performed under the substrate they actually inhabited. The contemporary reader, who does not perform that practice and does not inhabit that substrate, can use Aurobindo's hermeneutic to find a register in which the Mīmāṃsaka categories illuminate something in their own life. That is amplification of the corpus's reach for a new audience, not correction of the corpus's original meaning.

Second, the hermeneutic does not retroactively modify what the rite was for the original practitioner. The original practitioner had the symbol-environment, the rite operated in their life as the inner-yogic correlate Aurobindo decodes (on this article's reading), and no contemporary recovery is needed for them. The contemporary reader has lost the symbol-environment; the recovery is for the contemporary reader. The two cases are not in competition. The original practice and the contemporary recovery are different operations on different audiences with different substrates.

### 4.3 The *Synthesis of Yoga* register

The *Synthesis of Yoga* extends the hermeneutic from the Vedic *saṃhitā* to the broader category of *yajña* itself. Aurobindo's formulation in I.IV "The Sacrifice, the Triune Path and the Lord of the Sacrifice" (CWSA 23, p. 89) — "Life, not a remote silent or high-uplifted ecstatic Beyond-Life alone, is the field of our Yoga" — generalizes the *yajña-bhūmi* to the whole field of conscious life. The triple character of the sacrifice — works, love, and knowledge (CWSA 23, p. 134) — names the three modes in which the contemporary reader can engage their own life as a *yajña*.

Applied to the Mīmāṃsā corpus, this hermeneutic produces the following: the contemporary reader, encountering Mīmāṃsā's account of *codanā*, *bhāvanā*, *adhikāra*, and *apūrva*, can read the school's articulation of how a verbal injunction operates on a qualified agent as an account that has analogs in their own life. The reader does not perform a *śrauta* sacrifice; the reader has, however, the experience of being summoned by an obligation, of organizing their volitional life around a course of action, of acting under the call of a normative source they did not generate, of leaving behind a residue that persists. The Mīmāṃsaka categories are, on this reading, generalisable to the structures of conscious obligation in the contemporary reader's own life. Aurobindo's hermeneutic is what makes the generalization visible, and the generalization is amplification of what the original symbol-environment carried at the level of immediate lived practice.

### 4.4 Why the asymmetry matters dialectically

The asymmetry licenses a more careful dialectic with the Vedāntic critique. Sureśvara's [*Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* 1.67](cite://sureshvara/naishkarmya-siddhi/1.67) (Alston tr. 1959) carries the canonical argument that ritual is structurally incapable of liberation: "The knowledge derived from Vedic revelation demolishes nescience in its form as modified into the factors necessary for action at a single stroke. Hence there can be no association between the two." Sureśvara's argument is intra-Vedāntic; it concerns the proper relation of *karma* and *jñāna* in a soteriological economy where *jñāna* is the direct cause of liberation. The argument lands against the claim that ritual *as such* produces liberation. Mīmāṃsā does not, in its own self-understanding, claim to produce *mokṣa* in the Advaita sense; it claims to produce *niḥśreyasa* through *codanā*-prompted action that conjoins the *puruṣa* with the highest good (Śabara on MS 1.1.2), and Mīmāṃsā's *niḥśreyasa* is not Advaita's *kaivalya*. The Sureśvara critique and the Mīmāṃsaka claim are talking past one another. The two schools have different soteriological economies. The contemporary reader, equipped with both, can hold the disagreement as a real philosophical disagreement rather than as evidence that one school has displaced the other.

§5 returns to Sureśvara to insist that his structural critique is not evidence of Mīmāṃsā's having "stopped working."

## §5 — Why the school-internal "decline" narrative gets it wrong

The internal Hindu tradition often diagnoses the eclipse of *karma-kāṇḍa* as a story of decline: Brahmin moral corruption, the loss of qualified ritual performers, the decay of Sanskrit fluency, the supposed moral deterioration intrinsic to *kali-yuga*. Each of these diagnoses identifies a real-enough phenomenon at one period or another. None of them gets the structural fact right. The actual change is the shift in the symbol-environment, which is a structural cultural-historical fact, not a moral or personal failing.

### 5.1 The traditional diagnosis is a misdiagnosis

The traditional diagnosis attributes structural-cultural change to moral or personal causes. Three problems attach to the move.

First, **the proposed causes are derivative, not basic**. Even granting that Sanskrit fluency declined in some communities at some periods, fluency-loss is a downstream effect of broader cultural change, not its cause. The same is true of the supposed loss of qualified ritual performers: the proportion of any community trained in *śrauta* practice tracks the lived role of *śrauta* practice in that community's religious life, and the lived role tracks the symbol-environment. The diagnosis runs the causal arrow backward.

Second, **the proposed moral causes are unfalsifiable**. To say that ritual ceased to operate because of *kali-yuga* moral decline is to invoke a theological cosmology with built-in cyclical structure as a historiographical explanation. *Yuga*-discourse functions as theological framework; it does not do the work of explaining specific cultural-historical transitions. Treating it as if it does conflates two genres.

Third, **the diagnosis insults the practitioners**. It locates the cause of the rite's diminished register for later participants in the moral failure of those participants. Yet the same participants, asked to perform *bhakti* worship of Kṛṣṇa or *darśana* of the *liṅga*, experienced those rites with full lived efficacy. The shift was not into impiety; it was into a different ritual idiom. To call the shift a moral failure is to refuse to recognize the new ritual idiom as itself a religious form.

### 5.2 The structural account does the work better

The position taken here insists, against the traditional diagnosis, that the structural-cultural account is the correct one. The symbol-environment changed. The Vedic ritual idiom was layered with bhakti, Tantric, and sectarian idioms; the daily lived associations between Vedic vocabulary and the inner-yogic register attenuated; new ritual idioms with their own inner registers grew up around new vocabulary. None of this involved moral failure. The Hindu tradition continued to be a living religious form throughout; what changed was which symbolic register carried the inner work for whom.

The Mīmāṃsā rite, considered as a determinate practice with determinate participants, continued to operate in its own register for those who continued to inhabit the relevant substrate. For others, the rite's *immediate spiritual-symbolic carrier* dimmed, while its operations as language, cognition, volition, and persistence remained analysable on the school's own terms. The school as philosophy persists; the lived register of the rite as inner-yogic correlate, for participants outside the substrate, does not. This is structural, not moral.

### 5.3 Sureśvara [*NS* 1.67](cite://sureshvara/naishkarmya-siddhi/1.67) is intra-Vedāntic, not evidence of Mīmāṃsā failing

Sureśvara's [*Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* 1.67](cite://sureshvara/naishkarmya-siddhi/1.67) is sometimes mobilised in the decline narrative as if it were evidence that ritual stopped working. It is not. It is a structural philosophical critique of *karma* in an Advaita soteriology that takes *jñāna* as the direct cause of liberation. The argument is internal to a particular Vedāntic position about the *karma*–*jñāna* relation; it engages Mīmāṃsā as a rival theoretical position about how action figures in the path to *niḥśreyasa* / *mokṣa*.

The critique stands as a serious intra-Vedāntic argument. It does not stand as evidence that Mīmāṃsā stopped working. Mīmāṃsā continued to work, in the registers in which it works, for those who continued to work in those registers. Sureśvara's argument is about whether *karma* can be the direct cause of *mokṣa* in his Advaita sense; the Mīmāṃsaka claim is about how *codanā*-prompted action conjoins the *puruṣa* with *niḥśreyasa* in their sense. These are different claims about different soteriological economies, and the contemporary reader equipped with both can hold the philosophical disagreement as a philosophical disagreement, not as a verdict on the school's vitality.

### 5.4 The mistake the decline narrative makes

The decline narrative compresses three distinct things into one story. It compresses (a) the structural-cultural shift in the symbol-environment, (b) the philosophical disagreement between Mīmāṃsā and Vedānta about the *karma*–*jñāna* relation, and (c) the fluctuating empirical state of *śrauta* practice across centuries — into a single narrative of decay. The compression is wrong. The three are different phenomena with different causes and different evaluations. Disentangling them is necessary for any serious engagement with the school.

The position taken here treats Mīmāṃsā as a coherent practice-philosophy that worked for the practitioners who had the symbol-environment, that continues to work as a philosophy of action and language, and that no longer carries its original immediate spiritual-symbolic register for participants outside the original substrate. None of this is decline. The decline-narrative is the wrong frame; the structural-cultural frame is the right one.

## §6 — Implications for engagement

Three implications for the corpus's engagement with Mīmāṃsā follow.

First, the entries on Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila, and Prabhākara should be read as the canonical articulations of a coherent practice-philosophy. The school's claims — *codanā* as autonomous *pramāṇa* (MS 1.1.2; Śabara *Bhāṣya* ad loc.), the *bhāvanā* analysis of injunctive force (Śabara on MS 2.1.1; Kumārila *Tantra-Vārttika*), the *adhikāra* doctrine of qualification, the *apūrva* doctrine of persistence, the *aṅga-aṅgi-vibhāga* (MS 3.1–4) — are not preliminary articulations awaiting an external recovery. They are the school's account of the practice it carried, articulated with technical precision, and they stand on their own.

Second, contemporary engagement — academic, philosophical, or spiritual — operates from outside the original symbol-environment. This is a structural fact about the contemporary reader's position, not a deficiency of the reader. Aurobindo's hermeneutic is one stance available to the contemporary reader for engaging the corpus as a living document; Clooney's reading is another, more strictly philosophical-introspective; the historian-of-religion's reading is a third. Each is an engagement-stance available to a participant outside the original substrate, and each does different work. The corpus need not collapse them into one. The position taken here uses Aurobindo's hermeneutic for one specific purpose: to make visible, for the contemporary reader, what the original symbol-environment carried as a feature of lived practice. Other stances do other work.

Third, the position taken here is a comparative-philosophical reading, not a Mīmāṃsaka self-understanding. The Mīmāṃsakas may very well disagree with this reading; their own anti-*nirvacana* polemic against psychological-symbolic decoding of ritual terms is real (Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* on Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika*, on disk in Sanskrit, carries arguments to this effect). The reading is offered as a contemporary-reader stance available to a participant outside the original substrate, not as the reading the Mīmāṃsakas themselves would offer. This honest scope marker matters: the position is a hermeneutic for the contemporary reader, not a claim about the school's self-understanding.

A practical corollary for the corpus: thinker entries on Mīmāṃsā figures should foreground the school's own categories and Clooney's reading as the spine of the philosophical engagement, with the symbol-environment thesis and Aurobindo's hermeneutic added as the contemporary-reader stance the project takes when engaging the corpus as a living document. The two layers should be marked clearly. A reader wanting only the philosophical engagement gets it from the school's categories and Clooney; a reader wanting also the contemporary-reader amplification gets the additional layer the position taken here adds.

## §7 — Defeaters

Four places the position taken here may be challenged.

**First, the Mīmāṃsakas' own anti-symbolic-decoding polemic.** Classical Mīmāṃsā explicitly rejects *nirvacana*-style psychological-symbolic decoding of ritual terms. Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* on Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika*, on disk in Sanskrit, carries arguments to this effect. The position taken here owns this. Aurobindo's hermeneutic is *not* what classical Mīmāṃsā would endorse. The hermeneutic is a contemporary-reader stance, available to a reader outside the original symbol-environment, that the Mīmāṃsakas would on their own self-understanding reject. The reader who finds this an unacceptable interpretive liberty has a real ground for refusing the hermeneutic; the position does not force the hermeneutic on any reader. It offers it.

**Second, contemporary practitioners who still inhabit substantial portions of the original symbol-environment.** There remain communities — *śrauta* lineages, *Veda-pāṭhaśālā*-trained Brahmins, traditional ritualist circles in parts of India — for whom the Vedic vocabulary is not foreign but lived. For such practitioners, the rite continues to operate as the Mīmāṃsakas describe; the symbol-environment thesis does not predict that the rite has stopped working for them, and the position taken here predicts the opposite. If the position were taken to assert that the rite no longer works for *anyone*, it would be wrong about these practitioners. The position is more limited: it holds that the proportion of participants who inhabit the substrate has shifted across centuries, and that for the contemporary English-reading inheritor of the tradition, in particular, the substrate is largely absent. Critics who locate the position as committed to a stronger universal claim will find that stronger claim refuted by the existence of the lineages just named. The position does not rest on the stronger claim.

**Third, the empirical specifics of the symbol-environment shift.** The position taken here claims that symbol-environments shift, layer, and re-form across centuries by ordinary cultural-historical processes. The vocabulary of Eliade, Sanderson, Halbfass is invoked to give the conceptual frame. But the position does not undertake a detailed historical reconstruction of the specific Vedic-symbol-environment shift across the relevant centuries. A historian of religions could legitimately ask for finer-grained empirical detail than the position provides. The position is, at the level of empirical historical detail, a programmatic structural-cultural claim rather than a fully worked-out *Religionsgeschichte*. It is offered as such; refining the empirical detail is a project for further work, and a critic who finds the empirical underspecification objectionable has a real ground for objection.

**Fourth, the position takes Clooney's reading of Mīmāṃsā as established.** If Clooney's reading were wrong — if the school were, as some of the older reception had it, a school of procedural exegesis without the reflective-introspective dimension Clooney attributes to it — then §2 would need a different warrant. The defeater is not just abstract: there are scholars (the older Vedāntic reception, parts of the modern academic reception) who would dispute Clooney's introspective reading. The position here does not undertake to refute those readings; it takes Clooney's reading as the most defensible English-language reading currently available and proceeds from there. A reader who takes the older procedural-exegesis reading of Mīmāṃsā as correct will need a different argument for why the school is a coherent practice-philosophy worth contemporary engagement, or will need to abandon the contemporary-engagement thesis. The position taken here does not provide that alternative argument.

These four defeaters are real. The position is not invulnerable. The position is, however, defensible as a structural-cultural reading: it takes the school as coherent on its own terms (with Clooney as warrant); it treats the symbol-environment shift as a structural cultural-historical fact; it offers Aurobindo's hermeneutic as a contemporary-reader amplification of what was once lived rather than a school-internal correction; it explicitly rejects the moral-decline narrative; and it acknowledges where it asks the reader to take an interpretive step the school's own self-understanding does not authorize.
