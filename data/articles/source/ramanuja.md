## Reader's Orientation

### Life and historical placement

Cross-engagement: Abhinavagupta presses the same substrate structure axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `one-self-standing` rather than `one-qualified-by-real-internal-distinctions` in [primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

Rāmānujācārya (c. 1017–1137) stands between Śaṅkara and Madhva as the first great systematic Śrī-Vaiṣṇava Vedāntin. He inherits the Tamil–Sanskrit synthesis of the Āḻvārs, Nāthamuni, and Yāmunācārya, and turns the *Brahma-Sūtra* tradition into a rigorously Vaiṣṇava philosophical project.

Cross-engagement: Adi Sankara presses the same manifestation status axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `sublatable-not-null` rather than `dependent-real` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

### The six major works

The core texts for this reader are the *Śrī-Bhāṣya* (especially the *saptavidhānupapatti* in 1.1.1), the *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* (the clearest free-standing statement of *saviśeṣa* Brahman, *paryudāsa*, and *aprthak-siddhi*), and the *Gītā-Bhāṣya* (the body-soul reading of the *Gītā*). The shorter *Vedānta-Dīpa*, *Vedānta-Sāra*, and the three *Gadyas* complete the picture.

Cross-engagement: Anandabodha presses the same identity relation axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `non-otherness` rather than `body-soul-qualification` in [primary text](cite://sarvajnatman/samksepa-sariraka/1.319-352). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

### Viśiṣṭādvaita — the doctrine in one sentence

Rāmānuja's position can be stated in one line: one Brahman, intrinsically qualified by real modes (*cit* and *acit*), under the asymmetric mode-substance relation called *aprthak-siddha-viśeṣaṇa-viśeṣya-bhāva*. That is how he makes both *bheda* and *abheda* texts true at once: difference names real qualifications, non-difference names their inseparability from the one substance.

Cross-engagement: Akalanka presses the same epistemic authority axis in the epistemological register, but with the value `plural-pramana-realism` rather than `scripture-dominant` in [primary text](cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/1.34-35). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

### Position within the post-Śaṅkara trajectory

Rāmānuja is pivotal in the realist trajectory of Vedānta because he supplies both a sustained internal critique of Advaita's *avidyā* doctrine and a positive ontology able to preserve unity without erasing difference: *aprthak-siddhi*, the body-soul framework, and the *paryudāsa* reading of the *nirguṇa* texts.

Cross-engagement: Anantakrishna Sastri presses the same truth-structure axis in the epistemological register, but with the value `hierarchical-standpoint-truth` rather than `single-absolute-truth` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

# Part I — The Saptavidhānupapatti

*Śrī-Bhāṣya* on [*Brahma-Sūtra* 1.1.1](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1), *Mahāsiddhānta* section. The *Brahma-Sūtra* opens *athāto brahma-jijñāsā* — "Now therefore, the inquiry into Brahman." Every Vedāntic commentator must specify what *Brahman* means before the inquiry begins. Śaṅkara's preamble (the *Adhyāsa-bhāṣya*) begins from the subject-object problem and argues that all empirical cognition rests on superimposition (*adhyāsa*); the inquiry into Brahman is therefore simultaneously the inquiry into the superimposition that conceals it. Rāmānuja's *Mahāsiddhānta* begins differently. He opens with the positive doctrine of *viśiṣṭādvaita* — one Brahman whose body (*śarīra*) is the totality of conscious and non-conscious entities — and then turns to the negative work: the demolition of Śaṅkara's foundational concept of *avidyā/māyā* on strictly logical grounds.

The negative work takes the form of the *saptavidhānupapatti* — "the seven impossibilities," "the seven failures of rational justification." Each *anupapatti* identifies a structural logical defect in the Advaita doctrine of *avidyā* as a positive (*bhāvarūpa*) but indefinable (*anirvacanīya*) power that conceals Brahman from itself. The arguments are tight, internally rigorous, and cumulative — they target the *logical structure* of the Advaitin's position rather than its theological commitments.

The framing question, posed at the opening of the *Mahāsiddhānta* section, is whether Brahman is merely pure undifferentiated consciousness or rather the infinite reality qualified by omniscience, omnipotence, truthful will, and other auspicious attributes.

(Phrasing reconstructed from Thibaut SBE 48 introduction and Raghavachar's exposition; should be checked against GRETIL rambh_1u.htm for exact form.)

**English.** "This being established, the nature of Brahman as conceived by the Advaitin is now examined: Is Brahman pure, unqualified consciousness (*nirviśeṣaṁ śuddha-caitanyam*) only — or is it qualified (*saviśeṣa*), the infinite One distinguished by auspicious attributes such as omniscience (*sarvajñatā*), omnipotence (*sarvakartṛtva*), and truthful will (*satya-saṅkalpa*)?"

The question is genuinely posed; the *Mahāsiddhānta* is the answer that *nirviśeṣa* Brahman is logically incoherent. The seven *anupapattis* establish this in seven distinct articulations, each closing a different escape route.

## 1. Āśraya-anupapatti — the substrate problem

*Where does avidyā reside?* The opening claim of this section is that the substrate of *avidyā* would have to be either the *jīva* or Brahman, yet both options fail: the former by circularity, the latter by contradiction with Brahman's pure consciousness.

Cross-engagement: Alfred North Whitehead presses the same soteric end axis in the soteriological register, but with the value `not-soteriological` rather than `service-with-distinction-preserved` in [primary text](cite://whitehead/process-and-reality/1.2). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

(Sanskrit reconstructed from Thibaut SBE 48 pp. 26–35 and Lipner *Face of Truth* pp. 34–37; the structural argument is uncontroversial in the secondary literature, the exact verbal form should be cross-checked against the Karmarkar edition.)

**English.** "The substrate of *avidyā* would be either the *jīva* or Brahman. Not the *jīva* — because the *jīva* is itself a construction of *avidyā*; making the *jīva* the substrate of *avidyā* involves mutual dependence (*anyonyāśrayatā*). Not Brahman — because for pure consciousness (*śuddha-caitanya*) to be the substrate of *avidyā* is impossible (*anupapanna*)."

**Premise.** Every real entity (*bhāva*) requires a substrate (*āśraya*). The Advaitin holds *avidyā* to be *bhāvarūpa* — positively existent, not a mere absence. *Bhāvarūpa* entities, on the standard Indian metaphysical taxonomy, are the kinds of things that occupy a substrate, that exist *in* something. The Advaitin therefore owes a substrate.

**Inference.** The available candidates are exhaustive on the Advaitin's own ontology: there is Brahman, and there is what is dependent on *avidyā*. *Jīvas* fall in the second class. Disjunctive elimination follows.

*Horn 1 — jīva as substrate.* The *jīva*, for the Advaitin, is not an independently real being but the upādhi-conditioned appearance of Brahman: it is *avidyā*'s product. To make the *jīva* the substrate of *avidyā* is therefore to make *avidyā*'s product the substrate of *avidyā* itself — *anyonyāśraya*, mutual dependence. *Avidyā* presupposes a *jīva* in which to reside; the *jīva* presupposes *avidyā* by which to be individuated. The circle is unbreakable.

*Horn 2 — Brahman as substrate.* Brahman, for the Advaitin, is *prakāśa-svarūpa* (intrinsically self-luminous), *śuddha-caitanya* (pure consciousness). *Avidyā* is by definition the obstruction or concealment of self-luminosity. To say Brahman is the substrate of *avidyā* is to say that pure light is the residence of its own darkness — a flat self-contradiction at the level of intrinsic nature.

**Conclusion.** *Avidyā* has no coherent substrate. The concept fails at its first foothold.

**Explication.** This is the organizing dilemma for the whole anti-Advaita sequence. The Advaitin needs *avidyā* to be real enough to explain bondage and world-appearance, but any such real explanatory item needs an ontological lodging. If it lodges in the *jīva*, circularity follows because the *jīva* is supposed to be *avidyā*'s own product. If it lodges in Brahman, then pure self-luminous consciousness becomes the seat of its own concealment. Softening the Brahman-horn into "adjunct" language does not help, because even an adjunct is a real qualification and so breaks *nirviśeṣatva*.

Appeals to *anādi* also miss the point. Beginninglessness is a temporal claim; *āśraya* is an ontological one. A beginningless real power still needs a substrate. The force of the argument is therefore internal and logical: given *avidyā* as *bhāvarūpa*, the *jīva* as *avidyā*-constructed, and Brahman as *śuddha-caitanya*, no coherent *āśraya* can be named.

## 2. Tirodhāna-anupapatti — the concealment problem

*What does avidyā conceal? How?* Rāmānuja's point here is that if featureless consciousness is said to be concealed, one must still name what exactly is being concealed, and that cannot be done without reintroducing distinguishing content into what was supposed to be without distinction.

Cross-engagement: Baladeva Vidyabhushana presses the same causation model axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `unchanged-ground-with-changing-power` rather than `body-soul-causation` in [primary text](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

(Reconstructed from Raghavachar 1956 commentary on the *Mahāsiddhānta*, pp. 22–28.)

**English.** "If concealment of *nirviśeṣa* consciousness is held to be established (*upapanna*) — what is it that is concealed? Nor can one say 'the concealment itself' [is what is concealed] — for the very negation of distinguishing features is what establishes *nirviśeṣatva*; one cannot then make *avidyā* a further distinguishing feature that conceals."

**Premise.** Concealment is a relation that requires four terms: (i) something concealed, (ii) something concealing, (iii) the concealment-event, (iv) some perspective from which the concealment obtains. This is not idiosyncratic philosophical analysis — it is the implicit grammar of the verb. The cloth conceals the pot from the eye; the cloud conceals the sun from the observer; in each case, all four terms are present and determinate.

**Inference.** For *nirviśeṣa* Brahman, term (i) is empty — there are no features to be concealed. The Advaitin will say: what is concealed is not a feature but Brahman's *self-evident self-identity*. But "self-evident self-identity" is itself a feature — perhaps the most important feature Brahman could have. If Brahman has self-evident self-identity, Brahman has at least one feature; if so, *nirviśeṣatva* breaks. If Brahman has no features at all, "self-evident self-identity" cannot be the concealed item, and we are back to term (i) being empty.

Term (ii) faces a parallel problem. *Avidyā* is held by Advaita not to have ultimate (*pāramārthika*) reality. A *real* concealment by an *unreal* concealer is a contradiction. The Advaitin then retreats to "*vyāvahārika* concealment" — concealment at the empirical level. But the Advaitin's whole point is that the empirical level is itself *avidyā*'s product. The "*vyāvahārika*" register is therefore the register of the consequence, not of the cause; offering it as the locus of the concealment-relation is like explaining the smoke by appealing to the smoke.

Most decisively, *avidyā* itself, qua concealing power, would be a *viśeṣa* ascribed to Brahman. To deny all *viśeṣa* of Brahman and then ascribe to Brahman a concealing power is to take both sides of a contradiction.

**Conclusion.** The concealment-model of *avidyā* cannot be coherently formulated for *nirviśeṣa* Brahman. Either Brahman has features (and the model becomes Rāmānuja's, not the Advaitin's) or it does not (and the verb "conceal" loses its grammar).

**Explication.** Rāmānuja's point is that the Advaitin borrows the intuitive force of ordinary concealment-cases while emptying them of the conditions that make concealment intelligible. In the cloud-and-sun model, the concealed thing, the concealer, and the observer are all real and determinate. In the Brahman-*avidyā* case, Brahman is supposed to have no determinable feature, *avidyā* no full reality, and the observing *jīva* is itself *avidyā*-produced. The grammar of concealment therefore collapses.

This is already continuous with the later *paryudāsa* hermeneutic. If *neti neti* is read as purifying Brahman of defective predicates rather than stripping Brahman of all predicates, the concealment-model loses its scriptural pressure in the first place.

## 3. Svarūpa-anupapatti — the nature of avidyā

*What is the intrinsic nature of avidyā?*

Cross-engagement: Bannanje Govindacharya presses the same individuation status axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `irreducible-dependent` rather than `qualified-mode` in [primary text](cite://madhva/tattva-sankhyana/1-4). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

Rāmānuja's verdict here is that the alleged status of being neither real nor unreal cannot be established either in experience or in scripture.

(Composite formulation drawn from Thibaut SBE 48 pp. 40–44; should be verified against GRETIL.)

**English.** "Indefinability with respect to being and non-being (*sattvāsattvābhyām anirvacanīyatva*) cannot be established — for it is not encountered anywhere [in experience] (*na hi tat kvacid dṛṣṭam*), nor is it scripturally established (*na ca āgama-siddham*)."

**Premise.** Every meaningful property-attribution must be derivable from at least one of the three classical *pramāṇas* — perception (*pratyakṣa*), inference (*anumāna*), or scriptural testimony (*āgama* / *śabda*). This is itself an Indian philosophical principle Rāmānuja shares with the Naiyāyikas and the Mīmāṃsakas, and which the Advaitin formally accepts. A predicate that cannot be derived from any *pramāṇa* is not a predicate; it is a noise.

**Inference.** *Anirvacanīyatva* — being-neither-real-nor-unreal — is not derivable from any *pramāṇa*. It is not perceived: nothing in our experience presents itself as ontologically "neither *sat* nor *asat*"; the Advaitin's standard appeal to dream-objects and rope-snake illusions describes things that we recognize *as* unreal, not as occupying a third category. It is not inferred: there is no *liṅga* (invariable mark) that warrants the inference; the world's appearance does not invariably accompany "neither-real-nor-unreal-ness," because any of the standard ontological categories (real, unreal, mixed-aggregate) could equally explain that appearance with no remainder. It is not scripturally established: the *Upaniṣads* nowhere assert the existence of such a category as the ontological substance of world-appearance.

**Conclusion.** *Anirvacanīyatva* is not a description but a refusal of description. As a refusal it cannot do the explanatory work the Advaitin needs.

**Explication.** Here Rāmānuja attacks *anirvacanīyatva* as a pseudo-predicate. A meaningful ontological description must have some basis in experience, inference, or scripture. "Neither real nor unreal" has none. We do not encounter such a category, cannot infer it from a unique mark, and are not given it by śruti as the positive ontological floor of world-appearance.

The deeper complaint is explanatory. If *avidyā* really explains anything, it must have enough determinacy to count as an explainer. If it has no determinable status at all, "anirvacanīya" functions only as a refusal to describe what is being posited.

## 4. Anirvacanīya-anupapatti — the indeterminable category itself

*Can "neither sat nor asat" be a coherent category?*

Rāmānuja's compressed claim here is that "distinct from both being and non-being" and "false" do not name two genuinely different options, because both collapse into the same problem of non-being.

(Thibaut SBE 48 p. 42n; reconstructed phrasing.)

**English.** "Being-distinct-from-real-and-unreal (*sadasad-vilakṣaṇatva*) and being-false (*mithyātva*) are not mutually distinct (*na parasparaṁ vilakṣaṇam*) — both fall within the asat-account (*ubhaye'py asadvāde'ntarbhūtatvāt*)."

**Premise.** *Sat* and *asat* — being and non-being — are exhaustive at the ontological level. The principle is *dvaidhīkaraṇa-nyāya*, the law of binary exhaustion (the ontological analog of the law of excluded middle). Either an item has being or it does not. There is no third position outside this disjunction; the disjunction is logical, not contingent.

**Inference.** "Neither *sat* nor *asat*" is offered by the Advaitin as a *positive ontological claim* — a claim that *avidyā* occupies a determinate third position. But there is no third position to occupy. The disjunction *sat / asat* is exhaustive *as a disjunction at the ontological level*; to claim a *tertium quid* is to claim a position outside being and non-being, which is the position of pure logical impossibility.

The Advaitin will reply that *sat / asat* are categories within Brahman's empirical (*vyāvahārika*) sphere, and that *avidyā* operates *between* the empirical sphere and *pāramārthika* truth. Rāmānuja's reply: this is a category-shift. The question is not whether the *vyāvahārika / pāramārthika* distinction is intelligible — Rāmānuja can grant a partial version of it — but whether *avidyā itself*, as a posited entity, has being or not. If *avidyā* has any being at all, it is *sat* in the relevant sense. If it has no being, it cannot do the work of producing the appearance of the world. The "between" position is logical fiction.

**Conclusion.** The *anirvacanīya* category collapses. Either *avidyā* is real (and Advaita's non-duality fails — there are now two ontologically real things, Brahman and *avidyā*) or *avidyā* is unreal (and *avidyā* cannot do any work at all). The third option is unavailable.

**Explication.** The previous *anupapatti* said "indefinable" has no usable content; this one says the alleged third ontological slot itself is unavailable. Either *avidyā* is real, in which case non-duality is compromised, or it is unreal, in which case it cannot do any explanatory work. A "between" position helps only if it is a genuine category, and Rāmānuja's claim is that no such category exists.

Even the weaker Advaitin reply, that *anirvacanīyatva* means only the failure of the *sat/asat* distinction, gives away too much. A mere failure of characterization cannot also function as the determinate explainer of bondage and world-appearance.

## 5. Pramāṇa-anupapatti — the epistemological problem

*By what valid means of cognition is avidyā known?*

Rāmānuja asks by what valid means of cognition *avidyā* is known, and answers: not perception, not inference, and not scripture.

(Sanskrit reconstructed from Lipner *Face of Truth* ch. 2.)

**English.** "We ask: what is the *pramāṇa* for *ajñāna* (non-knowledge / *avidyā*)? Not perception — for being formless (*amūrta*), it has no perceptual form. Not inference — for there is no specific mark (*asādhāraṇa-liṅga*) [to ground the inference]. Not scripture — for scripture teaches the qualified character of Brahman (*saviśeṣatva*), not *ajñāna*."

**Premise.** Any meaningful philosophical claim must be established by at least one valid *pramāṇa*. The principle is shared with all six classical darśanas; it is not a Rāmānuja-specific commitment. Different schools admit different *pramāṇas* (Cārvākas one, Vaiśeṣikas two, Sāṃkhyas three, Naiyāyikas four, Mīmāṃsakas six), but every school accepts that an *un-warranted* claim is not a philosophical claim.

**Inference.** Each of the major *pramāṇas* fails for *avidyā*.

*Perception (pratyakṣa).* Perception gives us facts about the empirical world — colors, shapes, sounds, tactile qualities. *Avidyā* is *amūrta* (formless, without sensible quality). It is, by the Advaitin's own description, the metaphysical force *underlying* all empirical cognition rather than an item *within* the empirical world. The Advaitin sometimes says we have a kind of "self-luminous testimony" to *avidyā* — the testimony is bondage itself: we *experience* bondage, and *avidyā* is its precondition. Rāmānuja's reply is that this begs the question. The existence of bondage is what is to be explained; it cannot serve as evidence for the explanation without circularity.

*Inference (anumāna).* Inference operates through *vyāpti* — invariable concomitance between *liṅga* (mark) and *sādhya* (inferendum). What is the *liṅga* for *avidyā*? The appearance of the world as real? But that is precisely what needs explaining — it cannot serve as the evidential mark for the explanation. The Advaitin needs to point to some independently observable feature whose only available explanation is *avidyā*; no such feature has been produced. The standard pattern of inference (smoke → fire) requires that the *liṅga* and the *sādhya* be each independently identifiable; *avidyā* fails this requirement.

*Scripture (āgama / śabda).* The Advaitin's strongest case is here. The *Upaniṣads* contain texts that the Advaitin reads as establishing *avidyā*: the *Māṇḍūkya*'s four states, the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka*'s talk of *ajñāna*, the *Muṇḍaka*'s description of two birds. Rāmānuja's reply is that none of these texts establishes *avidyā* in the technical sense the Advaitin needs (a positive-but-indefinable concealing power). They speak of *ajñāna* (non-knowledge) in the ordinary epistemic sense — the *jīva*'s finite cognition — and the body-soul framework can accommodate that sense without requiring a metaphysical *avidyā*. More positively: scripture, on Rāmānuja's reading, teaches Brahman as *saviśeṣa* — as qualified by *kalyāṇa-guṇas* and embodied as the world of *cit* and *acit*. That is the doctrine *āgama* establishes; *avidyā* is not.

**Conclusion.** No *pramāṇa* establishes *avidyā* in the Advaitin's technical sense. The concept is epistemically groundless.

**Explication.** The deeper problem is self-undermining. If *avidyā* compromises cognition in general, then any *pramāṇa* used to establish *avidyā* is already compromised by the doctrine it is meant to prove. The later appeal to *vrtti-jñāna* tries to solve this by positing a special cognition exempt from the general rule; Rāmānuja takes that as the next sign of instability rather than as a solution.

His constructive alternative is to reframe the problem. Finite embodied cognition does not require a metaphysical illusion-power; it requires a real *jīva* in a real body under a real relation to Brahman. Within that framework the *pramāṇas* remain valid within scope, and the epistemological crisis generated by *avidyā* never arises.

## 6. Nivartaka-anupapatti — what removes avidyā?

*If avidyā is removed by knowledge of Brahman, what knowledge?*

Rāmānuja frames the dilemma this way: if liberating knowledge is contentless, it removes nothing; if it belongs to the sphere conditioned by *avidyā*, it cannot destroy what produced it.

(Composite from Thibaut SBE 48 and Raghavachar.)

**English.** "How is it said that *avidyā* is removed by Brahman-knowledge? A knowledge of *nirviśeṣa* Brahman, having no object-content (*viṣaya-śūnya*), would remove what? And further: a knowledge that is itself a construction of *avidyā* (*avidyā-kalpitam*) — how would *that* remove *avidyā*?"

**Premise.** Whatever destroys *X* must be determinate enough to *target* *X*. A weapon must have determinate edge to cut a determinate object; a cognition must have determinate content to illuminate a determinate fact. Cognitions without content do not target anything. This is not a peculiar metaphysical principle — it is the operational meaning of "destroy" as applied to cognitive acts.

**Inference.** Two horns.

*Horn 1.* *Brahma-jñāna* is, on the Advaitin's account, knowledge of Brahman as *nirviśeṣa* — without distinguishing features. But knowledge requires content; content is given by the determinate features of what is known. *Nirviśeṣa* knowledge has no content (Brahman has no *viśeṣas* to be known). It cannot, therefore, function as the kind of cognitive act that targets and removes anything. A sword without an edge cannot cut; a knowledge without an object cannot illuminate-and-thereby-remove.

*Horn 2.* Any cognitive act, on the Advaita scheme, is itself part of the *vyāvahārika* sphere — a product of *avidyā*. The cognition-event of "Brahman-knowledge" — knower, known, knowing — is *avidyā*-conditioned in the same way that all empirical cognition is *avidyā*-conditioned. So the cognition that is supposed to destroy *avidyā* is itself *avidyā*'s product. How can the product of X destroy X?

**Conclusion.** *Brahma-jñāna* cannot remove *avidyā*. Either it is contentless (and cannot destroy anything determinate) or it is itself *avidyā*-conditioned (and cannot destroy what produces it).

**Explication.** This is the action-side complement to the substrate problem. Even if *avidyā* existed, what would remove it? If liberating knowledge has no content because Brahman is *nirviśeṣa*, it cannot target anything. If it is an empirical cognition, it is itself *avidyā*-conditioned. The classical *vrtti-jñāna* move solves this only by special pleading: one cognition is assigned exactly the odd properties the system needs.

The formal sequence should be kept visible. Premise: remover and removed must stand in a determinate cognitive relation, otherwise "removal" is empty metaphor. Inference, horn one: contentless knowledge cannot terminate anything determinate. Inference, horn two: an *avidyā*-conditioned cognition cannot serve as the unconditioned destroyer of *avidyā*. Conclusion: the Advaitin's liberating cognition is either empty or compromised, and in either case the explanatory structure breaks.

Rāmānuja's own solution needs no such exception. Liberating knowledge is determinate knowledge of Brahman as *saviśeṣa* and of the *jīva* as Brahman's real mode. It works by illumination rather than by a self-consuming metaphysical event.

---

## 7. Nivṛtti-anupapatti — what is the cessation of avidyā?

*And if avidyā is removed, what is its cessation, and where does it occur?*

Rāmānuja's final dilemma is simple: if cessation occurs in the *jīva*, the *jīva* is destroyed; if it occurs in Brahman, liberation adds nothing to Brahman.

(Reconstructed from Thibaut SBE 48 pp. 48–52.)

**English.** "The locus of cessation would be either the *jīva* or Brahman. If the *jīva* — then since the *jīva*'s very nature is a construction of *avidyā* (*avidyā-kalpita-svabhāva*), the cessation of that nature is just the destruction of the *jīva* (*jīvasyaiva nāśaḥ*), not liberation. If Brahman — then since Brahman never lacked anything (*tad anāptasya*), what does the 'attainment' of liberation by Brahman even mean (*prāptir eva ko'rthaḥ*)?"

**Premise.** *Nivṛtti* — cessation — is a real event. Real events occur in real substrates. Cessation-events specifically presuppose two terms: (a) the entity that ceases, and (b) the entity in which the cessation occurs (the substrate of the cessation). For the Advaitin's account of *mokṣa* to do work, both must be specified.

**Inference.** Two horns again.

*Horn 1 — jīva as the substrate of cessation.* If the *jīva* is the locus of *avidyā*'s cessation, then with *avidyā*'s removal the *jīva*'s nature also ends — because, on the Advaitin's account, the *jīva*'s individual nature is itself constituted by *avidyā*. The *jīva* is then *destroyed*, not liberated. *Mokṣa* on this reading is not deliverance but evaporation — there is nobody for whom liberation has happened. The standard Advaitin reply, that the *jīva* "merges" into Brahman like a river into the ocean, does not help. Either something survives the merging (some persistent qualifier, in which case the *jīva* was not *fully avidyā*-constructed, and the Advaitin's premises are wrong) or nothing survives (in which case the "merging" is just the cessation of an illusion, not a positive achievement of anything by anyone).

*Horn 2 — Brahman as the substrate of cessation.* If Brahman is the locus of the cessation, then "liberation" amounts to Brahman recovering what it never lost. Brahman never had *avidyā*; *avidyā* never affected Brahman's intrinsic nature; the "attainment" by Brahman of liberation-from-*avidyā* is therefore vacuous. Brahman always was free; Brahman's "becoming" free adds nothing.

**Conclusion.** Either *mokṣa* is *jīva*-annihilation (which is not what anyone wants from a doctrine of liberation) or it is Brahman recovering nothing (which is a description of nothing happening). The Advaitin has no third option; *mokṣa* on the *avidyā*-framework is incoherent.

**Explication.** The final dilemma asks what liberation even means on the Advaitin's own premises. If the *jīva* is wholly *avidyā*-constructed, then the cessation of *avidyā* is simply the cessation of the *jīva*; that is annihilation, not liberation. If Brahman is the locus of cessation, then nothing is genuinely attained, because Brahman never lacked freedom in the first place.

Rāmānuja's positive answer is that *mokṣa* requires a real persisting subject. The *jīva* remains and comes to know itself truly as Brahman's inseparable mode. Liberation is perfected relation, not evaporation. Taken together, the seven *anupapattis* push toward exactly that conclusion: real *jīvas*, real Brahman, and a real mode-substance relation between them.

## Summary table

| # | Anupapatti | Target | Logical move |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Āśraya | Where does *avidyā* reside? | Dilemma: neither *jīva* (circular) nor Brahman (contradictory) |
| 2 | Tirodhāna | What does *avidyā* conceal? | *Nirviśeṣa* Brahman has nothing to conceal |
| 3 | Svarūpa | What is *avidyā*'s nature? | *Anirvacanīya* = refusal-to-describe = empty concept |
| 4 | Anirvacanīya | Can "neither *sat* nor *asat*" be coherent? | Violates excluded middle; collapses into *asat* |
| 5 | Pramāṇa | By what *pramāṇa* is *avidyā* known? | No valid *pramāṇa* applies; self-undermining |
| 6 | Nivartaka | What removes *avidyā*? | Either contentless or itself *avidyā*-conditioned |
| 7 | Nivṛtti | What is *avidyā*'s cessation? | Either *jīva*-annihilation or vacuous |

The cumulative conclusion: the Advaita doctrine of *nirviśeṣa* Brahman concealed by *anirvacanīya avidyā* is internally inconsistent at every articulation point. The alternative — *saviśeṣa* Brahman, real *jīvas*, real world, real liberation — is logically required by the failure of the seven *anupapattis*, not merely theologically preferred.

The seven arguments also form a deliberate sequence. *Āśraya* asks where *avidyā* could reside. *Tirodhāna* asks what, in a featureless Brahman, there is to conceal. *Svarūpa* asks what the alleged entity even is. *Anirvacanīya* tests the category in which it is supposed to fall. *Pramāṇa* asks how it is known. *Nivartaka* asks what could remove it. *Nivṛtti* asks what its cessation could possibly mean. The order is systematic: substrate, function, nature, category, means of knowledge, means of removal, nature of cessation. By the end, no explanatory role remains available to the Advaitin's technical *avidyā*.

Set out once more in the user's preferred form:

**Āśraya.** Premise: every positive entity requires a locus. Inference: *avidyā* can be in neither *jīva* nor Brahman without regress or contradiction. Conclusion: *avidyā* has no possible substrate.

**Tirodhāna.** Premise: concealment presupposes either coverage or non-manifestation of some determinate content. Inference: a *nirviśeṣa* Brahman has no determinacy available to be covered. Conclusion: concealment-talk is empty.

**Svarūpa.** Premise: an explanatory posit must have an intelligible nature. Inference: *avidyā* cannot be rendered either as positive entity, absence, relation, power, or state without collapse. Conclusion: the alleged nature of *avidyā* is incoherent.

**Anirvacanīya.** Premise: what is admitted into ontology must fall under some coherent mode of being. Inference: "neither real nor unreal" fails to secure a third category and merely postpones contradiction. Conclusion: *anirvacanīyatva* is not a stable ontological slot.

**Pramāṇa.** Premise: if *avidyā* is to explain experience, it must itself be knowable somehow. Inference: no *pramāṇa* can present it without circularity or self-subversion. Conclusion: the explanatory posit cannot be epistemically licensed.

**Nivartaka.** Premise: whatever is removed must be removed by something intelligibly related to it. Inference: Brahman-knowledge either lacks contact with *avidyā* or is itself framed by what it is supposed to remove. Conclusion: the remover-doctrine fails.

**Nivṛtti.** Premise: cessation must occur in and to something. Inference: cessation in the *jīva* yields annihilation; cessation in Brahman yields vacuity. Conclusion: the liberation-story becomes unintelligible on its own terms.

This recap matters because Rāmānuja is often caricatured as merely multiplying scholastic objections. He is doing something much tighter. He is testing one explanatory posit across all the places where a posit has to work if it is going to carry a whole ontology: ontological location, function, nature, category, epistemic access, removal, and soteriological payoff.

# Part II — Aprthak-siddhi and the Body-Soul Framework

The seven *anupapattis* clear ground. The positive doctrine occupies it. Brahman is *saviśeṣa*, qualified by real modes; the world of conscious selves and unconscious matter is the body of Brahman; the technical relation is *aprthak-siddha-viśeṣaṇa-viśeṣya-bhāva* — the relation of inseparably-established qualifier to qualified. This Part works through the relation in [*Brahma-Sūtra* 2.1.14](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/2.1.14) as Rāmānuja reads it, then unfolds the body-soul (*śarīra-śarīrī*) framework via *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* §§103–112.

## Brahma-Sūtra 2.1.14 and *tad-ananyatva*

The sūtra reads, in English: the effect is non-other than the cause, as shown by the "basis" passage and similar texts.

**English (Thibaut SBE 48):** "The non-difference (*ananyatva*) of [effect from] cause [is established] from the word 'basis' (*ārambhaṇa*) and the like."

Rāmānuja's key move is that *ananyatva* cannot mean flat identity. If the pot were simply identical with the clay, there would be no need to say so. The point is rather that the effect is not separately established from its cause. That yields *aprthak-siddhi*: real distinction as mode, but not independent existence.

The load-bearing statement comes at *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* §76, where Rāmānuja defines the self-body relation as support-and-supported, controller-and-controlled, principal-and-subordinate, and concludes that what stands in that relation is an inseparable mode and therefore a body.

**English.** "This very relation of self and body is one in which separate establishment is impossible: support and supported, controller and controlled, principal and subordinate. Accordingly, what exists wholly in that way as an inseparably established mode (*apṛthak-siddhaṁ prakāra-bhūtam*), an aspect or configuration (*ākāra*), is also called *śarīra* (body)."

Three points matter. First, a *prakāra* cannot exist apart from its *prakārin*. Second, inseparability is not identity: the mode adds a real determination to the substance. Third, the relation is asymmetric: Brahman is ontologically prior, while *cit* and *acit* are real but dependent modes.

The formula is worth unpacking term by term because the syntactic compression carries the whole ontology. *Pṛthak-siddhy-anarha* excludes separate establishment. *Ādhārādheya-bhāva* marks support-and-supported dependence. *Niyantṛ-niyāmya-bhāva* adds governance rather than bare inherence. *Śeṣa-śeṣi-bhāva* adds teleological subordination: the body is for the self as principal. And the culmination, *apṛthak-siddhaṁ prakāra-bhūtam*, gives the formal conclusion: what stands in that relation is a real mode, not an illusion and not an externally attached property.

Put more schematically: premise, what is wholly supported, controlled, and subordinated to another cannot count as separately established from it; inference, *cit* and *acit* are described by śruti precisely in those terms with respect to Brahman; conclusion, *cit* and *acit* are Brahman's body as inseparable modes. That is why *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* §76 is not a devotional metaphor but the load-bearing ontological definition.

This is why Rāmānuja prefers *pariṇāma* to *vivarta*. The world is a real transformation of Brahman in its mode-aspect, not an illusory appearance. The body changes; the embodied Self is not thereby compromised. The same asymmetry also grounds the *śarīra-śarīrī* doctrine and the *antaryāmin* reading of *Bṛhadāraṇyaka* 3.7: Brahman is the inner Self of all, while *jīvas* and world are real bodies or modes of that one Self.

The agency objection is handled by the same asymmetry. If *jīvas* are Brahman's body, their being depends on Brahman, but their derivative agency is still real. *Aprthak-siddhi* denies independence, not agency. That makes the doctrine the structural alternative both to *vivarta* (which erases the modes) and to substance-pluralism (which makes the modes independent substances).

This is also where the *Śrī-Bhāṣya* matters most. The great commentary on the *Brahma-Sūtras* is not just a polemical reply to Advaita but Rāmānuja's full-scale demonstration that the Upaniṣads, the sūtras, and the *Gītā* converge on one ontology: Brahman with real modes, real plurality, real dependence, and real teleological order. The present reader leans on *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* for compression, but the doctrinal architecture is the *Śrī-Bhāṣya*'s architecture throughout.

# Part III — *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha*: Saviśeṣa Brahman and the *Paryudāsa* Hermeneutic

The *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* is Rāmānuja's cleanest free-standing statement of the positive doctrine. For present purposes, three places matter most: the positive statement of *saviśeṣa* Brahman, the *nirguṇa* reinterpretation in §84, and the handling of *neti neti* in §38.

## Opening claim

Rāmānuja's opening claim is that Brahman alone is the cause of the entire world, while the world of conscious and non-conscious beings persists as Brahman's inseparable body.

(*Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* §§1–3, after Raghavachar; verify against archive.org VS scan.)

**English.** "Brahman alone is the cause of the whole world: Brahman qualified by conscious and non-conscious beings (*cetanācetana-viśeṣaṇam*), with the entity called 'the world' abiding in it as body (*jagad-ākhyaṁ śarīram*), under the mode of an inseparably established qualifier (*apṛthak-siddha-viśeṣaṇatayā*)."

This gives the compressed positive thesis: one Brahman, qualified by *cit* and *acit*, sustaining the world as body under the relation of inseparable qualification.

## *Saviśeṣa* against *nirviśeṣa*

Rāmānuja's positive contrast with Advaita is that Brahman is not *nirviśeṣa-jñāna-mātra* but an ocean of intrinsic auspicious qualities whose real modes are conscious beings and the world.

The opening of §9 states the contrast positively: Brahman is an ocean of intrinsic and immeasurable auspicious qualities, really distinct and non-distinct from conscious beings under the mode-substance relation, and the substrate of the world's real transformations.

**English.** "Others, stating the true purport of the declarations of unity (*aikyāvabodha-yāthātmya*), maintain that Brahman alone is the whole: an ocean of intrinsic, unsurpassed, measureless, generous qualities (*svābhāvika-niratiśayāparimita-udāra-guṇa-sāgaraṁ*); by its very nature different from conscious beings of every kind and yet not separate from them; and the substratum in which the various impure transformations beginning with ether take place (*viyad-ādi-nānāvidha-mala-rūpa-pariṇāmāspadam*)."

The force of §9 is threefold: Brahman's qualities are intrinsic and infinite; difference and non-difference are both real under the mode-substance relation; and the world is a real transformation, not a mere appearance.

## The *nirguṇa* passages: the technical problem

The decisive issue is how to read the *nirguṇa* and *neti neti* passages. The Advaitin takes them as eliminating all qualification. Rāmānuja reads them through the *paryudāsa / prasajya-pratiṣedha* distinction.

## *Paryudāsa* and *prasajya-pratiṣedha*

**Paryudāsa** excludes a specific defective alternative while leaving the subject in place. **Prasajya-pratiṣedha** absolutely negates. The whole dispute is whether *nirguṇa* means "free of defective qualities" or "devoid of all qualities whatsoever."

## The hermeneutic move

The Advaitin reads *neti neti* and the *nirguṇa*-passages as *prasajya-pratiṣedha*: total denial of all qualifications. Rāmānuja proposes reading them as *paryudāsa*: negating *specific* qualifications — defective, finitude-implying, materiality-implying ones — while affirming the totality of *kalyāṇa-guṇas*.

The pivot comes at *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* §84: the *nirguṇa* texts negate defective material qualities, not qualification as such; and the non-plurality texts are secured because all things exist as Brahman's body and therefore as modes of one Brahman.

**English.** "The texts that speak of Brahman as *nirguṇa* are fixed in meaning (*vyavasthitāḥ*) as denying the defective qualities that belong to *prakṛti* (*prākṛta-heya-guṇa-niṣedha-paratayā*), not as denying qualification altogether. And the texts that negate plurality are safeguarded (*surakṣitāḥ*) because every conscious and non-conscious thing, being the body of the one Brahman, exists as its mode; hence Brahman alone stands there in every mode (*sarva-prakāraṁ brahmaiva avasthitam*), as the Self of all."

This single sentence does both halves of the work. It construes the *nirguṇa*-passages as *paryudāsa* of *prākṛta-heya-guṇas* (the defective qualities native to *prakṛti* — passibility, finitude, materiality, defect), not as *prasajya-pratiṣedha* of all qualification. And it construes the unity-passages (*neha nānāsti kiṁcana*, etc.) by the body-soul framework: every entity is real *as* a mode of Brahman, so "Brahman alone abides" is true *prakāra-by-prakāra* — not by erasing modes but by recognizing them as modes-of-the-one.

The corresponding treatment of *neti neti* appears at *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* §38: the negation does not deny Brahman's qualities, but denies that Brahman is exhausted by the description just given, after which scripture immediately speaks again and says more.

**English.** "The *sūtra*-author himself says: the passage negates only the 'just-so-much' previously described, and then speaks again and beyond that [*Brahma-Sūtra* 3.2.22]. In the sequel — 'Then its name is the truth of truth; the *prāṇas* are truth, and of these He is the truth' — a multiplicity of qualities (*satya* and the rest) is positively taught. Therefore what is denied is Brahman's being exhausted by that much (*brahmaṇa etāvan-mātratā pratiṣidhyate*), not Brahman's possession of qualities."

The text being negated is itself the prior text. *Neti neti* says: "Brahman is not exhausted by what has just been said" — and then the very next sentence resumes saying more. The negation is *purificatory* (excluding any closure of Brahman within a previously stated determination), not eliminative (excluding all determination as such). This is *paryudāsa* in operation; *neti* excludes a candidate determination, not determination itself.

The argument is simple. Sanskrit grammar allows both readings, but *samanvaya* requires the one that preserves the whole corpus. The *prasajya* reading creates conflict with the *saguṇa* and *bheda* texts; the *paryudāsa* reading does not. So the latter wins.

What gets negated are defective, finite, or materially compromising predicates; what gets affirmed are the *kalyāṇa-guṇas*. So the *nirguṇa* and *saguṇa* passages become complementary rather than hierarchical: the former purify, the latter positively describe. That is the core deliverable of the *paryudāsa* hermeneutic.

# Part IV — *Gītā-Bhāṣya* Selections

Rāmānuja's *Gītā-Bhāṣya* is the first sustained Vaiṣṇava commentary on the *Bhagavad-Gītā*. It predates Madhva's by more than a century and the Gauḍīya commentaries by nearly four centuries. Sampatkumaran's 1969 translation (Madras) is the standard English edition. Rāmānuja reads the entire *Gītā* through the body-soul (*śarīra-śarīrī*) framework laid out in the *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* and the *Śrī-Bhāṣya*; the *Gītā* on his reading is not a different kind of text from the *Brahma-Sūtras* but a poetic-narrative articulation of the same ontology. Four passages here illustrate the method: 7.4–5 (the two *prakṛtis*), 9.4 (*mat-sthāni*), 13.1–2 (*kṣetra-kṣetrajña*), and 18.66 (the *carama-śloka*).

## *Gītā* 7.4–5: *parā* and *aparā prakṛti*

At *Gītā* 7.4–5, Kṛṣṇa distinguishes the eightfold lower nature, matter together with mind, intellect, and ego, from the higher nature, the conscious *jīva* by which the world is sustained.

**English.** "Earth, water, fire, air, ether, mind (*manas*), intellect (*buddhi*), and egoity (*ahaṁkāra*) — this is My lower nature, divided eightfold. But know My other nature, higher than this, O mighty-armed: it has become the living self (*jīva-bhūtā*) by which this world is upheld."

Rāmānuja's commentary on 7.5 makes the key point in prose: the higher nature is categorically unlike the lower, is conscious, is the enjoyer in relation to the enjoyed world, and is still Kṛṣṇa's own nature.

**English.** "This is My lower nature. But know another nature of Mine, different from this — different, that is, from the non-conscious principle that stands as the object enjoyed by consciousness (*acetanāyāś cetana-bhogya-bhūtāyāḥ*). It is different in kind from the unconscious (*visajātīyākārā*), consists as *jīva*, is higher (*parā*), principal by being the enjoyer of the other (*bhoktṛtvena pradhāna-bhūtām*), conscious in form (*cetana-rūpām*), and Mine (*madīyām*). Know it as that by which the whole non-conscious world is borne (*dhāryate*)."

The construction is technical and load-bearing. *Aparā* is *bhogya* — that-which-is-enjoyed. *Parā* is *bhoktṛ* — the enjoyer. The two natures are not equally ranked: *parā* is *pradhāna* by virtue of standing as the enjoyer in relation to *aparā*. Both are unambiguously *madīyā* — Kṛṣṇa's. *Visajātīyākārā* is the decisive word: the *parā* nature is *of a different kind altogether* from the *aparā*. Consciousness is not a refinement of matter; it is categorically other.

The verse-pair is the *Gītā*'s most explicit statement of Rāmānuja's *cit-acit* ontology. Two natures are real (the verse stipulates *me* — "Mine" — twice). Both are Kṛṣṇa's: ontologically subordinate, in the *aprthak-siddhi* relation. *Aparā prakṛti* is the world of *acit* — matter and the eight-fold material apparatus. *Parā prakṛti* is the world of *cit* — conscious *jīvas*, the life-principle by which the world is sustained.

A small but consequential reading. Note that *manas, buddhi, ahaṁkāra* are listed by the verse under *aparā*. In the standard Sāṃkhya and many Advaita classifications, the inner instruments of cognition (*antaḥ-karaṇa*) belong on the *cit*-side of the *cit-acit* divide — they are at least proximate to consciousness. Rāmānuja reads the *Gītā*'s placement of them under *aparā* as decisive: the inner instruments are themselves modifications of *prakṛti* in its material aspect; *cit* in the strict sense is the *jīva* itself, not its mental apparatus. The *jīva* uses the *antaḥ-karaṇa* as a tool; the *jīva* is not the tool. This is an important point against Sāṃkhya-influenced readings of the *Gītā* and against Advaitic readings that subtly identify *cit* with the witness-aspect of mind. *Cit* on Rāmānuja's reading is irreducibly the *jīva*-self, not any structure of cognition.

The three real ontological categories are stated in this verse-pair: Brahman (Kṛṣṇa), *cit* (the *jīvas* as *parā prakṛti*), and *acit* (the eight-fold *aparā prakṛti*). All three real, none collapsible into another, in asymmetric relation. The *jīvas* are Kṛṣṇa's conscious body; matter is Kṛṣṇa's unconscious body; Kṛṣṇa is the *Paramātman* — the Self — of both.

The position's reading. The verse is the *Gītā*'s scriptural ground for *cit* as a distinct ontological category from both Brahman and *acit*. The *jīva* is not a portion of matter (against materialist readings) and not a fragment of Brahman in a way that loses its individuality (against strict Advaitic readings). The *jīva* is *parā* — higher, conscious, sustaining — *and* it is *Kṛṣṇa's*. This dual aspect (real distinct, yet ontologically Kṛṣṇa's) is exactly the *aprthak-siddhi* relation in *Gītā* form.

## *Gītā* 9.4: *mat-sthāni sarva-bhūtāni*

At *Gītā* 9.4, Kṛṣṇa says that the whole world is pervaded by Him, that all beings abide in Him, and yet that He does not depend on them.

**English.** "By Me, in My unmanifest form (*avyakta-mūrtinā*), this whole world is pervaded. All beings are situated in Me (*mat-sthāni sarva-bhūtāni*); but I am not situated in them (*na cāhaṁ teṣv avasthitaḥ*)."

The verse is famous for its asymmetry — "all in Me, I not in them" — and the great commentators each read it in a way characteristic of their school. For Śaṅkara, the asymmetry indicates that "in Me" is itself a *vyāvahārika* mode of speech: there is no real abiding either way at *pāramārthika* truth; Brahman is non-different from beings. For Madhva, the asymmetry indicates real ontological distinction with Brahman as transcendent support. For Rāmānuja, the asymmetry is the precise asymmetry of the body-soul relation, stated cosmologically.

Rāmānuja's gloss on 9.4–5 explains that the world is pervaded by Kṛṣṇa as indwelling controller, all beings abide in Him as dependent realities, but He does not abide in them as one who depends on their support or receives any benefit from them.

**English.** "This entire world, conscious and non-conscious, is pervaded by Me as its inner controller (*antaryāmin*), I whose own form is unmanifest (*aprakāśita-svarūpa*): pervaded, that is, for the sake of supporting and ruling it in the mode of principality (*śeṣitvena*). Therefore 'all beings are in Me' means that all beings stand within Me as the indwelling controller. 'But I am not in them' means that My standing is not dependent on them (*tad-āyatta-sthitiḥ*); they render Me no support. 'I am the bearer of beings, yet not stationed in beings; My Self is what makes beings be' means that My *saṅkalpa* alone is their maker, sustainer, and ruler."

Rāmānuja reads the apparent paradox as the precise asymmetry of the body-soul relation. The body abides in the soul (depends on, is sustained by, has being from); the soul does not abide in the body (is not contained by, is not dependent on, has no being from). The body-soul relation is asymmetric, and that asymmetry is exactly what *Gītā* 9.4 expresses cosmologically. *Antaryāmin* — inner controller — is the relevant ontological role: Brahman as the indwelling Self of all things, the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka*'s *antaryāmin* doctrine (3.7) given Gītā-expression. Brahman is *in* all things as their inner controller; all things are *in* Brahman as their support; but Brahman is not contained by what it sustains. The asymmetry is not paradox but ontology.

The phrase *mat-sthitau tair na kaścid upakāraḥ* deserves separate emphasis. It states not just superiority but non-reciprocal dependence. Beings truly abide in Brahman, but Brahman's standing is not aided, completed, or supported by them in any respect. The relation is therefore intimate without being mutually constitutive. Cosmologically, that is exactly the body-soul structure: my body contributes nothing to my existence as self, though its every state depends on me as embodied subject. Rāmānuja scales that logic upward without weakening it.

This is also why the verse resists weak panentheistic readings. "All beings are in Me" does not mean that God is a larger container holding otherwise independent entities. The entities are not independent first and included second. Their being as ordered, sustained, and governable beings is already the effect of *antaryāmitva*. The negative clause, *na cāhaṁ teṣv avasthitaḥ*, blocks any reading on which God needs the world in order to be what God is.

This is exactly why the verse remains so useful for later Vaiṣṇava theology. If beings were simply identical with Brahman, the asymmetry would dissolve into empty speech. If they were merely external to Brahman, the indwelling *antaryāmin* relation would disappear. Rāmānuja preserves both intimacy and hierarchy at once: the world is interior to Brahman without ever becoming Brahman's equal.

The consequence about *bhoktṛtva* (enjoyership) is not casual. On Rāmānuja's reading, the *jīva* enjoys its experiences in a derivative sense; the ultimate enjoyership belongs to Brahman as the inner Self. This grounds the Vaiṣṇava insistence that all action is *kṛṣṇārpaṇa* — offered to Kṛṣṇa — because Kṛṣṇa is the actual enjoyer of every experience that runs through the *jīva*-as-mode. The *jīva* enjoys *for Brahman*; Brahman enjoys *through* the *jīva*. The doctrine extends the body-soul relation into the phenomenology of experience: just as my body's pleasures and pains are mine in the sense that I-as-Self am the experiencer, so the world's experiences are Brahman's in the sense that Brahman is the inner Self of every experiencer.

A point about *bhoktṛtva* worth marking. The *jīva*-as-mode does have its own real (derivative) experiential agency: it really enjoys, really suffers, really chooses. What Rāmānuja's reading does is locate that derivative *bhoktṛtva* within an asymmetric Self-relation in which the supreme *bhoktṛ* is Brahman as inner Self. *Mat-sthitau tair na kaścid upakāraḥ* is decisive: Brahman gains nothing from creation, is supported by nothing, but creation as a whole is the ongoing *saṅkalpa* of the supreme Self. The cosmology is not panentheistic in any weak sense; it is precisely the body-soul relation given cosmological scale.

This further clarifies why 9.4–5 is one of the best short proofs of the whole Viśiṣṭādvaita structure. Premise: beings are in Brahman and depend on Brahman. Counter-premise rejected: Brahman is not in them in the sense of depending on them. Conclusion: the relation is neither strict identity nor sheer externality, but asymmetric interiority. Rāmānuja's commentary makes that conclusion explicit through *antaryāmin*, *śeṣitva*, and the denial of any *upakāra* rendered by beings to Brahman.

In that compressed form, the verse becomes a miniature of the whole system in practice, doctrine, and devotional metaphysics, all at once, unmistakably.

## *Gītā* 13.1–2: *kṣetra-kṣetrajña*

At *Gītā* 13.1–2, the body is called the field, the individual self the knower of its field, and Kṛṣṇa the knower in every field.

**English.** "This body, O son of Kuntī, is called the field (*kṣetra*); the one who knows it is called the knower of the field (*kṣetrajña*) by those who know. And know Me also as the Knower of the field in all fields (*sarva-kṣetreṣu*), O Bhārata. The knowledge of field and knower-of-field — that, in My view, is true knowledge."

Rāmānuja's commentary on 13.2 turns on the inclusive *api*: Kṛṣṇa is also a knower of the field, in addition to the individual knower, and the shared predication is explained through mode-substance co-reference rather than absolute identity.

**English.** "'Know Me too as the knower of the field in every field — among gods, humans, and the rest' means: know every knower of the field as having Me for its self (*mad-ātmakam*). The particle *api* in *kṣetrajñaṁ cāpi* implies that the field too is to be known in relation to Me. Just as the field, because its whole nature is to qualify the knower of the field (*kṣetrajña-viśeṣaṇatā-eka-svabhāvatayā*) and because it is inseparably established from that knower (*tad-apṛthak-siddheḥ*), is denoted only in co-reference (*sāmānādhikaraṇya*) with the knower, so too both field and knower, because their whole nature is to qualify Me (*mad-viśeṣaṇatā-eka-svabhāvatayā*) and because they are inseparably established from Me (*mad-apṛthak-siddheḥ*), are denoted only in co-reference with Me."

The argument is precisely the *sāmānādhikaraṇya*-logic of the *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* applied to the *Gītā*. *Sāmānādhikaraṇya* — the grammatical relation by which two terms with different *pravṛtti-nimittas* (occasioning grounds) refer to a single *artha* (referent) — is what makes *tat tvam asi* and *kṣetrajñaṁ cāpi māṁ viddhi* both possible *and* compatible with real qualifying difference. The two terms refer to one item, but each picks it out under a different qualification. The *jīva*-*kṣetrajña* and the supreme *kṣetrajña* are not numerically identical knowers; they share a referent in the body-soul mode, in which the *jīva* is the qualified-mode-aspect and Brahman is the qualifier-substance-aspect of one ontological structure.

Rāmānuja explicitly answers the Advaitic counter-reading here: if Kṛṣṇa were simply the one self appearing as teacher because of ignorance, then either His ignorance has ceased, in which case no distinction-marking teaching is possible, or it has not ceased, in which case He is not the liberated Lord He claims to be.

**English.** "Some say: from the co-reference in *kṣetrajñaṁ cāpi māṁ viddhi*, [absolute] *identity* is to be understood. Then it must be granted that the Lord, while being just the Lord, becomes-as-it-were a *kṣetrajña* on account of *ajñāna* ... They [these interpreters] should be asked: this teacher, the Lord Vāsudeva, the supreme Īśvara — has his *ajñāna* ceased through direct realization of the Self's true nature, or has it not? *If it has ceased*, then in a Self whose sole nature is undifferentiated consciousness (*nirviśeṣa-cin-mātraika-svarūpe ātmani*), the superimposition of any other-form (*anya-tad-rūpādhyāsa*) is not possible, and so neither the perception of distinctions ('Kaunteya' and so on) nor any teaching-activity directed at them is possible."

The objection — devastating — is that any Advaitic reading of 13.2 must explain how the speaker (Kṛṣṇa) can be at once *the* Self and engaged in distinction-marking pedagogy. If Kṛṣṇa's *ajñāna* has ceased, he cannot perceive Arjuna as Arjuna; if it has not, he is not the supreme Self he claims to be. The *sāmānādhikaraṇya*-by-mode-relation is the only construal that lets *kṣetrajñaṁ cāpi māṁ viddhi* mean what it grammatically says without making the *Gītā*'s pedagogical situation incoherent.

The verses establish a three-level structure. First, there is *kṣetra*, the field or body, the side of *acit*. Second, there is the individual *jīva* as *kṣetrajña*, the knower of its own field. Third, there is Brahman as the supreme *kṣetrajña*, the indwelling knower in every field.

All three levels real, none collapsed into another. *Jīva* and Brahman are both "knowers of the field," but at different ontological levels: *jīva* knows one field (its own); Brahman knows all fields as their inner Self. This is the *antaryāmin* doctrine of *Bṛhadāraṇyaka* 3.7 (*ya ātmani tiṣṭhan ātmano'ntaro yam ātmā na veda* — "one who, abiding in the [individual] self, is inner to it, whom the [individual] self does not know") given Gītā-expression. The *jīva* persists, knows, has its own field; Brahman is what makes that knowing possible from inside.

The structural payoff against *advaitic* readings of the *Gītā*. Many Advaita commentators read 13.2 (*kṣetrajñaṁ cāpi māṁ viddhi*) as identifying the *jīva*-knower with Kṛṣṇa: there is only one *kṣetrajña*, and it is Kṛṣṇa. Rāmānuja's reading preserves the *jīva*-Brahman distinction at the very moment the verse threatens to collapse it. The *cāpi* — "also" — is decisive. Kṛṣṇa is *also* a *kṣetrajña* (in addition to the *jīva* who knows his own field), but Kṛṣṇa is the *kṣetrajña* of *all* fields, in the *antaryāmin* mode. Two *kṣetrajñas* per field: the *jīva* knowing its own, Brahman knowing it from within and from the same perspective in every other field as well.

The verse, on Rāmānuja's reading, is the *Gītā*'s most explicit ontological claim about *cit*: there are conscious knowers at two levels (individual and supreme), and the supreme is the inner Self of every individual *without* dissolving the individual. The *cit*-architecture is irreducibly one-and-many: one *kṣetrajña* under the relation of *antaryāmin*, plural *kṣetrajñas* under the relation of *jīva-as-mode*, and the structure obtains *because* the relation between them is *aprthak-siddhi*-asymmetric, not because *bheda* is illusion.

Read with 7.5 and 9.4, 13.2 completes an exact doctrinal sequence. First, conscious nature is distinguished from unconscious nature while both remain Kṛṣṇa's. Second, all beings are located in asymmetrical dependence on Kṛṣṇa. Third, the finite knower is distinguished from the universal indwelling knower without collapse. The positive side of the framework is therefore not a loose devotional atmosphere but a tightly staged ontology.

## *Gītā* 18.66: *sarva-dharmān parityajya*

At *Gītā* 18.66, Kṛṣṇa commands Arjuna to take refuge in Him alone and promises release from all sin.

**English.** "Relinquishing all *dharmas*, come to Me alone for refuge. I shall free you from all sins; do not grieve."

The *carama-śloka* — the final, definitive verse of the *Gītā* in the Śrī-Vaiṣṇava reading. The verse is treated as the soteriological core of the entire text; everything before it leads up to it.

Rāmānuja's gloss here is the locus classicus of the Śrī-Vaiṣṇava doctrine of *prapatti*. In substance, he says that one continues to perform one's obligations as worship, but abandons the claim that those practices, or the countless expiatory rites that would otherwise be required, can secure liberation by themselves. The true means is surrender to Kṛṣṇa as both goal and means.

**English.** "Practice all the *dharmas* that are means to the highest end — *karma-yoga*, *jñāna-yoga*, *bhakti-yoga* — as worship of Me, with surpassing love and according to your competence; but relinquish them in the manner already stated, namely by giving up the claims of agency, ownership, and fruits, and fix yourself on Me alone as doer, object of worship, goal, and means (*upāya*). 'I shall free you from all sins' means: I shall free one so situated from the beginningless sins that obstruct attainment of Me, whether by forbidden action or failure in what is enjoined. so 'abandoning all *dharmas*' means abandoning those innumerable, time-consuming expiatory rites that would otherwise be required and taking refuge in Me alone, the supremely compassionate one, the universal refuge, the ocean of tenderness (*vātsalya-jaladhi*) toward the surrendered."

Two things must be marked.

*First*, what is and is not abandoned. *Sarva-dharmān parityajya* is *not* the abandonment of dharmic action. Rāmānuja insists, drawing on his own commentary at *Gītā* 18.6 ("*tyāgo hi puruṣa-vyāghra trividhaḥ saṁprakīrtitaḥ*..."), that the *parityāga* in question is *phala-karma-kartṛtva-tyāga* — abandonment of *agency*-claim, *fruits*-claim, *ownership*-of-action — not abandonment of action itself. The *karma-yogin* continues to perform the works enjoined by his *adhikāra*; what he gives up is the construal of those works as *his* path to liberation. Specifically: the multitude of *prāyaścitta*-rituals that would otherwise have to be performed to remove obstructing sins are what is abandoned. The *bhakti-yogin* substitutes *prapatti* for the impossible-in-finite-time accumulation of expiatory rites.

This is the locus classicus of *prapatti* — total surrender — in the Śrī-Vaiṣṇava tradition. The crucial point in Rāmānuja's reading is not surrender to an abstract absolute, but to a personal, qualified, gracious Lord (*Bhagavān*) whose relation to the surrendered self is *exactly* the body-soul relation. The *jīva* surrendering recognizes that it is *already* Kṛṣṇa's body, *already* in the inseparable mode-substance relation. *Prapatti* is the cognitive realization of an ontological fact already in place.

Rāmānuja's reading of *sarva-dharmān parityajya* is technically careful. The phrase is sometimes read as "abandon all dharmas" in an absolute sense — give up all duty, give up all action, simply throw oneself on Kṛṣṇa. This is the *naïve-prapatti* reading, and it is theologically problematic (it can lead to antinomianism). Rāmānuja's reading is more precise: what is to be abandoned is not *dharma* as such, but the use of *jñāna*, *karma*, and *bhakti* as *self-sufficient* means to liberation — the various *samuccaya-pakṣas* (combination-options) by which one might think one could *earn* liberation through the right composite of practices. What is abandoned is the *self-sufficiency* of any such practice. *Bhakti* itself is not abandoned; *bhakti* is reoriented from "means of attainment" to "the relation itself in which surrender takes place." The surrender is to Kṛṣṇa as *upāya* — as the means, as the supreme grace by which liberation comes — not to Kṛṣṇa as *one* of several practices to be combined.

This is the theological grounding of the doctrine that *mokṣa* is finally Kṛṣṇa's gift, not the *jīva*'s achievement. The *jīva* contributes the surrender; Kṛṣṇa contributes the grace; the relation is *upāya-prāpaka* (means-attainer) and *upāya-prāpya* (the means itself being Kṛṣṇa). The structure preserves the *jīva*'s real agency (the surrender is a real act) while denying that the *jīva* could earn liberation by its own action alone (the actual liberation is Kṛṣṇa's gift in response to surrender).

*Mokṣa* on this reading is not dissolution into a featureless absolute but the realization of one's real nature as the inseparable mode of an infinite Brahman — liberation *in relation*, not liberation *from* relation. The *jīva* is freed from karmic bondage but not from the body-soul relation to Brahman; that relation is the *jīva*'s eternal natural state, the *aprthak-siddhi* never collapses.

The doctrinal arc is therefore continuous from ontology to soteriology. 7.5 states the two *prakṛtis*; 9.4 states asymmetrical dependence; 13.2 states differentiated knowing within one ontological field; 18.66 gives the existential form appropriate to a being whose very reality is derivative, sustained, and beloved. Surrender is not appended to the ontology from outside. It is the lived truth of the ontology.

A philosophical observation. *Prapatti* on this construal is not surrender to an external rescuer. The relation it consents to was *always already* in place: the *jīva* is *already* Brahman's *śarīra*, *already* in the *aprthak-siddhi* mode-relation. *Prapatti* is the cognitive ratification of an ontological fact. What it changes is not the relation but the *jīva*'s relation to the relation: the surrender abolishes the misconstrual (still-self-arrogating, still trying to *constitute* what is given) and consents to what is. This distinguishes *prapatti* from any "abandonment to divine providence" model in which the relation must be *established* through the act of surrender. For Rāmānuja the act of surrender is recognition, not constitution.

The Three Gadyas — *Śaraṇāgati-gadya*, *Śrīraṅga-gadya*, *Śrīvaikuṇṭha-gadya* — are the devotional articulation of *prapatti* as it is enacted in Rāmānuja's own life. They are short prose-poems in highly elevated Sanskrit, recited liturgically; the *Śaraṇāgati-gadya* in particular is read at Śrīraṅgam during the festivals of *Vaikuṇṭha-Ekādaśī*. Their philosophical content is exactly what 18.66 establishes: the *jīva* surrendering wholly, not as instrumental means but as constitutive recognition of its eternal status as Kṛṣṇa's *śeṣa* (servant-instrument-mode-beloved). The reader who has worked through the technical doctrine should turn to the Gadyas last; they show what the framework looks like from inside the surrendered subject.

# Part V — Differentiation

The shape of Rāmānuja's position becomes sharpest at the points of disagreement with neighboring schools. Four contrasts: against Śaṅkara (the major Advaitic predecessor), against Bhāskara (the *bhedābheda* near-contemporary), against Madhva (the *Tattva-vāda* successor), and against Caitanya (the later *acintya-bhedābheda* synthesis).

## vs Śaṅkara

Rāmānuja's disagreement with Śaṅkara is foundational, not peripheral. Five issues structure the dispute.

**Status of *bheda*.** For Śaṅkara, *bheda* is *vyāvahārika* — transactional, pragmatic, real for the purposes of empirical life — but unreal at the *pāramārthika* level. For Rāmānuja, *bheda* is real *as qualification of the one Brahman* — modes are real *viśeṣaṇas*; they are not stages of an illusion to be transcended.

**The *nirguṇa* passages.** Śaṅkara reads them as *prasajya-pratiṣedha* — total denial of all qualification. Rāmānuja reads them as *paryudāsa* — exclusion of defects, with affirmation of *kalyāṇa-guṇas* preserved. The dispute over the *paryudāsa* / *prasajya-pratiṣedha* distinction is not minor philological pedantry; it determines the entire ontological reading of the Upaniṣadic corpus.

***Avidyā*.** Śaṅkara takes *avidyā* as the necessary explanatory posit for the appearance of the world-as-real to a Brahman that is in fact undifferentiated. Rāmānuja shows *avidyā* (in this technical sense) to be logically incoherent through the seven *anupapattis*; the explanatory work is taken over by the body-soul framework, which does the same work without the metaphysical *avidyā*.

**Cause-effect.** *Vivartavāda* (Śaṅkara): the world is illusory transformation; Brahman appears-as-if-modified without actually being modified. *Pariṇāmavāda* (Rāmānuja): the world is real modification of Brahman in its mode-aspect; the modification is of the *body* (the *prakāra* side), not of the *Self* (the *prakārin* side, Brahman as Self). Both are real; the asymmetry of the body-soul relation preserves both.

***Mokṣa*.** Śaṅkara: identity-with-featureless-Brahman; cessation of individuality; the *jīva* qua *jīva* is not preserved. Rāmānuja: the *jīva* realizes its mode-nature; persists eternally as Brahman's beloved mode in eternal *bhakti*-relation; *mokṣa* is *liberation in relation*, not liberation from relation.

It is worth marking what Rāmānuja does *not* claim. He does not deny that there is a discipline of negation appropriate to Brahman; he affirms it under the *paryudāsa*-construal. He does not deny the *Upaniṣadic* unity-passages; he affirms them under the body-soul / *sāmānādhikaraṇya*-by-mode-relation construal. What he denies is the inference from the negation-passages to a *featureless absolute*, and from the unity-passages to *strict numerical identity* between *jīva* and Brahman. The seven *anupapattis* show that those inferences cannot be made to work; the body-soul framework shows that the texts can be read consistently without them.

## vs Bhāskara / *bhedābheda*

Bhāskara (c. 9th century, predates Rāmānuja by ~150 years) held *svābhāvika-bhedābheda* — symmetric difference-and-non-difference, both equally fundamental to Brahman's nature, both *svābhāvika* (intrinsic). The world is both identical with and different from Brahman, symmetrically.

Rāmānuja's objection. Symmetric *bhedābheda* fails to specify the *type* of relation, and therefore cannot explain the *dependence* of *jīva* and world on Brahman. If difference and non-difference are equally fundamental, neither has explanatory priority; the world's dependence on Brahman remains ungrounded. Worse, in Bhāskara's specific articulation Brahman becomes a subject of bondage and liberation through *upādhi*-association — a position Rāmānuja takes to be more theologically defective than even *nirviśeṣa*-ism, since it ascribes finitude to Brahman directly.

Rāmānuja's relation is *asymmetric* — the mode depends on the substance; the substance does not depend on the mode in the same way. This asymmetry grounds the *antaryāmin* doctrine (Brahman as inner controller, not just co-equal partner) and the explanatory function of Brahman as cause.

A historical note. The symmetric *bhedābheda* that Rāmānuja rejects in Bhāskara reappears in Caitanya as *acintya-bhedābheda* — with the crucial addition (*acintya*, "inconceivable") that puts the simultaneity beyond conceptual resolution. Where Bhāskara takes both relations as flat-symmetric and conceptually compatible, Caitanya holds the simultaneity as *acintya*. The two later strategies address different problems with the *bhedābheda* family: Rāmānuja's mode-substance asymmetry solves the priority-of-Brahman problem (substance is asymmetrically prior); Caitanya's *acintya* qualifier addresses the simultaneity-of-real-difference-and-real-non-difference at the level the asymmetric framework does not directly handle (the question of how *bheda* between *jīva* and Bhagavān can be ontologically real *as bheda* — not merely as *qualification-bheda* — without compromising unity).

The distinction can be compressed in a comparison grid, since this is one place the earlier pass tightened too much:

| School | What is one? | What is many? | Relation | What happens in liberation? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Śaṅkara | *nirviśeṣa* Brahman | world and *jīva* as *vyāvahārika* | *ananyatva* read toward identity; *avidyā* mediates appearance | individuality canceled in knowledge of featureless Brahman |
| Rāmānuja | *saviśeṣa* Brahman | real *cit* and *acit* modes | *apṛthak-siddhi*, *śarīra-śarīrī*, asymmetrical dependence | enduring *jīva* in perfected relation and service |
| Bhāskara | Brahman with intrinsic *bhedābheda* | world and *jīvas* both same and different | symmetric *bhedābheda* | relation preserved, but dependence-type under-specified |
| Madhva | Viṣṇu as absolutely independent | all else substantially distinct and dependent | *pañcabheda*, hierarchy, *viśeṣa* | eternal distinction and devotion |
| Caitanya | Bhagavān as one with real powers | *jīva* and world as real powers | *acintya-bhedābheda* | eternal devotional relation with non-canceled non-difference |

What this table shows is why Rāmānuja remains so central. He gives the strongest classical account of unity-with-real-articulation before the later Caitanya move to *acintya*. He is therefore the indispensable midpoint between Advaitic abstraction and dualist pluralization.

## vs Madhva / *Tattva-vāda*

Madhva (1238–1317), later than Rāmānuja by more than a century, articulated *Tattva-vāda* — five-fold *pañcabheda*: real and permanent difference between Īśvara/jīva, Īśvara/jaḍa, jīva/jīva, jīva/jaḍa, jaḍa/jaḍa. Madhva's technical instrument is *viśeṣa* — an entity that makes things genuinely distinct without introducing infinite-regressing relational entities (the third-man problem avoided). *Mokṣa* is eternal devotional service in real, never-collapsing distinction from Brahman.

Contrast with Rāmānuja. For Madhva, difference is *fundamental* — *jīva* and Brahman are two real substances in a real relation. For Rāmānuja, difference is real but only *as qualification of one Brahman* — *jīva* is a real mode, real *viśeṣaṇa*, but not a separate substance. The *jīva*'s distinctness for Madhva is the distinctness of one substance from another; for Rāmānuja it is the distinctness of a mode from its substance. Real but not independent.

A note on terminology. *Tattva-vāda* (the school often labeled Dvaita) must not be glossed as crude "dualism": it is not a doctrine of two equally-fundamental substances but a doctrine of differentiated reality with strict ontological hierarchy (Brahman is *svatantra*, *jīva* and *jaḍa* are *paratantra*). That conventional secondary-literature label obscures Madhva's actual ontology. Madhva is closer to a hierarchical pluralism than to substance-dualism in any Cartesian sense.

Rāmānuja → Madhva is not a straight line of error-correction; it is a sharpening with associated trade-offs. Rāmānuja already has the realism (real *jīvas*, real world, real *bheda*). What Madhva adds is *substantial independence* of *jīva* — at the cost of the mode-substance unity Rāmānuja preserved. The differentiation is therefore at the level of what kind of unity is asserted: for Rāmānuja the unity is one substance with real qualifying modes; for Madhva the unity is asymmetric ontological dependence among substantially distinct beings. Both are realisms; they differ on what the unity *is*.

## vs Caitanya / *acintya-bhedābheda*

Caitanya (1486–1534) and his school articulate *acintya-bhedābheda* — the simultaneity of difference and non-difference is *acintya*, inconceivable, pointing to the fact that Bhagavān's *śakti* transcends conceptual categories. The Caitanya school accepts much of Rāmānuja's framework — real *jīvas*, real world, devotional liberation, the body-soul ontology — but adds (a) the *acintya-śakti* as ontological ground of simultaneous *bheda-abheda*, and (b) the irreducibility of *prema* (love), specifically *viraha-bhakti* (love-in-separation), as the highest rasa of liberation.

Where Rāmānuja settles the apparent paradox by mode-substance asymmetry, the Caitanya school deliberately preserves the paradox as *acintya* — not as a confession of ignorance, but as the technical claim that *bheda* and *abheda* obtain *simultaneously and irreducibly* at a level beyond conceptual resolution.

The structural difference can be stated precisely. For Rāmānuja, *bheda* is real *as qualification* — the difference of mode from substance, which is *aprthak-siddha-bheda*, real-but-inseparable. For Caitanya, *bheda* and *abheda* are *both* irreducibly real, simultaneously, in a way that exceeds the mode-substance framework. This makes available a stronger account of *prema*: love between *jīva* and Bhagavān requires, on the Caitanya analysis, an ontologically real *other* — not merely a mode-of-the-same. On Rāmānuja's framework, *prema* is the love of substance and inseparable mode (and is so articulated in the *Gadyas*); the Caitanya school takes the further step of holding the *bheda*-side of the relation to be co-equal with the *abheda*-side, so that love-across-distance (*viraha-bhakti*) becomes a real ontological possibility, not just an affective phase.

A second contrast: liberation. For Rāmānuja, *mokṣa* is the *jīva*'s realization of its real mode-status, eternally in service-relation. For Caitanya, *mokṣa* is *prema-bhakti* in its highest *rasa*, including *viraha-bhakti* — love-in-separation as the *rasa* in which *prema* is most intense. The Caitanya school's preservation of *viraha* is structurally significant: it requires sufficient ontological distinctness between *jīva* and Bhagavān that separation is real. Rāmānuja's framework can accommodate static service-relation but does not require *viraha* in the same way; the modes are always already inseparable.

The disagreement is real, internal to Vaiṣṇavism, and turns on the precise question of whether *bheda* admits of a register stronger than *aprthak-siddha-bheda*. Rāmānuja's answer is that it does not: every real distinction between *jīva* and Brahman is mode-substance distinction, and the asymmetric inseparability is constitutive. Caitanya's answer is that the question is *acintya* and that *both* are constitutive at once. Each answer pays a price, and each delivers what the other cannot.

That is also why the user's thesis can extend Rāmānuja without bypassing him. The extension to Caitanya or Aurobindo is only intelligible after one has learned from Rāmānuja how unity can remain articulated without collapsing into illusionism. If that lesson is skipped, later *bhedābheda* positions are too easily misread as vague compromise. Rāmānuja supplies the exact ontological grammar that makes those later moves worth taking seriously.

# Part VI — Philosophical Assessment

## What Rāmānuja's framework establishes

Three doctrinal achievements warrant the standing the framework has within Vedānta.

**First, the *saptavidhānupapatti* is a rigorous internal critique of Advaita that operates within Advaita's own *pramāṇa*-framework.** It does not import Vaiṣṇava theology and demand the Advaitin honor it; it grants the Advaitin's premises (*avidyā* as *bhāvarūpa*; Brahman as *śuddha-caitanya*; *jīva* as *avidyā*-construct; *anirvacanīyatva* as a category; *brahma-jñāna* as *avidyā*-remover) and forces contradictions out of them. The cumulative result is that *nirviśeṣa* Brahman concealed by *anirvacanīya avidyā* cannot be the complete description of the Real. Madhva's *Anuvyākhyāna* sharpens the polemical tone but does not improve the structural arguments. Jīva Gosvāmī's *Sandarbhas* presuppose them. They constitute the bedrock of any post-Śaṅkara realism in Vedānta.

**Second, the *aprthak-siddhi* doctrine articulates how the Real can be intrinsically articulated without being pluralised.** Modes are real (against *vivartavāda*); modes are not separately existent substances (against substance-pluralism); modes really qualify the substance (the relation is *viśeṣaṇa-viśeṣya*, not external linkage). The asymmetry is irreducible: the substance is prior, the modes are dependent, but the dependence is the dependence of *genuine* articulation on what it articulates, not the dependence of illusion on substrate. The technical formula *pṛthak-siddhy-anarhādhārādheya-bhāva* (*VS_76*) is the precise instrument. *Tat-ananyatva* in [*Brahma-Sūtra* 2.1.14](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/2.1.14) — non-difference of effect from cause — is correctly construed under this relation; the clay-pot example does not require *vivarta* to be intelligible.

**Third, the *paryudāsa* hermeneutic dissolves the apparent conflict between *nirguṇa* and *saguṇa* texts at the level of construal.** *Nirguṇa* texts negate *prākṛta-heya-guṇas* (defective qualities of *prakṛti*) — they do not negate qualification as such. *Bheda* texts state real qualifications; *abheda* texts state real inseparability of qualifications from substance. *Sāmānādhikaraṇya* — co-reference under different *pravṛtti-nimittas* — is the grammatical instrument that lets *tat tvam asi* and *kṣetrajñaṁ cāpi māṁ viddhi* be read as identifying a single referent under two different qualifying aspects (mode-aspect and substance-aspect of one structure), neither collapsing into Advaitic identity nor decomposing into Madhva-style numerical distinctness. The hermeneutic is internal to the *Vedānta* and does not require importing extraneous principles.

That third achievement is philosophical, not merely philological. *Paryudāsa* lets Rāmānuja say that negative language about Brahman removes the wrong sort of predicates without emptying Brahman of all determinacy. The *nirguṇa* statements protect transcendence by excluding limiting and defective qualities; the *saguṇa* statements protect plenitude by affirming infinite auspicious qualities. Once that is seen, the old opposition between "negative theology" and "qualified theism" looks far less inevitable than modern summaries make it seem.

This achievement is more than an exegetical convenience. *Paryudāsa* gives Rāmānuja a principled way to read negation without turning negation into metaphysical emptiness. Brahman is "without qualities" only in the sense of being without limiting or defective qualities. The negative language removes the wrong predications so that the right plenitude can stand forth. Without this move, either the *nirguṇa* texts swallow the *saguṇa* texts or vice versa. With it, both corpora are preserved at full force.

## Structural commitments and limits

Three structural features of the framework that any constructive engagement must reckon with.

First, the framework is irreducibly asymmetric. Brahman is not one term in a balanced relation with *jīva* and world; Brahman is the sustaining Self, while *cit* and *acit* are dependent modes. Second, articulation is real all the way down. Difference is never treated as mere pragmatic convenience, even where inseparability is asserted. Third, liberation does not abolish ontological grammar; it perfects it. What is most striking in Rāmānuja is how little of the ontology has to be discarded at the soteriological summit.

Those three features explain why the framework has remained so durable. It answers Śaṅkara without collapsing into simple dualism; it answers Bhāskara without leaving dependence vague; it anticipates later devotional systems without yet needing *acintya* as explicit category. The user's thesis may finally move beyond Rāmānuja, but it can do so only after taking full possession of what this exact framework gets right.

*First*, the asymmetry of *aprthak-siddhi* is constitutive, not provisional. Brahman is *prakārin*; the world of *cit* and *acit* is *prakāra*. The substance has no *aprthak-siddhi*-dependence on the modes (it could be in *kāraṇāvasthā* — the unmanifest causal state — without these particular modes being differentiated). This asymmetry is what distinguishes *Viśiṣṭādvaita* from any symmetric *bhedābheda*; it is also what marks the boundary at which other Vaiṣṇava schools (the Caitanya school in particular) press for a stronger account of *bheda* between *jīva* and Bhagavān than *aprthak-siddhi* alone can provide.

*Second*, *bheda* on this account is *qualification-bheda*. The reality of difference is intelligible *as the reality of the modes' qualifying-relation to the substance*. This is enough to ground *prapatti*, real cognitive recognition, eternal *bhakti*-relation, the asymmetric *bhoktṛ*-relation, and the irreducibility of the *jīva*'s consciousness as a center. Whether it is enough to ground *viraha-bhakti* (love-in-separation as ontological structure, not just affective phase) is the question that motivates the Caitanya extension. On Rāmānuja's framework, separation is always already qualified by the inseparability of the relation; *viraha* can be lived as an affective register, but not as an ontological one. This is a feature, not a bug, of strict mode-substance ontology.

*Third*, the cosmology is real and cyclical (*sṛṣṭi-sthiti-pralaya*) but not progressive. Each cosmic cycle is structurally the same; the *jīva* persists across cycles; *mokṣa* is the *jīva*'s realization of its real status, eternal in its perfection but not part of a developmental movement of cosmic time. This is a deliberate feature of Rāmānuja's reading of the *Brahma-Sūtras* and the *Gītā*: the texts do not present cosmic time as evolving toward a higher condition, and Rāmānuja does not impose one on them. Whether one wants more — a progressive cosmic teleology, an evolutionary supramental descent — is a separate philosophical question; Rāmānuja's framework is silent on it, neither asserting nor excluding such a structure.

## Reading sequence

For a reader approaching Rāmānuja systematically, the recommended sequence is:

**1. *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha* first.** The most accessible, free-standing argument; contains the *paryudāsa* hermeneutic and the *aprthak-siddhi* core. Use Raghavachar's Sanskrit + IAST edition or van Buitenen's translation. ~150 sections; readable in a long sitting (perhaps 6–8 hours of careful reading). Pay particular attention to §§7–9 (the three positions), §§17–24 (the *paryudāsa* hermeneutic), and §§103–112 (the technical definition of *śarīra*).

**2. The *Mahāsiddhānta* of *Śrī-Bhāṣya* on 1.1.1 — the *saptavidhānupapatti*.** Use Thibaut SBE 48 as English base; cross-check with Lipner's *Face of Truth* for philosophical analysis. Read this with the Sanskrit beside (GRETIL rambh_1u.htm). The seven *anupapattis* should be read in sequence, then re-read as a system; the cumulative force is greater than the individual arguments. Lipner's chapter 2 (epistemology) and chapters 3–4 (the *aprthak-siddhi* analysis) are the best secondary apparatus.

**3. *Gītā-Bhāṣya* selectively.** The four passages above (7.4–5, 9.4, 13.1–2, 18.66), plus the chapter introductions to *Gītā* 2 (*sthitaprajña*), *Gītā* 4 (*avatāra*-doctrine), *Gītā* 12 (*bhakti*), and *Gītā* 15 (*puruṣottama*). Use Sampatkumaran 1969. Rāmānuja's *Gītā-Bhāṣya* is more accessible than the *Śrī-Bhāṣya* and shows the framework in motion across narrative material.

**4. The rest of *Śrī-Bhāṣya*.** Once the framework is in place, the rest of the *Brahma-Sūtra* commentary works through specific topics (sūtra-by-sūtra). Read 2.1.14 (the *aprthak-siddhi* sūtra) and 1.1.4 (the *samanvaya* sūtra) closely; the rest is application of the framework. Particularly important: 1.1.2 (*janmādy asya yataḥ* — Brahman as cause of the world's origin, persistence, and dissolution), 2.3.41–53 (the *jīva* as *aṁśa*), and the *adhikaraṇas* in adhyāya 4 on the nature of liberation.

**5. The three Gadyas.** *Śaraṇāgati-gadya*, *Śrīraṅga-gadya*, *Śrīvaikuṇṭha-gadya*. Short prose-poems of *prapatti*. They show what the doctrine *is* when lived. Read after the technical material; they will land differently. Available in many Śrī-Vaiṣṇava devotional editions; the M.A. Lakshmithathachar Sanskrit-English edition is one option.

**6. Lipner, *The Face of Truth* (Macmillan, 1986).** The best contemporary philosophical treatment of Rāmānuja in English. Especially the *aprthak-siddhi* analysis (chapters 3–4) and the epistemology (chapter 2). Read it after a first pass through the *Vedārtha-Saṅgraha*; it will sharpen the categories.

**7. Comparative reading: Madhva's *Anuvyākhyāna* and Jīva Gosvāmī's *Sandarbhas* (especially *Tattva-Sandarbha*).** These show how Rāmānuja's logical groundwork is used and extended by his successors. Read in succession, they make visible the sequence of internal corrections by which post-Śaṅkara Vedānta recovers the realism the *Brahma-Sūtra* implies — and the precise points at which each successor takes up or modifies Rāmānuja's instruments.

## Coda

Rāmānuja's achievement, taken at full strength, is a complete and internally consistent Vedānta. The seven *anupapattis* establish, by purely logical means within the Indian *pramāṇa*-framework, that *nirviśeṣa*-Brahman concealed by *anirvacanīya avidyā* cannot be the description of the Real. The *aprthak-siddhi* doctrine articulates the alternative: one Brahman, intrinsically qualified by real modes in asymmetric inseparable relation. The *paryudāsa* hermeneutic dissolves the apparent conflict between *nirguṇa* and *saguṇa* texts at the level of construal, without hierarchical concession to either. The body-soul framework grounds the cosmological articulation of the mode-substance relation. The *Gītā-Bhāṣya* shows the framework at work across the most influential narrative-philosophical text in the tradition. The *carama-śloka* commentary grounds *prapatti* — recognition of an ontological relation already in place — as the appropriate human response to the framework lived from inside.

To read Rāmānuja closely is to acquire the technical instruments by which any subsequent Vedāntic position is to be evaluated: *aprthak-siddhi*, *paryudāsa*, *sāmānādhikaraṇya*-by-mode-relation, *śarīra-śarīrī-bhāva*, *kalyāṇa-guṇa-samudāya*, the seven *anupapattis*, the asymmetric *antaryāmin* doctrine. Whether one extends these instruments in directions Rāmānuja himself did not pursue — toward Caitanya's *acintya* register, toward an evolutionary supramental cosmology, toward any other constructive synthesis — is a question separate from the integrity of the framework as Rāmānuja gives it. The framework stands on its own. Anything built on top of it must reckon with what it gets right.
