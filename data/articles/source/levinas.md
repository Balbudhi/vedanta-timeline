## Reader's Orientation

### Life in one paragraph

Emmanuel Levinas (1906–1995) was born in Kovno (Kaunas), Lithuania, into a Jewish family steeped in the Russian-language Haskalah and the rabbinic learning of the Lithuanian *yeshivot* — he read the Hebrew Bible at home before he read any philosophy, and the rabbinic tradition was for him not a later vocation but his first language of thought. He moved to Strasbourg in 1923; in 1928–29 he attended Husserl's last seminars and Heidegger's lectures in Freiburg. His 1930 doctoral thesis, *La théorie de l'intuition dans la phénoménologie de Husserl*, introduced phenomenology to France. Captured in June 1940, he spent five years as a prisoner in Stalag XI-B; his mother, father, and two brothers were murdered by Lithuanian collaborators and the *Einsatzgruppen* in 1941. He published *De l'existence à l'existant* (1947) and *Le temps et l'autre* (1947) immediately after release; *Totalité et infini* (1961) as his thèse d'État; *Autrement qu'être ou au-delà de l'essence* (1974) as his late masterwork. From 1957 onward he gave Talmudic readings annually before the Colloque des Intellectuels Juifs de Langue Française — these were not occasional pieces but a parallel philosophical career, taught from the Aramaic with the methods of *pilpul* and *aggadah*. He died in Paris on 25 December 1995. **The popular framing of "Levinas the French Continental philosopher" strips half of him; he was, by his own self-description, a reader of Talmud as much as a reader of Husserl, and the two readings illuminate one project, not two.**

### The arc — in Levinas's own systematic order

Levinas's own self-presentation (in interviews, in the prefaces, in the *Note préliminaire* to *AE*) does not begin with the face. It begins with a polemic: **Heidegger gave ontology priority over ethics, and that priority is itself the original violence**. *Sein und Zeit* asks first about the meaning of being and only then locates Dasein among other beings; ethics, in that order, is a regional discipline derivative of fundamental ontology. Levinas's life-work is the reversal: **ethics is first philosophy** — the relation to the Other is not derivable from a prior understanding of being, it is what *opens* the understanding of being in the first place. Every later move (the *visage*, the idea of the infinite, *autrement qu'être*, *substitution*, *illéité*) executes this single reversal in successively deeper registers. To read Levinas as he read himself is to keep the polemical setup against Heidegger always in view.

**Early (1935–1947).** *De l'évasion* (1935), *De l'existence à l'existant* (1947), *Le temps et l'autre* (1947). These are the books that have to come *first* before the face can mean anything. They work out the ***il y a*** — anonymous, impersonal *being*, the murmur that does not stop when everything is removed. *L'il y a* is Levinas's deliberate inversion of Heidegger's *es gibt* (the gracious "there is" of the gift of being): for Heidegger, *es gibt Sein* is the openness within which beings come to presence; for Levinas, *il y a* is the suffocating, insomniac, faceless persistence of being-without-anyone. *Sein und Zeit* offers being as the horizon of possibility; Levinas's *il y a* offers being as oppression. Out of the *il y a* the existent must perform a ***hypostasis*** — a standing-out, an emergence of *someone* from the no-one of impersonal being. Only an existent that has so emerged can then be *addressed*. The early Levinas establishes: anonymous being is *not* salvation; salvation has to come from elsewhere; the Other will be that elsewhere. *Le temps et l'autre* already names the Other (*Autrui*) as the source of *time* itself — the future I cannot anticipate is the time *of the Other*. The vocabulary of *visage* is not yet there, but the architecture is in place: ethics will not be a regional ontology under Being; ethics will be what cracks open the prison of impersonal being.

**Middle (1948–1961).** *Totalité et infini* (1961) declares **l'éthique comme philosophie première**. Western philosophy from the Pre-Socratics through Heidegger has been a philosophy of ***totalité*** — the reduction of the Other (*Autre*) to the Same (*Même*); this reduction is, structurally, *violence*; the breach in the totality is ***infini***, which "comes to me from the Other." Five sections in Levinas's own order: I. *Le Même et l'Autre* (the founding distinctions, the polemic against ontology, *Désir métaphysique*, the idea of the infinite); II. *Intériorité et économie* (the separated I, jouissance, the dwelling); III. *Le visage et l'extériorité* (the heart of the book — the face as expression, the originary command, asymmetry, the third); IV. *Au-delà du visage* (eros, fecundity, paternity); V. *Conclusions* (eschatology of peace, *l'être est extériorité*). The systematic reader works the sections in order: I sets the polemical frame; II shows what kind of I the Other comes *to*; III shows the encounter; IV its futurity; V the conclusion against ontology.

**Late (1962–1995).** *Autrement qu'être ou au-delà de l'essence* (1974) radicalizes *TI* in response to Derrida's *Violence et métaphysique* (1964) — Derrida had argued that *TI* still spoke the language of the metaphysics it sought to overcome. *AE* answers with new vocabulary at a deeper register: ***dire*** vs ***dit***, ***autrement qu'être***, ***au-delà de l'essence***, ***illéité***, ***substitution***, ***otage***, ***persécution***, ***traumatisme***, ***diachronie***. The *Lectures talmudiques* run in parallel through this whole period: *Quatre lectures talmudiques* (1968), *Du sacré au saint* (1977), *L'Au-delà du verset* (1982), *À l'heure des nations* (1988). The final ethical-religious phase: *De Dieu qui vient à l'idée* (1982), *Difficile liberté* (essays 1948–1976, collected in two editions), the late interviews. **God appears only as trace (*illéité*) in the face of the neighbor — never as a thematic presence, never as a partner in the relation, only as the *Il* who has always already passed.**

### The central thread

If one had to compress Levinas to a single sentence: **the relation to the Other is prior to ontology, and any attempt to subsume the Other under a category — under being, under the concept, under the totality of history — is the original violence**. Four corollaries, in Levinas's own order. (1) **Ethics is first philosophy** — against Heidegger, who placed *Seinsfrage* before all ethics. (2) **The Other comes from a *height* (*hauteur*), not from a peer-position.** *Autrui* is the *Très-Haut* — the Most-High — and the destitute, in the same epiphany; the asymmetry is constitutive, not contingent; *the relation is not mutual, not reciprocal, not symmetric*. (English translations sometimes soften *hauteur* into "height" without the verticality, as if it were merely a metaphor of distinction; in Levinas it is a strict structural claim: the Other addresses me from above, commands me, puts me into question, and *I* am the recipient of a command I did not author.) (3) **The face cannot be thematized.** *Le visage* is not a phenomenon among phenomena; it is what *resists* phenomenality. (4) ***Autrement qu'être***. The relation to the Other is *otherwise than being* — outside the categorial machinery of ontology. Each of these is a turn of the same screw: ontology is reversed by ethics; the Other is *above* not *beside*; the face is *expression* not *appearance*; being itself must be exited.

### The user's interest — T0, T11, T9

**T0 (the violence-of-unification critique).** Levinas is the strongest articulation in the Western tradition of why **any totalizing ontology — Hegelian Spirit, Heideggerian Being, classical Advaitic Brahman read as *nirviśeṣa*-only — is structurally violent**. Among modern critiques of unification (Adorno, Derrida, Deleuze, late Heidegger), Levinas's is the most uncompromising because it locates the violence not in a contingent abuse of unification but in the *structural form* of any totalization-claim. **The user's thesis must reckon with Levinas; it cannot wave him away.** (See Part VII for the user's counter-move via *aperspectivity* and *acintya-bhedābheda*.)

**T11 (phenomenology of the individual).** Levinas's *separated I* — the I made unique by jouissance, dwelling, body, election to responsibility — is the closest twentieth-century philosophical articulation of the user's commitment to individual divine realization (*caitya puruṣa*, not absolute *Geist*). **The user's individuality-thesis aligns structurally with Levinas's separated-I.**

**T9 (unity-in-multiplicity).** Levinas insists the Self–Other relation is **asymmetrical**. The user's *acintya-bhedābheda* wants something similar: a unity that does not abolish the Self–Other distinctness, that preserves *bhakti* (loving relation as ultimate, not provisional). The user's question to Levinas — taken up via Banerji's reading of *tat tvam asi* in the Buber-Levinas-Derrida lineage (Monsoon 2022, pp. 82–83) — is whether the I-Thou must remain *only* asymmetrical, or whether at the supramental register the asymmetry is itself a poise of the divine's self-relation.

### Glossary (untranslated French preserved)

- ***visage*** — face. Not the visible face as object, but the *expression* of the Other, what overflows every form. The face *speaks* before being seen.
- ***Autre*** / ***autre*** — Other / other. *L'Autre* as absolutely Other; *l'autre* in general.
- ***autrui*** — the other person, used almost as a proper-name substitute. Concrete: the human Other in front of me.
- ***totalité*** — totality. The horizon within which beings are reduced to their place in a system.
- ***infini*** — infinite. Not a quantity, not the negation of finite, but the *idea of the infinite* in Descartes's Third Meditation: a thought that thinks more than it can think.
- ***dire*** / ***dit*** — saying / said. *Le dit* is propositional content; *le dire* is the act of addressing the other prior to and beyond any content.
- ***autrement qu'être*** / ***au-delà de l'essence*** — otherwise than being / beyond essence.
- ***illéité*** (illeity) — the "He-ness" of the Infinite, the trace of God in the face. The *trace* of a *Il* that has always already passed.
- ***substitution*** — the structure of subjectivity in *AE*: the self as already in-the-place-of-the-other, "the one-for-the-other," prior to all consent.
- ***proximité*** — the structural nearness of the neighbor (*prochain*) — ethical, not spatial.

## Part 0 — The Early Works: *il y a*, *hypostasis*, the Other as time

Before *Totalité et infini*, Levinas had already laid the architecture in two short books written between the camp and his return to academic life. They are essential, not preparatory: *TI*'s arguments do not work without them.

### *De l'existence à l'existant* (1947): the *il y a*

Levinas's signature early move is the phenomenology of the ***il y a*** — "there is." The thought-experiment runs as follows. Imagine the destruction of all things. Imagine, *per impossibile*, the annihilation of every being, every form, every quality. What remains? Not nothing — Levinas's claim is decisive — but a *murmur*, a *bruissement*, an impersonal "there is" that does not stop. The *il y a* is *being-without-anyone*: existence without an existent, presence without anyone present, the bare fact of "there is something rather than nothing" stripped of every *something*.

The polemical target is exact: Heidegger's *es gibt Sein*. For Heidegger, "there is being" is the gracious gift, the opening, the *Lichtung* (clearing) within which beings come to presence and Dasein finds itself thrown into possibilities. For Levinas — who had attended Heidegger's lectures — *es gibt* is suspect: it makes anonymous being feel like grace. **Levinas's *il y a* inverts this. Anonymous being is not the gift but the prison.** The murmur of being-without-anyone is suffocating, insomniac, oppressive — what one experiences in the vigil of insomnia, when one cannot sleep and the bare presence of being keeps one awake without offering any object to be awake *to*.

This is not a curiosity. It is the structural argument that being-as-such cannot save: an ontology that grants ultimate priority to being is granting ultimate priority to the murmur of the *il y a*. Salvation, if there is any, must come from elsewhere — not from being but from what *interrupts* being.

### *Hypostasis*: the existent emerges from the *il y a*

The next move: the existent (*l'existant*) — *someone*, an "I" — must perform a ***hypostasis***, a standing-out from the impersonal murmur. The Greek *hypostasis* (a standing-under, a substantial subsistence) names the event by which the anonymous "there is" condenses into a *someone* who has a name, who occupies a here, who can begin a sentence with *I*. This is not a metaphysical doctrine of substance; it is a phenomenological description of the very event of *being-an-existent* — of crossing from the no-one of *il y a* into the someone of an "I" that can address and be addressed.

But — and this is the early Levinas's discovery — *hypostasis* alone is not yet liberation. The existent who has emerged from the *il y a* is still alone, still chained to itself (*enchaîné à soi*), still encumbered by the very fact of being-itself. The I that has stood out from the murmur now finds itself burdened *with itself*. Solitude is not an accident of the existent; it is the existent's structural condition. The existent must therefore find a way *out of itself*, and this way cannot be furnished by anything within being.

### *Le temps et l'autre* (1947): the Other as the source of time

The second early book names what the way out is: ***Autrui*** — the Other. *Le temps et l'autre* makes the decisive identification: the future is not a modification of the present projected forward (Heidegger's *Vorlaufen*, running-ahead toward the possibilities of one's own being); the future is *of the Other*. **Time itself is the structure of the relation to the Other.** The future is what I cannot anticipate, cannot project, cannot pre-figure — and what I cannot anticipate is precisely what comes from the Other. A solitary existent has no time, only the present extended; only the Other gives time, because only the Other introduces what is *not yet* and *cannot be* mine.

This is the seed of every later move. Asymmetry is already there: the Other is what I cannot incorporate, what gives me what I could not give myself. The structure of subjectivity as exposed-to-what-it-cannot-master is already announced. *Visage* is not yet the term; *autrui* is. But the relation Levinas will spend the rest of his life describing is already in place: solitary existence is unbearable, anonymous being is oppressive, the Other is the only opening, and that opening is *time*.

For the systematic reader, the lesson of the early works is this: **the polemic against Heidegger is not added later; it is the founding gesture**. *Sein und Zeit* gave being priority and made time an existential of Dasein; Levinas, already in 1947, has reversed both. Being is not gracious but suffocating; time is not Dasein's own but the Other's gift. By the time *TI* opens with "*Le 'vrai vie' est absente*," the whole architecture is already standing.

## Part I — *Totalité et Infini* (1961), Section I: "Le Même et l'Autre"

### The Preface: war, totality, eschatology

*Totalité et infini*, p. ix:

> It matters supremely to know whether one is not duped by morality. Lucidity, the mind's openness to the true, begins by glimpsing the permanent possibility of war. The state of war suspends morality and strips eternal obligations of their eternity. Politics opposes morality as philosophy opposes naivete.

The provocation is exact. *We must take seriously the possibility that morality is a delusion*. The realist — the lucid one — sees that war is permanent, that "being reveals itself as war to philosophical thought" (one need not, Levinas says, prove this *par d'obscurs fragments d'Héraclite*). And once being is war, totality is the form of war: the mobilization of beings, "their being-set-in-movement, anchored until then in their identity, a mobilization of absolutes by an objective order from which one cannot withdraw" (TI p. 6). Nothing remains *outside*. The face of being shown in war "is fixed in the concept of *totality* which dominates Western philosophy." Individuals reduce to *bearers of forces* that command them without their knowledge. The unicity of each present *sacrifices itself ceaselessly to a future called upon to release its objective sense*. Only the last act counts.

This is the Hegelian totality as Levinas reads it: each individual finds its truth only in the whole, and the whole completes itself only in the End. **The individual disappears into the meaning the whole assigns it retrospectively.** This is the violence of totality — not the visible violence of the bullet, but the structural violence by which "the unicity of each present is sacrificed to a future."

Against this, Levinas opposes ***eschatology*** — not foretold end of history, but a *relation with being beyond totality*. TI p. 10:

> Eschatology puts thought in relation with being beyond totality or history. It is relation with a surplus always exterior to totality. The idea of the infinite expresses a transcendence that no totality can engulf and that is as primordial as totality itself.

### Descartes's idea of the infinite

The decisive philosophical anchor is Descartes's Third Meditation. TI p. 4:

> In the Third Meditation, Descartes encountered a thought that was not commensurate with its object, an idea that dazzled instead of resting inside intuitive evidence, a thought thinking more than it could fully think.

The *idea of the infinite*: a thought of which the *ideatum* infinitely exceeds the *idea*. Such a thought, says Levinas, is the structure of every authentic ethical relation. **In thinking the Other, I think more than I can think.** The Other is not a noema correlative to my noesis; the Other "overflows" my thought of the Other. This overflow — *débordement* — is, properly, the *production of infinity*: TI p. 12: "*Dans ce débordement, se produit précisément son infinition même.*" The infinite is not contained in any of my representations of it; the infinite *is* this exceeding, this overflowing, this dazzling.

Every other thought (a thought of an object, a concept, a structure) is adequate to its ideatum: I think a triangle, and the triangle in my thought is fully delivered. But the Other — the human Other — is structurally inadequate to my thought of him or her. **What is given of the Other is always less than the Other.** The face is what overflows the form.

### *Cette idée d'infini me vient d'autrui*

The line, repeated across Levinas's corpus, is best stated in Section I. **The face of the Other is the production of infinity in me.** TI p. 23 (Section I.A):

> The idea of the infinite in consciousness is an overflowing of consciousness itself, giving the soul powers of welcome, gift, and hospitality. Moral consciousness is not an experience of values, but access to exterior being, and exterior being par excellence is the Other.

The infinite is the *form of the relation to the Other*; moral consciousness is structural access to the *exterior*; the metaphors (*accueil*, *don*, *mains pleines*, *hospitalité*) are not decorative — they name the only mode in which the relation to the infinite is enacted.

### The polemic against ontology

For Heidegger especially, Levinas's polemic is sharp. TI Section I.B.1:

> The primacy of ontology does not rest on the truism that one must understand the being of a being in order to know it. To affirm the priority of being over beings is already to subordinate the ethical relation with someone to an impersonal relation of knowledge, grasp, and domination. It subjects justice to freedom.

This is the heart of the book's polemic. Heidegger's *Seinsfrage* *seems* to be more fundamental than ethics. But for Levinas, the move from "the Other person is a being" to "first let us understand being and then we can place the Other within that understanding" is *itself* the violence: it has *already* placed the Other under the neuter category of "being," already made the Other a moment of an impersonal totality. Levinas's claim is that **the relation to the Other is not derivable from a prior understanding of being**; it is what *opens* the understanding of being in the first place.

The polemic against Heidegger here is not primarily about Heidegger's 1933 Rektorat. The disagreement is structural. *To make Being prior to ethics is to subject justice to freedom* — to subject the call of the Other to my own self-determining will. The Heideggerian Dasein chooses itself, projects itself, takes up its possibilities. The Levinasian subject is *put-into-question* by the Other before any project. The Other does not appear within my horizon of possibility. The Other *is* the rupture of horizon.

### Desire vs need

Section I.B distinguishes *désir* from *besoin*. Need is consumption: lack, obtain, incorporate; the digestive structure of the Same. Desire — *Désir métaphysique* — is the inverse: the closer it comes the deeper it grows. **Desire deepens by the giving of what I "have."** TI Section I.A.1:

> True life is absent, but we are in the world. Metaphysics is turned toward elsewhere, otherwise, and the other. This alterity is not another myself. Between me and the other there is first a distance irreducible to spatial and logical structures.

Levinas honors Buber but distances himself: the I-Thou for Buber is *symmetrical*; for Levinas the relation is *asymmetrical*. The Desired *creuse* (hollows) rather than *comble* (fills) desire: where Plato makes the Good *plērōma*, Levinas makes the Other a *creuse-ment*. TI Section I.A.1 closes:

> For Desire, this alterity that cannot be contained in the idea still has sense. It is the alterity of the Other and of the Most High. Metaphysical desire opens the dimension of height, no longer the sky but the invisible. To die for the invisible: that is metaphysics.

The phrase *Très-Haut* (Most-High) is decisive: the verticality of the relation to the Other *is* the structure within which the word "God" can have any sense.

### The face as expression, not phenomenon

TI Section I.A.5, p. 43:

> The way the Other presents himself, exceeding the idea of the Other in me, is what Levinas calls face. It does not appear as a theme under my gaze or as a set of qualities forming an image. The face overflows and destroys that image at every moment. It presents itself in itself and expresses itself.

Several points to insist on. (i) The *visage* is not the face seen in a photograph; it is *the manner of presenting* of the Other. (ii) The visage *destroys at each moment* the plastic image — the visage is what is given precisely as not-given, as overflowing every given. (iii) The visage manifests *kath'auto* — Aristotle's phrase for *in itself*, on its own terms, not through anything else; the face presents itself by itself, it does not require my interpretive Sinngebung. (iv) The face *expresses itself*. **Expression is a structurally different mode from manifestation**. To manifest is to be there as object; to express is to address. The face *speaks*:

> The face speaks. The manifestation of the face is already discourse. To undo the form adequate to the Same in order to present oneself as Other is already to signify, and to signify is already to speak.

**The face is language before words**. Before any spoken sentence, the face *addresses*. To attend to the face is already to be in discourse, in *parole*. And this is why the face cannot be reduced to a phenomenon: phenomena are seen by a subject; the face *speaks to* a subject, and in speaking puts the subject in question.

Heidegger's *Wahrheit* is *aletheia* — *unconcealment*, *Entbergung*, *dévoilement*. Truth is the unveiling of being to a Dasein that lets it show itself. Levinas inverts: the absolute experience is not *dévoilement* but ***révélation*** — the *coincidence of the expressed and the one who expresses*. A phenomenon is unveiled by my looking; the face is revealed by speaking *to me*. The structure is irreducibly second-personal. Section I.B:

> Absolute experience is not unveiling but revelation: the coincidence of what is expressed with the one who expresses, the privileged manifestation of the Other, the manifestation of a face beyond form.

Two consequences. First, Levinas's truth-claim is not Heideggerian *aletheia* but *parole* — the speech of the Other as the originary site of truth. Second, the face is not what is *behind* the face; the face is its own behind. There is no noumenon-in-itself behind the appearance; the appearance *is* the appearing-of-the-not-appearing, the speaking-of-the-not-spoken. Levinas's expression *kath'auto* names this: the face is itself, on its own terms, not derived from a deeper something.

### The architectonic of Section I

Section I lays out, in compressed form, the entire program: (1) *Métaphysique et transcendance* — desire vs need, metaphysics as movement toward the absolutely Other, the dimension of height. (2) *Séparation et discours* — the Same is separated (positively, as enjoyment), discourse is the relation across separation. (3) *Séparation et absolu* — the absolute is the Other in its absolute alterity; the relation to the absolute is *non-allergic*. (4) The polemic against ontology as first philosophy. (5) The positive thesis: ethics is first philosophy; the face is the originary scene of meaning; the idea of the infinite is the structure of the ethical relation.

### *Athée*: separation as the condition of the relation to the Infinite

A precise and underappreciated thesis in Section I: the separated I must be *atheistic* in order to be capable of the relation to the Infinite. *TI* p. 29:

> One may call atheism this separation so complete that the separated being maintains itself alone in existence without participating in the Being from which it is separated. One lives outside God, at home with oneself, as ego. The soul, as the accomplishment of separation, is naturally atheist.

The argument is exact and has the air of paradox until one sees the structure. Participation-metaphysics (whether Plotinian, Hegelian, or Vedāntic-Mayavadin in its strict reading) holds that the finite being is a *moment* of the absolute, and the recognition of this moment-status is the truth of the finite. Levinas reverses: if the finite is *only* a moment of the absolute, then the relation between the finite and the absolute is not a relation but an *internal articulation of the absolute itself*. There is no Other for the finite because there is no genuinely separate finite. The *separation* must be ontologically real for the *relation* to be ontologically real. Hence atheism (the felt experience of being-without-God, alone, at home with oneself) is the structural prerequisite of the genuine relation to the Infinite. The atheist is closer to God than the participant.

The user's reading of Madhva and the *bhakti*-traditions converges here. Madhva's *bheda* (real distinction) — and the Vaiṣṇava insistence on the eternal *jīva-īśvara-bheda* even in liberation — is the metaphysical articulation of what Levinas names atheistically as the necessary separation. Without real distinction there is no *bhakti*; without atheism (in Levinas's sense) there is no relation to God. The same structural insight in two registers.

## Part II — *Totalité et Infini*, Section II: "Intériorité et économie"

Section II is the phenomenology of the **Same** as positive — not as imperialism that absorbs the Other but as the prior condition of the Other's address being *to me*. **The Other addresses an I, not a No-One.**

### *Vivre de…* and jouissance

*TI* p. 112:

> We live from good soup, air, light, spectacles, work, ideas, sleep. These are not first objects of representation, nor are they mere tools. We live from them.

The list is deliberate: *bonne soupe*, air, light, *spectacles*, work, ideas, sleep. We do not relate to good soup as we relate to a hammer (Heidegger's *Zeug* — equipment ready-to-hand for a project). We *live from* it. This is the originary structure of life: not Heideggerian *Sorge* (care, projection toward future possibilities), not Husserlian intentionality (consciousness *of* an object), but ***jouissance*** — *enjoyment*, the *sinking-into* of life into its contents.

**(i) Independence through dependence.** Living from soup or air *depends* on the soup or air; but this dependence is itself how the I becomes *independent*. The I is not a Cartesian *res cogitans* prior to the world; the I is constituted as I *through* its absorption in what nourishes it. *Vivre est comme un verbe transitif dont les contenus de la vie sont les compléments directs.*

**(ii) Jouissance is not satisfaction-of-need.** Need is the *taking-of-the-other-into-the-Same*. *Jouissance* is the *being-pleased-with-life* that exceeds any particular need. *Jouissance is the ultimate consciousness of all the contents that fill my life — it embraces them.*

**(iii) The world before utility.** Before utility, before theory, there is *jouissance*. **Utility is a derivation of enjoyment, not its ground.** *Jouissance* is structurally *naïve*: it does not know that it might be challenged. Section II describes this naïveté — and Section III describes how the face of the Other interrupts it.

### *Chez soi* and the dwelling

*Chez soi* — at home with oneself — is not first a building but the structural fact that the I has somewhere to *come back to*. *TI* Section II.E ("La demeure") works through this. The dwelling is the place from which the I *sets out* into the world to work and possess and to which the I *returns*.

**(i) The feminine.** Levinas writes — controversially — that the dwelling is opened by *the feminine*: a "discreet welcome" (*accueil discret*) by which the I finds the world habitable. Not biological woman but a *category* of welcome. (Levinas himself was critical of his early formulation later.) The dwelling is *received* before it is built.

**(ii) Recollection.** The dwelling permits *recueillement* — gathering oneself, withdrawing from the immediate flux of jouissance into the interiority where representation, memory, and labor become possible. Levinas's reply to the Cartesian *cogito*: the *cogito* presupposes a dwelling from which the philosopher can withdraw to think.

**(iii) Property and labor.** From the dwelling, the I *takes hold* of the *element* and *fixes* it into a thing — possession — economy. **The welcome of the Other into my dwelling presupposes that I have a dwelling**. If I am not at home, I cannot welcome. If I do not have, I cannot give. *Hospitality* — Derrida's later theme — requires the prior structure of the *chez soi*.

### The body and separation

The body is not an obstacle; it is *the very mode of separation*. *TI* p. 119: *Le corps est la possession même de soi par laquelle le moi, libéré du monde par le besoin, arrive à surmonter la misère même de cette libération.* The I must first be separated, alone, in jouissance, before the Other can come to it as truly Other. *TI* Section II.A.5:

> The uniqueness of the I translates separation. Solitude and enjoyment are isolation itself. The I is the concrete way in which the rupture of totality is accomplished and the absolutely other becomes present.

This argues against Heidegger's *Mitsein*: if I am always already with-others, the others are *features of my structure*, not absolute Others; the relation has already been totalized. **The I must first be separated, alone, at home with itself, in jouissance, before the Other can come to it as truly Other.** The I is *the way* totality is broken; *as separated*, it is what makes the relation to the absolutely Other possible.

### Representation as derivative

Section II also argues that representation is a *derivative* mode — derivative of the dwelling, of *recueillement*, of the I's having taken time-to-think. This is the polemic against Husserl: before representation, the I *enjoys*; before the I represents, it *lives from*. Husserl's noetic-noematic correlation is real but not first. **The relation to the Other is not a representation.** Husserl had grounded intersubjectivity in *Einfühlung* (empathy) — a kind of representation. Levinas's response: the Other is not represented; the *visage* does not fit into the I's representational frame.

By the end of Section II: the I is *separated*, at home with itself, capable of recollection, representation, labor, possession; has a *body* through which it lives both dependence and independence; has *time* through labor and dwelling. All this is *prior* to the encounter with the Other. When the face arrives, it arrives *to a separated I that has something to give*.

## Part III — *Totalité et Infini*, Section III: "Le visage et l'extériorité"

This is the heart of *TI*.

### The face is not given to vision

*TI* p. 200:

> Is not the face given to vision? In what way does epiphany as face mark a relation different from the one characteristic of all sensible experience?

The face is *neither* sensible-jouissant *nor* objectified. *TI* III.A:

> The face has no added form, but neither is it a formless thing awaiting form. Neither sensible nor intelligible, it is a presence that escapes the schema of form and content.

The face is *between* — or *beyond* — the categories that organize phenomena. This is the technical sense of *épiphanie*: a showing that is not a revealing-of-what-was-hidden but a revealing-of-the-hidden-as-hidden. The face is given precisely as that which exceeds every giving.

### The face refuses possession

*TI* p. 211:

> The face refuses possession and refuses my powers. In expression, the still-graspable sensible turns into total resistance to grasping. The face withdraws into the infinity of resistance to my power, and that withdrawal is precisely the hardness of its resistance.

Note the precise paradox: the face is *defenseless*, fragile, mortal — and *for that very reason* offers an *infinite* resistance. The skin of the face is thin; the throat can be cut. And yet what the face *says* — its address — *cannot be taken*. The expression cannot be possessed even when the body can be killed. This sets up the argument about murder: the face's resistance is not physical but *ethical*.

### *Tu ne tueras point* / *Tu ne commettras pas de meurtre* — the originary command

The line appears in TI as *« tu ne commettras pas de meurtre »* (Septante/Vulgate); Levinas more often paraphrases by the standard French rendering of the Decalogue: *« tu ne tueras point »*. Both forms refer to the same originary content. *TI* p. 217:

> This infinite, stronger than murder, already resists us in its face, is its face, is the original expression, is the first word: thou shalt not kill. The infinite paralyzes power by its infinite resistance to murder. Here one encounters not a very great resistance, but something absolutely Other: the resistance of what has no resistance, ethical resistance.

**(i) The face *is* the first word.** Not "the face *contains* the first word"; *the face IS the first word*. The face does not have to be made to mean something; the face *means*, ineluctably. Its meaning is the prohibition.

**(ii) The prohibition is *infinite resistance to murder*.** This does not mean murder is impossible — it is, banally, possible; the body is fragile. What is impossible is *that murder be ethically licit*. The face commands "thou shalt not kill" with infinite force precisely *because* the body offers no force. The asymmetry: the body is weakest; the command is strongest.

**(iii) "Ethical resistance."** Ethics is not a *subjective state* (a feeling of obligation); ethics is *a kind of resistance* — the resistance of what cannot resist. The face, defenseless, *resists* my power not by force but by addressing me. The address itself is the resistance.

**(iv) Second person, not third.** It is not a moral law deduced from a principle ("one must not commit murder"); it is the Other's *direct address to me*, here, now. The Other says it from the height. *I* am the recipient. This is why ethics, for Levinas, cannot be derived from a Kantian universal: the commandment is not universal first; it is *to me*, in this face, before any universal.

### The face as language, before language

*TI* p. 209:

> The face speaks. The manifestation of the face is already discourse. The one who manifests himself comes to the aid of his own word.

The Platonic reference is to the *Phaedrus* (275d–278b): writing cannot defend itself when questioned, but a living speaker can come to the assistance of his own words, can answer back. The face, says Levinas, *is* this living self-defense — the Other is not a frozen text but an interlocutor who *answers back*. This means the face *is* discourse before any specific words are exchanged; the face is the *condition* of all subsequent discourse.

This argument cuts both ways. Against reducing the relation to the Other to a relation between *meanings* I have and *meanings* the Other has — meanings being repeatable, exchangeable, totalizable. The relation to the Other is not the exchange of meanings but the *encounter with the source of meaning* — the *speaking* itself, irreducible to anything spoken. (This becomes the *dire* / *dit* distinction in *AE*.) And against the view that the relation to the Other is silent communion — Buberian I-Thou as wordless presence, mystical fusion. For Levinas the I-Thou is *spoken* — the face *speaks*, addresses, commands.

### Asymmetry, height, command — and the Other absolved from the relation

The Other is ***hauteur*** — height. This is the word the Anglophone reception most often softens. Lingis renders *hauteur* and *Très-Haut* in ways that can sound merely metaphorical, as if Levinas were saying the Other is "elevated" or "to be respected" — keeping the relation among peers who happen to differ in dignity. *That is not what Levinas writes.* The Other addresses *me* from a position I do not occupy and cannot reciprocate. The Other does not stand beside me as a fellow rational being whose claims I weigh against my own; the Other *commands* from above, *teaches*, *puts into question*, while I, the addressed, am the one who answers. The asymmetry is structural, not contingent — and not eventually overcome by mutuality, justice, or fraternity. Justice, when it arrives (with the third), does not abolish the verticality; it *measures* one verticality against another.

*TI* III, "Asymétrie de l'interpersonnel":

> This presence dominates the one who receives it, coming from on high, unforeseen, and therefore teaching by its very novelty. The eye does not shine; it speaks.

> The presence of the face comes from beyond the world while engaging me in human fraternity. It does not crush me as a numinous essence. To be in relation while absolving oneself from that relation is to speak. The Other enters the relation while remaining infinitely distant from it, present there at once as absolute.

The line *l'œil ne luit pas, il parle* — the eye does not shine, it speaks — is one of the great compressions in Levinas. The eye seen as an *object* (as in Sartre's analysis of the look in *L'Être et le néant*) glows, glints, returns the light. The eye seen as *visage*, addressed and addressing, *speaks*. To meet the eye of the Other is already to be spoken to.

The phrase *être en relation en s'absolvant de cette relation* — to be in relation while absolving oneself from this relation — is one of Levinas's most precise formulations. The Other is *in* the relation but is not *exhausted* by it. The Other does not become a moment of the dyad I-Other; the Other remains *absolute* (etymologically: *ab-solutus*, untied, unbound) even within the binding of the relation. This is what distinguishes the Levinasian face from any dialectic: in dialectic, both terms are constituted by the relation; in the face-to-face, the Other *exceeds* the relation that enacts the face-to-face. This is the precise sense in which Levinas's I-Other is **not** Buber's I-Thou.

### The destitute and the Most-High

*TI* III.B:

> The poor person, the stranger, presents himself as equal. His equality in this essential poverty consists in referring to the third already present in the encounter. He joins himself to me, but joins me to him in order to serve. He commands me as a master, and commands me to command.

This is one of Levinas's most beautiful and most demanding formulations. The destitute Other commands me as a *Master* — but the command is to *serve*. The command is the command-to-command — i.e., the command to *take responsibility*, to *become master of myself in service*. The widow does not order me; the widow's destitution *orders me to order*. The face is at once humble and exalted — the same epiphany shows *Très-Haut* and *misérable*, because the height of the command and the destitution that calls forth the command *are the same structural feature*.

This matters for the user's thesis. The user's notion that the divine is encountered in the *individuality* (T11) — that the psychic being is the access to the divine, not an impersonal whole — has structural affinity with Levinas here. The *Très-Haut* is encountered *in* the face of the destitute Other; God is not behind, beyond, or beside the face but accessed *through* and *as* the face. Levinas would resist any direct identification (the Other is not God; the trace of God is *in* the relation), but the structural point is shared.

### The third (le tiers): from face-to-face to justice

*TI* p. 234: *Le tiers me regarde dans les yeux d'autrui — le langage est justice. (...) L'épiphanie du visage comme visage, ouvre l'humanité.* The third is what makes the dyadic face-to-face open into *humanity* — into the social, the political, the question of justice. If there were only I and one Other, my responsibility would be infinite and unbounded; with the third, I have to *compare* the Other's claim with the third's claim, and so *justice* — the weighing, the law, the institution — becomes necessary. The political, for Levinas, is not the *origin* of the ethical but a *consequence* of the ethical: the face-to-face is always already inhabited by the third. (Totalitarianism is when the political *forgets* its origin in the face-to-face.)

## Part IV — *Totalité et Infini*, Sections IV and V: Eros, fecundity, eschatology

The architectural argument is complete by Section III; what follows extends it into the *futurity* of the relation. We treat them briefly, holding only what bears on the user's project.

### Eros and the feminine

> Eros therefore goes beyond the face. Erotic nakedness weighs on the face and burdens expression itself. (TI IV)

The erotic register is *with* a face but goes *beyond* it: *caress*, *play*, *the not-yet*. The treatment of the feminine is the most controversial part of *TI* and Levinas himself qualified it later; the structural point is that there is a relation to the other that is not the strict ethical face-to-face but opens into *futurity*.

### Fecundity (la fécondité)

*TI* p. 299:

> The profanation that violates a secret does not discover, beyond the face, a deeper self that the face had expressed. It discovers the child. Through total transcendence, the self is, in the child, another. My child is a stranger, yet not only mine, because he is me, myself estranged from myself.

The child is *me-as-other*, neither metaphor nor dialectic. The future is *of the other*; my time opens into the time of the other I have engendered. Fecundity is also *election*: the parent responsible *for this one*, irreplaceably (anticipating *substitution* in *AE*).

### Creation against emanation

*TI* p. 325:

> To affirm origin from nothing through creation is to contest the prior community of all things within eternity. Creation best says the absolute separation transcendence requires, affirming both kinship among beings and their radical heterogeneity, their reciprocal exteriority from out of nothing.

Creation *ex nihilo* preserves absolute separation; emanation collapses beings into totality. The user's *acintya-bhedābheda* is closer to creation in Levinas's sense than to emanation, since it preserves *bheda* as ontologically real.

### Section V: the eschatology of peace

> Being is exteriority.

Against Hegel (interiority) and Heidegger (unveiling). *TI* p. 337:

> Peace must be my peace, in a relation that goes from an I toward the Other in desire and goodness, where the I remains itself without egoism. To have meaning is to be situated with respect to an absolute, to come from the dimension of height and transcendence.

Peace is not the conquering of war but *another structure*: beings in non-allergic relation, in welcome. Subjectivity as *welcoming the infinite*.

## Part V — *Autrement qu'être ou au-delà de l'essence* (1974)

*AE* is Levinas's response to a precise challenge: Derrida's *Violence et métaphysique* (1964) had argued that *TI*, for all its anti-ontological intent, still spoke the *language* of the metaphysics it sought to overcome. *TI* speaks *of* the face, *of* infinity, *of* the Other — and to do so is to thematize, to subordinate the Other once again to the categorial machinery of ontology. *Cannot the deepest critique of metaphysics be carried out only in the very language it tries to leave?* — that was Derrida's question. Levinas accepted the pressure and answered it not by retreating but by writing a more violent book.

The prose of *AE* is deliberately strained, broken, paratactic, hyperbolic — it *enacts* the unsaying-of-the-said rather than merely thematizing it. Where *TI* still permits the rhetoric of phenomenological description (the face *appears*, *manifests itself*, *expresses itself*), *AE* substitutes a vocabulary of trauma, hostage, expiation, substitution — terms that name a relation prior to the appearance/manifestation register, a relation *deeper* than any phenomenology can describe. *AE* is the book where Levinas's polemic against Heidegger's ontology reaches its sharpest form: not "ethics is more fundamental than ontology" but "the ethical is *otherwise than being*, *au-delà de l'essence*" — outside the entire register where ontology operates.

Levinas states the book's program with unusual bluntness in the *Note préliminaire*:

> The proposal of this book is to recognize in subjectivity an exception that deranges the conjunction of essence, being, and difference; to see in the hard kernel of the unique in me a substitution for the other; to think this abnegation, prior to willing, as an exposure to transcendence more passive than receptivity, passion, or finitude; and to derive worldly praxis and knowledge from that unassumable susceptibility.

This is the load-bearing statement of intent. The late Levinas is not merely revising terminology; he is relocating subjectivity itself. The "hard kernel of the unique" in me is no longer what survives every relation as self-identical substance; it is what, precisely in being most itself, proves already substituted for the other. The book's notorious violence of diction — *traumatisme*, *otage*, *persecution*, *expiation* — is therefore not rhetorical excess but a disciplined refusal to let subjectivity slide back into the comfortable nominative of self-possession.

### The dire / dit distinction

The decisive technical innovation of *AE* is the distinction between *le Dire* (the Saying) and *le Dit* (the Said). *AE* Ch. II:

> The Said, the word, is not simply the sign of a meaning, nor merely the expression of a meaning. The word at once proclaims and consecrates an identification of this with that within the already-said.

Le **Dit** is propositional content — what can be repeated, fixed, transcribed, communicated, archived; the domain of ontology, of *logos* in its standard sense. Every philosophy, every doctrine, every linguistic act *qua* its semantic content is Said. Le **Dire** is the *act* of address, the *exposition of myself to the other* prior to and irreducible to any propositional content. *AE* Ch. II.B–C:

> Saying is approaching the neighbor, sending meaning. But it is not reducible to the delivery of a sign.

> Saying without the Said is pre-original saying preceding every text. Saying is exposure of oneself without reserve, sincerity and veracity prior to thematization.

(Saying is approaching the neighbor, sending of sense. But it is not reducible to the delivery of a sign. Saying — without the Said — as pre-original saying preceding every text. Saying is exposition of self, without reserve, sincerity, veracity, that precedes thematization.)

The *Argument* had already prepared this move in even stronger terms:

> Saying is not a game. Prior to the verbal signs it joins together, prior to linguistic systems and semantic shimmer, it is proximity of one to another, commitment of approach, one-for-the-other, the signifyingness of signification itself.

This is the sentence to keep against any reduction of Levinas to a philosophy of discourse. The Saying is not one linguistic layer among others; it is the ethical event that makes signification possible at all. Before words *mean*, one person is already exposed to another. Before a sentence can be true or false, one speaker is already answerable. The Saying is not supplementary to semantics; semantics is a late and precarious stabilization of the Saying.

Levinas immediately adds the counter-pressure:

> The correlation of Saying and Said, the subordination of Saying to the Said, to linguistic system and ontology, is the price manifestation demands. In language as Said, everything is translated before us, even if that translation costs a betrayal.

This is why *AE* is not a romantic cult of ineffability. The Said is necessary. Without the Said nothing would show itself, nothing would become communicable, nothing could be taught, remembered, criticized, or transmitted. But the necessity of the Said is paid for in betrayal. Every manifestation already subordinates proximity to theme, approach to content, the one-for-the-other to ontology. *Dédire le dit* therefore does not mean abandoning language; it means working within language so that the betrayal does not pass itself off as innocence. Levinas's late prose is difficult because it is built to keep that wound open.

The decisive structural claim: **the Said *betrays* the Saying — but the Saying *requires* the Said**. Without the Said, there is no determinate communication; without the Saying, the Said is mere speech-without-address, propositional content without addressee. The two are co-implicated, but the Saying is *prior* — and is *betrayed* the moment it crystallizes into a Said.

Hence the philosophical task: *dédire le dit* — the *unsaying* of the Said. The philosopher does not write to fix doctrines; the philosopher writes *and unwrites*, *says and unsays*, *establishes and disestablishes*, knowing that any Said betrays the Saying it had to use. *AE* is the enactment of this method: every doctrinal moment in the book is followed by its retraction, every propositional formulation by its qualification, every thematization by its de-thematization.

**The Saying is *passive* — more passive than passivity** (*passivité plus passive que toute passivité*; *sensibilité plus passive que toute passivité*). I do not first decide to expose myself; I am already exposed. Subjectivity is *constitutively* exposed-to-the-other. To "decide" to be ethical would already be a free act, a project, a choice — and would already be too late, because the ethical demand has *already* commanded me before any free act. The ethical is *anarchic* — without an *archē*, without origin, prior to every founding choice.

### Substitution: the one-for-the-other

*AE* Ch. IV is the heart of the late book, and the central concept is *substitution*. *AE* Ch. IV.4:

> Substitution for the other, the one in place of the other, is expiation. Responsibility for the Other, prior to my freedom and prior to the present and representation, is a passivity more passive than every passivity, exposure to the other without assumption, exposure without reserve, expression, Saying.

(Substitution for the other — the one in the place of the other — expiation. Responsibility for the Other — in its anteriority in relation to my freedom, in its anteriority in relation to the present and to representation — is a passivity more passive than any passivity, exposure to the other without assumption of this exposure itself, exposure without reserve, exposure of exposure, expression, Saying.)

**Substitution is structural, not metaphorical.** Subjectivity *is* this being-for-the-other (*l'un-pour-l'autre*); the for-itself (*pour-soi*) is *derived from* the for-the-other (*pour-l'autre*). The classical hierarchy is reversed: ego does not first constitute itself and then optionally take on responsibility; ego is constituted *by* having been already substituted-for-the-other before any constituting act.

The vocabulary is shocking: *expiation*, *otage* (hostage), *persécution*, *traumatisme* — the language of being-violated, being-on-behalf-of, having-been-claimed-without-consent. The Other does not need to *do* anything to claim me — the Other's mere *being-there* already commands me, *traumatizes* my sufficient self-relation. **The structure of the I, at the deepest level, is to be a hostage of the Other.** *TI*'s asymmetry is now *constitutive* of the I, not a feature of the I-Other relation that befalls a separated I.

> I cannot escape responsibility by lacking power. The possibility of every power goes back to the impossibility of escaping. Hostage to the other, the I ceases to be in its place in its being.

(I cannot escape responsibility by default of power: the possibility of all power goes back to the impossibility of escaping — to the sign that my omnipotence undergoes in the encounter. Hostage of the other, the I ceases to be in its place in its being.)

The *otage* metaphor is precise: a hostage is held *for* someone else, *in place of* the someone else, *as ransom for* the someone else. To be a hostage is not a fate that befalls a self that exists prior to the hostage-condition; it is the structure of selfhood as such.

Levinas condenses the entire move into one of the decisive formulas of the late work:

> I have done nothing and have always been in question: persecuted. Selfhood, in its passivity without origin, is hostage. The word I means: here I am, answering for everything and for everyone.

("I have done nothing and I have always been in question: persecuted. Ipséité, in its passivity without *archē* of identity, is hostage. The word I means here-I-am, answering for everything and for everyone.")

This is the point where the subject shifts from nominative to accusative. The "I" is no longer first of all the one who says "I think," "I will," "I posit," "I choose." It is first the one who answers *me voici* before it can master what it is answering to. Levinas's subject is not an autonomous legislator but a respondent. The self is called before it is constituted as self-possession. This is why *responsibility* in Levinas is not an ethical supplement to subjectivity; it is the grammar of subjectivity itself.

The user's project should pause over this sentence because it is the strongest Western formulation of a truth the bhakti traditions often say devotionally: the self is already bound before it chooses to bind itself. The Vaiṣṇava can then specify the beloved to whom the self says *me voici*; Levinas suspends that specification and leaves only the structure. But the structure is exact, and it is stronger for being stripped of consolatory imagery.

Levinas then makes the point still sharper by naming the subject's case:

> The uniqueness of oneself is precisely the fact of bearing the fault of the other. In responsibility for the Other, subjectivity is the unlimited passivity of an accusative that does not result from a prior nominative. Everything is in advance in the accusative of the self.

This should be read slowly. The accusative is not a nominative that has later undergone inflection. The self is not first a substantial "I" and only afterward placed under obligation, assigned duties, burdened by guilt, or asked to answer. The self is *from the start* in the grammatical position of being-addressed, being-claimed, being-charged. Levinas is trying to think a subject deeper than self-reference and older than self-constitution. The nominative "I" of modern philosophy — Descartes, Kant, Husserl, even Heidegger's existential analytic — is for him a derivative stabilization. Beneath it lies the accused self, the self already answerable.

The moral consequence is severe. Responsibility is not recompense for voluntary action, not judicial liability for what "I" have chosen, not the reflective admission that I too am implicated in structural wrong. It is more primitive than all of these. Levinas can therefore say that the innocent self is accused without absurdity, because the accusation does not concern a prior freely authored deed. It concerns the very fact that the self is singularly answerable where no one can answer in its place. This is why *substitution* is not reducible to juridical guilt and not translatable into a liberal language of chosen obligations. The self's uniqueness is precisely its irreplaceability under accusation.

And this is also why Levinas insists that the relation is not altruistic psychology:

> Responsibility for others can never mean altruistic will, an instinct of natural benevolence, or love. The subject is hostage.

Responsibility is not a noble motive one might or might not have; not an affective tendency toward kindness; not philanthropy; not even love, if love is still thought as a movement beginning in the subject. All of those accounts secretly preserve the nominative ego that first possesses itself and then elects to go outward. Levinas is after something more drastic: a self that is already dispossessed in being itself. In that sense, the late ethics is anti-psychological all the way down.

The culmination of the movement is political as well as ethical:

> This way of responding without prior commitment, responsibility for the other, is human fraternity itself, prior to freedom.

Fraternity, then, is not a civic feeling superadded to autonomous individuals. It is not the warm affect that follows social contract. It is older than contract, older than consent, older than institutions. Freedom is real, but it arises inside a relation that has already obligated the self. This is why Levinas can be read as an antidote to both liberal voluntarism and communitarian fusion. Against the liberal picture, I do not first own myself and then enter relations; against the communitarian picture, relation does not dissolve singularity into a prior whole. The self is singularized by being unable to hand off the other's claim. That is Levinas's most exact answer to the violence of unification.

### Proximity, sensibility, and the neighbor

*AE* Ch. III develops *proximité* — the structural nearness of the *prochain* (neighbor) — as ethical, not spatial. *Proximité* is not geometric closeness; it is the *immediacy* of the ethical demand, the way the Other is *near to me* in a register prior to any spatial measurement.

> Sensibility is exposure to the other. The immediacy of the sensible is the immediacy of enjoyment and its frustration, the gift painfully torn away, not the gift of the heart but of the bread from one's own mouth, the opening of one's dwelling beyond the purse.

(Sensibility is exposure to the other. The immediacy of the sensible is the immediacy of enjoyment and its frustration — the gift painfully torn away, in the tearing-away guaranteeing immediately that very enjoyment, not a gift from the heart but from the bread of one's mouth, from one's mouthful of bread; opening — beyond the wallet — of the doors of one's dwelling.)

The bread is *torn from* my mouth: more violent, more imposed than the welcome of *TI*'s *demeure*. Where *TI* describes hospitality as the welcome extended *from* the dwelling I have constituted *to* the Other who arrives, *AE* describes the ethical demand as already having *displaced me* from any settled possession. I do not *decide* to share my bread; the demand has *already* taken the bread from my mouth before I could constitute myself as the one who decides what to do with bread.
Proximity is therefore not empathy, fusion, fellow-feeling, or mystical togetherness. It is the non-chosen nearness by which the neighbor is already under my skin without ever becoming mine. The body remains decisive, but now as vulnerability rather than enjoyment. Sensibility is not first a power of receiving impressions; it is the site at which the other can wound, interrupt, and oblige me.

### Illeity (illéité): the trace of the Infinite

*AE* Ch. I introduces *illéité* — Levinas's neologism for the way the Infinite signifies in the ethical relation:

> Illeity, a neologism built from "he," indicates a way of concerning me without entering into conjunction with me.

(Illeity — neologism formed on *il* or *ille* — indicates a way of concerning me without entering into conjunction with me.)

The *Il* (Latin *ille*, "he, that one over there") names a *third-person* mode that is neither the I, nor the Thou (Buber), nor the It of an object. The illeity-mode is the trace-mode: a *Il* who has always already passed, who concerns me by *having gone by* before I could be present to the passage.

> If relation with illeity were a relation of consciousness, the "he" would designate a theme. The illeity of the beyond-being is the fact that its coming toward me is a departure that leaves me able to move toward the neighbor.

(If the relation with illeity were a relation of consciousness, "he" would designate a theme — as probably is indicated by the "thou" in Buber's I-Thou relation. The illeity of the beyond-being is the fact that its coming to me is a departure that lets me accomplish a movement toward the neighbor.)

God is encountered *only as trace in the face of the Other*. Not negative theology — a *trace-theology* in which the Infinite signifies *only* through the ethical relation. The "departure that lets me accomplish a movement toward the neighbor" is decisive: the Infinite *gives* itself *as* the gift of the ethical relation; its "presence" is the presence of *another* (the neighbor), not its own. **Religion *is* ethics** — ethics is the locus where transcendence is actually *given*.
This is why *illéité* must not be confused either with Buber's Thou or with Heidegger's concealed Being. It is third-person transcendence that never offers itself as thematic presence and yet intensifies responsibility. The Infinite does not enter the relation as another member of the relation; it passes, leaving the neighbor as imperative. Levinas's God is therefore neither an object nor an interlocutor but the trace by virtue of which my relation to *autrui* can never be closed inside anthropology.

### Diachrony: temporality refused to synchronization

The *Argument* states the temporal thesis in a form that should be quoted whole:

> Within recoverable temporality there must appear a lapse of time without return, a diachrony resistant to every synchronization, a transcendent diachrony, a past older than every representable origin, pre-original and anarchic.

There must, inside recoverable temporality itself, appear a non-returning lapse of time, a diachrony resistant to every synchronization, a past older than every representable origin. This is the decisive anti-Hegelian and anti-Heideggerian safeguard in the late work. If all time could be synchronized — recollected into one present of Spirit, or gathered into one ecstatic unity of Dasein — then the other would once again become a moment of the Same. Levinas's claim is that responsibility arrives from a past I can never have presentified. I am always late to the command that constitutes me.

*AE* thus introduces *diachronie réfractaire à toute synchronisation* — diachrony resistant to all synchronization. The trace of the Other has *passed* before I could be present to its passing; my responsibility *precedes* my consciousness. A past that has never been present. Levinas's response to Heidegger's temporality (in which all temporality is gathered into the ecstatic-horizonal unity of a single Dasein) is that the Other introduces a temporality *I cannot synchronize* — a past that was never *my* present, a future that will never be *my* present, a now that is not a now-for-me but a now-for-the-Other to which I am exposed.

The structural force of diachrony for the user's project: it forbids the move by which any totalizing temporality (Hegel's *Geist* unfolding through time, Heidegger's *Geschick*) could close history into a single time-of-the-Same. The Other's time, in Levinas, is *un-gatherable*. T9 (unity-in-multiplicity) must reckon: any unification of multiplicity-in-time risks the Hegelian closure Levinas blocks. The user's *acintya-bhedābheda* answer: simultaneity at the supramental level is not synchronization across one time but the *aperspectival* holding of multiple temporalities, each preserved as its own, none reducible to a unifying meta-time.
The important pressure here is not merely negative. Levinas is also specifying what any non-violent account of simultaneity would have to preserve: not just plurality of contents, but plurality of temporalities. Aurobindo's and Gebser's more positive talk of integral simultaneity only avoids the Levinasian objection if it preserves exactly this irreducibility. Otherwise the "through-all" becomes only a more sophisticated synchronization-machine.

## Part VI — Talmudic Readings and the Late Ethics

The Anglophone reception, taking its cue from Lingis's translations of the philosophical books and from secondary literature that places Levinas alongside Husserl, Heidegger, Sartre, Merleau-Ponty, has often produced a "Levinas the French Continental philosopher" who happened to be Jewish. **That picture is wrong, and Levinas himself rejected it.** From 1957 until his death he gave annual *Lectures talmudiques* before the Colloque des Intellectuels Juifs de Langue Française, working from the Aramaic of Babli and Yerushalmi with the rabbinic methods of *pilpul* (dialectical reasoning), *aggadah* (narrative-exegetical), and *halakhah* (legal). He translated the readings into French as he went and published them in volumes that he treated as continuous with — not parallel to — *TI* and *AE*. His self-description was a reader of *the Talmud and Greek philosophy* as two languages addressing one question: how does the absolutely Other claim the self, and how must thought respond?

Levinas drew the streams sharply at the level of *method*: the philosophical writings make no appeal to revelation, no citation of scripture as authority; the Talmudic readings work explicitly from the rabbinic text. But at the level of *content* and *deepest claim*, the two streams converge. The structure of subjectivity-as-hostage-of-the-Other in *AE* and the rabbinic claim that the human is created *betzelem Elohim* (in the image of God) and is therefore answerable to every other human as if to God — these are one structural insight in two registers. *Difficile liberté* p. 33:

> Judaism is the extreme consciousness that the sacred cannot manifest itself without the putting-into-question of the self.

The sacred manifests *only* through the breaking-open of self-sufficient subjectivity — the *mise en cause* (putting-into-question) of the I by the demand of the Other. The philosophical *TI*/*AE* account of subjectivity-as-hostage-of-the-Other and the Talmudic emphasis on *teshuvah* (return, repentance) and *bein adam la-chavero* (between person and neighbor) name the same structural insight from two registers.

### A representative reading: *Yoma* 85a–b

The locus classicus is *Yoma* 85a–b on *bein adam la-makom* (between person and God) vs *bein adam la-chavero* (between person and neighbor). The mishnah states: Yom Kippur atones for sins between person and God; for sins between persons, Yom Kippur does *not* atone until the offended party has been appeased. Levinas's reading (in *Quatre lectures talmudiques*, "Toward the Other") draws the structural consequence: **the relation to God is ethically constituted by the relation to the human Other.** Forgiveness for what concerns God *flows through* rectification of what concerns the neighbor. The vertical relation (to God) is not parallel to the horizontal relation (to the neighbor); the vertical *passes through* the horizontal. There is no shortcut to God that bypasses ethical responsibility for the neighbor. This is the rabbinic-textual articulation of the *AE* claim that *illéité* is given *only* in the trace, in the face of the Other.

### *Sanhedrin* 36b–37a: the unique as world

A second canonical Talmudic locus: *Sanhedrin* 36b–37a, on the verse "whoever destroys a single soul has destroyed an entire world; whoever saves a single soul has saved an entire world" (Mishnah *Sanhedrin* 4.5). Levinas reads this as the rabbinic articulation of what the philosophical writings name as the *unicity* of the Other: **each face is the entire world**. The Other is not a member of a class; the Other is *the unique* whose loss is the loss of *all*. The mathematical universe of fungible exemplars (the violence-of-totality Levinas opposes throughout) is here refused at the level of rabbinic exegesis: every individual is metaphysically *the world*.

The structural consequence for politics: *no calculus that treats individuals as exchangeable for one another can be ethically licit*. Utilitarianism, the calculus of the greatest good for the greatest number, is structurally incompatible with the unicity-of-the-Other. Levinas's politics is therefore neither liberal-utilitarian nor communitarian-organicist; it is a politics of irreducible singulars whose claims cannot be aggregated.

### Athens and Jerusalem

Levinas insists, in the methodological essays, that philosophical conceptuality (Greek) and ethical revelation (Jewish) are *both* required. Greek without Jewish becomes ontology — the long Western movement from pre-Socratic *physis* to Heideggerian *Sein* in which the Other is reduced to the Same. Jewish without Greek becomes pious enthusiasm — the immediate religious confession that cannot, by itself, secure rational communicability. The task is *to translate* the Jewish into the Greek without losing the Jewish.

The slogan: *traduire la Bible en grec* — to translate the Bible into Greek — does not mean replacing the Bible with Greek philosophy; it means using Greek conceptual resources to *say* what the Bible *commands*, while preserving what cannot be reduced to Greek conceptuality. The Talmudic readings exhibit one direction of the translation; the philosophical writings exhibit the other. *AE*'s broken, paratactic, hyperbolic prose is itself an effect of the translation: Greek (the philosophical Said) under unceasing pressure from Jewish (the prophetic Saying).
This matters for the integrity of the whole corpus. The philosophical and Talmudic works are not parallel hobbies or double careers; they are two disciplines imposed on the same problem. The philosophy prevents revelation from becoming pious immediacy; the Talmud prevents philosophy from forgetting that it was answerability, not neutral contemplation, that set thought in motion. Read separately, each can look one-sided. Read together, they show why Levinas insists that transcendence must be both rationally sayable and perpetually resistant to being stabilized as theme.

### *De Dieu qui vient à l'idée* (1982)

*De Dieu* p. 110:

> The idea of God comes to a mind that does not have the measure of that idea. That this idea comes to thought at all, that is transcendence.

Three threads in the late work. (i) The *à-Dieu* — *adieu* — the structure of religion is *being-toward* God (à-Dieu), not *being-with* God. The *à* preserves the structural directionality: religion is movement-toward, not co-presence. (ii) *Témoignage* (witness): the I bears witness to the Infinite by *being-for-the-other*. The witness is not a witness of one's own experience of God; the witness is the ethical relation enacted, which is the only place where the Infinite registers. (iii) Ethics is first philosophy; ontology second; religion third — religion is the *thematization* of the structure ethics opens, the linguistic codification of what is given pre-thematically in the ethical relation.

The theme of *témoignage* is especially important here. Witness is not a report on a private mystical experience; it is the fact that the self, in saying *me voici*, lets the trace of the Infinite pass into language without converting that trace into knowledge. Prophecy, in this sense, is ordinary before it is extraordinary. Every response to the neighbor is already a minimal prophecy, because it speaks from a command that did not originate in the speaker. Levinas thereby gives a philosophical account of revelation that avoids both supernatural spectacle and secular reduction: revelation is the ethical event's pressure on language.

This also explains why the late Levinas repeatedly prefers *témoignage* to *vision* or *intuition*. Vision implies theme, object, field, horizon, some mode of gathered givenness. Testimony implies exposure, risk, and speech that cannot fully guarantee itself. The witness does not master what is witnessed; the witness is mastered by the fact that something has laid claim to speech. That is why prophecy, for Levinas, should not be imagined first as ecstatic exception. Prophecy is the ordinary condition of a subject who has been put in question before having put anything in question. When the self says *me voici*, it does not transmit a supernatural message from elsewhere; it lets the very structure of responsibility become audible.

For the user's project this is a crucial constraint. Any positive metaphysics of the divine that speaks too quickly in the register of vision, presence, or achieved gnosis risks bypassing the Levinasian warning and slipping back into spiritualized totality. But a positive metaphysics that keeps the witness-structure in view — that lets ontology remain answerable to the neighbor — can take Levinas not as a veto but as a discipline. The test is simple: does divine language intensify responsibility, or does it excuse one from responsibility by claiming a higher synthesis? Levinas's whole corpus is written to make the second move impossible.
That is also why the late religious essays never simply "add God" to the philosophical project. God does not appear as a final explanatory term completing the system. The divine name arrives as what prevents completion, what keeps the ethical relation from settling into anthropology, sociology, or ontology alone. In this sense Levinas is not less demanding in the religious writings than in the philosophical ones; he is more demanding. He insists that if the word "God" is to have sense, it must increase answerability rather than reduce it. The user can disagree about whether this is the only possible sense of God, but cannot responsibly ignore the force of the criterion.

This ordering matters for the user's thesis: Levinas's "ethics first" does not deny the user's ontological priority of Saccidānanda; it insists that *for us, here, in our finite consciousness, the divine is given only through ethics*. The user's position can integrate this as a description of *the structure of access* without giving up its ontological commitment. The dispute is whether the structure of access is the only legitimate philosophical content, or whether positive ontological metaphysics is also possible.

### *Otherwise than Being* and Aurobindonian *bhakti*

The most surprising convergence the user can make with the late Levinas is on *bhakti*. *AE*'s subjectivity as exposed-to-the-other, as substituted-for-the-other, as hostage, as *passivité plus passive que toute passivité* — this structure is, formally, the structure of *bhakti*-surrender (*śaraṇāgati*) at the level of the relation to the Divine. *Surrender from fullness* (*pūrṇatva-icchā*, Abhinavagupta) is *not* the surrender of an emptied self to an external master; it is the recognition that the self has *always already been substituted* for what it surrenders to. The Vaiṣṇava *dāsya-bhāva* (servant-mood), *sakhya* (friendship), *vatsalya* (parental affection), *mādhurya* (lover-beloved) — each is a register of an *asymmetric* relation in which the *bhakta* is *constituted by* being-for-the-Beloved. Levinas's structural analysis of the I-as-hostage gives a Western philosophical articulation of what the Vaiṣṇava traditions have always claimed: subjectivity is structurally surrendered before any free act of surrender.

The difference, of course, is the *who*. For Levinas, the one to whom I am surrendered is *the human Other*, with God registering only as *trace*. For the Vaiṣṇava, the one to whom I am surrendered is *Krishna* or *Kṛṣṇa-Caitanya* directly, with the human relation participating in the divine relation. The user's reading: Levinas describes the *structure* of surrender accurately; the Vaiṣṇava traditions specify the *content* of surrender that Levinas refuses to specify. Both can be held — the structural rigor of Levinas plus the ontological positivity of the Vaiṣṇava — at the supramental register where the asymmetry of the I-Other relation is *itself* the divine's mode of self-relation.

## Part VII — Reading Notes

### Where Levinas confirms the user's thesis

**(1) T0 — the violence-of-unification critique receives its strongest articulation in Levinas.** *Totality is violence* not because totalizers tend to be violent but because *the structure of totalization is the reduction of the Other to the Same*. The user's individuality-thesis (T11) aligns structurally with Levinas's separated-I.

**(2) T9 — unity-in-multiplicity must preserve asymmetry.** Any unity that abolishes multiplicity has reduced the Other to the Same. **The user's *acintya-bhedābheda* answers Levinas's challenge by holding multiplicity as ontologically primary alongside non-difference, rather than reducing one to the other.**

**(3) Ethics through the face matches the Vaiṣṇava intuition.** *Bhakti rasa* presupposes maintained distinction (*dāsya*, *sakhya*, *vatsalya*, *mādhurya*). Levinas gives a sophisticated philosophical articulation of why the Vaiṣṇava preservation of distinction is structurally required.

**(4) Ethics first vs ontology first matches T11's methodology.** Both proceed phenomenologically from the lived first person and let metaphysics emerge — not by deducing experience from a prior metaphysics.

### Where Levinas diverges

**(1) Levinas refuses any positive ontology of the Real.** The user's thesis *is* a positive ontology: Saccidānanda, the diaphanous through-all, the supramental.

**The user's reply must run as follows. The supramental, in Aurobindo, is not a perspective from which the multiplicity is viewed as moments of the Same. It does not thematize the Other from a perspective — it *is* the through-all of all perspectives.** This is Gebser's *aperspectival*: not a viewpoint that organizes others, but the simultaneous holding of all viewpoints from inside each, none of them privileged. The Levinasian objection assumes any *one-claim* must be made *from a perspective*. The user's claim is that the supramental is precisely the register at which the one-claim is *not* made from a perspective. **The asymmetry of the face-to-face is *included*, not abolished, in the supramental — because the supramental *is* the holding-of-the-asymmetry, not its dissolution.**

This does not fully satisfy Levinas — even "aperspectival" is itself a position. **This is a genuine philosophical fork.** The user must defend the possibility of a non-violent positive ontology; Levinas defends the structural impossibility.

**(2) Levinas refuses any direct relation to God.** The user's tradition assumes *darśana*, *bhakti*, *sākṣi-caitanya*. Levinas's structural claim that ethics is the *only* access to transcendence is, the user holds, an over-generalization. But the user's position *integrates* Levinas as a corrective: *whatever direct access there is to the divine, it cannot bypass or override the ethical responsibility for the Other*. Aurobindo himself insists the supramental descent does not abrogate the ethical; it *fulfills* it.

**(3) Levinas would judge the user's "supramental" as still a thematization.** **The user's thesis is a *Said* that gestures at a *Saying* it cannot fully say.** The user is offering *a particular Said* that, the user claims, *gestures more honestly* at what exceeds it than the totalizing Saids of Hegel. Levinas's prose strains *against* its own Said; the user's prose more confidently inhabits its Said while marking that the Saying exceeds it. This is closer to Aurobindo, who writes long, leisurely, affirmatively about the supramental — *Said* as finger pointing at the moon. The user's defense is *adhikāra*: the same Said works differently for different audiences.

### How Banerji uses Levinas (Monsoon 2022, pp. 82–83)

Banerji brings Levinas into the discussion of *tat tvam asi*. The standard Advaitic reading: *tat tvam asi* declares identity of *ātman* and *brahman*; the apparent Self–Other distinction dissolves. Banerji's worry: read this way, *tat tvam asi* is the totalizing move Levinas warns against.

Banerji's move (drawing on Buber, Levinas, Derrida): read *tat tvam asi* as a *relational-ethics* mahāvākya rather than identity. Monsoon 2022, pp. 82–83:

> *Contemporary philosophers such as the Hasidic existentialist Martin Buber or the French-Lithuanian philosopher Emmanuel Levinas or the philosopher of deconstruction and messianism, Jacques Derrida, come to mind in comparative analogy and contemporary relevance to this praxis. If we can see the One Being in other beings along with their uniqueness, we can be free from the demands of the ego and assume responsibility for the other as a form of alterity of oneself.*

The "*tat*" is not the *tat* of identity but the *tat* of the Other-in-its-Otherness *recognized as* the divine. **Thou art That** means: the Thou you face *is* the divine — not by being absorbed but by being divine *as Thou*, in its alterity. The I-Thou *is* the divine's mode of self-relation. This aligns with *acintya-bhedābheda*.

Where the user can take this *further than Levinas would permit*: the I-Thou is not just the trace of the divine (*illéité*) but the *living dynamic* of the divine — *līlā* as the very mode of the divine's being. Levinas would refuse this last step; the user can take it because the user's commitment is to a *positive* metaphysics holding multiplicity and non-difference simultaneously real.

### Relations to Buber, Derrida, Lacan

**Buber.** I-Thou is *symmetrical* (mutual constitution); for Levinas asymmetrical (the Other comes from a height). The user can hold both: Banerji's reading lets the *symmetrical-mutual* I-Thou (Buber) be the *human* face of what is, at the divine level, an *asymmetrical* responsibility (Levinas), and at the supramental level an *aperspectival* through-all that includes both poises.

**Derrida.** *Violence et métaphysique* (1964) was the great challenge to *TI*; *AE* is partly a response. *Adieu à Emmanuel Levinas* and *De l'hospitalité* develop Levinasian themes deconstructively. The user's relation to Derrida is mediated: where Derrida tends negative, the user holds positive ontology with the *aperspectival* qualification.

**Lacan.** Lacan's *l'Autre* is the symbolic order — closes into a system. Levinas's Other is irreducible. The user sides with Levinas.

### Reading sequence for Levinas

For a reader new to Levinas with the user's interest, the recommended sequence:

**1. *Totalité et infini*, Section I.** The framing is laid out in the first 80 pages: *Métaphysique et transcendance*, *Séparation et discours*, the polemic against ontology, the founding distinctions. Read in French if possible (Martinus Nijhoff, 1961; later Kluwer/Springer); Lingis's English (Duquesne, 1969) is accurate but heavy. Allow a week for slow reading.

**2. *Totalité et infini*, Section III.** *Le visage et l'extériorité* — the heart of the book. The arguments about the face as expression, refusal of possession, the originary command, the destitute and the Most-High, the third. Read after Section I has set the stage; Section II can be read more quickly on first pass.

**3. *Difficile liberté*, selected essays.** Particularly "Une religion d'adultes," "Aimer la Thora plus que Dieu," "Espace et géométrie," "Signature." These give the bridge between the philosophical and the Talmudic.

**4. *Quatre lectures talmudiques* (1968).** The most accessible Talmudic readings; "Toward the Other" on *Yoma* and "The Temptation of Temptation" on *Shabbat*. Reading these gives concrete substance to what otherwise risks being too abstract.

**5. *Autrement qu'être ou au-delà de l'essence* (1974).** The hardest of Levinas's books and indispensable for the user's project. Cohen's English (*Otherwise than Being*, Duquesne 1981; reissued Pittsburgh 1998) is the standard. Read after the *TI* sequence; the prose presupposes familiarity with the *TI* framework. Particular attention: Ch. II (Saying and Said), Ch. IV (Substitution), Ch. V (Subjectivity and Infinity).

**6. *Of God Who Comes to Mind* / *De Dieu qui vient à l'idée* (1982).** The late Levinas on religion; "God and Philosophy" the most important essay.

**7. Secondary literature.** Robert Bernasconi and Simon Critchley, *Re-Reading Levinas* (Indiana 1991); Jacques Derrida, "Violence et métaphysique" (in *L'écriture et la différence*, 1967) — the foundational critique to which *AE* responds; Adriaan Peperzak, *To the Other* (Purdue 1993). For the Vedānta-Levinas dialogue: Banerji 2022 (*Monsoon* pp. 82–83); the user's POSITION_BRIEF and forthcoming work.

### Closing note

Levinas is, for the user's working position, simultaneously the strongest *external* critic and the deepest *internal* ally. The strongest critic, because his articulation of the violence of totality is the most rigorous in the modern Western tradition; any positive ontology that does not reckon with him is naïve. The deepest ally, because the user's *methodological* commitments — phenomenology of the individual psychic being, the asymmetry of *bhakti*, the irreducibility of the Other in *acintya-bhedābheda*, the resistance to Hegelian *Aufhebung* — match Levinas's most uncompromising claims.

The conversation is therefore a *productive friction*. The user's position lives by taking Levinas's challenge with full seriousness and answering it with the resources of *acintya-bhedābheda* and the supramental. The face of the Other is not the enemy of the user's thesis. The face of the Other is the *site* where the user's thesis is tested and where, properly understood, it gives its account: the divine *is* the asymmetry; the divine *is* the through-all of all asymmetric relations; the supramental is *not* the abolition of the I-Thou but its *fulfillment* as a poise of the divine's *līlā*.

The user must hold three points simultaneously when working with Levinas. First, the structural rigor of Levinas's analysis is not negotiable: any Said betrays its Saying, any thematization risks the violence of identity, any totality (including any "supramental totality") is structurally vulnerable to the critique of *Aufhebung*. The user's writing, when it reaches for positive ontological language, must accept this pressure and write *under* the *Bilderverbot*'s shadow, not against it. Second, Levinas's structural analysis is *complete* as an account of how *we, in finite consciousness, encounter the divine* — but is *not* a complete ontology, because Levinas explicitly refuses to make ontological claims that go beyond the structure of access. The user can work with the access-structure intact while supplying the positive ontological content (Saccidānanda, *acintya-śakti*, the supramental as *vijñāna*) that Levinas refuses. Third, the most fruitful zone of engagement is where Banerji has already opened the door: the *tat tvam asi* relational-ethics reading in *Monsoon* 2022 pp. 82–83, where the *tat* is not the *tat* of identity but the *tat* of the Other-in-its-Otherness *recognized as* the divine. This is the move Levinas would not himself make, but it is the move available to a thinker who can read both Levinas and the *Bhāgavata* together.

Levinas's late wish, in the closing pages of *AE*: *Que la signification ne soit point absorbée par l'être, voilà ce que vise une pensée pour qui l'au-delà de l'essence n'est pas un mot vain.* (That signification be not absorbed by being — that is what a thought aims at for which the beyond-essence is not an empty word.) The user's thesis aims at the same target by a different path: the supramental is the level at which signification is *not absorbed by being* because at that level being and signification are non-different in their very difference. The two paths converge on the refusal of totalization; they diverge on whether the convergence is ontologically nameable. Both, in the user's view, are needed.
