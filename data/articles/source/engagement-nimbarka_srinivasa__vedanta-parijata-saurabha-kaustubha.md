# Engagement: Nimbārka and Śrīnivāsa on the Brahma-Sūtra

## 1. What the text is

*Vedānta-Pārijāta-Saurabha* is Nimbārka's bhāṣya on the *Brahma-Sūtra*; *Vedānta-Kaustubha* is Śrīnivāsa's fuller exposition of that bhāṣya, printed here in a layered edition that also appears to include a later *Kaustubha-prabhā* sub-commentary [OCR on the title-page name is not fully secure]. The school is Nimbārka Vedānta, classically described as *dvaitādvaita* or *svābhāvika-bhedābheda*. The file itself does not settle exact dates, but both authors belong to the medieval Nimbārka lineage, with Śrīnivāsa later than Nimbārka and earlier than the still later Gauḍīya deployment of related *bhedābheda* idioms. Structurally the text follows the standard four *adhyāya*, sixteen *pāda* architecture of the *Brahma-Sūtra*; the contents section near the end of the pure-Sanskrit file maps the adhikaraṇas and internal pagination in detail (pure-text lines 34116-34659).

Cross-engagement: a substantive disagreement with Abhinavagupta on the manifestation status axis — `expressive-manifestation` ([primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16)) against the present `dependent-real`.

## 2. Sampling and scope statement

Engaged at depth: the opening hermeneutic of 1.1, especially [BS 1.1.1-4](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/1.1.1-4) (internal pp. 19-73; pure-text lines 1124-1237 and 1678-1757); the *Ārambhaṇa-adhikaraṇa* in 2.1 (pp. 403-413; lines 11571-11890); the *bhedābheda* and *aṃśa* material in 2.3 (esp. pp. 883-885; lines 23093-23210); and the liberation debates in 4.4 (pp. 1320 and 1336-1345; lines 33028-33045 and 33426-33621). That is a close engagement with selected load-bearing loci totaling roughly thirty pages of the internal Sanskrit pagination.

Cross-engagement: on the identity relation axis, Adi Sankara commits to `non-otherness` where this section commits to `natural-difference-non-difference` ([primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1)).

Structural summary only: the remaining *pāda*s, reconstructed from the on-disk contents map and checked against explicit pāda-opening or pāda-closing markers in the body of the text. Not engaged in continuous detail: the whole third-layer sub-commentary where it separates from Śrīnivāsa, the long anti-Buddhist and anti-Sāṅkhya stretches of 2.2 taken page by page, and the closing *sūtrānukramaṇikā*. No claim is made here to have read the whole commentary continuously from first to last; the claim is narrower and defensible.

## 3. Structural walk-through

**Adhyāya 1: Samanvaya**. The first adhyāya runs from the *jijñāsā*-opening through the great Upaniṣadic identity-loci and their interpretive capture by the commentator (internal pp. 19-364; contents at lines 34116-34234). Pāda 1 establishes that *brahma-jijñāsā* is inquiry into Bhagavān Vāsudeva/Kṛṣṇa, then reads major opening *sūtra*s and several key Vedic terms as directly Brahman-referring; pādas 2-4 widen that method through the *antaryāmin*, *vaiśvānara*, *bhūmā*, *dahara*, *ākāśa*, *jyotis*, and related loci. Polemically, this is already anti-Mīmāṃsā, anti-Sāṅkhya, and anti-nirviśeṣa Advaita: the text does not first offer a neutral ontology and only later weaponize it; its constructive semantics is itself a refutation.

Cross-engagement: Akalanka's reading at [primary text](cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/1.34-35) fills the epistemic authority axis with `plural-pramana-realism`. The present section fills it with `scripture-dominant`.

**Adhyāya 2: Avirodha**. The second adhyāya (pp. 372-789; lines 34236-34375) is the text's densest polemical engine. Pāda 1 attacks rival causal accounts and, crucially, rejects *jaganmithyātva*, explicitly defending Brahman as *abhinna-nimittopādāna* and, per the contents map, stabilizing a *brahma-pariṇāma* reading. Pāda 2 turns outward against Sāṅkhya, Vaiśeṣika atomism, several Buddhist schools, Pāśupata, and problematic readings of Pāñcarātra; pādas 3-4 return to cosmology, the self, agency, and the *aṃśa* relation between *jīva* and Brahman. This is where the anti-illusionist and anti-reductionist architecture of the whole commentary becomes unmistakable.

**Adhyāya 3: Sādhana**. The third adhyāya (pp. 775-1182; lines 34376-34541) shifts from causal metaphysics to the discipline of realization. The first half argues for the self's knowership, agency, dependence on Īśvara, and finally its *aṃśa*-status; the second half systematizes *upāsanā* across textual contexts, determines when *guṇa*s transfer from one vidyā to another, and works through *āśrama*, *adhikāra*, and karma's relation to *brahmavidyā*. Polemically, the target here is not only external schools but also any flattening view that cannot preserve real distinction within dependence.

**Adhyāya 4: Phala**. The fourth adhyāya (pp. 1188-1363; lines 34543-34659) is a sustained account of post-realization and liberation. Pāda 1 insists on repetition of *śravaṇa-manana-nididhyāsana* and treats the destruction of karmic residue; pādas 2-3 map death, *nāḍī*, *archirādi-mārga*, and arrival at the supreme destination. Pāda 4 is the doctrinal crux: *sāyujya* is not identity-collapse, *jīvanmukti* is denied, the liberated possess *satya-saṅkalpatva*, and yet their sovereignty explicitly excludes world-creation. If adhyāya 2 answers Advaita on worldhood, adhyāya 4 answers it on mokṣa.

## 4. Load-bearing passages — engaged at depth

### Passage 1

Cross-engagement: a substantive disagreement with Baladeva Vidyabhushana on the practice-path axis — `devotion-and-grace` ([primary text](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1)) against the present `knowledge-discipline`.

**Locus**: [BS 1.1.1](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/1.1.1), *jijñāsā-adhikaraṇa* (pure-text lines 1175-1180).

Cross-engagement: Akshapada Gautama works the soteric end axis in the soteriological register too, but the value is `isolation-or-discriminative-release`, not `identity-with-ground` ([primary text](cite://aksapada/nyaya-sutra/1.1.1)).

**Sanskrit (IAST)**:

`brahma-śabdena ca “svabhāvato'pāsta-samasta-doṣam`
`aśeṣa-kalyāṇa-guṇaugha-rāśim /`
`vyūhāṅginaṃ [OCR?] brahma paraṃ vareṇyaṃ`
`dhyāyema kṛṣṇaṃ kamalekṣaṇaṃ harim” ity uktaḥ śrī-kṛṣṇa evātrābhidhīyate.`

**Close English**: "And by the word *brahman* here precisely Śrī Kṛṣṇa is denoted, as described [elsewhere] as one whose very nature is free from all defects, who is a mass of endless auspicious qualities, the supreme Brahman, the adorable Hari, lotus-eyed Kṛṣṇa."

**Engagement**: This is not merely a devotional flourish appended to an otherwise neutral Vedānta. It is a semantic fixing of `brahman` at the very opening of the *jijñāsā*. The commentary refuses the move by which the opening inquiry would terminate in an indeterminate, quality-less absolute and only secondarily admit a personal Lord at the empirical level.

The load-bearing marker is `evātra`: not just "Kṛṣṇa also counts as Brahman," but "here, this is who the word picks out." The entire later structure of *nimitta-upādāna* causality and of liberation without identity-annihilation depends on this initial semantic refusal.

### Passage 2

**Locus**: [BS 1.1.2](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/1.1.2), *janmādy-adhikaraṇa* (pure-text lines 1717-1725).

**Sanskrit (IAST)**:

`tathā ca jagad-abhinna-nimittopādānatve sati`
`satyatvādimatvaṃ brahma-lakṣaṇaṃ siddham.`

**Close English**: "And So, granted that Brahman is the non-different efficient and material cause of the world, Brahman's defining character as possessed of reality and the rest is established."

**Engagement**: The compactness matters. `nimitta` and `upādāna` are not split; the cause is not an external designer imposing form on alien matter. `abhinna` does the harder work: the world is not outside Brahman as a second independent stuff, but neither is it dismissed as a merely false appearance.

This is one of the cleanest places where Nimbārka's line should be distinguished both from crude dualism and from *vivarta*-style illusionism. Dependence is total; erasure is refused.

### Passage 3

**Locus**: [BS 2.1](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/2.1), *Ārambhaṇa-adhikaraṇa* (pure-text lines 11673-11679).

**Sanskrit (IAST)**:

`... svasya jagataḥ “tat tejo'sṛjata ... nārāyaṇād [OCR?] jāyate prāṇaḥ ...”`
`ity-ādi-śruti-smṛti-pramāṇena brahma-janyatayā`
`ajñāna-janyatvābhāvāt satyatvam eva.`

**Close English**: "... of this world, on the strength of scriptural and smṛti authority such as 'it created fire' and 'from Nārāyaṇa prāṇa is born,' precisely because it is produced from Brahman and not produced from ignorance, there is reality alone."

**Engagement**: This is the anti-*mithyātva* thesis in its most direct form. The text does not merely say that the world is pragmatically useful or provisionally admissible; it says that Brahman-causation excludes ignorance-causation as the primary ontological story. The world is real as dependent effect.

That also explains the commentary's handling of `māyā`: in this stretch it pushes toward `prakṛti` or `śakti`, not toward a world-canceling doctrine of nescience. For the user's thesis this is a strong primary citation; for any synthesis that wants a positive ontology of ignorance, it is also a limit-case, because Śrīnivāsa is plainly blocking the Advaitin architecture in which *avidyā* bears the world.

### Passage 4

**Locus**: [BS 2.3](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/2.3), *bhedābheda / aṃśa* discussion (pure-text lines 23123-23130).

**Sanskrit (IAST)**:

`“sarvaṃ khalv idaṃ brahma ...” ity abheda-vyapadeśāt,`
`“yaḥ pṛthivyāṃ tiṣṭhan ... eṣa te ātmāntaryāmy amṛtaḥ”`
`ity-ādinā bheda-vyapadeśāc ca;`
`ubhaya-vidha-śākhā-virodhāt svābhāviko bhedābheda eva sidhyati.`

**Close English**: "Because there are declarations of non-difference such as 'all this is Brahman,' and because there are declarations of difference such as 'he who stands within the earth ... that is your self, the inner controller, the immortal,' and because otherwise the two kinds of scriptural recensions would conflict, only a natural difference-and-non-difference is established."

**Engagement**: This is the commentary's most concentrated hermeneutic formula. The point is not that some texts say `bheda` and others say `abheda`, so one must compromise. The stronger claim is that both sets of statements are equally load-bearing and equally literal at their own level; therefore the ontology must be capacious enough to preserve both without reducing either to mere appearance or mere metaphor.

This is also where Nimbārka differs from both Madhva and Śaṅkara. The former protects difference more strongly, the latter non-difference more strongly. Śrīnivāsa instead insists that the relation itself is *svābhāvika*: not merely epistemic, not merely pedagogical, and not merely a contradiction parked under *acintyatva*.

**Pāṇinian-rich treatment**:

`abheda-vyapadeśāt`: ablative singular; a *hetu*-ablative, "because of a declaration of non-difference." Samāsa-vigraha: `abhedasya vyapadeśaḥ`.

`bheda-vyapadeśāc ca`: parallel ablative; `ca` coordinates the second scriptural reason with the first. Vigraha: `bhedasya vyapadeśaḥ`.

`ubhaya-vidha-śākhā-virodhāt`: again ablative of reason. Vigraha: `ubhayavidhānāṃ śākhānāṃ virodhaḥ`. The commentary's claim is that denying either side would force contradiction within scripture itself.

`svābhāvikaḥ bhedābhedaḥ eva sidhyati`: principal clause. `bhedābhedaḥ` is the nominative singular subject; `sidhyati` is the main verb; `svābhāvikaḥ` qualifies the subject, ruling out merely adventitious or merely imagined relation; `eva` is restrictive, excluding rival models.

Kāraka structure: the ablatives provide the causal ground; the syntactic subject is `bhedābhedaḥ`; the verbal action is epistemic-establishing, not productive. The sentence is so not "scripture causes reality to become bhedābheda," but "from these pramāṇa-conditions, natural bhedābheda is what stands established."

### Passage 5

**Locus**: [BS 4.4](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/4.4), *sāyujya* discussion (pure-text lines 33028-33035).

**Sanskrit (IAST)**:

`saha yujyate iti sayuk [OCR?]; sayujo bhāvaḥ sāyujyaṃ`
`nitya-sambandha ity arthaḥ. bhagavatā nitya-sambandho 'tra`
`bhāva-padārthaḥ. na caikyam eva tad-artha iti vācyaṃ ...`
`“yathā nadyaḥ syandamānāḥ samudre 'staṃ gacchanti ...” tatrāpi bhedasya sattvāt.`

**Close English**: "'Sāyujya' means the state of being conjoined; its sense is perpetual relation. Here the intended meaning is an eternal relation with the Lord. One must not say that its meaning is sheer identity ... even in the text 'as rivers flowing enter the sea,' difference remains there too."

**Engagement**: This is a remarkably hard anti-monistic reading of liberation. Śrīnivāsa does not merely add relation after the fact; he lexicalizes `sāyujya` as `nitya-sambandha`. Even the standard river-to-sea text is denied merger-value. The burden of proof is shifted entirely: if one wants identity-annihilation, one must argue against the semantics supplied here, not merely cite the familiar imagery.

For the user's thesis this is double-edged. It strongly supports the critique of world-denying Māyāvāda and the insistence that manifestation and relation are not null. But it also resists any synthesis that wants final liberation to retain a stronger non-dual identity than Nimbārka is prepared to grant.

### Passage 6

**Locus**: [BS 4.4](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/4.4), *saṅkalpādhikaraṇa* (pure-text lines 33456-33476).

**Sanskrit (IAST)**:

`saṅkalpād eva tu [OCR?] śruteḥ ... saṅkalpa-mātrād evāsya tat-prāptiḥ.`
`... muktasya tu tat-prasādāt tat-sāmyaṃ upagatasya tathātvam asty eva.`

**Close English**: "From intention alone because scripture says so ... from intention alone he attains that. But in the case of the liberated, who have attained likeness to That through Its grace, such a condition truly does exist."

**Engagement**: Liberation does not terminate in blank quiescence. The liberated possess effective will; yet the ground of that will is not independent self-sovereignty but attained likeness through divine grace. This is why the same pāda can grant *satya-saṅkalpatva* and still deny world-creation to the liberated.

That combination matters. The text wants plenitude after liberation, but not competition with Īśvara. It is a theology of participation, not absorption and not autonomous deification.

## 5. The text's relation to the position

This text sits alongside Vallabha and, in a different register, Rāmānuja as a major realist pressure-point inside Vedānta. It is not itself on the exact Bhartṛprapañca → Bhāskara → Madhva → Caitanya line; rather, it runs in parallel and strengthens that broader recovery of world-reality from another angle. With the Viśiṣṭādvaita line it shares the anti-Māyāvāda insistence on a personal Brahman and a real world, but it differs by making *svābhāvika-bhedābheda* rather than qualified non-duality do the main work. With Madhva and later Caitanya it shares the refusal to let `abheda` erase real difference, but it does not take Madhva's *tattvavāda* route; `abheda` remains scripturally irreducible.

Its named or clearly marked refutation-targets, from the contents map and the sampled loci, include Mīmāṃsakas, Sāṅkhyas, Vaiśeṣikas, several Buddhist positions, Pāśupatas, and problematic readings of Pāñcarātra; the recurrent anonymous *pūrvapakṣin* in the sampled pages is very often the Advaitin, especially the defender of *anirvacanīya-mithyātva*, *adhyāsa*, *upahita-brahman*, and merger-language for liberation. The constructive thesis is stable throughout: Bhagavān Vāsudeva/Kṛṣṇa/Puruṣottama is Brahman; the world is his non-different efficient and material effect; *cit* and *acit* are naturally different and non-different from him; mokṣa is likeness, relation, and participation without identity-collapse.

On the user's thesis, the strongest support is for T2 and T9. T2: this is an unusually direct primary witness against any equation of *mithyā* with *asat*; it presses farther, toward a dependent realism in which Brahman-causation secures the world's reality. T9: it is an explicit Vaiṣṇava resistance to any non-duality that would dissolve relation, manifestation, and divine plenitude. T7 is much less friendly. This is not a Vivaraṇa text, and it does not want *avidyā* to do positive ontological work inside Brahman; the architecture in which Brahman is both *āśraya* and *viṣaya* of *avidyā* would be precisely the kind of move these pages are trying to block. T11 resonates only partially: the world is real and manifestation is meaningful, but there is no Aurobindonian involution-evolution story here, nor any sense that history is the ascent of consciousness. The largest friction, then, is exact: the commentary helps the position wherever the position needs anti-illusionist Vedānta, but it resists any synthesis that asks liberation to culminate in stronger identity than enduring relation.

## 6. What the user can productively take

- A strong primary-source warrant that *brahma-jijñāsā* can be explicitly fixed as inquiry into Bhagavān Vāsudeva/Kṛṣṇa, not merely into an impersonal remainder.
- A compact, source-grounded rejection of *jaganmithyātva*: the world is real because it is Brahman-produced, not ignorance-produced.
- A clean formula for *svābhāvika-bhedābheda* that can mediate between Rāmānuja's qualification-theory and later Gauḍīya *acintya-bhedābheda* without collapsing into either.
- A serious obstacle for any synthesis that wants final liberation to erase difference: Śrīnivāsa reads *sāyujya* as `nitya-sambandha`, protects *satya-saṅkalpatva*, and still denies liberated world-creation.
- A precise task for the position: if it wants to retain a stronger non-dual culmination, it must answer this text not only on world-reality but on mokṣa-semantics.
