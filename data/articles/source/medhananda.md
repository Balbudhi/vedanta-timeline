# Swami Medhananda / Surendranath Maharaj — A Deep Reader

A long-form, mobile-readable companion to the contemporary monk-philosopher who has, over the last decade, produced what is now the strongest sustained academic articulation of much of the philosophical territory the user's thesis occupies. The function of this reader is precise: after working through it, you should not need to crack open *Infinite Paths to Infinite Reality* (OUP 2018) or *Swami Vivekananda's Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* (OUP 2022) before drafting the relevant sections of the dissertation. Long verbatim blockquotes, page-cited; methodical explication; honest mapping where his line confirms the user's, where it diverges, and where the user extends past it.

The crucial framing, which controls every sub-section: what Medhananda has done, in two long OUP monographs and a flurry of *Religions* / *Monist* / *Philosophy East and West* / *Journal of Religion* essays, is to convert into rigorous Anglophone philosophical idiom positions that until very recently were carried in the Bengali *Kathāmṛta*, in occasional Aurobindonian and Vivekananda-Order writings, and in scattered comparative-philosophy essays. He is, in the strict academic sense, the first scholar to have written a stand-alone philosophical book on Sri Ramakrishna (his own observation, *Infinite Paths* p. 1–2). He is also the first to have given Vivekananda's mature philosophy a chapter-by-chapter reconstruction adequate to the analytic philosophy of religion. The user's thesis — on T2 (the *Vijñāna Vedānta* / realist trajectory through Bhartṛprapañca-Bhāskara-Madhva-Caitanya-Aurobindo), T3 (*adhikāra-bheda* and the pedagogical-not-metaphysical structure of Ramakrishna's hierarchy), T6 (the honey-and-taste-of-honey doctrine, *paramādvaita*, integration of *jñāna* and *bhakti*), and parts of T1, T4, T7, T9, T11, T12 — finds in Medhananda's work an academic interlocutor whose page-citations can substitute for the user's having to rederive much of what is already in his books. That is the right outcome; the user's contribution becomes by contrast sharply visible — Madhva-as-register, Caitanya at the Sanskrit-source level, *bhāvarūpa-avidyā* + Trika *svātantrya* + Madhusūdana scaffolding for T7, the McGilchrist / Gebser / Deleuze / Prigogine / Whitehead constellation, the *matsya-nyāya* / Graeber political extension, Anirban-Sāṃkhya physics, and the directive turn (T5, T8, T10) sharper than Medhananda's own ethical pluralism.

This reader treats those two lists — what Medhananda already argues, what the user genuinely extends — as the destination. Parts I–IV walk the verbatim passages. Part V names the destinations precisely.

## Reader's Orientation

**The dual identity is real and is not an alias.** Ayon Maharaj is the academic name under which the philosopher published until roughly 2020. Swami Medhananda is the monastic name he received as a sannyāsin of the Ramakrishna Order. Both refer to the same person — currently Senior Research Fellow at the Ramakrishna Mission Vivekananda Educational and Research Institute (RKMVERI) at Belur Math, with a continuing affiliation as Tagore-Sabbagh Visiting Professor and prior teaching at Loyola Marymount University. He completed his PhD at the University of California, Berkeley (Comparative Literature, 2010), with a dissertation on Coleridge that has nothing to do with the work he is now known for; *Infinite Paths to Infinite Reality* was written after he had moved to RKMVERI in Belur and entered serious engagement with the Bengali *Kathāmṛta*, the *Lilāprasaṅga*, and the philosophical literature of the Ramakrishna Order. His earlier *The Dialectics of Aesthetic Agency* (Bloomsbury 2013), on Coleridge, Hegel, and German Idealism, is the formal training that explains the analytic precision and the comfort with Continental philosophy that runs through his later work. He took sannyāsa under the Ramakrishna Order; the change of name from Maharaj to Medhananda dates from around 2020 and reflects the formal entry into the monastic line. Books and articles published before then carry the name Maharaj; subsequent work carries the name Medhananda. *Infinite Paths to Infinite Reality* (2018) is therefore a Maharaj book; *Swami Vivekananda's Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* (2022) is a Medhananda book; the 2017 Schopenhauer paper is a Maharaj paper. Both names are used in this reader as the publication record requires.

Cross-engagement: Abhinavagupta presses the same epistemic authority axis in the epistemological register, but with the value `scripture-plus-transformative-experience` rather than `comparative-theological-reading` in [primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

This dual identity is not incidental to his authority. The standard objection in academic philosophy of religion to monastic-affiliated work is that doctrinal commitment compromises critical distance. The standard objection from inside the Ramakrishna Order to academic work is that detachment from the spiritual tradition produces philosophically clever but spiritually thin reconstructions. Medhananda is one of a very small number of contemporary scholars who has earned the institutional credentials that block both objections — Berkeley PhD, Oxford Press monographs, peer-reviewed articles in *Philosophy East and West*, *The Monist*, *Religions*, *Journal of Religion*, *Philosophy Compass*, alongside a sannyāsa initiation in the Ramakrishna Order and a permanent academic position at RKMVERI in Belur Math. The cost of this dual position to him personally is a discipline he has imposed on his own writing — careful refusal to inflate Ramakrishna's claims past what the Bengali sources support, careful refusal to deflate them below what the philosophy will defend. This reader treats his work as the model of what serious cross-cultural philosophy of religion can do when it is written by someone with both kinds of training.

**The project — to recover Sri Ramakrishna and Sri Vivekananda as serious philosophical voices in cross-cultural philosophy of religion.** The standard academic reception of Ramakrishna up to about 2010 was either psychobiographical (Kripal's *Kāli's Child* and its Western descendants reading Ramakrishna through psychoanalytic categories) or hagiographical (the Order's own devotional biographies). The standard academic reception of Vivekananda up to about 2015 was either nationalist-political (Vivekananda as ideologue of cultural Hinduism, the line running through Hacker and Halbfass on "Neo-Vedānta") or as a popularizing dilution of Śaṅkarite Advaita. Medhananda's books are aimed at a third option: read Ramakrishna and Vivekananda the way one reads, say, Maimonides or Aquinas — as substantive philosophical voices producing position-statements about real philosophical problems (the infinitude of God, religious diversity, mystical experience, the problem of evil, panpsychism, faith and reason), arguing those positions in dialogue with the philosophical literature of their day, and producing answers that can stand or fall on philosophical grounds. The result is that Ramakrishna's *vijñāna* doctrine is presented as a structured metaphysical position (six tenets, articulated, defended); Ramakrishna's pluralism (*yata mat tata path*) is presented as a structured account of religious diversity (the manifestationist alternative to Hick); Ramakrishna's response to evil is presented as a multi-track theodicy (skeptical theism plus saint-making theodicy plus a panentheistic standpoint of *vijñāna*); and Vivekananda's mature philosophy is presented as "Integral Advaita" or "Cosmopolitan Vedānta" — an articulated position at the level of metaphysics, ethics, religious pluralism, epistemology of mystical experience, faith, and philosophy of mind.

**The central thread for the user's project.** Three of the user's twelve theses run through Medhananda's books as their central argumentative spine.

*T2 — The realist trajectory of Vedānta.* Medhananda's *Religions* 2021 article ("Cutting the Knot of the World Problem") is the strongest contemporary academic case that Aurobindo's three-pronged critique of Śaṅkarite *māyā-vāda* (scriptural, experiential, philosophical) is correct. The 2018 *Infinite Paths* book argues that Ramakrishna's *vijñāna* — the post-*nirvikalpa-samādhi* "intimate knowledge" that Brahman has *become* the universe — is the experiential ground for that realist trajectory. The 2022 *Cosmopolitanism* book argues that Vivekananda's mature philosophy ("Integral Advaita") is the systematic-philosophical extension of Ramakrishna's *vijñāna* into a cosmopolitan-ethical-epistemological frame. The user's T2 trajectory (Bhartṛprapañca → Bhāskara → Madhva → Caitanya → Aurobindo) extends the line further back into the Sanskrit tradition than Medhananda generally goes, and adds Caitanya at primary-source depth where Medhananda's range is limited; but the *direction* of the trajectory — Vedānta's self-correction toward realism, against doctrinaire *māyā-vāda* — is the spine of three of Medhananda's four major works. He is the strongest textual ally on T2.

*T3 — Hierarchy as pedagogical, not metaphysical.* Medhananda's reading of Ramakrishna's *roof-and-stairs* analogy (K 50–51 / G 103–4, *Infinite Paths* p. 28–29) is precisely the user's T3. The *jñānī* reaches the roof by leaving the steps behind, one by one — *neti, neti.* The *vijñānī* discovers that the steps are made of the same materials as the roof. *That which is realized as Brahman through the eliminating process of neti, neti is then found to have become the universe and all its living beings.* The negation is real, the landing abolishes the privilege of the negating standpoint; both are real registers. Medhananda gives this its sustained Bengali-source treatment in chapter 1 of *Infinite Paths.* The user's T3 takes this further by reading the structure as *adhikāra-bheda* (audience-fit) — the climb is for the climber, the landing is for the lander — and by linking it to KCB's standpoint discipline and to Bhattacharyya's "cancellation ≠ falsification."

*T6 — The honey-and-taste-of-honey doctrine; the *paramādvaita* / supramental register.* Ramakrishna's most-quoted aphorism — "do you wish to taste honey, or become honey?" — is read by Medhananda not as a devotional metaphor but as a substantive philosophical claim: both the *advaitic* identity-realization (becoming honey, *jñāna*) and the *bhakti* loving-relation realization (tasting honey, *bhakti*) are real registers that culminate in *vijñāna*. The locus classicus is at K 288 / G 305, quoted verbatim by Maharaj at *Infinite Paths* p. 80:

> "The whole thing is to love God and taste His sweetness [*mādhurya*]. God is sweetness [*rasa*] and the bhakta is its enjoyer [*rasika*]. The bhakta drinks the sweet Bliss of God. Further, God is the lotus and the bhakta the bee. The bhakta sips the honey of the lotus. As a bhakta cannot live without God, so also God cannot live without His bhakta. Then the bhakta becomes the sweetness, and God its enjoyer. The bhakta becomes the lotus, and God the bee. It is the Godhead that has become these two in order to enjoy Its own Bliss. That is the significance of the episode of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa."
>
> — *Kathāmṛta* 288 / *Gospel* 305, quoted *Infinite Paths* p. 80.

Maharaj's gloss is decisive (*Infinite Paths* pp. 80–81): "Sri Ramakrishna goes much further than Marion in thinking through the logic of God as *agape* . . . Kṛṣṇa becomes His Lover Rādhā in order to enjoy His own bliss." And: "Sri Ramakrishna adopts the paraconsistent view that God is both the personal God of *agape* and the impersonal nondual Absolute" (p. 81). The doctrinal content the user takes from this is the *self-relation of the Real* — *the Godhead has become these two in order to enjoy Its own Bliss*. Both becoming-honey and tasting-honey are real registers of the Real's own self-enjoyment. The user's T6 ("paramādvaita / supramental as non-duality of duality and non-duality") is the more systematic articulation of the same content, integrated with McGilchrist's right-hemispheric "knowledge-by-identity" and Aurobindo's triple-status of the Supermind.

The user's interest in Medhananda, then, is precise: his two OUP books are the strongest contemporary academic articulation of much of what the user's thesis needs to say at the level of Ramakrishna-and-Vivekananda interpretation. The user can cite Medhananda for T2, T3, T6, and important parts of T1, T4, T9, T11, T12. The user extends past Medhananda — and this reader marks the extensions clearly — on T7 (the *bhāvarūpa-avidyā* + Trika *svātantrya* + Madhusūdana scaffolding for the realist reading of *māyā*), on the Sanskrit-tradition depth of T2 (Madhva-as-register, primary-source Caitanya, Vivaraṇa school), on the directive turn (T5, T8, T10), on McGilchrist / Gebser / Deleuze / Prigogine / Whitehead constellation, and on the political-theoretical extension via *matsya-nyāya* and Graeber.

## Glossary (terms used through the reader)

*Vijñāna* (Ramakrishna's Bengali usage; the central technical term of the 2018 book): the post-*nirvikalpa-samādhi* "intimate knowledge" of God as the Infinite Reality that is both personal and impersonal, with form and without form, immanent and transcendent. Distinguished sharply from *jñāna* in Ramakrishna's idiom — the latter reaches the formless Brahman; the former returns and finds that the formless Brahman has become the universe. Medhananda uses *vijñāna* and *bhāva-mukha* (the state of "being at the threshold" between the absolute and the empirical, or between the formless and form) interchangeably, following Ramakrishna's own usage at K 266 / G 287.

Cross-engagement: Akshapada Gautama presses the same method axis in the methodological register, but with the value `formal-proof-or-inference` rather than `comparative-reading` in [primary text](cite://aksapada/nyaya-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

*Vijñāna Vedānta* (Maharaj's coinage, *Infinite Paths* p. 17, ch. 1): the systematic philosophical position Maharaj reconstructs from Ramakrishna's *Kathāmṛta* on the basis of *vijñāna*. Six tenets, walked in detail in §I.1 of this reader.

*Integral Advaita* (Medhananda's term, *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* 2022, ch. 1–2): the systematic philosophical position Medhananda reconstructs from Vivekananda's mature lectures (1894–1901). Closely parallel to *Vijñāna Vedānta* in content; the distinct term is used in the Vivekananda book partly because Vivekananda himself did not use *vijñāna* as Ramakrishna did.

*Manifestationism* (Maharaj's coinage, *Infinite Paths* ch. 5): Ramakrishna's epistemology of mystical experience — mystics in different traditions experience genuinely different but real *manifestations* of one Infinite Reality. Distinguished from perennialism (all mystical experiences are at root the same; Stace, Forman) and constructivism (all mystical experiences are conditioned by tradition; Katz, Proudfoot).

*Paraconsistent God* (the move at *Infinite Paths* ch. 2, dialoguing with Göcke): Ramakrishna's Infinite Reality holds apparently contradictory predicates simultaneously — personal *and* impersonal, with form *and* without form, immanent *and* transcendent. The contradiction is internal to the finite intellect; the paraconsistent register is *vijñāna*.

*Adhikāra-bheda*: difference of qualification or audience-fit. Not used systematically by Medhananda himself; used in this reader to translate his structural moves into the user's idiom.

*Cosmopolitan Vedānta* (Medhananda's preferred term in 2022): Vivekananda's mature philosophy, characterized as a Vedānta articulated in dialogue with global thinkers and traditions, "rooted" in indigenous Indian sources but cosmopolitan in critical assimilation. Drawn from K. C. Bhattacharyya's *Svaraj in Ideas* and Jonardon Ganeri's "immersive cosmopolitanism."

*Iśvara-koṭi* / *jīva-koṭi* (Ramakrishna's distinction, *Infinite Paths* ch. 1 §IV, K 800 / G 749, K 505 / G 500): "incarnations of God and those born as a part of one of these incarnations" who can return from *nirvikalpa-samādhi* to teach, distinguished from "ordinary souls" (*jīvakoṭis*) who cannot. The classical-philosophical analog Medhananda points to is the *adhikārika* of late Vedānta and the *prakṛti-līna puruṣa* of Sāṃkhya (Saradananda's parallel, LP II.1.71–73).

*Yata mata tata patha* ("as many minds, so many paths"): Ramakrishna's famous pluralist saying. Medhananda's central interpretive claim is that this is *not* generic religious tolerance and *not* Hickian noumenon-pluralism but a structured "manifestationist" position grounded in the infinitude of God.

*K* / *G*: Bengali / English citation system Maharaj/Medhananda uses throughout for the *Kathāmṛta*. K = page in *Śrīśrīrāmakṛṣṇakathāmṛta* (one-volume Udbodhan ed.); G = page in Swami Nikhilananda's English translation, *The Gospel of Sri Ramakrishna*. *LP* / *DP* — analogous for Saradananda's *Lilāprasaṅga* / *Sri Ramakrishna and His Divine Play*. *CW* — *Complete Works of Swami Vivekananda*; *BCW* — Bengali *Vāṇī o Racanā*.

## Part I — *Infinite Paths to Infinite Reality* (Oxford UP 2018)

The 2018 book is the formal entry point. Eight chapters, four parts (Divine Infinitude; Religious Pluralism; Mystical Experience; Problem of Evil), 350 pages, more than 100 page-citations to the *Kathāmṛta* in the Bengali plus the English. The book is constructed in two registers simultaneously: an exegetical reconstruction of Ramakrishna's positions from the Bengali Bengali sources (the *Kathāmṛta* and Saradananda's *Lilāprasaṅga*), and a cross-cultural philosophy-of-religion engagement bringing those reconstructed positions into dialogue with — chapter by chapter — Śaṅkara, Rāmānuja, Viśvanātha Cakravartin, Duns Scotus, Nicholas of Cusa, Benedikt Paul Göcke, Jean-Luc Marion, John Hick, Walter Stace, Steven Katz, Robert Forman, William Alston, William Rowe, and Paul Knitter. The book's overall argument is that Ramakrishna's *vijñāna* doctrine is not a piece of devotional Bengali piety but a structured philosophical position with the technical resources to address central problems in contemporary cross-cultural philosophy of religion. The user can take that argument as essentially established at the analytic-philosophical level and build on it.

Cross-engagement: Adi Sankara presses the same truth-structure axis in the epistemological register, but with the value `hierarchical-standpoint-truth` rather than `context-indexed-without-final-hierarchy` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

### The methodological postulates — five interpretive principles

Chapter 1 §II lays down the five interpretive principles Maharaj uses to reconstruct Ramakrishna's philosophy from the *Kathāmṛta*. They are not merely technical exegetical principles; each blocks a standard reductive misreading. The principles are: (1) Distinguish Ramakrishna's own statements from his disciples' interpretive paraphrases; rely primarily on the former. (2) Read each statement in its specific context — to whom Ramakrishna was speaking, and on what occasion — because Ramakrishna explicitly tailored statements to the *adhikāra* of his audience. (3) Where Ramakrishna's statements appear to conflict, look for higher-order principles that resolve the conflict; do not flatten the conflict by reducing one statement to a sectarian framework. (4) Read Ramakrishna's analogies for their structural content, not as decoration. (5) Identify Ramakrishna's mature standpoint by reference to his self-descriptions, especially the self-identification as *vijñānī*.

Cross-engagement: Baladeva Vidyabhushana presses the same perspectivism axis in the epistemological register, but with the value `partial-perspectives-ranked` rather than `irreducible-true-perspectives` in [primary text](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

Principles (2) and (3) deserve underlining for the user's project. Principle (2) is *adhikāra-bheda* in academic-exegetical clothing. Principle (3) is the structural move that drives the entire book: where readers like Ghanananda, Ashokananda, and Omkarānanda flatten Ramakrishna into Śaṅkarite Advaita, and where readers like Kripal flatten him into Śākta-tantrism, Maharaj reads the apparent conflicts as evidence that *neither* sectarian flattening is correct — Ramakrishna's mature standpoint of *vijñāna* is a position that *ontologically integrates* the apparently conflicting strains. The user's T1 (register thesis) and T3 (hierarchy as pedagogical, not metaphysical) are doing the same structural work in their own idiom.

### The six tenets of Vijñāna Vedānta — the central reconstruction

Chapter 1 §III formally reconstructs Ramakrishna's position as six numbered tenets. This is the book's central argumentative structure. The user's T2/T3/T6 lift this reconstruction directly. Walked here, with Maharaj's verbatim statement of each tenet and the Bengali-source page-citation that grounds it.

**VV1 — The two-states thesis.** "After attaining brahmajñāna in nirvikalpa samādhi, ordinary people leave their body within twenty-one days, but certain divinely commissioned people known as īśvarakoṭis are able to return from the state of nirvikalpa samādhi and attain vijñāna — a spiritual state even greater than brahmajñāna — in which perfect jñāna and perfect bhakti are combined" (*Infinite Paths* p. 27).

The Bengali ground for VV1, quoted by Maharaj from K 505 / G 500:

> "When the kuṇḍalinī rises to the *sahasrāra* and the mind is absorbed in *samādhi*, outward awareness falls away. The body can no longer be kept going; if milk is put into the mouth, it spills back out. In that condition death comes within twenty-one days. But the *īśvarakoṭis* — the divine class to which the avatāras belong — can descend again from *samādhi*. They return because they delight in the company of devotees and in tasting God's love. God leaves in them the 'I of knowledge' or the 'I of devotion' so that they may instruct others. Their mind moves between the sixth plane and the seventh, as though racing a boat back and forth between the two."
>
> — *Kathāmṛta* 505 / *Gospel* 500, quoted *Infinite Paths* p. 28.

The structural import. The two-states thesis is not, primarily, a pious distinction between ordinary souls and Incarnations. It is the *operative existence-claim* on which the rest of *Vijñāna Vedānta* rests: there is a *post-nirvikalpa* register, lived after the absolute realization, in which the realiser shuttles between the *nitya* (the Eternal — *nirguṇa* Brahman) and the *līlā* (the Play — *saguṇa* manifestation), and from this register *both* registers are seen as the same Reality. Whether one wishes to call this register a "higher" state than *brahmajñāna* (Maharaj himself avoids the word "higher," see his footnote 40 on p. 30) or a *more comprehensive* state, the philosophical point stands: *nirvikalpa samādhi* is not the terminus of the path but a passage through which the *iśvara-koṭi* moves into *vijñāna*. This is — exactly — what the user's T3 calls the *roof-and-stairs landed* and what T6 calls the *paramādvaita* / supramental register.

The roof-and-stairs analogy itself, K 50–51 / G 103–4, paraphrased *Infinite Paths* p. 28–29:

> "The *jñānī* reaches Brahman by stripping away identification with worldly things through 'Not this, not this'; it is like climbing step by step until one reaches the roof. But the *vijñānī*, being more deeply familiar with Him, sees further: the roof and the staircase are made of the same brick, lime, and brick-dust. What was first realized as Brahman by negation is then seen to have become the universe and living beings. The *vijñānī* sees that the one who is *nirguṇa* is also *saguṇa*. No one can stay on the roof indefinitely; he comes back down. So too those who realize Brahman in *samādhi* return and see that Brahman alone has become the world and its creatures. This is what is called *vijñāna*."
>
> — *Kathāmṛta* 50–51 / *Gospel* 103–4, quoted *Infinite Paths* p. 28–29.

This is the locus classicus for the user's T3. The key Bengali clause is *kintu vijñānī jini viśeṣarūpe tāhār saṅge ālāp karen tini āro kichu darśan karen* — "but the *vijñānī*, who is more intimately acquainted with Him, sees something more." The word *ālāp* (intimate acquaintance, conversation) does the work of distinguishing *jñāna* (which knows Brahman as one's own self) from *vijñāna* (which knows Brahman as both the formless ground *and* as having become everything). Note also: the analogy is *bidirectional*. The roof and the stairs are made of the same materials. *Neither* the climb-by-negation nor the descent-into-affirmation is privileged; both are real, and the *vijñānī* lives the truth of both at once. The user's T3 reads the analogy precisely this way: the climb requires negation; the landing abolishes the privilege of the negating standpoint.

**VV2 — Suprarational realism about *vijñāna*.** "Since the rational intellect is inherently limited, spiritual experience is the only reliable basis for arriving at supersensuous spiritual truths. On the suprarational basis of *vijñāna*, we can affirm truths about God that appear to be contradictory or illogical to the rational intellect" (*Infinite Paths* p. 31).

Maharaj reads Ramakrishna here as committed to a "Hamiltonian pessimism" about reason, citing Ramakrishna's enthusiastic endorsement of William Hamilton's "learned ignorance is the end of philosophy and the beginning of religion" (K 255 / G 278). The point is not that reason is worthless; it is that reason is *constitutively limited* — Ramakrishna's "one-seer pot cannot hold ten seers of milk" (K 229 / G 257) — and that the *vijñānī*'s standpoint provides experiential access to truths the rational intellect cannot accommodate without contradiction. Maharaj's gloss:

> "Sri Ramakrishna's enthusiastic approval of Hamilton's statement about 'learned ignorance' and his own frequent teachings on the limitations of the rational intellect suggest that he shares Hamilton's metaphilosophical pessimism about reason… For Sri Ramakrishna, spiritual truths that might seem contradictory or illogical to the rational intellect are validated on the experiential basis of vijñāna. As we will see in chapter 2, this aspect of Sri Ramakrishna's thought bears striking affinities with the views of the contemporary theologian Benedikt Paul Göcke, who claims that God can possess various attributes and aspects that appear contradictory to the finite human mind."
>
> — *Infinite Paths* p. 32.

VV2 is the methodological license on which VV3–VV6 are then asserted. This matters for the user. VV2 is *not* anti-rationalism; it is *register-discipline applied to the question of what kinds of truth are accessible to which faculties* — exactly the user's T1 register thesis read upward into philosophy of religion. The *vijñānī*'s suprarational seeing is not a refusal of reason; it is the fact that the *paraconsistent* truth of God-as-personal-and-impersonal is *legible at one register and illegible at another*. The user's methodology §3 (standpoint discipline) does identical work.

**VV3 — Paraconsistent infinitude of God.** "The Infinite Divine Reality is both personal and impersonal, both with and without form, both immanent in the universe and beyond it, and much more besides" (*Infinite Paths* p. 33).

The Bengali grammatical move here is itself the philosophical move. Maharaj observes (p. 34) that Ramakrishna's frequent locution is the *relative-correlative* construction inherited from Sanskrit — *jini saguṇ, tini nirguṇ* ("That which is *saguṇa* is also *nirguṇa*"); *jini brahma, tini bhagavān* ("That which is Brahman is also Bhagavān"); *jini brahma, tini śakti* ("That which is Brahman is also Śakti"); *jāri rūp, tini arūp* ("That which has form is also without form"); *jāri nitya, tāhāri līlā* ("The *līlā* belongs to That to which the *nitya* belongs"). The grammatical subject is left *unnamed*. What is asserted is that *the same Reality* bears both predicates. Ramakrishna goes further: *tini sākār, nirākār, ābār kato ki* — "He is with form, without form, and much more besides" (K 602 / G 577). The "much more besides" is doctrinally load-bearing. Ramakrishna's God is not just paraconsistently *binary* (saguṇa-nirguṇa); the infinitude is technical — there are *infinitely many* genuine self-presentations of the Real, and the binary is only the most-mentioned pair.

The ocean-and-ice analogy, K 861 / G 802, *Infinite Paths* p. 36:

> "The *bhaktas* — and the *vijñānīs* as well — accept both the impersonal and the personal God, God without form and God with form. Think of a shoreless ocean, an endless expanse of water, in which masses of ice appear here and there under intense cold. In the same way, under the cooling power of *bhakti*, the Infinite shows itself to the devotee in form. Then, when the sun of knowledge rises, those masses melt again, and only the boundless sea remains."

Maharaj's key interpretive move (p. 36–37): *do not* read the analogy Advaitically — that is, do not read the "melting" of the ice as the sublation of the personal into the impersonal. Read it from the *vijñānī*'s standpoint: ice and ocean are equally water, the freezing and the melting are equally real modalities, and what *vijñāna* sees is that the same Reality undergoes both. Maharaj nails this interpretation by appealing to Ramakrishna's own December 27 1883 explanation of the same analogy at K 364 / G 370: *jāri nirākār, tāri sākār* — "form and formlessness belong to one and the same Reality." That clause is decisive against the Advaitic interpretation.

The user's T1 / T6 / T9 architecture is precisely this paraconsistent reading.

**VV4 — Two levels of Advaitic realization.** "There are two levels of Advaitic realization: while the jñānī realizes the acosmic nondual reality of nirguṇa Brahman in nirvikalpa samādhi, the vijñānī returns from the state of nirvikalpa samādhi and attains the richer, world-affirming nondual realization that God has become everything" (*Infinite Paths* p. 39).

This is the cleanest formulation of the user's T2/T3 in Maharaj's idiom. *Both* states are Advaitic realizations. The *jñānī*'s realization is *acosmic* — only *nirguṇa* Brahman is perceived; *jīva*, *jagat*, *Īśvara* are not perceived. The *vijñānī*'s realization is *world-affirming* — Brahman has *become* the universe; *jīva*, *jagat*, *Īśvara* are seen as the Real itself in differentiated form. Maharaj's gloss, *Infinite Paths* p. 41:

> "Sri Ramakrishna's Vijñāna Vedānta, then, is a world-affirming Advaitic philosophy that contrasts sharply with Śaṅkara's world-denying Advaita Vedānta. For Śaṅkara, the sole reality is the impersonal nondual Brahman, so jīva, jagat, and īśvara are all unreal. For Sri Ramakrishna, by contrast, the sole reality is the Infinite Divine Reality, which is equally the impersonal Brahman and the personal Śakti. Unlike Śaṅkara, Sri Ramakrishna maintains that both jīva and jagat are real manifestations of Śakti, which is itself an ontologically real aspect of the Infinite Reality."
>
> — *Infinite Paths* p. 41.

The user accepts every line of this *as a statement about doctrinaire Śaṅkarism*. The user's nuance: Maharaj treats Śaṅkara as *one* well-defined position, against which Ramakrishna's position is set. The user's T1 register thesis adds: Śaṅkara's two-level *vyāvahārika*-*pāramārthika* scheme, *correctly read* with *adhikāra*-discipline, is itself a register-distinction not an ontological hierarchy. The user is therefore *not* committed to opposing Śaṅkara to Ramakrishna in the way Maharaj does; the user is committed to opposing *doctrinaire Śaṅkara-as-flattener* to a register-disciplined Śaṅkara that is structurally compatible with Ramakrishna's *vijñāna*. This is a real divergence; it is named in §V below.

The Bengali-source ground for VV4 is the *jñānī* / *vijñānī* contrast Ramakrishna repeats in many places. K 247 / G 271–72:

> "Who is the highest devotee of God, the *uttam bhakta*? The one who, after realizing Brahman, sees that God alone has become all beings, the universe, and the twenty-four cosmic principles. At first one reasons, 'Not this, not this,' and reaches the roof. Afterwards one sees that the staircase is made of the very same materials as the roof: brick, lime, and brick-dust. The devotee realizes that Brahman alone has become all this: living beings, the universe, everything. Dry reasoning by itself? I spit on it; I want none of it."

That spitting-on-mere-reasoning gesture is doctrinally significant. It is not anti-philosophical; it is anti-*deflationary*-philosophical. *Vijñāna* is the standpoint from which dry *neti, neti* alone is exposed as inadequate to the Real. The user's T6 (the supramental register that is non-duality of duality and non-duality) is doing the same work in Aurobindonian-McGilchrist idiom.

**VV5 — Multiple modes of relation with God.** "The vijñānī, who accepts the reality of both the nitya and the līlā, is able to adopt various attitudes toward — and attain various forms of union with — God on different planes of consciousness, all of which are true" (*Infinite Paths* p. 41).

The classical *bhāva* taxonomy from Vaiṣṇava aesthetics — *śānta* (peaceful), *dāsya* (servant), *sakhya* (friend), *vātsalya* (parent), *madhura* (lover) — is what Ramakrishna draws on. The *vijñānī* is the practitioner who realizes that all of these *bhāvas* are real *and* that *advaitic* identity-realization is real. K 1098–99 / G 1009–10, quoted *Infinite Paths* p. 42:

> "Why should I produce only a monotone when I have an instrument with seven holes? Why should I say nothing but, 'I am He, I am He'? I want to play various melodies on my instrument with seven holes. Why should I say only, 'Brahman! Brahman!'? I want to call on God through all the moods — through śānta, dāsya, sakhya, vātsalya, and madhura. I want to make merry with God. I want to sport with God."

The flute-with-seven-holes analogy is the user's T5/T12 in image-form: the directive question (what to aim at) is settled by the *vijñānī* not by elimination but by *plurality of modality*. Ramakrishna's own self-attestation, K 594 / G 638, quoted *Infinite Paths* p. 43:

> "I have come to the final realization that God is the Whole and I am a part of Him, that God is the Master and I am His servant. Also, I think every now and then that He is and I am He."

The Hanuman verse Ramakrishna repeatedly invokes — *dehabuddhyā dāso'ham, jīvabuddhyā tvadaṃśakaḥ; ātmabuddhyā tvamevāham iti me niścitā matiḥ* — "When I identify with the body, I am Your servant; when I identify with the *jīvātman*, I am a part of You; when I identify with the Supreme Ātman, I am You" — is, on Maharaj's reading, *not* an Advaitic ranking (with the third clause as the only -true one) but an *iśvara-koṭi*'s declaration that *all three modes* of relation are real and lived simultaneously by the *vijñānī*.

The user's T6 takes this further by linking it to the right-hemispheric "knowledge by identity" toward which McGilchrist's Western trajectory points and to Aurobindo's triple-status of the Supermind. Maharaj does not make those linkages.

**VV6 — Manifestationist religious pluralism.** "Various religious faiths and spiritual philosophies are salvifically efficacious paths to realizing God" (*Infinite Paths* p. 44).

VV6 is the *yata mata tata patha* tenet. Crucially, on Maharaj's reading, VV6 follows from VV3 — since God is paraconsistently infinite (personal *and* impersonal *and* much more besides), there must be correspondingly infinite paths. Ramakrishna's saying *tini ananta, patho ananta* — "He is infinite, the paths are infinite" (K 511 / G 506) — is therefore not generic religious tolerance but a *technical inference* from the infinitude of the Real. Maharaj's argumentative move (chapters 3 and 4): this distinguishes Ramakrishna's pluralism from Hick's quasi-Kantian noumenon-pluralism (in which the personal and impersonal ultimates of various religions are different *phenomenal* responses to a strictly unknowable noumenon). Ramakrishna's pluralism is *manifestationist*: each religion's ultimate is a real *manifestation* of the Infinite Reality, ontologically real *as* what it is.

This is a substantive philosophical position, and it is the user's T1 / T4 / T12 in cross-cultural philosophy of religion idiom.

### Why the *Infinite Paths* argument matters for the user's T2

The *Infinite Paths* argument is the analytic-philosophical defense of a *register-distinction-tolerant* Vedānta. The book's strongest single move is the *manifestationist* alternative to Hickian pluralism (chapters 3–4). Hick's mature 1989 position — articulated in *An Interpretation of Religion* — distinguishes the "Real-an-sich" (a Kantian noumenon, strictly unknowable) from the various "personae" (personal divine images: Yahweh, Allah, Vishnu) and "impersonae" (impersonal divine images: Brahman, Tao, Dharmakāya) that different religious traditions take to be ultimate. On Hick's view, the personae and impersonae are *phenomenal* projections; only the Real-an-sich is real. Maharaj's critique is precise:

> "Between 1970 and 1974, the early Hick espoused a Vedāntic theory of religious pluralism — based explicitly on Sri Aurobindo's 'logic of the infinite' — that comes remarkably close to Sri Ramakrishna's *vijñāna*-based pluralist model. According to the early Hick, the Infinite Divine Reality is both personal and impersonal, even though our finite minds are unable to grasp how this is possible. The early Hick derived a robust model of religious pluralism from this Aurobindonian premise of unfathomable divine infinitude: since each religion captures at least one true aspect of the Infinite Reality, the various conceptions of the Divine Reality taught by the major world religions are complementary rather than conflicting. By 1976, however, Hick abandoned this Aurobindonian line of thought in favor of his now well-known quasi-Kantian theory of religious pluralism, according to which the personal and nonpersonal ultimates of the various world religions are different phenomenal forms of the same unknowable 'Real an sich.' As numerous critics have pointed out, Hick's quasi-Kantian pluralist model fails to honor the self-understanding of most religious practitioners, who take their respective ultimates to be literally, and not merely phenomenally, true. On this basis, I argue that Sri Ramakrishna's *vijñāna*-based model of religious pluralism, which grants full ontological reality to the personal and nonpersonal ultimates of the various religions, is more robust and philosophically viable than Hick's."
>
> — *Infinite Paths* pp. 7–8 (Introduction summary of ch. 4).

Maharaj's archeology of the *early* Hick (1970–1974) is even more striking: the early Hick — pre-Kantian-turn — had explicitly drawn on Sri Aurobindo's "logic of the infinite" to defend the very position Maharaj now defends from Ramakrishna. Hick subsequently abandoned this position for the Kantian-noumenon position which is now standard. Maharaj's argument: Hick made a wrong turn; the *Aurobindonian* (and *vijñāna-Vedāntic*) road is the better road; the Real *is* knowable in its various manifestations because the manifestations *are* the Real in differentiated form. This is the user's T1/T4 in cross-cultural philosophy of religion idiom.

The further verbatim from chapter 5, on manifestationism vs perennialism vs constructivism, *Infinite Paths* p. 8:

> "After identifying the strengths and limitations of these two positions, I argue that Sri Ramakrishna champions what I call a 'manifestationist' approach to mystical experience that provides a compelling dialectical alternative to both perennialism and constructivism. In Sri Ramakrishna's view, 'God manifests Himself in the form which His devotee loves most' (K 101 / G 149–50). From the standpoint of *vijñāna*, Sri Ramakrishna asserts that mystics in various traditions experience different real manifestations of the same impersonal-personal Infinite Reality. Accordingly, while Sri Ramakrishna agrees with the constructivist view that a mystic's background beliefs are reflected in his or her mystical experiences, he rejects the epistemic subjectivism of constructivists like Katz and Hick. At the same time, Sri Ramakrishna takes the Infinite Reality to be the common ontological object of all mystical experience, but he rejects the reductive perennialist view that all mystical experiences are phenomenologically identical."
>
> — *Infinite Paths* p. 8.

This is the structural anti-correlationist move at the level of mystical experience: the manifestations *are* the Real, not phenomenal projections of an unknowable noumenon. The user's T1 register thesis takes this as the cross-religious version of *adhikāra-bheda* — the experience differs because the *register of access* differs, and the registers are themselves real registers of the Real, not subjective constructions.

### Manifestationism on mystical experience — chapter 5

Chapter 5 develops the *Infinite Paths* model for mystical experience itself. Two positions dominate the contemporary debate. *Perennialism* (Stace, Forman): all mystical experiences are at root the same; the differences across traditions are interpretive, not phenomenal. *Constructivism* (Katz, Proudfoot): all mystical experiences are constructed by tradition; there is no "raw" mystical experience. Maharaj's *manifestationist* alternative: mystics in different traditions have *genuinely different but real* experiences of *different aspects or manifestations* of the same Infinite Reality. Hindu mystics realizing *nirguṇa* Brahman, Christian mystics realizing the Trinity, Sūfī mystics realizing *Allāh as al-Ḥaqq*, Buddhist mystics realizing *śūnyatā* — these are not the same experience under different interpretations (perennialism), nor different experiences with no common referent (constructivism), but genuinely different experiences of *real, distinct manifestations* of the one Real.

The position is grounded in Ramakrishna's own multi-religious *sādhanā*. Ramakrishna practiced Hindu *bhakti* in five *bhāvas*; he practiced classical Tantric *sādhanā*; he practiced Vedantic *advaita-jñāna* under Tota Puri; he practiced Christian devotional *sādhanā* and reported a vision of Christ; he practiced Islamic *sādhanā* under a Sufi guide and reported the vision of "a radiant person with grave countenance and a long beard." These practices were not, on Ramakrishna's own account, the same experience under different cultural masks; they were *different experiences* of the *same Infinite Reality* in *different manifestations*. The Bengali source for the multi-religious *sādhanā* is Saradananda's *Lilāprasaṅga*, treated in *Infinite Paths* chapter 1 §I and chapter 5.

The user's T1 (register thesis) and T4 (all darśanas truly access the Real) are doing structurally identical work. Maharaj's *manifestationism* is the user's *manifestationism* in cross-cultural philosophy of religion vocabulary. Note especially that *manifestationism* refuses the standard Western dichotomy of perennialism-vs-constructivism — the user's structural opponent (postmodern relativism) is *constructivism* under another name; the user's structural opponent inside Indian thought (sentimentalist universalism — "all paths the same") is *perennialism* under another name. *Manifestationism* refuses both, exactly as the user does.

### The problem of evil — chapters 7 and 8

The fourth part of *Infinite Paths* takes Ramakrishna's response to the problem of evil and reconstructs it as a multi-track theodicy. Three components.

(a) *Skeptical theism.* Following Alston and Wykstra, Ramakrishna grants that finite minds cannot rationally see why an omnipotent, omnibenevolent God permits evil — but this epistemic limitation does not constitute evidence *against* God's existence. The Bengali-source analogy is the *one-seer pot*: a finite mind cannot hold infinite content. (b) *Saint-making theodicy.* Ramakrishna's positive claim: the world is structured to produce *saints* — that is, beings capable of God-realization — and the experiential conditions necessary for such production include the possibility of suffering, misuse of agency, and pedagogical bondage. This is structurally similar to John Hick's "soul-making" theodicy but, Maharaj argues, more philosophically defensible because it is grounded in the wider ontology of *vijñāna*. (c) *The panentheistic standpoint of vijñāna.* The deepest move: from the *vijñānī*'s standpoint, the apparent evil of the world is itself part of the *līlā* — the Infinite Reality's own self-presentation — and is *not* the gratuitous evil that the standard problem of evil presupposes. This is the *only* part of the theodicy that requires the full *vijñāna* register; the other two (a), (b) are accessible to the rational intellect.

The user's T7 (*divine play through willful self-ignorance*) goes one step further than (c). T7 reads *māyā* itself — the cosmic ignorance that makes finitude possible — as a *positive expression* of the Real's own *svātantrya* (sovereign freedom), not as a defect to be explained away. *Infinite Paths* chapter 7 stops short of this move; the panentheistic standpoint of *vijñāna* is invoked as a *standpoint* from which evil is differently seen, but the *ontological* reading of *māyā* as a positive divine self-veiling is not developed. The user's T7 develops it through *bhāvarūpa-avidyā* (Madhusūdana) + Trika *svātantrya* (Utpaladeva, Abhinavagupta) + Vivaraṇa school (Brahman as both *āśraya* and *viṣaya* of avidyā) — a Sanskrit-tradition apparatus that Maharaj does not engage. The *direction* of T7 is structurally identical to the *Infinite Paths* (c) move; the Sanskrit-source apparatus is the user's contribution.

### Beyond Neo-Vedānta — the meta-historical move

Chapter 1 §IV ("Beyond Neo-Vedānta: Implications of Sri Ramakrishna's Philosophy of Vijñāna for Discourse on Modern Vedānta") is a polemic against the Halbfass / Hacker / Fort framework that sorts modern Indian thinkers into the catch-all category "Neo-Vedānta." Maharaj's three reasons against the category: (1) it is too vague to capture the philosophical differences between Ramakrishna, Vivekananda, Aurobindo, Radhakrishnan; (2) it implies a *novelty* or *deviation* from traditional Vedānta that is descriptively wrong — Ramakrishna, Vivekananda, and Aurobindo all explicitly read themselves as *recovering* the original Vedānta of the Upaniṣads and the Gītā, not as creating something new; (3) the Hacker-Halbfass usage is normatively loaded with the suggestion that "Neo-Vedānta" is a corruption of Śaṅkarite Vedānta under Western (typically Christian, post-Schopenhauerian) influence.

This polemic matters for the user's T2. The user's T2 is *exactly* the line Maharaj is defending here: Vedānta is *not* a Śaṅkarite stable point against which everything subsequent is "Neo-Vedānta"; it is a *living trajectory* with multiple branches, of which Bhartṛprapañca-Bhāskara-Madhva-Caitanya-Aurobindo is one. Maharaj's chapter 1 §IV is the academic argument that the user can cite for this; the user extends it backward into the Sanskrit tradition (Bhartṛprapañca, Madhva at primary-source depth) and forward into Aurobindo's Realistic Adwaita.

### Where *Infinite Paths* stops short — a partial but real list

(i) The book does not engage Madhusūdana Sarasvatī. *Advaita-siddhi* defending Advaita against Madhva, and *Bhakti-rasāyana* defending bhakti's autonomy *within* Advaita, are the proof-texts that classical Advaita already contains the bhakti-affirming move Maharaj treats as unique to *Vijñāna Vedānta*. (ii) The book treats Śaṅkara's *vyāvahārika*-*pāramārthika* as a doctrinal *demotion* of the personal God. The user's register-discipline reading (T1, methodology §3) reads it as standpoint-grammar, not ontological hierarchy — the *pāramārthika* claim is a *limit-claim about independent secondness*, not a denial that the personal God is real *as God*. Maharaj does not address this distinction. (iii) The book treats Madhva briefly as a "Tattva-vāda (Dvaita)" position contrasted with Ramakrishna's *vijñāna* (e.g. *Infinite Paths* chapter 2 in the dialogue with Viśvanātha Cakravartin). Madhva's *Tattvavāda* — explicitly not a flattening exonym — and Madhva's role in the user's T2 trajectory (as a register-corrective against doctrinaire *māyā-vāda*) is not developed. (iv) Caitanya at primary-source depth (Jīva Gosvāmin's *Sandarbhas*, Baladeva's *Govinda-Bhāṣya*) is not engaged; Caitanya appears in chapter 2 only through Viśvanātha Cakravartin. (v) The Aurobindonian apparatus is invoked but not foregrounded; *Infinite Paths* is a Ramakrishna book, with Aurobindo as a corroborator. The full Aurobindonian *involution-evolution-supramental* architecture is developed in Maharaj's later articles (the 2021 *Religions* paper and the 2022 *Monist* paper, treated in §IV below).

These are not failures of the book; they are the boundaries of its scope. The user's task is to fill them in, with the user's own apparatus.

## Part II — *Swami Vivekananda's Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* (Oxford UP 2022)

The 2022 book extends the *Vijñāna Vedānta* line forward into Vivekananda. Ten chapters, four parts (Integral Advaita; Experiential Basis of Religion; Faith and Reason; Consciousness), 433 pages. The book's overall argument is that Vivekananda's mature philosophy, far from being an ethically-tinged dilution of Śaṅkarite Advaita or a Hindu-nationalist apologetic, is a structured cosmopolitan philosophical position — *Integral Advaita* — that systematically extends Ramakrishna's *vijñāna* into a substantive metaphysics, ethics, religious-pluralist epistemology, philosophy of mind, and faith-and-reason discussion. The book is the strongest contemporary academic case against (a) the Hacker / Halbfass "Neo-Vedānta" framing, (b) the Hindu-nationalist appropriation of Vivekananda, and (c) the dilution-of-Advaita reading. The user's project takes Medhananda's reconstruction of Vivekananda as essentially settled and moves to questions Medhananda does not address.

### The hermeneutic / methodological frame — "immersive cosmopolitanism"

Chapter 1 of the book opens by drawing on K. C. Bhattacharyya's *Svaraj in Ideas* (1928) and on Jonardon Ganeri's reading of Bhattacharyya's "immersive cosmopolitanism." The Vivekananda Medhananda is reconstructing is *not* an unrooted-cosmopolitan ("citizen of the world" in the thin sense) and *not* a parochial Indian nationalist; he is the *immersive cosmopolitan* who combines reverential immersion in the indigenous tradition with active critical assimilation of foreign ideas. Medhananda's own voice on this:

> "Vivekananda, I would argue, both explicitly theorized as well as practiced the kind of immersive philosophical cosmopolitanism that Bhattacharyya would champion decades later. In 1895 letters to his disciple Alasinga Perumal, Vivekananda affirmed that 'truth is my God, the universe my country' (CW 5:92), and that 'I belong as much to India as to the world, no humbug about that' (CW 5:95). Similarly, in an 1897 letter to Mary Hale, he gave voice to his self-consciously cosmopolitan identity: 'What am I? Asiatic, European, or American? I feel a curious medley of personalities in me' (CW 8:395)."
>
> — *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 5 (Introduction).

Medhananda's verbatim explication of Bhattacharyya's *Svaraj in Ideas* (*Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* pp. 4–6) is itself the model of immersive method. Three features:

> "First, Bhattacharyya stresses the need for *reverential immersion* in one's own indigenous tradition, calling on his Indian philosophical peers to 'resolutely think in our own concepts' ([1928] 2011: 110). He contrasts this conception of an immersive cosmopolitanism with a radically unrooted cosmopolitanism that is equally critical of *all* traditions, including one's own. For Bhattacharyya, this kind of unrooted cosmopolitanism amounts to a mere 'patchwork of ideas of different cultures' ([1928] 2011: 106), collapsing into a radical subjectivism that arbitrarily privileges 'the accidental likes and dislikes of the person judging' ([1928] 2011: 108). Second, Bhattacharyya claims that the uncritical acceptance of Western ideas, which was prevalent during his time, amounts to a form of intellectual bondage. According to him, foreign ideas should be approached with 'critical reserve and not docile acceptance . . . docile acceptance without criticism would mean slavery' ([1928] 2011: 110). Third, he contrasts the slavish acceptance of Western ideas with his preferred method of thought: 'vital assimilation,' a dialectical engagement with Western views that combines receptive openness with critical scrutiny."
>
> — *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* pp. 4–5.

Vivekananda's own *Vartamān Bhārat* (1899) makes the same point, quoted *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 6:

> "O India, this is your terrible danger! The intoxication of imitating the West has grown so strong that what is good or what is bad is no longer decided by reason, judgment, scripture, or discrimination."
>
> — Vivekananda, *Vartamān Bhārat* (BCW 6:193 / CW 4:477), quoted *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 6.

The user's project is itself an exercise in this exact methodology. The triad — reverential immersion, refusal of docile acceptance, vital assimilation through critical discernment — is the methodological skeleton that the user's reader-tree exemplifies. Citation: Medhananda's *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* Introduction is the contemporary academic articulation of *Svaraj in Ideas* as a philosophical method, not just as a political slogan.

The hermeneutic move is itself substantive. Medhananda is doing two things at once: (1) he is *modeling* on Vivekananda the immersive-cosmopolitan posture that the user's project also exemplifies (rooted in the Sanskrit-Vedic tradition; critically assimilating Western philosophy without docile acceptance); and (2) he is *defending* that posture — against the Hacker-Halbfass tendency to read any modern Indian philosophical engagement with Western ideas as already a corruption of the indigenous tradition. The user's relationship to Medhananda's hermeneutic is straightforwardly affirmative.

### Chapter 1 — The making of an Integral Advaitin

Chapter 1 reconstructs Vivekananda's intellectual and spiritual development from his early Brāhmo theism (1878–1882), through his initial encounter with Ramakrishna and resistance to Advaita (1882–1884), to his decisive shift into what Medhananda calls "Integral Advaita" (1884–1886). The shift is documented with the *touch-experience* of December 1881 / early 1882 and the more sustained transformation in late 1884.

The decisive Bengali-source passage. Vivekananda, recalling his initial resistance to Advaita and Ramakrishna's response, is quoted at *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* pp. 23–24 (translating from *Lilāprasaṅga* 2.ii.81–82 / *Divine Play* 802):

> "On that day, at the Master's astonishing touch, my whole mind was overturned. I stood amazed to discover that in truth there was nothing in the universe but God. Everything I saw was God. When I sat down to eat, I found that the food, the plate, the server, and I myself were nothing but That. Afterwards the same perception kept recurring whatever I was doing, whether eating, sitting, lying down, or going to college. When the first intoxication eased somewhat, the world began to look dream-like. This lasted for several days. Once I returned to an ordinary condition, I understood that I had received at least a glimpse of Advaitic knowledge. From then on I could no longer think the scriptures false, nor could I deny the verdict of Advaita."
>
> — *Lilāprasaṅga* 2.ii.81–82 / *Divine Play* 802, quoted *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* pp. 23–24.

Medhananda's diachronic reconstruction (*Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 25) is decisive against the standard Advaita-only reading of Vivekananda's first realization:

> "His nuanced diachronic description of the phenomenology of his Advaitic experience is revealing. Initially, he seems to have had a panentheistic experience of everything in the world as a real manifestation of God. However, after this state 'lessened a bit,' he began to look upon the world as unreal and dream-like. In other words, this single prolonged spiritual experience enabled him to inhabit firsthand two distinct nondual standpoints: first, an integral, all-encompassing nondual standpoint akin to Tantra and Śaiva Nondualism; and subsequently, a more traditional, world-denying nondual standpoint."
>
> — *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 25.

Note the Bengali phrase *advaita-vijñāner ābhās* — "a glimpse of the knowledge of Advaita." Vivekananda himself uses *vijñāna* here, not *jñāna*, in the same Ramakrishnan sense the *Infinite Paths* book reconstructed. The structural significance is decisive: Vivekananda's first sustained Advaitic realization was *not* an *acosmic* realization of *nirguṇa* Brahman alone but an *integrated* realization that *everything is God* — eating, sitting, the food, the plate, the person serving. This is the *vijñānī*'s realization in Ramakrishna's terms (cf. K 740 / G 700: "the bhakta says that God has become everything" — *Infinite Paths* p. 39), not the *jñānī*'s acosmic *nirvikalpa-samādhi*.

That is the central interpretive wedge of the book. Vivekananda's mature Advaita is not Śaṅkarite Advaita; it is what Medhananda calls *Integral Advaita* — the systematic-philosophical extension of Ramakrishna's *Vijñāna Vedānta* into a cosmopolitan idiom.

### Chapter 2 — The metaphysics and ethics of oneness

This is the book's central reconstructive chapter. The four foundational tenets are formalized, paralleling the six tenets of Ramakrishna's *Vijñāna Vedānta* in *Infinite Paths*. The summary statement at *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 49:

> "Vivekananda followed Ramakrishna, rather than Śaṅkara, in holding that (1) the impersonal Brahman and the personal Śakti are equally real aspects of one and the same Infinite Divine Reality; (2) the universe is a real manifestation of Śakti; and (3) all four Yogas — Bhakti-Yoga, Jñāna-Yoga, Karma-Yoga, and Rāja-Yoga — are direct and independent means to salvation."
>
> — *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 49.

The book's coinage *Integral Advaita* is itself given a verbatim definition (*Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 51):

> "The philosophy of Advaita Vedānta that Vivekananda preached throughout the world in the 1890s was not the traditional Advaita Vedānta of Śaṅkara and his followers but the world-affirming, *vijñāna*-based Advaita philosophy taught to him by Ramakrishna. Throughout this book, I will refer to Vivekananda's world-affirming, ethically oriented Advaita Vedānta as 'Integral Advaita.' Although he never referred to his own philosophy as 'Integral Advaita,' I believe this designation aptly captures the expansive and harmonizing nature of his Advaitic philosophical worldview. Vivekananda's philosophy of Integral Advaita, I argue, upholds an all-inclusive Divine Oneness that includes not only the impersonal Brahman but also the dynamic Śakti as well as the entire universe of sentient and insentient entities. As Ramakrishna taught Vivekananda in 1885, 'These three, Brahman, the insentient world, and living beings, together constitute One Reality' (K 778 / G 733)."
>
> — *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* pp. 51, condensed.

These four tenets — bivalent ontological realism, world as real manifestation, divinity-of-the-soul + service-as-worship, equal-status of the four Yogas — constitute Vivekananda's *Integral Advaita* in Medhananda's reconstruction. Chapter 2 walks each in detail with verbatim citations from the *Complete Works*.

**The bivalent ontological realism.** *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 51, quoting CW 9:195:

> "[T]he central conception of Hindu philosophy is of the Absolute; that is the background of the universe. This Absolute Being, of whom we can predicate nothing, has Its powers spoken of as She — that is, the real personal God in India is She. This Śakti of the Brahman is always in the feminine gender."

Medhananda's gloss: "Following Ramakrishna, Vivekananda holds that while the impersonal nondual Brahman is utterly transcendent and ineffable, Śakti is the same Absolute in its dynamic aspect as the 'real personal God' who creates, preserves, and destroys the universe. Notice his emphasis on the *reality* of the personal God, which … is more consistent with Ramakrishna's Vijñāna Vedānta than with Śaṅkara's Advaita Vedānta" (*Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 51). The contrast with Śaṅkara on Brahmasūtra 2.1.14 (the *Īśvara* as dependent on *upādhis* "conjured up by ignorance") is then drawn explicitly. The "personal-impersonal" God Vivekananda explicitly affirms — CW 3:335–336 — is identified by Medhananda with the *vijñānī*'s realization of God-as-both-personal-and-impersonal.

**The world as a real manifestation.** Vivekananda's frequent reinterpretation of *māyā* and *vivarta* away from the illusionistic reading. *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 59, quoting CW 2:167:

> "[W]e have to go through the negation, and then the positive side will begin. We have to give up ignorance and all that is false, and then truth will begin to reveal itself to us. When we have grasped the truth, things which we gave up at first will take new shape and form, will appear to us in a new light, and become deified. They will have become sublimated, and then we shall understand them in their true light. But to understand them, we have first to get a glimpse of truth; we must give them up at first, and then we get them back again, deified."

Medhananda's gloss: "The spiritual realization Vivekananda describes here, I would suggest, is not the Advaitic *jñānī*'s realization that Brahman alone is real and everything else is unreal, but the Ramakrishnan *vijñānī*'s realization that everything in the world is God Himself in various forms." The structural parallel to Ramakrishna's roof-and-stairs analogy is exact: the world is *given up* in the negating process, then *received back deified*. The negation is real, the return is real, both are real registers.

The Īśā Upaniṣad opening — *īśāvāsyam idam sarvam* — is then read as Vivekananda's scriptural ground for this position. Medhananda observes (p. 64) that Vivekananda interprets *bhuñjīthāḥ* — "enjoy" — at face value, against Śaṅkara who reads it as renunciation: "When we have given up desires, then alone shall we be able to read and enjoy this universe of God."

**The divinity of the soul and service-as-worship.** This is Vivekananda's *Practical Vedānta*. The classic formulation Medhananda emphasizes throughout: *jīve prema kare yei jana, sei jana sevichhe īśvara* — "the one who loves the *jīva* serves God"; *śiva-jñāne jīver sevā* — "service of the *jīva* in the knowledge that he is Śiva." Crucially, on Medhananda's reading, *Practical Vedānta* is *not* a humanitarian add-on to a metaphysics-first Advaita; it is a *first-order ontological claim*. Because every being is the divine in differentiated form, service is worship — that is the metaphysical fact, not a derived ethical exhortation.

This reading has substantive philosophical work to do. The standard "Neo-Vedānta" charge (Hacker, Halbfass, Fort) is that Vivekananda imported Western humanitarian-ethical concerns into a Vedānta that had no resources for them. Medhananda's reply (chapter 2 §5): the resources are *internal* to *Vijñāna Vedānta* — they follow from the bivalent ontological realism (tenet 1) and the world-as-real-manifestation thesis (tenet 2). If everything is the divine in differentiated form, then service of any being is service of the divine — and this follows by ontology, not by importation.

The Bengali ground is at *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* pp. 73–78, where Medhananda walks Vivekananda's 1894 lectures and his 1894 letter to Pramadadas Mitra (*Vāṇī o Racanā* / CW 6) on what would become the Ramakrishna Mission's institutional *raison d'être*.

**The four yogas as direct and independent paths.** Tenet 4 is in some ways the most distinctive. Vivekananda's mature position is that *Bhakti-Yoga, Jñāna-Yoga, Karma-Yoga,* and *Rāja-Yoga* are *equally direct* paths to liberation, none subordinate to any other. This is the philosophical content of his pluralism. Medhananda's chapter 3 §4 walks the textual evolution of this position — Vivekananda's mature December 1895–1901 articulation explicitly abandons the earlier "three stages of Vedānta" (Tattva-vāda → Viśiṣṭādvaita → Advaita) framework in favor of the *four-yogas* framework. The "three stages" framework would have been hierarchical (Tattva-vāda as preliminary, Viśiṣṭādvaita as intermediate, Advaita as final); the *four-yogas* framework is *non-hierarchical* — each yoga is a complete path on its own, with its own competent terminus, and the *vijñānī*'s integration is the realization that all four reach the same Real.

The user's T12 (decentralized philosophy: all traditions work for those they work for) is precisely this. The user's T1 (register thesis) reads it more granularly: each yoga corresponds to a different *register* of the Real (Bhakti-Yoga: relational register; Jñāna-Yoga: identity register; Karma-Yoga: action register; Rāja-Yoga: somato-psychic register). Medhananda's reconstruction does not articulate the register-thesis explicitly, but the structural content is there.

### Chapter 3 — Vedāntic universal religion as cosmopolitan religious pluralism

Chapter 3 walks the *diachronic* evolution of Vivekananda's views on religious diversity from 1893 to 1901. Medhananda's argumentative wedge against the "synchronic" reading of Vivekananda is decisive: *most* scholars treat Vivekananda's pluralism as a single static position; in fact, the position evolved through three phases.

Phase 1 (Sept 1893–March 1894). At the Parliament of Religions and immediately after, Vivekananda defended the equal *salvific efficacy* of the major world religions but treated "universal religion" as an ideal that did not yet exist. Phase 2 (Sept 1894–May 1895). Vivekananda claimed that the universal religion *already exists* in the form of Vedānta, expounded in terms of the "three stages" of Tattva-vāda → Viśiṣṭādvaita → Advaita. This is the period that gives the inclusivist reading its purchase: at this stage, Vivekananda *does* treat Advaita as the highest stage, with Tattva-vāda and Viśiṣṭādvaita as preliminary. Phase 3 (Late 1895–1901). The mature position. Vivekananda *abandons* the three-stages framework and reconstitutes the universal religion in terms of the four-yogas paradigm. Each yoga is a direct and independent path to liberation; each religion corresponds to at least one yoga; the universal religion is the recognition that *all four* reach the same Real.

Medhananda's central claim against the literature: the *vast majority of scholars* have read Vivekananda's mature position as the Phase-2 three-stages framework, when in fact Vivekananda explicitly abandoned that framework by late 1895. This matters enormously. The Phase-2 reading supports the Hacker / Halbfass charge of Advaitic inclusivism (Advaita as the highest stage, all others demoted). The Phase-3 reading is non-hierarchical *pluralism* — and is what Vivekananda actually held in his mature 1896–1901 lectures.

The Phase-3 reading is the user's T12 in cross-cultural philosophy of religion idiom. It is also the substantive philosophical position the user's T4 ("all darśanas true, structurally homologous to Sarvāmnāya") generalizes into the Sanskrit-philosophical context.

### Chapters 4–6 — the experiential basis of religion

Part II of the book — chapters 4 through 6 — develops Vivekananda's "Science of Religion" as a *cosmopolitan response to the global crisis of religious belief*. The argument is two-pronged: a critique of nineteenth-century scientism (chapter 4) and a positive defense of *supersensuous perception* as an epistemic warrant for religious knowledge (chapters 5 and 6, in dialogue with traditional Indian *pramāṇa* epistemology, William Alston, and contemporary epistemology of perception).

The structural philosophical move. Vivekananda's claim is that religion can be reconstituted as a science *without* reducing to natural science — by extending empiricism to include *supersensuous perception* (Indian *yoga-pratyakṣa*) as a genuine perceptual mode that delivers epistemic justification for religious beliefs in the same structural way that ordinary perception delivers epistemic justification for ordinary beliefs. The argument is reconstructed by Medhananda as a seven-premise argument (chapter 5), with chapter 6 dealing with the standard objections (cross-checkability, speckled-hen, gullibility, conflicting-claims, Kumārila's classical critique of *yoga-pratyakṣa*, Anantanand Rambachan's contemporary critique).

The user's relationship to this part is straightforwardly affirmative. The user's methodology §1 (*adhikāra*: persons not interchangeable; statements must be read for both truth-value and audience-fit) presupposes that the truths of *vijñāna* are *experientially* accessible to those constituted to access them — exactly Vivekananda's *yoga-pratyakṣa* / supersensuous perception thesis. The user's T11 (phenomenology of involution-evolution in the individual psychic being) likewise presupposes a defensible epistemology of supersensuous experience.

### Chapter 7–8 — faith and reason: Kantian-Vedāntic "metagnosticism"

Chapter 7 develops Vivekananda's "Kantian-Vedāntic critique of theological reason" — Vivekananda's Hamiltonian-Kantian rejection of speculative-rationalist theology, paired with a positive Vedāntic claim that reason's limits open onto experiential access. Medhananda coins "metagnosticism" for this position — *not* agnosticism (we cannot know God), and *not* gnosticism (we know God by reason), but *metagnosticism*: reason can *know its own limits* and thereby make space for the experiential access that supersensuous perception provides.

Chapter 8 reconstructs Vivekananda's "three-rung ladder of religious faith" in dialogue with the Clifford-Huxley-James debate of the 1890s. The three rungs: Stage 1 — sub-doxastic intellectual assent (faith as initial willingness to take a hypothesis seriously); Stage 2 — doxastic belief (faith as committed belief that survives serious testing); Stage 3 — self-authenticating realization (faith as the experiential certitude of supersensuous perception). The structural move: rungs 1 and 2 are *partial* — faith as belief is necessary but not sufficient; rung 3 is the experiential *terminus* in which faith and knowledge become non-different.

The user's T11 (ontology of phenomenology) is structurally compatible with this. The user's directive turn (T5, T8, T10) — "what to aim at, how to act, total surrender from fullness" — extends the rung-3 register into the phenomenology of an *individual* psychic-being's progressive realization. Medhananda stops at the epistemology; the user takes the further step into directive ethics.

### Chapters 9–10 — panentheistic cosmopsychism

Part IV — chapters 9 and 10 — is the most analytically ambitious section of the book. Medhananda reconstructs Vivekananda's mature position on consciousness as a *Sāṃkhya-Vedāntic panentheistic cosmopsychism* and brings it into dialogue with contemporary analytic cosmopsychism (Goff, Shani, Mathews, Albahari).

The structural philosophical position. Cosmopsychism is the contemporary panpsychist alternative to micropsychism: instead of starting with consciousness at the micro level (individual particles or fundamental physical entities) and trying to combine it up to macro consciousness (the *combination problem*), cosmopsychism starts with consciousness at the cosmic level and tries to *individuate* it down to finite subjects (the *decombination problem*). The set-up at *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 304:

> "Cosmopsychist views arguably face the obverse problem — variously called the 'individuation problem' (Mathews 2011: 145), the 'decombination problem' (Albahari 2020), and the 'derivation problem' (Nagasawa and Wager 2017) — of explaining how the consciousness of the cosmos as a whole *individuates* into the conscious experiences of individual humans and non-human animals."
>
> — *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 304.

Medhananda's reconstruction of Vivekananda: the cosmic consciousness is the *Saccidānanda* of Integral Advaita; the individuation is *grounding by self-limitation* — the cosmic consciousness *grounds* finite subjects by *limiting itself*, a willed self-limitation that produces apparent finite individuality without compromising the underlying unity. The Sāṃkhya apparatus enters because Vivekananda inherits the *prakṛti-puruṣa* distinction as the metaphysical machinery for the consciousness-mind dualism (consciousness — *puruṣa* — is the witness; mind — *citta* — is *prakṛti* and unconscious in itself). Medhananda's broader definition of panpsychism (*Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 304) is itself the user's contribution to register-discipline:

> "Since most Indian philosophical traditions take mind to be a subtle form of insentient matter, I believe it would be more accurate and helpful to define panpsychism more broadly as the view that *consciousness* is fundamental and ubiquitous."
>
> — *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* p. 304.

This is doctrinally load-bearing. Western panpsychism (Goff, Nagel) conflates mind and consciousness; the Indian-philosophical traditions (Sāṃkhya, Yoga, Vedānta, Trika Śaivism) sharply distinguish them — *citta* is *prakṛti*-stuff (insentient in itself), *puruṣa* / *cit* is the witnessing consciousness that lights up *citta*. Medhananda's broadened definition makes the conversation with Western panpsychism *possible*; without it, the categories miss each other. The user's T1 register thesis (Sāṃkhya as register of *form*; Yoga as register of *somato-psychic praxis*; Advaita as register of *ground*) does the same disambiguating work in a different idiom.

The crucial connection to the user's project: Medhananda's "grounding by self-limitation" is *structurally identical* to what Aurobindo (per Medhananda's *Monist* 2022 paper) calls *exclusive concentration* — the divine's playful self-limitation into apparently distinct centers of consciousness. The user's T7 (divine play through willful self-ignorance) names the same gesture from the Vivaraṇa-Madhusūdana-Trika side.

The dialogue with Albahari, Shani, and Goff (chapter 10 §5) is particularly useful for the user's project: Albahari's "perennial monism" is treated by Medhananda as a position structurally close to Vijñāna-Vedāntic panentheism but missing the bivalent realism (Albahari treats the personal aspect as illusion, in a Śaṅkarite-flavored way, where Vivekananda treats it as a real manifestation). The user's T1 register thesis can use this dialogue without modification.

## Part III — *Vivekananda's Vedāntic Critique of Schopenhauer's Doctrine of the Will* (Philosophy East and West 2017)

The 2017 paper is a 31-page article in *Philosophy East and West* 67.4: 1191–1221. Maharaj's argument: Vivekananda's brief but pregnant critical remarks on Schopenhauer (delivered in lectures in America and London 1895–1896) constitute a *substantive philosophical critique* of Schopenhauer's doctrine of the will, not the off-the-cuff dismissals they have usually been read as. The paper's argumentative wedge: Vivekananda's reading of Schopenhauer is a *Vedāntic* critique that anticipates several lines later developed by 20th-century Schopenhauer-critics (Atwell, Janaway, Young), and that *exposes* the ways in which Schopenhauer's pessimistic ontology is incompatible with the Vedāntic ontology Schopenhauer himself elsewhere claimed as a parallel.

### The structural problem in Schopenhauer

Maharaj sets up the problem precisely. Schopenhauer in *Die Welt als Wille und Vorstellung* volume 1 (1819) identifies the *will* as the Kantian *thing-in-itself* — beyond time, space, and causation. In WWR 2 chapter 18 (1844), he qualifies this: the will is known through introspection and is therefore subject to *time*; the strict thing-in-itself is unknowable. In his late letters (1844–1853), he goes further: the strict thing-in-itself may have, "apart from the attribute of will, still other qualities, determinations, properties, modes of being, which lie outside all possible appearance, and which are absolutely unknowable and ungraspable" — a remark Maharaj quotes at p. 1196.

Schopenhauer himself is therefore inconsistent. His Indological readers — Paul Deussen and Max Hecker — each tacitly reconstructs his position to save its coherence: Deussen (*Elemente der Metaphysik*, 1877) distinguishes a "pole of willing" from a "pole of not-willing" and identifies *Brahman* with the latter; Hecker (*Schopenhauer und die indische Philosophie*, 1897) distinguishes the will as "genus" (the unknowable thing-in-itself) from the will as "species" (what we know through introspection). Both readings preserve Schopenhauer's coherence at the cost of making his Indian-thought parallels selective.

### Vivekananda's five-pronged critique

Maharaj's reconstruction of Vivekananda's critique, as a numbered list (p. 1199–1200):

> "1. The will is changeable and phenomenal. 2. The will is in the mind. The will is subject to all three phenomenal forms of time, space, and causation. The will is at least subject to the phenomenal form of time. 3. Schopenhauer's thesis that the will is the noumenal thing-in-itself conflicts with Vedāntic soteriology, according to which one can transcend the will through self-denial and meditation and realize one's noumenal nature as the pure, blissful Self (Ātman). 4. Schopenhauer's equation of the will with the noumenal thing-in-itself is rooted in a misinterpretation of Vedānta. 5. Schopenhauer may have been led to a mistaken interpretation of Vedānta for two reasons: (a) Schopenhauer did not know Sanskrit, so he had to rely on the Oupnek'hat, an unreliable translation of the Upaniṣads. (b) The explicitly Vedāntic strain in Schopenhauer's philosophy conflicts with a largely implicit Buddhistic strain, which makes the will fundamental and refuses to posit a noumenal reality beyond the will."

The compressed core passage. Vivekananda's 1896 London lecture "The Absolute and Manifestation," quoted at *Philosophy East and West* 67.4: 1200:

> "I think Schopenhauer's philosophy makes a mistake in its interpretation of Vedanta, for it seeks to make the will everything. Schopenhauer makes the will stand in the place of the Absolute. But the Absolute cannot be presented as will, for will is something changeable and phenomenal, and over the line, drawn above time, space, and causation, there is no change, no motion; it is only below the line that external motion and internal motion, called thought, begin. There can be no will on the other side, and will therefore, cannot be the cause of this universe. Coming nearer, we see in our own bodies that will is not the cause of every movement. I move this chair; my will is the cause of this movement, and this will becomes manifested as muscular motion at the other end. But the same power that moves the chair is moving the heart, the lungs, and so on, but not through will. Given that the power is the same, it only becomes will when it rises to the plane of consciousness, and to call it will before it has risen to this plane is a misnomer. This makes a good deal of confusion in Schopenhauer's philosophy."
>
> — Vivekananda, "The Absolute and Manifestation" (London 1896), CW 2:135–6, quoted Maharaj 2017: 1200.

The second crucial passage. Vivekananda's 1896 London lecture "Buddhism and Vedānta," quoted Maharaj 2017: 1201:

> "We do not agree with the will-theory of these German philosophers at all. Will itself is phenomenal and cannot be the Absolute. It is one of the many projections. There is something which is not will, but is manifesting itself as will. That I can understand. But that will is manifesting itself as everything else, I do not understand, seeing that we cannot have any conception of will, as separate from the universe. When that something which is freedom becomes will, it is caused by time, space, and causation. Take Kant's analysis. Will is within time, space, and causation. Then how can it be the Absolute? One cannot will without willing in time. If we can stop all thought, then we know that we are beyond thought. We come to this by negation. When every phenomenon has been negatived, whatever remains, that is It. That cannot be expressed, cannot be manifested, because the manifestation will be, again, will."
>
> — Vivekananda, "Buddhism and Vedānta" (London 1896), CW 5:280–1, quoted Maharaj 2017: 1201.

Maharaj's key gloss (2017: 1202): "Vivekananda's explicit reference to Kant ('Take Kant's analysis') suggests that his objection — like Herbart's — should be taken as an *internal critique* of Schopenhauer's philosophy. As Vivekananda was aware, Schopenhauer himself accepts Kant's distinction between the noumenon and the phenomenon, so he contradicts himself when he identifies the will — which is at least subject to time — with the noumenal reality beyond time."

### The structural significance for the user's T2

The paper matters for the user's T2 (the realist trajectory) because it establishes that Vivekananda's reading of Schopenhauer is a *critique* of Schopenhauerian pessimism, not an *extension* of it. The standard Western framing — going back to Deussen — has been that Schopenhauer "discovered" the Indian *māyā*-doctrine in the West and produced a systematic Western pessimist philosophy that aligns Brahman with the *will*. Vivekananda explicitly rejects this alignment. *Brahman is not will*; the will is phenomenal, the will is *in the mind*, the will rises to the plane of consciousness only when consciousness illuminates it. Brahman is the *prior* condition that makes will possible; Brahman is also the condition into which the practitioner *transcends* the will via meditation and self-denial.

This is the crucial T2 move: Indian realism — Nyāya, Mīmāṃsā, Madhva, Bhāskara, *and* Advaita correctly read — never fell into the correlationist / pessimist / Mayavada-as-illusionism trap that Schopenhauer's Western reception produced. The user's T2 takes this further: the realist trajectory of Vedānta is precisely the *self-correction* of any tendency in Indian thought toward Schopenhauerian pessimism. Vivekananda's critique of Schopenhauer is therefore *not* an external criticism of a Western philosopher; it is an *intra-Vedāntic* defense of the realist trajectory, deploying its resources against a Western misreading that had been re-imported into Indian self-understanding via Deussen and the British-colonial reception.

### The deeper structural critique — Buddhistic versus Vedāntic strains

Maharaj's most striking move comes in §III. Vivekananda is reading Schopenhauer as containing *two* strains in tension. The *explicit* strain is Vedāntic — Schopenhauer himself often aligns the will with *Brahman* and aligns the world of representation with *māyā*. The *implicit* strain is Buddhistic — Schopenhauer's actual doctrine, that the will is *fundamental* and that there is *no* noumenal reality beyond willing, is structurally closer to Buddhist *taṇhā* (thirst, craving) than to Vedāntic *Brahman*. Schopenhauer's pessimism — that existence is suffering and the goal is the *abolition* of will — is the Buddhist soteriological structure. The contradiction Maharaj diagnoses: Schopenhauer wants to claim Brahman *as* the will *and* claim that liberation is the *transcendence* of will. These two claims are inconsistent on any natural reading. Vivekananda's critique exposes the contradiction.

### Why this matters for the user's project

Three connections to the user's T2 and T11.

(a) The 2017 paper is the textual ground for the claim that Vivekananda's Vedānta is *world-affirming* in a way that explicitly resists Schopenhauerian pessimism. The user's T2 trajectory toward Aurobindo's Realistic Adwaita is therefore *also* a trajectory away from any pessimistic-illusionist misreading.

(b) The paper's diagnosis of the *Buddhistic-Vedāntic* tension in Schopenhauer is structurally analogous to the user's diagnosis of the *Mayavada-Realist* tension in interpretations of Śaṅkara. In both cases, the resolution is the *realist* reading.

(c) The paper anticipates the critique of correlationism the user develops via Meillassoux. The argument is structurally important and worth laying out at length. Meillassoux's *After Finitude* (2008) diagnoses post-Kantian philosophy as captive to the *correlationist circle*: thought can only ever know the *correlation* between thinking and being, never being itself; the noumenon is forever sealed off. Schopenhauer's WWR is a particularly subtle form of correlationism — it tries to *break out* of the Kantian closure by claiming privileged introspective access to the noumenon (the will), only to re-introduce phenomenality by admitting (in WWR 2 ch. 18) that the will is itself "under the veil of time" and therefore *not* the unconditioned noumenon. Schopenhauer ends up either with a will-as-correlate (still inside the circle) or with an unknowable-something-beyond-the-will (Kantian closure preserved).

Vivekananda's critique is — on Maharaj's reconstruction — that Schopenhauer's dichotomy mistakes a *phenomenal-mental projection* (the will) for the noumenon, and that the *Vedāntic* alternative *breaks the correlationist circle* by a different route: *yoga-pratyakṣa* / supersensuous perception accesses the noumenal Ātman/Brahman directly, in a register that is not the discursive subject-object correlation but the dissolution of that correlation into knowledge-by-identity. The strict Vedāntic claim is *exactly* the claim Meillassoux's "speculative" turn aspires to — that thought *can* access the absolute, that the Real is *not* sealed off behind a phenomenal veil — but with a methodological apparatus (yogic *samādhi*) that is not available in the Western post-Kantian tradition. This is what the user's T11 names: an *ontology of phenomenology grounded in the individual psychic being* (Aurobindo's *caitya puruṣa*), not in Hegel's universal Spirit (still discursive) and not in Heidegger's epistemic-conditioning closure (a sophisticated correlationism).

The further structural point: Vivekananda's 1896 statement that "there is something which is not will, but is manifesting itself as will" is the *exact* Vedāntic counter to correlationist closure. The "something which is not will" is Brahman; *manifestation* is the relation; the will is one register among many. Brahman is not sealed off — it is *manifest* as the universe. The correlationist circle is broken by the manifestationist ontology Maharaj reconstructs through *Infinite Paths* chapter 5: the world is the Real, in differentiated form. Vivekananda's critique of Schopenhauer and Maharaj's defense of Ramakrishna's *vijñāna*-based manifestationism are *the same anti-correlationist move* in two registers: against the post-Kantian noumenon-closure, and against the Hickian phenomenon-projection.

This makes Medhananda's Vivekananda-Schopenhauer paper the textual ground for one of the user's most important convergences: that the *anti-correlationist* position the user reads through Meillassoux is *already* present in Vivekananda's 1896 lectures, defended on Vedāntic grounds, and that the realist trajectory of Vedānta (T2) is precisely what *deflects* the modern Western turn into correlationism *before* that turn becomes structurally inevitable in the West.

The 2017 paper is shorter than the books and is less often cited; it is, however, the cleanest single statement of the *anti-pessimist anti-correlationist* dimension of Medhananda's Vivekananda-reconstruction. The user's T2 reading should cite it explicitly when the polemic against Mayavada-as-pessimist-illusionism comes up, *and* when the structural homology with Meillassoux's anti-correlationism is being drawn.

## Part IV — Other Maharaj/Medhananda papers and shorter writings

The four major journal articles and shorter pieces beyond the two books and the 2017 Schopenhauer paper. ### "Cutting the Knot of the World Problem" (*Religions* 12.9, 2021)

This is Medhananda's most direct philosophical engagement with Aurobindo, and the paper that most directly underwrites the user's T2 trajectory toward Aurobindo's Realistic Adwaita. The argument: Aurobindo's three-pronged critique of Śaṅkarite *māyā-vāda* — scriptural, experiential, philosophical — is correct, and "has not yet received the sustained scholarly attention it deserves" (the abstract verbatim).

(1) *Scriptural critique.* The Veda, the principal Upaniṣads, and the Bhagavad-Gītā teach an *all-encompassing, world-affirming Advaita* — not the world-denying Advaita Śaṅkara reads into them. The Īśā opening *īśāvāsyam idam sarvam* and the Brahma-Sūtra's *athāto brahma jijñāsā* are taken by Aurobindo as committing Vedānta to the world's reality as Brahman's manifestation.

(2) *Experiential critique.* Aurobindo concedes Śaṅkara's realization as *real but partial* — the impersonal *nirguṇa* absolute disclosed in *nirvikalpa samādhi*. Aurobindo himself underwent this in 1908 with the yogi Lele. He insists, however, that the realization was a *first step*:

> "Nirvana in my liberated consciousness turned out to be the beginning of my realisation, a first step towards the complete thing, not the sole true attainment possible."
>
> — Aurobindo, *Letters on Yoga*, quoted *Religions* 2021 §III.

The further realizations — divine immanence in everything, the descent of the Supermind — *integrate* rather than *supersede* the static Brahman.

(3) *Philosophical critique.* Aurobindo's locus-of-avidyā dilemma: if the *jīva* perceives the illusion, the *jīva* is itself a product of ignorance — circularity. If Brahman perceives the illusion, Brahman is subject to illusion — contradicts perfection. Śaṅkara's qualified illusionism (*vyāvahārika* / *pāramārthika*) collapses into thoroughgoing illusionism since empirical entities have only "unreal reality, real only to our ignorance, unreal to any true knowledge." Aurobindo's conclusion (*Life Divine* II.5): "all illusionistic attempts to explain the relationship between Brahman and this world-appearance end up in self-contradiction."

The positive Aurobindonian doctrine — *Realistic Adwaita*. The Real is *Saccidānanda* with two inseparable aspects: static *Sat* (the impersonal Absolute) and dynamic *Cit-Śakti* (Consciousness-Force). Cit-Śakti is the explanatory ground of manifestation; the world is a real self-expression of the Divine, not an illusion. The Supramental, not the silent Brahman, is the integrating principle.

The crucial connection to the user's T2 / T7. Medhananda's *Religions* 2021 paper is the strongest contemporary academic articulation of Aurobindo's three-pronged critique. The user accepts every line of the diagnosis. The user *extends* past Medhananda by arguing that Madhusūdana's *bhāvarūpa-avidyā* and the Vivaraṇa school's account of Brahman as both *āśraya* and *viṣaya* of avidyā *answer* the locus-of-avidyā objection from *inside* Advaita. Aurobindo's dilemma is fatal only against *flat-footed* Śaṅkarite Advaita; Madhusūdana's defensive apparatus blocks it. This is a real and substantive divergence with Medhananda, who treats classical Advaita as *not having* such resources.

### "The Playful Self-Involution of Divine Consciousness" (*The Monist* 105.1, 2022)

Medhananda's reconstruction of Aurobindo as a panpsychist / cosmopsychist who *escapes* the standard objections by introducing two specifically Aurobindonian moves: *involution* and *exclusive concentration*.

*Involution.* Before the evolutionary process from matter → life → mind → supermind, the Divine *Saccidānanda* has *involved* itself into matter. Evolution is the inverse arc: the playful self-recovery of what was self-veiled. This is *līlā* with a metaphysical structure: the descent is real, the veiling is real, the unveiling is real.

*Exclusive concentration.* Aurobindo's response to the individuation problem (the "decombination problem" of contemporary cosmopsychism): the Divine *individuates* into apparently distinct centers of consciousness by an act of *playful exclusive concentration* (*ekāgratā* / self-limitation). Each *jīva* is the universal *Saccidānanda* concentrating itself "as if" it were a finite locus. The seeming finitude is not metaphysically primitive; it is a willed self-limitation by the One.

Why this matters for the user's T7. Medhananda's "playful self-involution" is *structurally identical* to the user's *bhāvarūpa-avidyā-as-divine-self-veiling* clause; the difference is purely terminological. Where Medhananda treats the move as Aurobindo's innovation against Advaita, the user reads it as the *Vivaraṇa-Madhusūdana* view of *bhāvarūpa avidyā* extended by *Kashmir-Śaiva svātantrya* — already present in the Sanskrit traditions Medhananda treats as exhausted by Śaṅkara. The doctrinal content is the same; the ground texts the user invokes (Madhusūdana's destructive dilemma; Utpaladeva's *Pratyabhijñā*; Abhinavagupta's *Tantrāloka*) are different. The user's T7 puts the entire move *inside* Vedānta-Tantra as a recovery of latent realist resources, where Medhananda puts it *outside* Śaṅkarite Advaita as Aurobindo's Vedāntic alternative.

### Pattern across the corpus

Three constants run through Maharaj/Medhananda's published work since 2015:

Aurobindo writes in a register that is simultaneously metaphysical, mystical, and yogic — the *Life Divine* is not "philosophy" in the analytic sense; it is metaphysics presented as the discursive expression of yogic realization. Medhananda writes in a register that is academic-philosophical — the books are addressed to the contemporary philosophy-of-religion conversation; the citations are to Hick, Plantinga, Göcke; the apparatus is the apparatus of the contemporary academy. Both are necessary. Aurobindo without Medhananda is metaphysics without contemporary philosophical defense; Medhananda without Aurobindo is contemporary-academic apparatus without the depth-metaphysics it is articulating.

The user sits at the synthesis. The user's project takes Aurobindo's metaphysics as the deep substantive content and Medhananda's apparatus as the contemporary academic articulation, *and extends both* — backward into the Sanskrit tradition (Madhva, Caitanya at primary-source depth, Vivaraṇa school, Madhusūdana, Trika *svātantrya*), sideways into Western philosophical movements Medhananda does not engage (McGilchrist, Gebser, Deleuze, Prigogine, Whitehead, Meillassoux), and forward into a directive ethics, politics, and personal-philosophical position (T5, T8, T10) that neither Aurobindo nor Medhananda fully articulates in the user's register.

### Reading sequence — what to read in what order

The user's project does not require reading Medhananda's two OUP books cover-to-cover. The recommended sequence:

**Phase 1 — Foundational frame.** *Infinite Paths to Infinite Reality* chapter 1 (the methodological postulates and the six tenets of *Vijñāna Vedānta*); chapters 3 (manifestationist religious pluralism) and 4 (against Hick). About 80 pages. This establishes the Ramakrishna-as-philosopher reconstruction at the level the user needs.

**Phase 2 — The Vivekananda extension.** *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* Introduction; chapter 1 (the making of an Integral Advaitin); chapter 2 (the metaphysics and ethics of oneness — particularly §§2–6); chapter 3 (the diachronic evolution of Vivekananda's pluralism — particularly §§3–4 on the shift from Phase 2 to Phase 3). About 100 pages. This establishes the Vivekananda-as-philosopher reconstruction.

**Phase 3 — The Aurobindo articulation.** Medhananda's *Religions* 2021 paper ("Cutting the Knot of the World Problem") and *The Monist* 2022 paper ("The Playful Self-Involution of Divine Consciousness"). Each is open-access and roughly 18–20 pages. This establishes the Aurobindonian articulation in modern academic idiom.

**Phase 4 — The Schopenhauer paper.** Maharaj 2017 (*Philosophy East and West* 67.4) on Vivekananda's critique of Schopenhauer's will. About 30 pages. This establishes the anti-pessimist anti-correlationist dimension of the realist trajectory.

**Phase 5 — Optional deepening.** *Infinite Paths* chapter 7 (the multi-track theodicy) and chapter 5 (the manifestationist epistemology of mystical experience); *Vedāntic Cosmopolitanism* chapters 9–10 (the panentheistic cosmopsychism). These are the most substantive philosophical chapters but are less central to the user's T2/T3/T6 axis than Phases 1–3.

The total reading load if all phases are completed is about 250 pages. The user's project should be able to draft on Medhananda from Phases 1–3 alone (about 200 pages) and consult Phases 4–5 only as specific T-level questions arise.

### Final assessment

Medhananda is the user's strongest contemporary academic ally on the modern-phase content of T2, T3, T6, and large parts of T1, T4, T11, T12. He is a *non-ally* on T5, T8, T10 (he does not develop the directive turn), on the Sanskrit-tradition primary-source ground for T2 (Madhva, Caitanya, Vivaraṇa, Madhusūdana), on T7 (the realist reading of *māyā* through *bhāvarūpa-avidyā* + *svātantrya* + Madhusūdana scaffolding), on the cross-Western constellation (McGilchrist, Gebser, Deleuze, Prigogine, Whitehead, Meillassoux), and on politics.
