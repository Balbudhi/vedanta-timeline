# Engagement: Madhusūdana Sarasvatī, *Bhagavad-bhakti-rasāyana*

## 1. What the text is

*Bhagavad-bhakti-rasāyana* is a short but technically dense Advaita Vedānta bhakti treatise by Madhusūdana Sarasvatī, traditionally placed in the late sixteenth to early seventeenth century. The on-disk witness is a three-*ullāsa* work, announced in the *viṣayānukramaṇī* at pure-Sanskrit file lines 422-540 and concluded at lines 4972-4991, 6399-6421, and 6876-6897 respectively. The first *ullāsa* explicitly carries an authorial *ṭīkā* in this edition; the editor’s prefatory material states that the remaining two do not preserve that same authorial commentary, and the file presents them instead with the later Hindi apparatus. Structurally, the work moves from the claim that bhakti is a *puruṣārtha*, to a taxonomy of bhakti’s emotional forms, to a final rasa-theory defense that recruits Vedānta, Sāṅkhya, Mīmāṃsā, and alaṅkāra categories.

## 2. Sampling and scope statement

Because printed page numerals are unstable in the OCR, the locators below use line ranges in the pure-Sanskrit file. Engaged at depth: the opening and doctrinal core of *prathamollāsa* (roughly lines 574-2105), the central classificatory and polemical stretches of *dvitīyollāsa* (4993-5905 and 6328-6401), and the opening and concluding arguments of *tṛtīyollāsa* (6423-6780). Structural summary only: the remainder of *prathamollāsa* and *dvitīyollāsa*, especially the long sequences of exempla and the full eleven-stage bhakti ascent, which I tracked by headings, transitions, and closure formulae but did not translate continuously line by line. Not engaged except for orientation: the Hindi front matter, biographical preface, and publisher material before line 422 and after line 6889.

## 3. Structural walk-through

### *Prathamollāsa*: bhakti as *puruṣārtha*, its definition, and its ontological mechanics

The first *ullāsa* opens with the work’s programmatic claim that bhakti toward Mukunda is either mixed with the standard rasas or stands independently as a supreme human end (lines 574-581). It immediately frames itself against the familiar triad of karma, jñāna, and bhakti by arguing that aṣṭāṅgayoga is absorbable into jñānayoga and that bhakti is not merely instrumental within that scheme (631-760). The middle of the *ullāsa* gives the text’s most important constructive move: bhakti is a stable *bhagavad-ākāratā* arising in a mind that has become fluid through hearing and related *bhagavad-dharma*; once the mind has “melted,” the form that enters it persists as *sthāyibhāva* (1578-1650). The latter half then shifts into a more overtly polemical-metaphysical register, arguing that this *sthāyibhāva* is not merely a psychological disposition but a manifestation of *parānanda* itself, and that bhakti-rasa exceeds laukika rasa precisely because its *ālambana* is Bhagavān rather than finite objects (1929-2105). The closing movement catalogues the ascent of devotion through eleven *bhūmikā*s and culminates in examples of perfected God-directed love, especially in the Bhāgavata’s devotees (463-477 in the contents; 3494-4971 in the body). Polemically, this *ullāsa* constructs bhakti against two fronts at once: against the view that bhakti is only a subsidiary means to something else, and against the aesthetic reduction that would leave it at the level of a merely analogous emotion.

### *Dvitīyollāsa*: what counts as bhakti proper, what only prepares it, and how bhakti mixes with rasa

The second *ullāsa* announces itself as the treatment of bhakti’s *viśeṣa* after the general account of the first (4993-5006). Its opening claim is that bhakti subdivides according to the distinct causes of mental melting, so Madhusūdana classifies the relevant affective states, beginning from kāma and moving through anger, fear, sorrow, laughter, wonder, generosity, and so on (5012 onward). The decisive middle argument comes at lines 5544-5604: not every affect that can issue in morally or spiritually valuable orientation counts as bhakti-rasa. *Dharmotsāha*, *dayotsāha*, *jugupsā*, and *śama* can be respectable and even preparatory, but they are not direct bhakti-rasa because they do not carry the same full *citta-druti* or because their immediate object is not Bhagavān. The next movement tightens the point: envy-based or fear-based hostility toward God may still relate one to God, but because it is *prīti-virodhin*, it cannot be savored as bhakti-rasa proper (5584-5625). Madhusūdana then reconstructs which mixed rasas are admissible when Bhagavān is the *ālambana* (5641-5667), distinguishes three unmixed bhakti-rasas, and finally ranks Bhagavad-rati over deva-rati by insisting that the former alone discloses full *parānanda* because other deities, treated as finite beings, do not function as the same ontological support (6332-6401). The polemical target here is any generalized rasa theory that would flatten Bhagavad-rati into ordinary deva-rati, or ordinary emotionality into bhakti merely because God is somehow involved.

### *Tṛtīyollāsa*: what rasa is, where it abides, how language manifests it

The third *ullāsa* begins with four explicit questions: what rasa is, where it resides, what manifests it, and what sort of cognition its apprehension is (6423-6441). It first gives the classical-seeming answer that *sthāyibhāva* manifested as happiness through *vibhāva*, *anubhāva*, and *vyabhicāribhāva* is rasa (6428-6449), but then it subjects that answer to revision through a more ambitious synthesis. The text walks through aesthetic theory, discusses collective apprehension and *dhvani*, distinguishes *asaṃlakṣya-krama-vyaṅgya* from *saṃlakṣya-krama*, and argues that the word-body of poetry is the proximate manifestor of rasa (6609-6740). Its strongest doctrinal move comes near the close: rasa is ultimately the manifestation of self-luminous bliss itself, and this can be defended not only by rasa-theory but also by appeal to Mīmāṃsā debates on verbal cognition and to Vedāntic claims about *parānanda* (6739-6780). The final conclusion states that whether words are taken as independent or context-bound, they manifest *parānanda* in accordance with the relevant auxiliaries, thereby securing bhakti-rasa within both poetics and śāstric epistemology (6869-6878). Polemically, this *ullāsa* turns outward most explicitly: its interlocutors are the *tārkika*, the rasa-theorist, and the Mīmāṃsaka, all pressed into service for a Vedāntic theology of affect.

## 4. Load-bearing passages — engaged at depth

### 1. Opening claim

**Locus:** *Prathamollāsa* opening *pratijñā* (lines 576-581).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** *navarasamilitaṃ vā kevalaṃ vā pumarthaṃ / paramam iha mukunde bhaktiyogaṃ vadanti / nirupamasukhasaṃvidrūpam aspṛṣṭaduḥkhaṃ / tam ahaṃ [sarvaśāstradṛṣṭyā OCR?] vyañjmi*

**Close English:** “Bhakti-yoga toward Mukunda, whether mixed with the nine rasas or standing by itself, they call here the supreme human end: of the form of incomparable bliss-consciousness, untouched by suffering. That I make manifest [OCR?] from the standpoint of the śāstras.”

**Engagement:** Even with OCR corruption, the argumentative center is plain. Madhusūdana is not promising a pious supplement to mokṣa-doctrine; he is staking out bhakti as either an autonomous or at least para-teleological end. The phrase *aspṛṣṭa-duḥkha* matters: this is not merely pleasurable affect, but a mode of consciousness whose defining mark is non-contact with suffering. The text’s later insistence that bhakti-rasa is not reducible to laukika rasa is already compressed here.

The other decisive word is *mukunde*. Bhakti is not being treated as a free-floating devotional disposition. It is indexically fixed to Bhagavān, and the later argument of the second *ullāsa* shows that this restriction is load-bearing, not ornamental.

### 2. Definition of bhakti

**Locus:** *Dvitīyollāsa* opening definition (lines 4996-4997).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** *drute citte praviṣṭā yā [OCR? printed as pravisṭayā] govindākāratā sthirā / sā bhaktir ity abhihitā viśeṣas tv adhunocyate*

**Close English:** “That stable Govinda-shapedness which, having entered a melted mind, is called bhakti; its specific forms are now stated.”

**Engagement:** This is the book’s cleanest definition. Bhakti is neither bare intention nor generalized God-feeling. It is a transformation of mental form: the mind, once melted, receives and stably bears *govindākāratā*. The relative-correlative syntax is exacting: *yā ... sā*. Not anything in a softened mind qualifies; only that specific form does.

What is doing the philosophical work is the sequence: melting, entry, stabilization, naming. The text is phenomenological in grammar but ontological in ambition. It is not merely saying “one experiences God intensely.” It says that the mind becomes configurationally God-shaped, and that this state is what the śāstra means by bhakti.

**Pāṇinian-rich treatment:**

*drute citte* — locative singular pair; “in a melted mind.” *druta* is the past passive participle of the verbal root *dru* in the sense of liquefaction; *citta* is neuter, locative singular. The phrase gives the condition-locus.

*praviṣṭā yā* — feminine nominative singular relative clause: “which, having entered.” The OCR’s *praviṣṭayā* is almost certainly for *praviṣṭā yā*. The participle agrees with the feminine subject to come.

*govindākāratā* — feminine nominative singular. Samāsa-vigraha: *govindasya ākāraḥ* = *govindākāraḥ*; then *govindākārasya bhāvaḥ* = *govindākāratā*. It is not simply “thought of Govinda,” but “the state of having Govinda’s form.”

*sthirā* — nominative feminine singular adjective qualifying *govindākāratā*: stable, non-evanescent.

*sā bhaktiḥ iti abhihitā* — correlative pronoun plus predicate noun: “that is called bhakti.” *abhihitā* is a feminine nominative singular past passive participle; the copula is understood.

*viśeṣaḥ tu adhunā ucyate* — “but the specification is now stated.” Here the finite verb is *ucyate*; *viśeṣaḥ* is nominative singular masculine.

The principal predication is therefore twofold: first, a relative-correlative identification (*yā ... sā bhaktiḥ*); second, a forward transition (*viśeṣas tv adhunocyate*). Kāraka-wise, the mind is the adhikaraṇa-locus in which the entry happens; Govinda-form is the entity predicated; and the act of “calling” is a passive nominal classification, not a causal production claim.

### 3. Bhakti-rasa as manifestation of *parānanda*

**Locus:** *Prathamollāsa*, *sthāyibhāva* and rasa (lines 1955-1966).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** *bhagavān paramānandasvarūpaḥ svayam eva hi / manogatas tadākāro rasatām eti puṣkalam*

**Close English:** “Bhagavān himself, whose very nature is supreme bliss, when present in the mind as that form, fully attains the condition of rasa.”

**Engagement:** This is the central metaphysical compression of the work. Bhakti-rasa is not a merely subjective savor laid over an otherwise neutral God-concept. Bhagavān, precisely as *paramānanda-svarūpa*, enters the mind in reflected form and there becomes *sthāyibhāva* and hence rasa. The editor’s prose immediately following the verse makes the intended structure explicit: *bimba* and *pratibimba* distinguish Bhagavān as *ālambana* from Bhagavān-as-reflected-in-mind as the stable affective form.

The passage therefore commits Madhusūdana to more than devotional psychology. The aesthetic state is ontologically answerable to Bhagavān’s nature. That is why bhakti-rasa can exceed laukika rasa without ceasing to be rasa.

### 4. Not every God-related affect is bhakti-rasa

**Locus:** *Dvitīyollāsa*, exclusion of certain affects (lines 5544-5550 and 5584-5585).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** *ṣaḍ apy ete na viṣayā bhagavadviṣayā na hi / dharmotsāho dayotsāho jugupsā trividhā śamaḥ*  
*īrṣyājabhayajad dveṣo bhagavadviṣayāv api / na bhaktirasatāṃ yātaḥ sākṣād drutivirodhataḥ*

**Close English:** “These six are not directly Bhagavān-directed objects: zeal for dharma, zeal for compassion, the threefold disgust, and tranquility. Even envy-born and fear-born hatred, though directed toward Bhagavān, do not attain the status of bhakti-rasa, because they directly oppose melting.”

**Engagement:** This is where Madhusūdana becomes sharply discriminating. Moral seriousness, compassion, disgust at impurity, tranquility, even obsessive God-directed antagonism: none of these is yet bhakti-rasa as such. The criterion is not superficial theism but *sākṣād druti*.

That matters for the architecture of the book. Madhusūdana is refusing two easy expansions of bhakti: first, the ethical expansion in which every God-adjacent virtue becomes devotion; second, the dramatic expansion in which every intense relation to God, even hostile fixation, counts as bhakti-rasa proper. The work therefore protects *prīti* as non-negotiable.

### 5. Kṛṣṇa-rati and deva-rati are not equivalent

**Locus:** *Dvitīyollāsa* closing polemic (lines 6334-6339, 6365-6369).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** *ratir devādi-viṣayā ... bhāvaḥ prokto raso neti yad uktaṃ rasakovidaiḥ / devāntareṣu jīvatvāt parānandaprakāśanāt / tadyojyaṃ paramānandarūpe na paramātmani*  
*paripūrṇarasā juḍḍharasebhyo bhagavad-ratiḥ / khadyotebhya ivādityaprabheva balavattarā*

**Close English:** “The statement of the connoisseurs of rasa that attachment directed toward deities and the like is called merely a *bhāva*, not a rasa, should be applied to other deities, because they are living beings and do not disclose full supreme bliss; it should not be applied to the Paramātman, whose nature is supreme bliss. Bhagavad-rati, a full rasa, is stronger than the lesser rasas, like the sun’s radiance compared with fireflies.”

**Engagement:** This is one of the most useful passages in the whole text, because it shows where Madhusūdana will not compromise. He accepts the aesthetic commonplace that deva-rati may be only *bhāva*; he then sharply restricts its scope. The restriction depends on ontology: if the object is merely a finite deity, the affect cannot disclose full *parānanda*; if the object is Bhagavān-Kṛṣṇa as Paramātman, the restriction collapses.

This is both a theological hierarchy and a theory of rasa. The force of the analogy with fireflies and the sun is not only quantitative. It marks an ontological disproportion between finite objecthood and Bhagavān as the plenary locus of bliss.

### 6. What rasa-cognition is like

**Locus:** *Tṛtīyollāsa* on the cognition of rasa (lines 6739-6740).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** *nityaṃ sukham abhivyaktaṃ raso ve sa iti śruteḥ / pratītiḥ svaprakāśasya nirvikalpasukhātmikā*

**Close English:** “Since the śruti says ‘rasa indeed is that,’ rasa is eternally manifest bliss; its apprehension, of what is self-luminous, is of the nature of non-conceptual bliss.”

**Engagement:** Here the book’s rasa-theory shows its Advaitin edge. Rasa is not merely an inferentially constructed literary effect. Its apprehension is *svaprakāśa* and *nirvikalpa-sukhātmikā*. Madhusūdana is not denying the mediating role of poetic structure; he is saying that what ultimately appears through it is self-luminous bliss.

This passage is also why the third *ullāsa* cannot be treated as a detachable appendix. Without it, the earlier claims about bhakti as *puruṣārtha* and Bhagavān as *paramānanda* would remain edifying but theoretically underdefended. Here they acquire epistemic form.

## 5. The text's relation to the position

This text sits squarely in the Madhusūdana side of the Padmapāda-Prakāśātman-Vidyāraṇya-Madhusūdana line, but it does so by giving that line a devotional and aesthetic expression rather than by rehearsing the better-known *Advaitasiddhi* polemics. It is therefore not part of the Bhartṛprapañca-Bhāskara-Madhva-Caitanya-Aurobindo realism-recovery trajectory; rather, it is one of the strongest reminders that post-Śaṅkara Advaita itself contains an internally serious theology of Bhagavān and bhakti. That matters because the realism-recovery line cannot simply treat “late Advaita” as a uniformly world-thinning foil.

- On T2: the book is not a direct treatise on *mithyā*, but it is strong evidence against any reading that treats bhakti, Bhagavān, or rasa as negligible pedagogical residue. Madhusūdana’s whole argument depends on the reality of devotional transformation and on Bhagavān as the genuine locus of *parānanda*.
- On T7: this text is only indirect evidence. It does not here stage the destructive dilemma about avidyā in the way the position wants; for that, one must go to Madhusūdana’s more strictly Advaitic works. What it does provide is a compatible phenomenological schema: Bhagavān reflected in the mind becomes stable affective form without ceasing to be rooted in the ontologically primary.
- On T11: this is the strongest point of contact. *Bhakti-rasāyana* offers a genuine ontology of phenomenology. Melted mind, entry of form, stable God-shapedness, manifestation of self-luminous bliss: these are not merely descriptive states but ontological disclosures.
- On T9: the text strongly supports the claim that a purely quiescent or non-relational consummation is not the whole story. Madhusūdana explicitly refuses to let *śama* or generic God-relatedness count as bhakti-rasa; *prīti* toward Bhagavān is structurally indispensable.

Its named or near-named interlocutors are revealing. The work invokes Kumārila and Prābhākara in the third *ullāsa*, uses Śaṅkara’s *Brahma-sūtra-bhāṣya* constructively, and explicitly engages Sāṅkhya, *tārkika* objections, and the rasa-theorists who classify deva-rati as mere *bhāva*. The anonymous *pūrvapakṣin* appears whenever Madhusūdana asks whether bhakti is only a means, whether mokṣa alone is the final *puruṣārtha*, whether all intense God-directed states count equally as bhakti, or whether Kṛṣṇa-rati can be collapsed into ordinary deva-rati.

Where the text resists the position’s synthesis should also be stated plainly. Madhusūdana is not decentralizing the field of divine forms. He sharply subordinates deva-rati to Bhagavad-rati, and he does so by an ontological hierarchy. Nor is he offering the broader realism of Madhva, Vallabha, or Nimbārka; he remains an Advaitin who explains devotional experience through reflection, manifestation, and *parānanda*. The position can use him, but not as a simple ally for plural parity.

## 6. What the user can productively take

- This text gives a first-rate primary citation for the claim that a major Advaitin can treat bhakti not as a concession but as *parama-puruṣārtha*.
- It supplies precise technical vocabulary for T11: *citta-druti*, *govindākāratā*, *sthāyibhāva*, *parānanda*, and *svaprakāśa* are the book’s load-bearing terms.
- It gives the position a disciplined way to distinguish preparatory states from devotion proper. That is useful wherever “everything sincere is bhakti” would blur the argument.
- It forces a real decision about divine hierarchy. Madhusūdana’s claim that deva-rati is only *bhāva* while Bhagavad-rati is full rasa cannot simply be absorbed; it must be either accepted in some restricted sense or argued against.
- It strengthens the anti-caricature case against readings of Advaita that cannot accommodate real devotion, real divine form, and real affective transformation.
- It does not by itself secure the position’s stronger claims about *avidyā* as *bhāvarūpa*. For that, this text should be read alongside Madhusūdana’s stricter polemical Vedānta works rather than made to bear more than it does.
