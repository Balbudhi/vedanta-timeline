# Engagement: Vidyāraṇya, *Vivaraṇa-Prameya-Saṃgraha*

## 1. What the text is

*Vivaraṇa-Prameya-Saṃgraha* is a fourteenth-century Advaita digest by Vidyāraṇya (Mādhava), situated squarely in the Vivaraṇa line of post-Śaṅkara Advaita. The work identifies itself, in its second maṅgala verse, as a *saṅgraha* built out of *bhāṣya*, *ṭīkā*, and *vivaraṇa* material: `bhāṣya-ṭīkā-vivaraṇaṃ tan-nibandhana-saṅgrahaḥ` (opening extracted witness, lines 69-101). That is already enough to say what kind of book this is: not an independent *prakaraṇa* in the mode of the *Pañcadaśī*, but a scholastic compression of an existing commentarial inheritance, traditionally understood as a compendium of Prakāśātman’s *Pañcapādikā-Vivaraṇa* positions. Structurally, the recoverable on-disk witness gives three maṅgala verses and then, in the first *varṇaka* under `atha prathamaṃ sūtram`, a fifteen-verse pedagogical digest on *śravaṇa*, *manana*, *nididhyāsana*, sentence-born *vṛtti*, and the destruction of ignorance. The full chapter or *pariccheda* count of the complete work is [NOT YET RETRIEVED] from the currently readable witness chain.

## 3. Structural walk-through

The opening maṅgala is not ornamental. Maṅgala 1 places the goal immediately in the register of realized all-selfhood: the state that delights beings “here” and, “there,” by *sarvātmabhāva*, shines in the heart-lotus and is entered by renunciants (lines 19-65). Its function is constructive rather than polemical. Before any dispute about method, the text locates Advaitic inquiry in a realized standpoint rather than in merely exegetical technique.

Maṅgalas 2-3 define the genre and the audience (lines 69-145). The text is a digest of layered commentary, and its proper reader is not the novice but the Veda-trained student who still doubts the purport of Vedānta. Polemically, this matters: the book is not presenting itself as devotional exhortation or as first exposure. It is an intervention inside a scholastic community already conversant with *sūtra*, *bhāṣya*, and *vivaraṇa*.

Digests 1-4 state the pedagogical architecture (lines 149-314). Digest 1 gives the familiar triad: one must hear scriptural sentences, reflect by reasoning, and then meditate steadily. Digest 2 sharpens *śravaṇa*: it is not bare hearing but *śakti-tātparya-nirṇaya*, the ascertainment of semantic force and intended purport by the standard indicators beginning with *upakrama*. Digest 3 correspondingly defines *manana* as the cultivation of sentence-supporting reasoning, not free philosophical speculation. Digest 4 folds *nididhyāsana* back into one-pointedness within hearing and reflection themselves. The architecture here is constructive, but it is already aimed against two familiar distortions: anti-rational scripturalism and autonomous rationalism.

Digests 5-7 move from sequence to efficacy (lines 318-443). Reflection is said to remove *asambhāvanā* and *viparīta-bhāvanā* root and branch; hearing is called the more intimate cause of knowledge, but reflection is granted strategic priority in removing resistance. The text then classifies *śānti*, *dānti*, and related virtues under the heading of *itikartavyatā*: they belong to what must be done. This is important for the work’s polemic-architecture. The text is not yet refuting another school by name, but it is quietly refusing a merely propositional Advaita. Knowledge arises through sentences, but not in a psychologically unprepared subject.

Digests 8-12 give the core epistemic mechanism (lines 447-659). Once the seeker is established in all the limbs, a pure mental *vṛtti* arises from the Vedic sentence, with *pratyag-brahmaikya* as its object. In that *vṛtti* there is a *cid-abhivyakti*; that manifestation is itself *brahma-vijñāna* and, at the same time, the destruction of ignorance. The next verses insist that the self manifested in that *vṛtti* is the seer, self-luminous, and Brahman in its own nature. Here the work sits exactly where one expects a Vivaraṇa digest to sit: not at the point of world-metaphysics, but at the point where the sentence-born cognition is made to do liberating work.

Digests 13-15 describe the status of ignorance and the residue of realization (lines 663-789). Ignorance is said to be established only through cognition, likened to darkness feared in daylight, and swallowed by the self itself. What remains is not sheer blankness but the self in its own full Brahman-form, finally accessible only to direct experience, *svānubhūty-eka-gocara*. Polemically, the opening unit ends before any named opponent appears; but the target is already visible. The book wants to defend the claim that sentence-born knowledge is both meaningful and existentially decisive, and that the internal obstacles to its efficacy are removable.

## 4. Load-bearing passages — engaged at depth

### Passage 1

**Locus:** Maṅgala 2, opening extracted witness, lines 69-101.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `bhāṣya-ṭīkā-vivaraṇaṃ tan-nibandhana-saṅgrahaḥ | vyākhyāna-vyākhyeya-bhāva-kleśa-hānāya racyate ||`

**Close English:** “A compendium based on that *bhāṣya*, *ṭīkā*, and *vivaraṇa* is composed for the removal of the difficulty concerning the relation between exposition and what is to be expounded.”

**Engagement:** This verse states the book’s program with unusual candor. The work does not posture as pristine revelation; it acknowledges commentarial layering and makes its own task one of compression and clarification. The phrase `vyākhyāna-vyākhyeya-bhāva-kleśa` is scholastically exact: the problem is not just ignorance of doctrine, but confusion about how the inherited expository apparatus is related to the thing expounded. That is why the book belongs in the Padmapāda-Prakāśātman-Vidyāraṇya line. It is a second-order intervention inside the Vivaraṇa tradition itself.

### Passage 2

**Locus:** Digest 2, opening extracted witness, lines 194-232.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `tatra tāvan muni-śreṣṭhāḥ śravaṇaṃ nāma kevalam | upakramādibhir liṅgaiḥ śakti-tātparya-nirṇayaḥ ||`

**Close English:** “Among these, *śravaṇa* is simply this: the ascertainment, by the indicatory marks beginning with opening statement, of semantic power and intended purport.”

**Engagement:** This is one of the strongest concise statements of what scholastic Advaita means by *śravaṇa*. The gerundive idiom of “one should hear” in the previous digest is now unpacked as a technical act of Mīmāṃsā-style purport-determination. The sentence matters because it blocks a sentimental reading of hearing as pious exposure to scripture. To “hear” is to determine what the sentence can and does mean.

The polemical consequence is internal. A Vedāntin who bypasses *tātparya-nirṇaya* in the name of immediacy is not more direct; he has simply failed to do the first piece of work.

### Passage 3

**Locus:** Digest 8, opening extracted witness, lines 447-485.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `tataḥ sarvāṅga-niṣṭhasya pratyag-brahmaikya-gocarā | yā vṛttir mānasī śuddhā jāyate veda-vākyataḥ ||`

**Close English:** “Then, for one established in all the limbs, there arises from the Vedic sentence a pure mental *vṛtti* whose object is the identity of the inward self and Brahman.”

**Engagement:** This is the load-bearing methodological verse of the retrievable unit. Knowledge does not arrive as an extra-conceptual flash unrelated to language; it arises *veda-vākyataḥ*, from the Vedic sentence. Nor is the sentence’s work merely eliminative in the thin sense of pointing away from itself. It produces a determinate *mānasī vṛtti* whose intentional object is `pratyag-brahmaikya`.

The grammar matters. `jāyate` takes as its subject `yā vṛttiḥ`; the sentence is not simply a condition but the explicit source, `veda-vākyataḥ`. `sarvāṅga-niṣṭhasya` marks adhikāra: this is not any mind whatsoever, but one established in the full preparatory apparatus. The compound `pratyag-brahmaikya-gocarā` is also exact: the *vṛtti* does not have Brahman and self as two objects, but their identity as its field.

**Pāṇinian-rich treatment:** `tataḥ` is the discourse-marker “then,” signaling a result after the prior disciplines. `sarvāṅga-niṣṭhasya` is a genitive tatpuruṣa, “of one established in all the limbs,” where the genitive marks the qualified subject for whom the cognition arises. `pratyag-brahmaikya-gocarā` resolves as `pratyak ca brahma ca tayoḥ aikyaṃ gocaro yasyāḥ`: “whose object-field is the identity of the inward self and Brahman.” `vṛttiḥ` is the nominative feminine subject. `mānasī` and `śuddhā` are co-predicative qualifiers. `jāyate` is the principal verb, middle voice, with `veda-vākyataḥ` as ablative source: the *vṛtti* comes into being from the Vedānta sentence. Kāraka-wise, the crucial relations are source (`veda-vākyataḥ`), qualified possessor (`sarvāṅga-niṣṭhasya`), and subject (`vṛttiḥ`). The sentence’s whole force is to secure the thesis that liberating cognition is linguistically generated without being reduced to mere verbalism.

### Passage 4

**Locus:** Digest 9, opening extracted witness, lines 489-531.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `tasyāṃ yā cid-abhivyaktiḥ svataḥ-siddhā ca śāṅkarī | tad eva brahma-vijñānaṃ tad evājñāna-nāśanam ||`

**Close English:** “The manifestation of consciousness in that, self-established and Śaṅkarite, that alone is Brahman-knowledge; that alone is the destruction of ignorance.”

**Engagement:** The repetition `tad eva ... tad eva` is the doctrinal hinge. The verse does not say that Brahman-knowledge later causes ignorance-destruction as a second event. It identifies the manifestation of consciousness in the *vṛtti* with both. This is why the opening unit is more than pedagogical advice. It is a compressed theory of liberating cognition.

The adjective `śāṅkarī` matters too. The verse is not merely describing cognition in general; it is positioning this account as recognizably Śaṅkarite, though in a specifically Vivaraṇa key. What is being defended is a lineage reading of how sentence, cognition, and liberation are related.

### Passage 5

**Locus:** Digests 13-15, opening extracted witness, lines 663-789.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `pratītyā kevalaṃ siddhaṃ divābhītāndhakāravat | abhūtaṃ vastu-gatyā eva svātmanā grasate svayam || ... evaṃ rūpāvaśeṣas tu svānubhūty-eka-gocaraḥ | yena siddhyati viprendrās tad dhi vijñānam aiśvaram ||`

**Close English:** “It is established only through cognition, like darkness feared in daylight; unreal by the very nature of the case, it is swallowed by the self itself ... But the remainder in this form is accessible only to one’s own direct experience; by that indeed the lordly knowledge is accomplished.”

**Engagement:** This cluster is the point at which the opening witness becomes philosophically awkward for any overhasty attempt to recruit it for a strong positive ontology of ignorance. On the face of these verses, ignorance is not being described as a robustly real entity; it is cognized, compared to feared darkness in daylight, and then swallowed by the self. Whatever later Vivaraṇa materials may do with *bhāvarūpa avidyā*, this opening unit is framed soteriologically rather than ontologically.

At the same time, the ending blocks a flat reduction of liberation to mere negation. What remains is a `rūpāvaśeṣa`, and it is accessible only as `svānubhūty-eka-gocara`. The final emphasis is not on public dialectic but on first-person realization. The scholastic method culminates in something that is not exhaustively scholasticizable.

## 5. The text’s relation to the position

This text belongs, unmistakably, on the Padmapāda-Prakāśātman-Vidyāraṇya-Madhusūdana line rather than on the realism-recovery trajectory of Bhartṛprapañca-Bhāskara-Madhva-Caitanya-Aurobindo. Its function in the corpus is to show the internal machinery of Vivaraṇa Advaita: how scriptural sentences generate liberating cognition, how *manana* removes resistance, and how *brahma-vijñāna* and *ajñāna-nāśa* are coordinated. Relative to Yāmuna-Rāmānuja-Vedānta Deśika, Vallabha, and Nimbārka, the retrievable opening does not yet stage direct controversy. By name, it refutes no opponent at all. The only visible adversary is an implied internal *pūrvapakṣin* who would either misconstrue *śravaṇa*, detach reasoning from scripture, or deny the efficacy of sentence-born knowledge.

On T2, the bearing is limited. Nothing in the engaged unit licenses the vulgar equation *mithyā = asat*; but equally, nothing here yet furnishes the realist recovery argument the user wants. This is not a world-affirmation text in the manner of Aurobindo, Bhāskara, or Madhva. It is a methodological defense of Advaita cognition.

On T7, the bearing is mixed and should be handled carefully. The larger Vivaraṇa school is indeed central to the claim that Brahman can function as both *āśraya* and *viṣaya* of *avidyā*. But the currently retrievable unit does not explicitly state that thesis. What it does say is that the manifestation in the *vṛtti* is itself *ajñāna-nāśana*, and that ignorance is like feared darkness in daylight. So this opening unit is not a strong citation for the full positive ontology of *bhāvarūpa avidyā*; if anything, it resists that use unless supplemented by other Vivaraṇa materials.

On T11, the text is much more directly useful. It gives a disciplined ontology of phenomenology in the precise sense that matters for the project: cognition is not merely epistemic surface, because the sentence-born *vṛtti* is the locus in which consciousness manifests as liberating knowledge. The movement is from semantic purport, to mental form, to self-luminous realization. That is not Aurobindonian involution-evolution, but it is a rigorous account of how phenomenological transformation can be ontologically load-bearing.

On T9, again, the support is indirect at best. The opening unit does not articulate unity-in-apparent-multiplicity, nor does it answer the Vaiṣṇava complaint that non-duality evacuates manifestation. If the user wants this text for that debate, the present engagement only shows where the later Vivaraṇa defense begins: with cognition, not yet with cosmology or divine play.

## 6. What the user can productively take

The immediate value of this text is not that it gives the position a ready-made citation for realism. It does not. Its value is that it supplies the internal scholastic grammar of the Vivaraṇa school with unusual compactness.

First, it gives a clean primary-source statement that *śravaṇa* means *tātparya-nirṇaya*, not passive listening. Second, it gives a concise formula for *manana* as `vākyānugrāhaka-nyāya-śīlana`: reasoning in service of the sentence, not as a rival source. Third, it gives a very strong citation for the claim that sentence-born *vṛtti* is the operative vehicle of liberating knowledge. Fourth, it lets the user state, with textual backing, that *brahma-vijñāna* and *ajñāna-nāśa* are not two separable episodes.

The caution is equally important. This opening unit should not be overused as evidence for the stronger constructive claims the user wants from the Vivaraṇa school, especially the positive ontological status of ignorance. For that, one needs either later retrievable portions of this very work or other Vidyāraṇya and Madhusūdana loci. So the corpus changes here in a precise way: the text strengthens the project’s account of Advaitic method and cognition, but it also forces discipline about where the stronger metaphysical claims really need to be sourced.
