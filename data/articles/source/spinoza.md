# Spinoza — A Reader's Companion to the *Ethica*

*A guided reading of the* Ethica Ordine Geometrico Demonstrata *(1677), with the Latin alongside, oriented toward the user's thesis: one Real said in many senses, of which Aurobindo's triple-status supermind, Deleuze's univocity, and Gebser's aperspectival-integral are equiprimordial articulations.*

---

## Reader's Orientation

### Bio: lens-grinder of The Hague

Baruch Spinoza was born in Amsterdam on **24 November 1632**, into the Sephardic *Nação* that had fled the Iberian Inquisitions. Educated in the *Talmud Torah* school of the Portuguese Synagogue (Hebrew Bible, Maimonides, Crescas, ibn Ezra, Gersonides), then in Latin under the ex-Jesuit Franciscus van den Enden (c. 1654), he absorbed Descartes, Hobbes, the late Scholastics, and the Collegiant-Mennonite-Quaker sectarians who became his closest friends.

In **July 1656**, at 23, the synagogue council pronounced against him the harshest *cherem* in the surviving Amsterdam register — excommunication, anathema, prohibition on contact within four cubits or on reading his writings. The *cherem* document does not list the doctrines, but the surrounding evidence (Juan de Prado, the Inquisitor Solano y Robles, the lost *Apologia*) converges on his already-formed conviction that scripture is a human document, the soul not separately immortal, and "God" not the personal creator of the *Tanakh*. The Amsterdam *Nação*, a recently-emancipated diaspora on the sufferance of the Calvinist States, had its own reasons for severity.

Spinoza did not contest. He took the Latinized name *Benedictus*, lived as a lens-grinder — Amsterdam, then **Rijnsburg** (c. 1660), **Voorburg** (c. 1663), finally **The Hague** (1670 to his death) — supported by his trade and a small annuity from Simon de Vries. He turned down the Heidelberg chair in 1673 to retain philosophical independence. He died on **21 February 1677**, of a lung disease aggravated by glass-dust inhalation, at 44.

### The corpus arc: TIE → KV → PPC → TTP → Ethica → TP

Six titles, in approximate compositional order:

**1. *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione*** (TIE, c. 1661, unfinished). The methodological clearing: the young Spinoza thinks his way out of the *bona fortuna* of wealth, fame, and pleasure into the search for the *summum bonum* — union of mind with nature. Autobiographical preface to the *Ethica* in everything but name.

**2. *Korte Verhandeling*** (KV, c. 1660–62, Dutch). An early sketch of the *Ethica* in scholastic-dialogue form; recovered 1862.

**3. *Renati des Cartes Principiorum Philosophiæ*** (PPC, 1663) — the only book Spinoza published under his name, a geometrical exposition of Descartes with an appended *Cogitata Metaphysica*.

**4. *Tractatus theologico-politicus*** (TTP, **1670**, anonymous). Immediately banned, denounced by the Synod of Dordrecht in 1673. The founding document of modern biblical criticism (prophecy as imagination, miracles as natural events misdescribed, scripture as historically composed) and the first systematic argument for *libertas philosophandi*.

**5. *Ethica ordine geometrico demonstrata*** (composed 1661–75; withheld after the de Witt lynchings in 1672; published in the *Opera Posthuma*, **1677**). The mature system. Five parts: De Deo (I), De Natura et Origine Mentis (II), De Origine et Natura Affectuum (III), De Servitute Humana (IV), De Potentia Intellectus, seu de Libertate Humana (V).

**6. *Tractatus politicus*** (TP, 1675–77, unfinished). Mature political philosophy, closer to Machiavelli and Hobbes than the TTP.

Of the seventy-eight surviving letters, two are decisive for our reading: Letter 64 (Schuller, 1675), where Spinoza names the *intellectus infinitus* as the *modus infinitus immediatus* of thought; and Letter 66 (Tschirnhaus, 1675), where he confesses he cannot deduce the existence of the other infinite attributes from the two we know.

### The three load-bearing structures

Within the *Ethica*, three structures bear the weight of everything else.

**(i) Substance / attribute / mode.** Pars I builds the architecture: substance is one (Prop. 14); attributes are infinite, each expressing the whole essence under its own form (Def. IV, Prop. 10 schol.); modes are affections of attributes (Def. V), and the totality of modes is *natura naturata* (Prop. 29 schol.). This is the Western articulation of one-Real-said-in-many-senses — structural ancestor of Deleuze's univocity and of *acintya-bhedābheda*.

**(ii) The *intellectus infinitus Dei*.** E1p16 introduces it (*omnia, quæ sub intellectum infinitum cadere possunt*); E1p21–22 specify its modal status (immediate infinite mode of thought); Letter 64 confirms; E2p7c articulates the parallelism: *quicquid ex infinita Dei natura sequitur formaliter, id omne ex Dei idea eodem ordine eademque connexione sequitur in Deo objective*. The infinite intellect contains, as ideas, the formal essences of all things in all attributes simultaneously — the closest pre-Aurobindonian Western articulation of the divine experiencing all registers at once, non-destructively.

**(iii) *Amor Dei intellectualis*.** E5p36+cor+schol: the mind's intellectual love of God *is* the love by which God loves himself, *quatenus per essentiam humanæ Mentis explicari potest*. *Jñāna by identity* in Latin: the knower–known–knowing distinction collapses.

### The three decisive divergences

**(a) Necessitarianism rules out *līlā*.** Spinoza's God acts *ex solis suæ naturæ legibus* (E1p17); E1p33 closes the modal margin entirely. The *svātantrya* of Trika is unsayable in Spinoza's grammar by Def. VII alone.

**(b) *Deus sive Natura* is too tight.** Pars IV Praefatio's *Deum, seu Naturam* is convertibility, not perspectival distinction. Brahman is not nature *simpliciter*; manifestation is the play of *svātantrya*; the distinction between Saccidānanda-as-such and manifestation is the room for *līlā*. Spinoza closes that distinction.

**(c) Impersonalism.** What is eternal in the mind (E5p23) is the impersonal *eternum quoddam* of adequate ideas, not the *caitya puruṣa*. No story-bearer, no trans-incarnational continuity, no person who deepens.

### Don Garrett's contemporary revival

The third divergence is most softened by recent scholarship. **Don Garrett** (NYU), in *Nature and Necessity in Spinoza's Philosophy* (OUP 2018) and "Spinoza's Conatus Argument" (2002), has been recovering — against the Hegelian shadow — a Spinoza in whom finite modes are not absorbed into substance but enjoy real individuation. Hegel's "acosmism" is a misdiagnosis. The *conatus* doctrine (E3p6–7) is the doctrine of real individuality; modes are *quasi-substances*. We give Garrett substantial space, with a further section on Melamed (Hopkins), Della Rocca (Yale), and Macherey (Lille).

### The surrounding texts that matter for this project

The *Ethica* is the center of gravity, but three surrounding works matter if the user's thesis is the lens. The *Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione* matters because it supplies the methodological mood of the whole system: purification of confused cognition, ascent from mutilated perception to adequate understanding, and the conviction that salvation is inseparable from a reform of knowing. The unfinished text gives the lived entrance into the geometrical project that the *Ethica* later hardens into definitions, axioms, and propositions.

The *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* matters because it clarifies how imagination, prophecy, scripture, and communal religion fit into Spinoza's architecture. Chapter I on prophecy, Chapter IV on divine law, and Chapter XIV on the universal faith show that Spinoza is not only building an ontology but also sorting registers of discourse. For the user's T1 through T4, this is crucial: prophecy is not false because it is imaginative; it is true at a lower register, practical and affective rather than metaphysical.

The *Tractatus Politicus* matters because it extends *conatus* into the collective field. A state, for Spinoza, is not a covenant over and above power; it is organized power itself, a composition of finite strivings seeking stability. This bears directly on the user's political register, because it shows how far Spinoza can go toward an account of plural embodied order without ever introducing *līlā*, descent, or supramental transformation. It is the political completion of the same necessitarian grammar.

## PART I — *Ethica* Pars I (De Deo)

Eight definitions, seven axioms, thirty-six propositions, an Appendix. The *more geometrico* is not decoration; the architecture earned here is presupposed by every later part.

### Definitiones I–VIII (G. II/45)

I. By *causa sui* I understand that whose essence involves existence; or, that whose nature cannot be conceived except as existing.

II. A thing is said to be finite in its own kind which can be limited by another of the same nature. For example, a body is called finite because we always conceive another greater. So a thought is limited by another thought. But a body is not limited by a thought, nor a thought by a body.

III. By *substantia* I understand that which is in itself, and is conceived through itself: that is, that whose concept does not require the concept of another thing from which it must be formed.

IV. By *attributum* I understand that which the intellect perceives of substance as constituting its essence.

V. By *modus* I understand the affections of substance, that is, that which is in another, through which it is also conceived.

VI. By *Deus* I understand a being absolutely infinite — that is, a substance consisting of infinite attributes, of which each one expresses an eternal and infinite essence.

VII. That thing is said to be *free* which exists from the sole necessity of its nature, and is determined to action by itself alone.

VIII. By *æternitas* I understand existence itself, insofar as it is conceived to follow necessarily from the definition alone of an eternal thing.

**Reading.** Def. II's "finite in its own kind" prepares the attribute doctrine: extension is not limited by thought, nor thought by extension; they share *nihil commune* (E1ax5). Defs. III and V generate a strict bipartite ontology — *in se* (substance) or *in alio* (mode); no third kind of being.

Def. IV is load-bearing. *Per attributum intelligo id, quod intellectus de substantia percipit, tanquam ejusdem essentiam constituens*. The attribute is what *intellectus percipit as* constituting substance's essence: the same one substance, under thought *res cogitans*, under extension *res extensa*, with *infinita attributa* of which we know two. The attributes are not parts (else composite, contra Def. III), not numerically distinct from substance (else many substances), but perceptually-irreducibly distinct from each other. The Sanskrit name for this structure is *acintya-bhedābheda*; the Latin name is *Definitio IV*. The "intellect" here is not psychologistic: in the limit (third kind of knowledge), it is God's infinite intellect of which the human mind is a part (E2p11c). Adequate cognition (E2def4) precludes any skeptical wedge between *what is perceived as constituting* and *what constitutes*.

Def. VI: *Deus* as substance of infinite attributes, each *exprimit* an eternal and infinite essence. The vocabulary of expression is laid down here before any proposition — Deleuze's reading is not imposition but development of Def. VI.

Def. VII inscribes our first divergence: *libertas* is existence-from-the-necessity-of-one's-own-nature. Spinoza's God is *libera causa* but not free in the Trika sense of *svātantrya*. By Def. VII alone, no *līlā*-room.

### Axiomata I–VII (G. II/46)

I. Whatever is, is either in itself or in another.

II. What cannot be conceived through another, must be conceived through itself.

III. From a given determinate cause an effect necessarily follows; and conversely, if no determinate cause is given, it is impossible that an effect should follow.

IV. The knowledge of an effect depends on, and involves, the knowledge of its cause.

V. Things which have nothing in common with each other cannot be understood through each other; that is, the conception of the one does not involve the conception of the other.

VI. A true idea must agree with its *ideatum* (its object).

VII. Whatever can be conceived as not existing, its essence does not involve existence.

**Reading.** *Ax. I* with Defs. III–V generates the bipartite ontology. *Ax. III* is PSR in causal form — engine of the necessitarianism culminating in Prop. 33; Della Rocca's PSR-reading takes its support here. *Ax. IV* and *Ax. VI* set up the rationalist epistemology of Pars II Prop. 40 schol. 2 and the apex of Pars V. *Ax. V* — strict separation of things with nothing in common — does the work in Pars II: thought and extension share nothing, neither can cause the other, both are parallel expressions under different attributes. E2p7 is already implicit. None of these is innocent: the axioms are philosophical decisions, and granting them is already most of the way to granting the system.

### Propositio V — uniqueness within an attribute (G. II/48)

Proposition V. *In the nature of things there cannot be given two or more substances of the same nature, that is, of the same attribute.*

*Demonstration.* If there were several distinct substances, they would have to be distinguished from each other either by a diversity of attributes or by a diversity of affections. If only by a diversity of attributes, then it will be granted that there is given but one substance of the same attribute. If by a diversity of affections, then since substance is prior in nature to its affections (Prop. 1), setting the affections aside and considering substance in itself — that is (Def. 3 and Ax. 6), considered truly — it could not be conceived as distinct from another; that is, there cannot be given several substances, but only one. Q.E.D.

**Reading.** Prop. 5 is the engine of monism. Affections cannot individuate substances (substance is *prior natura* to its affections, E1p1); all multiplicity lives in the modal register. Substances differ, if at all, in attributes; but attribute *just is* essence under one form (Def. IV), so two substances of the same attribute would be the same substance.

This is *not* the Madhva move. Madhva's *viśeṣa* secures real distinction within substance before modal differentiation; Spinoza has no *viśeṣa*. Distinction within substance is confined to attribute-level (intellect-relative, Def. IV) and mode-level (downstream). Spinoza's monism is structurally tighter than any Indian non-dualism: no *bhāvarūpa* difference at substance-level. Garrett's recovery of individuation has to happen at the modal level, because that is the only level where Spinoza permits real plurality.

### Propositio XI — *Deus necessario existit* (G. II/52–54)

Proposition XI. *Deus* — that is, a substance consisting of infinite attributes, of which each one expresses an eternal and infinite essence — necessarily exists.

*Demonstration.* If you deny this, conceive, if it be possible, that God does not exist. Then (by Ax. 7) his essence does not involve existence. But this (by Prop. 7) is absurd. Therefore God necessarily exists. Q.E.D.

The proposition is followed by three further demonstrations:

*Otherwise.* For each thing there must be assigned a cause or reason, both for its existence and for its non-existence. (...) Since, therefore, the reason or cause which would annul the divine existence cannot be given outside the divine nature, it would have to be given necessarily, if God did not exist, in his own nature, which would consequently involve a contradiction. But to affirm this of an absolutely infinite and supremely perfect Being is absurd; therefore neither in God nor outside God is any cause or reason given which would annul his existence, and consequently God necessarily exists.

*Otherwise.* To be able not to exist is impotence; conversely, to be able to exist is potency (self-evident). If, then, what now necessarily exists is nothing but finite beings, finite beings are more potent than an absolutely infinite Being — which is absurd. Therefore either nothing exists, or an absolutely infinite Being necessarily exists. But we exist, either in ourselves or in another which necessarily exists (Ax. 1 and Prop. 7). Therefore an absolutely infinite Being — that is (Def. 6), God — necessarily exists. Q.E.D.

And the *a priori* scholium that adds a fourth proof:

*Scholium.* In this last demonstration I wished to show God's existence *a posteriori*, that the demonstration might be more easily perceived; not, however, because God's existence does not follow *a priori* from the same foundation. For since *posse existere* is potency, it follows that the more reality belongs to a thing's nature, the more powers it has of itself to exist; and so an absolutely infinite Being — God — has of himself an absolutely infinite power of existing, and therefore exists absolutely.

**Reading.** Four arguments converge on *necessitas existendi*. First: ontological *modus tollens* on Ax. 7 — God's essence (Def. VI) is absolutely infinite, so denying existence contradicts Prop. 7. Second: internalized PSR — no reason for God's non-existence can lie outside (Prop. 14) or inside (supreme perfection) God. Third: reductio — finite beings exist; if only the finite existed, finite would exceed infinite in actuality, absurd. The scholium converts *a posteriori* to *a priori*: more reality entails more power-to-exist.

What matters is the convergence. Spinoza's God is the being whose essence *involves* existence (Def. I, *causa sui*); there is no contingency-margin from the start. The proof of God's existence *is* the proof that everything following from God's nature is necessary. The link Prop. 11 → Prop. 33 is rigorous: necessitarianism cannot be softened from inside the system.

### Propositio XIV — *Deus est unicus* (G. II/56)

Proposition XIV. *Apart from God no substance can be given, nor conceived.*

*Demonstration.* Since God is an absolutely infinite being, of whom no attribute that expresses substance's essence can be denied (Def. 6), and who necessarily exists (Prop. 11): if any substance besides God were given, it would have to be explained through some attribute of God, and so two substances of the same attribute would exist, which (Prop. 5) is absurd. Therefore no substance can be given outside God, and consequently none can even be conceived.

*Corollary I.* From this it most clearly follows: (1) that God is unique — that is, there is in the nature of things but one substance, and it is absolutely infinite.

*Corollary II.* It follows: (2) that *res extensa* and *res cogitans* are either attributes of God or (Ax. 1) affections of attributes of God.

**Reading.** Cor. I is what Lessing in the *Pantheismusstreit* (1783–86) and Goethe gather as *Deus sive Natura*. Spinoza uses the formula explicitly only at Pars IV Praefatio. The *sive* is convertibility: there is no *natura* that is not God.

But Cor. II keeps the disjunction alive: *res extensa* and *res cogitans* are *either* attributes *or* affections of attributes. Extension is not a thing-among-things; it is one of infinitely many ways the divine essence is expressed. This is closer to Trika *prakāśa-vimarśa* — world internal to consciousness without consciousness being a thing — than to naturalistic God-=-universe-of-bodies. Hold both: Spinoza's identification is too tight to allow *līlā* (divergence on freedom); his "nature" is not physics-plus-God (convergence on architecture).

### Propositio XV + Scholium — *Quicquid est, in Deo est* (G. II/56–60)

Proposition XV. *Whatever is, is in God, and without God nothing can either be or be conceived.*

*Demonstration.* No substance can be given or conceived apart from God (Prop. 14), that is (Def. 3), nothing which is in itself and conceived through itself. But modes (Def. 5) can neither be nor be conceived without substance; whence they can be only in the divine nature, and only through it conceived. But nothing is given besides substances and modes (Ax. 1). Therefore nothing can either be or be conceived without God. Q.E.D.

The Scholium opens:

*Scholium.* There are those who imagine God in the likeness of a man, consisting of body and mind, and subject to passions; but how far these wander from the true knowledge of God is sufficiently established from what has already been demonstrated.

**Reading.** The Scholium defends *omnia in Deo* against crude pantheism. Extension belongs to God *as attribute* — the whole essence under that attribute (Def. IV); this does not make God a body, because *body* is a mode of extension, not extension itself. The scholium demolishes the anthropomorphisms that ascribe limbs, parts, location, divisibility — categories applicable only to modes of extension, not to the attribute.

*Quicquid est, in Deo est* is not a flattening of the divine into the cosmos but the closure of the cosmos within the divine — convergent with T11 and with the discipline that gods are real modalities, not anthropomorphic agents-among-agents.

### Propositio XVI — infinity follows infinitely (G. II/60)

Proposition XVI. *From the necessity of the divine nature, infinite things in infinite modes — that is, all things which can fall under an infinite intellect — must follow.*

*Demonstration.* This proposition must be evident to anyone who attends to the fact that the intellect concludes from the given definition of any thing many properties which truly follow necessarily from that definition (that is, from the very essence of the thing); and the more so, the more reality the definition of the thing expresses, that is, the more reality the essence of the defined thing involves. Since the divine nature has absolutely infinite attributes (Def. 6), each of which expresses an infinite essence in its own kind, from its necessity infinite things in infinite modes — that is, all things that can fall under an infinite intellect — must necessarily follow.

**Reading.** *Quæ sub intellectum infinitum cadere possunt* — "what can fall under an infinite intellect." This introduces the *intellectus infinitus*, the *immediate infinite mode* of thought (E1p21–22; Letter 64 to Schuller, July 1675). It contains, as ideas, the formal essences of all things in all attributes — the closest pre-Aurobindonian articulation of the divine experiencing all registers at once. *Infinita infinitis modis*: not finite-many but *infinite in infinite ways*, exhausting what an infinite intellect can think. Structural ancestor of what Aurobindo calls *vijñāna* in its comprehending poise.

What Spinoza's infinite intellect is *not* yet: *Cit-Śakti*. It is logical-comprehensive containment, not living self-luminous power; the *ānanda*-dimension is not articulated. The architecture is there, the phenomenology is not.

The demonstration's engine — *ex data cujuscunque rei definitione plures proprietates intellectus concludit, quæ revera ex eadem ... necessario sequuntur* — is a rationalist commitment: geometry of essences = geometry of being. Della Rocca will read this as unrestricted PSR; whatever the diagnosis, *real* and *intelligible* are tightly identified. This is what makes E2p7's parallelism a necessary consequence rather than a happy coincidence.

### Propositio XVII + Scholium — God acts from his own laws (G. II/61–63)

Proposition XVII. *God acts from the laws of his nature alone, and is compelled by no one.*

*Demonstration.* From the necessity of the divine nature alone — or (which is the same) from the laws of his nature alone — infinitely many things absolutely follow, as we just showed in Prop. 16; and in Prop. 15 we demonstrated that without God nothing can be or be conceived, but that all things are in God. Therefore nothing can be outside him by which he could be determined or compelled to act; and so God acts from the laws of his nature alone and is compelled by no one. Q.E.D.

*Scholium.* (...) For it follows from the sole perfection of the divine nature that God can be compelled by no one, because it follows from God's very nature that God acts from the laws of his own nature alone. But if God could produce things in another way, or by another order, than they have been produced, it would follow that God's intellect and his will could be different from his essence, which is absurd.

**Reading.** Prop. 17 is *libera causa* in Spinoza's strict, anti-voluntarist sense: freedom as absence of external constraint, not as *svātantrya*. The scholium makes it explicit: if God could have produced otherwise, God's intellect and will would be other than his essence — absurd, given unity-of-substance.

This is where Leibniz, fifty years later, will insert the modal margin Spinoza closes (compossibility). Aurobindo goes further: the divine *chooses this play freely from inexhaustible self-generating delight*, the choosing constitutive of the divine. Spinoza leaves no margin.

### Propositio XXV + Corollary + Scholium — God is cause of essence as well as existence (G. II/68)

Proposition XXV. *God is the efficient cause not only of the existence of things but also of their essence.*

*Demonstration.* If you deny this, then God is not the cause of the essence of things; and so (Ax. 4) the essence of things can be conceived without God; but this (Prop. 15) is absurd. Therefore God is the cause of the essence of things as well. Q.E.D.

*Scholium.* This proposition follows more clearly from Prop. 16. For from it follows that from the given divine nature both the essence and the existence of things must necessarily be concluded; and, in a word, in the same sense in which God is said to be the cause of himself, he must also be said to be the cause of all things, as will appear still more clearly from the following corollary.

*Corollary.* Particular things are nothing but affections of God's attributes — that is, modes by which God's attributes are expressed in a certain and determinate way.

**Reading.** The Scholium makes explicit that in the same sense God is *causa sui*, God is cause of all things — *causa immanens* in its strongest articulation. Macherey will build out from this scholium: causation is internal-productive, the cause *in* its effects as their being.

The Corollary is the clearest statement on *res particulares*: *nihil sunt, nisi* affections of God's attributes. Read deflationarily, this dissolves the individual that our psychic-being doctrine requires. But Garrett's revival presses the *certo et determinato modo*: each finite mode is the divine attribute in a specific determination, with its own essence (E1p25) and *conatus* (E3p7). The "*nihil sunt, nisi*" is identificational, not subtractive — particular things are nothing *other than* the divine attributes in determinate self-expression.

### Propositio XXIX + Scholium — *natura naturans / naturata* (G. II/71)

Proposition XXIX. *In the nature of things nothing contingent is given; rather all things have been determined from the necessity of the divine nature to exist and operate in a certain way.*

*Scholium.* Before I proceed further, here I wish to explain — or rather to remind — what we are to understand by *Natura Naturans* and what by *Natura Naturata*. For from what precedes I think it already established that by *Natura Naturans* we are to understand that which is in itself and is conceived through itself — that is, such attributes of substance as express an eternal and infinite essence — that is (by Coroll. 1 of Prop. 14 and Coroll. 2 of Prop. 17), God insofar as he is considered as a free cause. By *Natura Naturata*, on the other hand, I understand all that which follows from the necessity of God's nature, that is, of each of God's attributes, that is, all the modes of God's attributes insofar as they are considered as things which are in God and which without God can neither be nor be conceived.

**Reading.** Prop. 29 denies contingency outright: everything is necessitated by the divine nature. The scholium gives the architectural distinction. *Natura naturans* — God qua free cause, substance under its attributes. *Natura naturata* — all modes considered as things-in-God. Both are God; they are God under two aspects of God's own self-causation. The Latin grammar — gerundive *naturans* (active, in-the-doing) vs. perfect-passive *naturata* (having-been-done) — captures this as temporality of self-expression, not hierarchy. Macherey will return to this.

Structural homology with Bhāskara's *kāraṇa-/kārya-Brahman* and with Pratyabhijñā's *prakāśa-vimarśa* doublet is striking. *Naturans* ≈ *prakāśa* (active luminous self-affirmation); *naturata* ≈ *vimarśa-as-creative-display* — but stripped of freedom-to-self-veil. The Spinozistic *naturata* unfolds *necessarily*; the Trika *vimarśa* unfolds *spanda*-ously. Take the architecture; supplement with *spanda* that Spinoza forecloses.

### Propositio XXXIII + Scholia — the necessity of all things (G. II/73–76)

Proposition XXXIII. *Things could not have been produced by God in any other way nor in any other order than they have been produced.*

*Scholium I.* Although by all this it is clearer than the noonday sun that things have been produced by God with supreme perfection, since they have followed necessarily from a supremely perfect nature, yet, in order to remove the envy of men who try to ascribe some imperfection to God, the most perfect Being, I shall now undertake the refutation of those who measure the necessity of things by another standard.

*Scholium II.* By this we have clearly shown what truth is, what necessity is. God's will, which is eternal and infinite, could not have willed this or that. (...) Next, it follows for the same reason for which will and intellect, of the kind people are accustomed to imagine, are denied of God, that we may not similarly say that God acts from freedom of will.

**Reading.** The strongest necessitarianism in Western philosophy before German Idealism. Hegel reads it as *Substanz* needing to become *Subjekt*; Schelling uses it as foil for the *Freiheitsschrift*.

Read carefully: Prop. 33 is not external dictation but internal necessitation. God's nature *is* the necessitating ground; one substance expresses itself completely, and the expression is necessary because it is the substance's own nature. Garrett stresses this internality. The disagreement is sharper for it: we hold that the substance's own nature *includes* freedom-to-be-otherwise — *svātantrya* as constitutive of *Cit-Śakti*, not contingency added but essential feature. Spinoza closes this off; the user's position holds the divine nature is the freely-self-determining ground.

Scholium I refutes Cartesian-Suarezian voluntarism (things necessary because God willed them so, willing itself contingent). The Spinozistic God could not have made 2+2=5. The user's position would incorporate both — necessity at the level of the divine essence, freedom at the level of self-determining actualization — but that combinatorial move is unsayable in Spinoza.

### Appendix to Pars I — the critique of teleology (G. II/77–83)

The Appendix is one of the most rhetorically powerful passages in early modern philosophy. It opens by recapitulating the achievements of Pars I, then turns polemical:

In these I have explained the nature of God and his properties: that he necessarily exists; that he is unique; that he is and acts from the sole necessity of his own nature; that he is the free cause of all things, and how; that all things are in God and so depend on him that without him they can neither be nor be conceived; and finally that all things have been predetermined by God — not indeed from freedom of will or absolute good pleasure, but from God's absolute nature, that is, his infinite power.

Spinoza then diagnoses the great prejudice that has obscured these truths from the philosophical and theological tradition: the projection of *causæ finales* — final causes — onto a nature that operates by efficient necessity:

(...) It follows, first, that men think themselves free because they are conscious of their volitions and appetites, and are unaware of the causes by which they are disposed to appetition and willing — because they are ignorant of them, and do not even dream of them. It follows, secondly, that men do everything for the sake of an end; namely, for the sake of utility, which they desire. (...) Hence it has come to pass that they have undertaken to explain natural causes only by appeal to final causes.

The Appendix then performs a brilliant genealogical analysis: men attribute purpose to nature because they themselves act from purpose; they construct gods who act for ends because their own actions are end-directed; the gods become the projection of the human teleological imagination onto a nature that, in its own terms, is non-teleological. The Appendix culminates in an attack on the categories of *bonum/malum*, *ordo/confusio*, *pulchrum/turpe*, *laus/vituperium*, *peccatum/meritum*: all of these are imaginative projections, not features of the world *sub specie aeternitatis*.

**Reading.** *Suarum volitionum suique appetitus sunt conscii, et de causis, a quibus disponuntur ad appetendum et volendum, ignari* — conscious of volitions, ignorant of their determining causes. One of the deepest critiques of folk-psychological freedom in modern philosophy. The Appendix continues with the attack on *causæ finales* — projection of purpose onto a nature operating by efficient necessity.

Embrace the critique at its register — exoteric freedom is illusion, *jīva-as-doer* is *abhimāna* — but read it Aurobindonically: the surface-being's *cit* mistakes its *adhibhautika* operations for its *parātparā* willing. Spinoza's diagnosis is correct at one register; it does not allow a register at which the divine's *willing* manifests as Brahman's *willed self-ignorance* in *līlā*. The ignorance is itself, on our reading, *bhāvarūpa* self-veiling.

The attack on teleology is where Spinoza is most decisively not Aurobindonian: no *parā prakṛti*, no Self-disclosing *Saccidānanda* directing the cosmos toward an end. Only efficient causes through the modal ladder. (Garrett, in "Teleology in Spinoza and Early Modern Rationalism," recovers teleology at the *conatus* level — modal, not cosmic.) Real divergence, not softened.

Partial alliance at the methodological register: *Deum instar hominis fingunt* is a critique the user's position adopts. Divine forms are real modalities, not anthropomorphic agents-among-agents.

## PART II — *Ethica* Pars II (De Natura et Origine Mentis)

Pars II turns to mind. Three load-bearing places: Prop. 7 (parallelism), Prop. 11 corollary (mind as part of the infinite intellect), Prop. 40 scholium 2 (three kinds of knowledge).

### Propositio VII + Corollary + Scholium — *ordo et connexio* (G. II/89)

Proposition VII. *The order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things.*

*Demonstration.* This is evident from Ax. 4 of Pt. I. For the idea of anything caused depends on the knowledge of the cause of which it is the effect.

*Corollary.* Hence it follows that God's power of thinking is equal to his actual power of acting. That is, whatever follows formally from the infinite nature of God, all of it follows objectively in God from God's idea, in the same order and the same connection.

*Scholium.* Here, before we go further, we must call to mind what we have shown above: that whatever can be perceived by the infinite intellect as constituting the essence of substance pertains to one substance only, and consequently that thinking substance and extended substance are one and the same substance, which is now comprehended under this attribute, now under that. So too, a mode of extension, and the idea of that mode, are one and the same thing, expressed in two ways.

**Reading.** The *parallelism doctrine*. The corollary's *quicquid ex infinita Dei natura sequitur formaliter, id omne ex Dei idea eodem ordine eademque connexione sequitur in Deo objective* grounds parallelism in the identity of substance under diversity of attributes. The same *res* is *formaliter* extended and *objective* known.

The scholium goes further to a strict identity-thesis: *modus extensionis, et idea illius modi una eademque est res, sed duobus modis expressa* — one and the same thing expressed in two ways. Not parallelism between two things but dual-aspect expression of one. The mind *is* the body, in thought.

This is the most direct Western architectural model of how the divine experiences-all-registers-at-once. The same modal series is in divine intellect *as idea*, in divine extension *as body*, and in infinitely many other attributes as their respective expressions. The *intellectus infinitus is* the parallel-of-all-attributes from the side of thought.

Where Spinoza falls short of supramental is the *ānanda* dimension: parallelism is logical-structural, not yet self-luminous-self-delighting. The architecture is rigorous; the phenomenology is missing.

Subtlety: some readers (Curley, Bennett) restrict parallelism to thought-and-extension. The corollary's wording is general: parallelism holds between *any* attribute and thought, because thought is the meta-attribute that contains the ideas of every other attribute's modes. There is one modal series in the infinite intellect that is the parallel-of-all-attributes from the side of thought. (Della Rocca and Melamed have pressed the question whether this gives thought privileged status — see below.)

### Propositio XI + Corollarium — the human mind as part of the infinite intellect (G. II/93)

Proposition XI. *The first thing that constitutes the actual being of the human mind is nothing other than the idea of some singular thing that actually exists.*

*Corollary to Prop. XI.* From this it follows that the human mind is part of the infinite intellect of God; and accordingly when we say that the human mind perceives this or that, we are saying nothing other than that God — not insofar as he is infinite, but insofar as he is explicated through the nature of the human mind, that is, insofar as he constitutes the essence of the human mind — has this or that idea.

**Reading.** *Mentem humanam partem esse infiniti intellectus Dei.* The clarification — *non quatenus infinitus est, sed quatenus per naturam humanæ Mentis explicatur* — is essential. God-qua-finite-mind contains the ideas of *that mind's* body. The human mind is a finite mode of God's thinking; our perceiving is God's perceiving under the restriction of being-this-finite-mind.

Structurally homologous to Pratyabhijñā *paśu-paramātma* identity, but Spinoza has no *mala* and no *svātantrya*: the restriction is metaphysical and necessary, not free. The recognition (Pars V) does not undo the modal status; it lifts the mind to the third kind, in which the mode-of-God *recognizes itself as such*. *Jīva* as *aṃśa* of the *parātparā*, with the freedom-margin removed.

The proposition that the human mind is the idea of an actually existing singular thing (the body) is the foundation of parallelism applied to humans. No "soul" in the dualist sense; the mind is the body, in thought.

The corollary is where *jñāna by identity* becomes possible: if the human mind is part of the infinite intellect, it has access to the totality from within itself. The mind's adequate ideas are the same ideas God-qua-infinite-intellect has, restricted to the finite mode's scope. The finite-infinite distinction is not a wall but a *quatenus*-clause picking out a region of the same one cognition.

### Propositio XL Scholium II — the three kinds of knowledge (G. II/122)

*Scholium II to Prop. XL.* From all that has been said above it clearly appears that we perceive many things and form universal notions: (1) from singulars represented to us through the senses in a mutilated, confused way and without order to the intellect; and so I have been accustomed to call such perceptions *cognitio ab experientia vaga* — knowledge from vague experience. (2) From signs — for example, from the fact that, on hearing or reading certain words, we recall things and form certain ideas of them similar to those through which we imagine things. I shall call both of these knowledge of the first kind, *opinio* or *imaginatio*. (3) Finally, from the fact that we have common notions and adequate ideas of the properties of things; and this I shall call *Ratio* and knowledge of the second kind. Besides these two kinds of knowledge, there is also a third, which we shall call *Scientia intuitiva*. And this kind of knowledge proceeds from an adequate idea of the formal essence of certain attributes of God to an adequate knowledge of the essence of things.

**Reading.** Three kinds: *imaginatio* (confused sense and signs), *ratio* (discursive grasp via common notions), *scientia intuitiva* (direct grasp of singular essences from divine attributes).

Mapping onto the user's hierarchy: *imaginatio* ≈ *manas* in *vāsanā*-saturated mode; *ratio* ≈ *buddhi* discursive (the level at which the *Ethica* itself operates); *scientia intuitiva* approaches Aurobindo's Higher / Illumined / possibly Intuitive Mind, but is not yet supramental (no threefold *comprehending-apprehending-projecting*).

A difference of *direction*: Vedāntic *jñāna* tends to ascend by negation (*neti neti*); Spinoza's *scientia intuitiva* descends *into* singular essences from God's attributes — *ab adæquata idea essentiæ formalis quorundam Dei attributorum ad adæquatam cognitionem essentiæ rerum*. Structural ancestor of Aurobindo's reversal of the *via negativa*; E5p24 will make this directional reversal explicit.

Spinoza's three kinds, taken at the surface, are *substitutive* (second corrects first, third corrects second). Garrett reads them as more layered: *ratio* uses common notions that are adequate ideas drawn from imagination but irreducible to it; *scientia intuitiva* uses the same notions in their connection to divine attributes. Closer to our layered model, though not yet aperspectival simultaneity. Partial convergence.

E2p40s1 introduces *notiones communes* — ideas of what is common to all bodies, necessarily adequate because equally in part and whole. This is how Spinoza secures *ratio* against skepticism — structurally adjacent to Bhartṛhari's *sphoṭa* in function: ideation by structural sharing of being, not induction.

## PART III — *Ethica* Pars V (De Potentia Intellectus seu de Libertate Humana)

The salvific apex: how the mind, by the third kind of knowledge, comes to coincide with God's love of himself. Read in sequence; central passage at Prop. 36.

### Propositiones XIV, XV, XVI — the love of God begins (G. II/289–290)

Proposition XIV. *The mind can bring it about that all the affections of the body — that is, the images of things — are referred to the idea of God.*

*Demonstration.* There is no affection of the body of which the mind cannot form a clear and distinct conception (Prop. 4 of this part); and so it can bring it about (Prop. 15 of Pt. I) that all of them are referred to the idea of God.

Proposition XV. *He who understands himself and his affects clearly and distinctly, loves God, and the more so the more he understands himself and his affects.*

*Demonstration.* He who understands himself and his affects clearly and distinctly rejoices (Prop. 53 of Pt. III), and this with the accompanying idea of God (preceding Prop.); therefore (by Affects Def. 6) he loves God, and (for the same reason) the more so the more he understands himself and his affects.

Proposition XVI. *This love toward God must occupy the mind most.*

**Reading.** Where the love-of-God begins. Adequate understanding brings all affections under the idea of God; clear understanding *is* loving God, because joy with the idea of God as cause is *amor erga Deum* by Affects Def. 6 (*Amor est Lætitia, concomitante idea causæ externæ*; God is not strictly external, but the structure of the affect is preserved).

The love is not affective in the imaginative sense but the active joy of the mind in its adequate operation as a mode of God's thinking. *Lætitia* is transition to greater perfection (E3def aff 2); adequate ideas are the mind's actuality. Loving God is not a feeling-toward but the mind's own act of being-the-thinking-mode-of-God under the idea of God as cause.

Structural homology with *jñāna-yoga*: *viveka* coincides with *bhakti* because the object of clear understanding *is* the divine. *Jñāna* and *bhakti* are the same act under two descriptions. Not yet *jñāna by identity* in the Aurobindonian sense — *jñāna toward identity*. The crucial step is Prop. 36.

### Propositiones XXII, XXIII — the eternal part of the mind (G. II/295)

Proposition XXII. *In God, however, there is necessarily given an idea which expresses the essence of this and that human body* sub specie aeternitatis.

*Demonstration.* God is the cause not only of the existence of this or that human body but also of its essence (Prop. 25 of Pt. I), which therefore must necessarily be conceived through God's essence (Ax. 4 of Pt. I), and this with a certain eternal necessity (Prop. 16 of Pt. I); which conception must necessarily be given in God.

Proposition XXIII. *The human mind cannot be absolutely destroyed with the body, but there remains of it something that is eternal.*

**Reading.** *Sub specie aeternitatis* — the cognitive standpoint that grasps things in their following-from God, abstracted from durational sequence. Eternity is not duration-without-end but duration-less existence following from the definition of an eternal thing (Def. VIII).

This is *not* personal immortality. The *eternum quoddam* is the mind insofar as it has adequate ideas — what survives is the adequate-idea-content held in the infinite intellect, with eternal status because eternity is not temporal extension. No transmigration; no deepening relation across lives; no evolving psychic being.

There is an interpretive question (Bennett: thin logical residue; Yovel/Nadler/Garrett/Della Rocca: structural individuality, the eternal aspect of *this* mind) turning on Prop. 22's *hujus et illius Corporis humani essentiam*. Even the more robust reading does not give us the *caitya puruṣa*. Structural individuality is not personal continuity. The *eternum quoddam* does not deepen, has no history, does not love or surrender or play. It is the *jīva* in its metaphysical aspect, not the *caitya puruṣa* in its evolutionary-relational aspect.

### Propositio XXIV — knowing singulars more is knowing God more (G. II/296)

Proposition XXIV. *The more we understand singular things, the more we understand God.*

**Reading.** Structural inversion of the abstractive trajectory: the path to God is *into* singulars, into their adequate ideas as their following-from the divine essence. Spinoza precisely anticipates Aurobindo's reversal of the *via negativa* (the divine found *in* and *as* the descended manifestation).

*Quo magis res singulares intelligimus, eo magis Deum intelligimus* is structurally adjacent to the Upaniṣadic *yad iha tad amutra* (Kaṭha 4.10). The detail of the world is not what God is veiled by; it is what God is expressed as. T11 made explicit: every singular adequately known is the divine in determinate self-expression. Strong reason to keep Spinoza in the canon.

The proof appeals to E1p25: God is cause of the essence of singulars, so the essence is conceived through God's essence; understanding the singular *is* understanding God. The reciprocal follows because God's essence is fully expressed in modal differentiation — no surplus beyond what is in modes. Formal counterpart to *omnia in Deo* (E1p15) and *Deus est causa immanens* (E1p18). Method and metaphysics are mutually internal.

### Propositio XXXVI + Corollary + Scholium — *amor Dei intellectualis* (G. II/302–303)

This is the critical passage of the entire *Ethica*. It is given here in full, with extended explication.

Proposition XXXVI. *The intellectual love of the mind toward God is the very love of God by which God loves himself — not insofar as he is infinite, but insofar as he can be explicated through the essence of the human mind considered* sub specie aeternitatis*; that is, the intellectual love of the mind toward God is part of the infinite love by which God loves himself.*

*Demonstration.* This love of the mind must be referred to the actions of the mind, which is therefore the action by which the mind contemplates itself, accompanied by the idea of God as cause; that is, the action by which God, insofar as he can be explicated through the human mind, contemplates himself, accompanied by the idea of himself; and so this love of the mind is part of the infinite love by which God loves himself. Q.E.D.

*Corollary.* From this it follows that God, insofar as he loves himself, loves men; and consequently that God's love toward men and the mind's intellectual love toward God are one and the same.

*Scholium.* From this we clearly understand wherein our salvation, or blessedness, or liberty consists: namely, in the constant and eternal love toward God, that is, in God's love toward men. And this love or blessedness is called *Glory* in the Sacred writings — and not without reason.

**Reading (extended).** *Mentis Amor intellectualis erga Deum est ipse Dei Amor, quo Deus se ipsum amat.* The identity is exact: one love (*unus amor*), the human mind's loving-of-God and God's loving-of-himself-through-the-human-mind being the same act under different aspects.

This is *jñāna-by-identity* in Western articulation. In the third kind of knowledge, the mind is no longer a knower facing a known; it is the locus where God's self-knowing-and-self-loving manifests *as* this finite mind's adequate cognition. The *quatenus* clauses are essential: the infinite love exceeds any finite mind, but the finite mind in its third-kind cognition *is* God's loving-himself-as-this-finite-mode. *Quatenus* is not limitation but structural specification of locus.

The demonstration's four moves: (1) the mind's love is action, not passion (*amor intellectualis* is active affect); (2) action of contemplating itself with the idea of God as cause; (3) by E1p25c and E2p11c, this is God-qua-explicated-through-this-mind contemplating himself; (4) by E5p35 (God loves himself with infinite intellectual love), this is part of the infinite self-contemplation.

The Corollary is the explosive claim: *Deus, quatenus seipsum amat, homines amat*; *Amor Dei erga homines, et Mentis erga Deum Amor intellectualis, unum et idem sit*. God's love for men and the mind's love for God are one and the same. The Vaiṣṇava resonance is profound — the asymmetry of finite-and-infinite collapses in the act of mutual love, the heart of *bhakti*-realization in Caitanya. Take wholesale at the structural register.

The Scholium gives the salvific endpoint: *salus, seu Beatitudo, seu Libertas* in the constant eternal love toward God = God's love toward men. *Beatitudo* is not future reward but present operation of adequate cognition. The identification with *Gloria* in the Sacred Scriptures gestures at Hebrew *kavod* and Greek *doxa*: the salvific name in the traditions is what geometric philosophy has demonstrated. (Spinoza expects his demonstration to be the *only* fully adequate articulation; we expect many.)

The Trika and Aurobindonian resonance: *Vijñāna Bhairava*'s *bhairavīmudrā* (the subject discovers its identity with Bhairava); *Life Divine* II.6 on supramental gnosis as the divine knowing-itself in its modes of self-extension. Same structural form: one knowing-loving, individual locus as the place of particular self-disclosure.

Three Aurobindonian additions unstatable in Spinoza's grammar:

**1. The individual is preserved.** For Aurobindo, the *psychic being* persists as the irreducible locus of *jñāna-by-identity*. For Spinoza, the *quatenus* is necessary modal restriction, not celebrated locus of divine self-play. Garrett narrows but does not close this.

**2. The love is play, not necessity.** Spinoza's love is necessary self-affirmation of the *causa sui*; it could not be otherwise. The Corollary comes very close to the *bhakti* claim, but uttered under necessity, not *līlā*'s freedom. The *unus amor* must be free in the sense of *svātantrya*; Spinoza requires necessity *ex sola suæ naturæ legibus*.

**3. There is a *descent*, not only ascent.** *Life Divine* II.27–28: the supramental love also descends as transformation of mind, life, body. Spinoza has no descent. His *amor Dei intellectualis* is a *jñāna* in the Vivekanandic sense — it leaves lower poises as they are. No Spinozistic correlate to *Cit-Śakti*'s descent.

Textual note: *pars est infiniti amoris* explicitly refuses to identify the finite mind's love with the *whole* of God's self-love. The finite mind is locus through which a part of the divine self-love is articulated. Structurally the doctrine that the *jīva* is *aṃśa* of the divine — real part, participating in the divine self-recognition, not exhausting it.

### Propositio XLII + Scholium — *beatitudo non est virtutis præmium* (G. II/307)

Proposition XLII. *Blessedness is not the reward of virtue, but virtue itself; nor do we rejoice in it because we restrain our lusts, but on the contrary, because we rejoice in it, we can restrain our lusts.*

*Demonstration.* Blessedness consists in the love toward God (Prop. 36 of this part and its Scholium), which love arises from the third kind of knowledge (Coroll. Prop. 32 of this part); and so this love (Prop. 59 and 3 of Pt. III) must be referred to the mind insofar as it acts; and so (Def. 8 of Pt. IV) it is virtue itself. Which was the first point. Next, the more the mind enjoys this divine love or blessedness, the more it understands (Prop. 32 of this part), that is (Coroll. Prop. 3 of this part), the greater the power it has over the affects, and (Prop. 38 of this part) the less it suffers from affects which are evil; and so from the fact that the mind enjoys this divine love or blessedness, it has the power of restraining the lusts.

*Scholium (closing).* By this I have completed everything I wished to show concerning the mind's power over the affects and concerning the mind's freedom. From this it appears how strong the wise man is, and how much more powerful than the ignorant man, who is moved only by lust. For not only is the ignorant man troubled in many ways by external causes, and never possesses true peace of mind, but he lives, moreover, almost unconscious of himself, of God, and of things; and as soon as he ceases to suffer, he ceases to be also. By contrast, the wise man, considered as such, is hardly moved in spirit; but, conscious of himself and of God and of things by a certain eternal necessity, he never ceases to be, but always possesses true peace of mind. If the way I have shown to lead to this seems very arduous, it can nonetheless be found. And it must indeed be arduous, since it is so rarely discovered. For how could it be that, if salvation lay ready to hand and could be found without great labor, it would be neglected by almost everyone? *But all things excellent are as difficult as they are rare.*

**Reading.** *Beatitudo* is not the reward of virtue but virtue itself — the same inversion the Gītā makes: the *sthitaprajña*'s adequate cognition *is* both virtue and beatitude in a single act. *Niṣkāma karma* in structure: action without attachment to fruit because the action itself, rooting in the divine, is its own *ānanda*.

The closing portrait — *vix animo movetur*; *sui, et Dei, et rerum æterna quadam necessitate conscius*; *nunquam esse desinit* — is recognizably the *sthitaprajña*, the *jīvanmukta*, the *kevalin*. Convergence at the level of philosophical anthropology is almost complete.

*Omnia præclara tam difficilia, quam rara sunt* is an *adhikāra* claim: the *Ethica*'s salvific endpoint, like the supramental, is available in principle and rare in practice.

## PART IV — Don Garrett's Contemporary Revival

The Spinoza we have read — *causa immanens*, necessitating substance, finite modes as nothing-but affections, *intellectus infinitus* as parallel-of-all-attributes from thought's side, *amor Dei intellectualis* as the one love — is the Spinoza of the geometric text. But over this Spinoza falls Hegel's long shadow.

Hegel's diagnosis (*Lectures on the History of Philosophy*, *Science of Logic*): Spinoza's substance "swallows" finite modes; modes have vanishing reality; finite individuality dissolves in the One. *Spinozismus ist Akosmismus* — there is no real *cosmos*, only the One Substance with negligible modifications. For Hegel this is why Substance had to become Subject — the dialectical self-mediation needed to recover the finite. The diagnosis hardened into the textbook reading and was inherited by twentieth-century analytic philosophy without scrutiny.

**Don Garrett's *Nature and Necessity in Spinoza's Philosophy* (Oxford 2018)** — with his foundational "Spinoza's *Conatus* Argument" (2002) — is the most sustained contemporary undoing of the Hegelian misreading. Garrett's central claim: Spinoza's necessitarianism is more nuanced than the textbook allows, and finite modes are *quasi-substances*.

### Move 1: necessitarianism is strict but internal

Things could not have been otherwise (E1p33); but this is because of the divine nature itself, not external constraint. *Ex sola suæ naturæ legibus* — the "alone" is doing the work. God's nature *is* the law. This changes what the user's *līlā* doctrine argues against. Not Spinoza-as-textbook-determinist (causal series have no margin for free play) but Spinoza-as-internal-necessitarian: the user agrees that the divine's expression is internal to its own nature, but disagrees about whether the divine's own nature includes the freedom-to-be-otherwise. *Svātantrya* is constitutive of the divine nature, not external; the divine's actual self-determination is one among possible self-determinations, all expressions of the same nature.

### Move 2: the *conatus* doctrine recovers real individuation

E3p6 says that each thing, insofar as it is in itself, strives to persevere in its being.
E3p7 says that this striving is nothing but the actual essence of the thing itself.

The *conatus* is the actual essence of the finite mode — not a tendency added to the mode but the mode's own self-affirming activity, identical with its essence. Garrett's "Spinoza's Conatus Argument" (2002) gives the canonical defense: from Pars I premises about inherence, conception, and causation, the *conatus* thesis follows validly.

If the *conatus* is the actual essence, the finite mode is not absorbable into substance without remainder. Modes have real essences, real strivings, real internal principles of self-maintenance — not Hegelian vanishing-points but real centers of activity. Garrett calls them *quasi-substances*: substance-like in possessing real essence and self-affirming activity, but not *causa sui*. The gap between Spinoza's mode and the user's psychic being narrows considerably.

### Move 3: rereading E1p25 corollary

Garrett rereads *Res particulares nihil sunt, nisi Dei attributorum affectiones, sive modi* not as deflationary denial but as positive identification: particulars are God's attributes in *certo et determinato modo*. The *nihil sunt, nisi* is identificational, not subtractive. Particular things *are* affections of God's attributes — not less than, not other than, with full ontological weight.

### Move 4: God's power is the totality of modal powers

God's power *is* the totality of finite modes' powers in their interactions. To diminish the modes is to diminish God. Textual support: E1p36 (*nihil existit, ex cujus natura aliquis effectus non sequatur*); E2p3 cor; E3p6–7. Modes are not ontological deficits but the very *manner* in which God's power is exercised. Hegel's "acosmism" is a misreading produced by his own dialectical needs.

### Move 5: necessity does not entail occasionalism or fatalism

Spinoza is not occasionalist: finite modes really cause finite modes. He is not fatalist: actions, including understanding, are really part of the causal series. The third kind of knowledge is a real achievement, *amor Dei intellectualis* a real consummation. The necessitarianism is at the level of the totality, not of individual modal contributions.

### Why Garrett matters

Garrett softens the impersonalism divergence considerably without eliminating it. The *conatus* is not yet *caitya puruṣa* — no trans-incarnational continuity, no deepening evolutionary relation, no personal-loving capacity — but it is a real principle of individuation that resists collapse into substance. Read with Garrett, the modes are structurally close to the Trika view that every *paśu* is Śiva-as-self-restricted.

What Garrett does not give: freedom-to-be-otherwise. *Svātantrya* in the strong sense is not in Spinoza on any reading. *Līlā* requires that the divine *could* have not played, or played otherwise; this is unsayable in Spinoza's grammar. The divergence we keep.

Without Garrett's recovery, Spinoza is the Hegelian totalizer; with it, the comparison sharpens to the actual core: *the difference between necessitated and freely playful immanent self-expression*. That is the real philosophical disagreement.

## PART V — Melamed, Della Rocca, Macherey

Three further scholars triangulate the contemporary reception past Hegel's reading.

### Yitzhak Melamed: substance/attribute architecture

Melamed's *Spinoza's Metaphysics: Substance and Thought* (Oxford 2013) takes Hegel's "acosmism" reading more seriously than Garrett does, but detaches it from the Hegelian dialectical framework. Spinoza really does subordinate modes to substance; the philosophical question is what kind of priority this is and whether it allows the modes any real being.

Melamed distinguishes two kinds of inherence: the *in-se / in-alio* distinction of E1ax1, and a further distinction between *what an attribute expresses* and *what is in the attribute as a mode*. Attributes are not epistemic projections of intellect; they are real ontological constituents of substance. Modes inhere in them genuinely subordinatively — neither Hegelian vanishing-points nor Garrett's quasi-substances but real modal inhabitants of attributes that are themselves real expressions of substance.

The technical payoff: a layered architecture of three ontological levels — (i) substance, (ii) attributes, (iii) modes — each with its own ontological weight. Melamed defends the *infinite-modes architecture* as genuine ontological structure: the *immediate infinite mode* of thought is the *intellectus infinitus* (Letter 64); of extension is *motion-and-rest*; the mediate infinite mode is *facies totius universi*. Substance / attributes / immediate infinite modes / mediate infinite modes / finite modes maps structurally onto Aurobindo's Saccidānanda / Supermind / Overmind / Mind / Life / Body — same form of layered self-articulation. Doctrinal differences (necessitarianism, impersonalism) are real; the structural form is mutually translatable.

### Michael Della Rocca: PSR-driven monism

Della Rocca's *Spinoza* (Routledge 2008) reads the entire *Ethica* as the working-out of an unrestricted Principle of Sufficient Reason. Every fact has an explanation, no exception. Substance must be unique because two would be inexplicable; attributes must be infinite because restriction would be inexplicable; modes must follow necessarily because contingency would be unexplained; parallelism holds because non-correlation would be inexplicable.

For our reading: this sharpens the disagreement. The user's *līlā* is, on Della Rocca's reading, explicit rejection of unrestricted PSR — the divine could have done otherwise without that "could have" being explicable by prior reason. The user's position rejects PSR at the level of divine self-determination, holding the divine's freedom constitutive, not derivative. *Within* the actualized cosmos, things have explanations; the disagreement is precisely about whether divine self-determination falls under PSR's reach.

Della Rocca's more controversial claim — "monism all the way down," with all multiplicity modally derivative — is the limit case of textbook Spinoza, what the user is most decisively *not*. The user's position is *acintya-bhedābheda*: real difference and real non-difference held simultaneously.

### Pierre Macherey: against Hegel's reading

Macherey's *Hegel ou Spinoza* (Maspero 1979; English: Minnesota 2011) is the most sustained continental critique of Hegel's reading. Working from the Althusserian tradition: Hegel's reading is a misreading structurally necessary to Hegel's own project; if Spinoza is not what Hegel says, Hegelianism loses its motivating ancestor.

The argument turns on *natura naturans / naturata*. Hegel reads this as a static doublet — substance on one side, modes on the other, substance "swallowing" modes because the distinction is unmediated, with the dialectical move (Substance becoming Subject) supplying the missing mediation. Macherey argues this misses the temporality of the Latin grammar: *naturans* is gerundive (nature *naturing*, in the act); *naturata* is perfect-passive participle (nature *natured*, having-been-natured). The distinction is not between two ontological ranks but between active and passive aspects of the same one process. The mediation Hegel claims is missing is internal to the distinction itself.

For our project: Macherey's argument is a powerful ally against the ontologization-of-grammar that T11 explicitly criticizes. Hegel mistook a grammatical distinction for an ontological distinction and introduced dialectic to fix what was never disjointed.

Macherey recovers a Spinoza closer to the Pratyabhijñā *prakāśa-vimarśa* doublet: *naturans* ≈ *prakāśa* (active luminous self-affirmation); *naturata* ≈ *vimarśa-as-creative-display*. But Spinoza lacks the *spanda* dimension — the rhythmic free pulsation that makes the *prakāśa-vimarśa* doublet free play rather than necessitated unfolding. The Spinozistic *naturata* unfolds necessarily; the Trika *vimarśa* unfolds *spanda*-ously. This is precisely where Spinoza ends and Aurobindo begins.

### Synthesis

Garrett recovers the *conatus* and real individuation against acosmism. Melamed preserves the layered architecture (substance / attributes / infinite modes / finite modes) while taking modal-subordination seriously. Della Rocca takes the *Ethica* as unrestricted-PSR and pushes Spinoza toward aggressive monism. Macherey reads *naturans / naturata* temporally and exposes Hegel's "acosmism" as category-error.

The four readings are complementary: Garrett softens impersonalism; Melamed gives layered architecture; Della Rocca sharpens necessitarianism; Macherey gives the *prakāśa-vimarśa* analogue. Together they replace the textbook Spinoza with a real interlocutor — closer to the user's position on architecture, sharper in disagreement on freedom.

## PART VI — Deleuze's Reading in *Spinoza et le problème de l'expression*

Gilles Deleuze's *Spinoza et le problème de l'expression* (Minuit, 1968), Joughin trans. *Expressionism in Philosophy: Spinoza* (Zone, 1990), is Deleuze's *thèse complémentaire* — a careful, systematic reading of Spinoza belonging to the period of his great monographs (Hume 1953, Nietzsche 1962, Kant 1963, Bergson 1966, Spinoza 1968, *Difference and Repetition* 1968). Unlike the later Deleuze-Guattari texts, this book is rigorous, analytical, and argumentative. It is, for our reading, the philosophically most generative contemporary engagement with the *Ethica* — and the one that most directly bears on the user's project, because Deleuze's reading is structurally what the user's thesis needs from Spinoza.

### The refusal of three alternatives

Deleuze's central interpretive instrument is *expression*, set against three alternative God-world models: *creation ex nihilo* (cause outside and prior, effect unrelated except by bare production); *emanation* (Plotinus/Proclus: each emanated level *less* than the source, the One untouched); *participation* (Platonic-scholastic copying of Ideas, the participated transcendent to participants).

Expression is the alternative to all three. The cause is *genuinely present in* the effect — not withdrawn, not diminished, not copied. The cause expresses itself fully in the effect without losing itself: *la cause immanente reste en soi pour produire, l'effet reste en la cause pour être produit*.

The expressive triad is *substance / attributes / modes*. Each attribute is not a *part* of substance but the *whole* essence under one form. Each mode is the attribute (and thereby substance) under a particular intensive determination. No part-whole, no resemblance — only expression: the whole of substance expressing itself entirely in each register.

For our project this is decisive: *each attribute is the whole of God expressed each time in a different register* — almost word for word the structural form of *one Real said in many senses*. Each *darśana* is a register under which the same Real is exhaustively expressed.

### Immanent causality

The *Ethica*'s E1p18 — *Deus est omnium rerum causa immanens, non vero transiens* — is for Deleuze the defining act of Spinoza's philosophy. The immanent cause does not stand outside what it produces; it *remains in* what it produces. God does not produce the world and then withdraw; God *remains in* the world as the ground of its being, which is also the world's continuous expression of God.

This is radical immanence. There is no gap between the divine and the natural. *Deus sive Natura* — God, or Nature — is not a metaphor. It is the logical consequence of immanent causality.

For our project the immanent causality of Spinoza, read with Deleuze, is convergent with the Vedāntic identification of *upādāna* and *nimitta* in Brahman, and with the Trika view of the world as internal to *cit*. *Causa immanens* is the structural form of *abheda*; the Trika doctrine that *prakāśa* and *vimarśa* are non-different is the structural form of *causa immanens*. The user's position takes this convergence wholesale at the architectural level. The divergence — that Spinoza's immanent causation is necessitarian where the Trika *spanda* is free — is, as we have said, the divergence we keep.

### Univocity of being

Deleuze's reading positions Spinoza as the second great moment of univocity — after Duns Scotus, before Nietzsche. Scotus's univocity is *formal* and *neutral*: being is a formal concept applied univocally to God and creatures, but it remains abstract, not yet expressive. Spinoza makes univocity *expressive and affirmative*: substance's being is said univocally of the attributes and modes, and this saying is an affirmation of what they are — it is the univocal voice of the One in which all differences subsist.

The contrast Deleuze draws is with *equivocal* (analogical/hierarchical) being in Aquinas and the scholastic mainstream. Aquinas follows Aristotle: being is said *analogically* of God and creatures — the same concept is used, but in proportionally different senses (*analogia proportionis*). This preserves divine transcendence but denies univocity. For Deleuze, Spinoza's move is to say: no, being is said in one and the same sense of both God (substance) and finite modes. The difference is not in the *being* but in the *power* (*potentia*) and *form* of the expression.

This is convergent at the architectural level with the user's thesis as articulated in the position brief: "Being is said in a single and same sense of everything of which it is said, but that of which it is said differs: it is said of difference itself" (the Deleuzean univocity formula, quoted in the position brief from *Difference and Repetition*). Spinoza is the second moment of this univocity in Western philosophy, and his version is the one the user's thesis explicitly draws on, via Deleuze.

### The *intellectus infinitus* in Deleuze's reading

Yes structurally, no phenomenologically. The *intellectus infinitus Dei* is not a faculty God has; it is the immediate infinite mode of Thought, the totality of adequate ideas — substance's full expression under Thought. It does not survey all things from a unified perspectival vantage; it *is* the immanent self-understanding of substance through its own attribute. Deleuze's Spinoza says YES to the structural question (the divine is expressed in all modes at once; the *intellectus infinitus* holds all adequate ideas simultaneously) but in a strictly **impersonal** way. No experience, no feeling, no awareness in the psychological sense.

This is what the user's thesis must supplement. Required: divine personhood — or at minimum subjectivity — able to *will* self-veiling (*svātantrya*), *play* the *līlā*, *experience* registers, *surrender* and *receive* surrender. Aurobindo's advance on Spinoza: not just *ratio essendi* but *Cit-Śakti* — power of self-manifestation that is itself personal. Spinoza's substance is self-caused but not self-conscious. *Saccidānanda* is self-luminous, self-experiencing, self-delighting.

Deleuze's Spinoza gives the architecture (univocal expression, immanent causality, finite as center of expression); the user's thesis adds the phenomenology, drawing on Aurobindo, Abhinavagupta, and the Vaiṣṇava traditions.

### The argument against māyā-vāda and the Spinozist support

One additional connection, made explicit in Deleuze's reading. For Deleuze's Spinoza, the modes are genuine expressions of substance — they are not less real than substance; they are substance's reality in its finite determinations. The modes are not illusory or reducible. This is structurally parallel to the user's insistence (T2) that *mithyā ≠ asat* — the world is sublatable in the sense of being not independently real, but this is not the same as being unreal. The world is real *as* the expression of Brahman. The finite modes are real *as* the expression of substance. In both cases: the finite is real, not illusory; its reality is expressive, not independent. This is a structural alliance between Deleuze's expressive Spinoza and the user's anti-māyāvāda thesis.

## PART VII — Reading Notes

The reader has now traversed Pars I in full, the load-bearing passages of Pars II and Pars V, the contemporary revival in Garrett, the contextual triangulation in Melamed, Della Rocca, and Macherey, and the speculative reframing in Deleuze. What follows is the working summary: where Spinoza confirms the user's thesis, where he diverges, where Garrett's revival narrows the gap, and how to read the *Ethica* in sequence for our project.

### Where Spinoza confirms

**The substance / attribute / mode architecture is a Western articulation of one-Real-said-in-many-senses.** Definition IV — *id quod intellectus de substantia percipit, tanquam ejusdem essentiam constituens* — is the cleanest pre-modern Western articulation of *acintya-bhedābheda*. The same one substance, perceived by intellect under irreducibly many attributes, each of which expresses the *whole* essence under that respect. Read with Deleuze and Garrett, this is the architecture of univocity-with-irreducible-many. *Infinita attributa, quorum unumquodque æternam et infinitam essentiam exprimit*. It converges with the user's T1 (the *darśanas* address different registers, not competing total theories) and T4 (all *darśanas* ultimately true): each attribute is a register under which the same Real is exhaustively expressed.

**The *intellectus infinitus Dei* is the closest pre-Aurobindonian articulation of aperspectival comprehension.** E1p16 — *infinita infinitis modis (hoc est omnia, quæ sub intellectum infinitum cadere possunt)* — and E2p7 corollary — *quicquid ex infinita Dei natura sequitur formaliter, id omne ex Dei idea eodem ordine eademque connexione sequitur in Deo objective* — together give the doctrine of an infinite intellect that contains all ideas in all attributes simultaneously. This is the strongest Western architectural model of "the divine experiences all registers at once." *Sub specie aeternitatis* is, in Spinoza, an aperspectival condition in a strong sense: it is not "the view from nowhere" but the view that holds *all* finite views as its own modal differentiations. This converges with Aurobindo's supramental in its *comprehending* poise, and with Gebser's diaphanous-aperspectival in its structural form.

**Immanence over transcendence.** *Causa immanens, non vero transiens* (E1p18) blocks the transcendent-deistic picture. *Quicquid est, in Deo est* (E1p15) places everything internal to God. The Pars I scholium to Prop. 15 carefully distinguishes this from the imaginative pantheism of God-as-Big-Body. Both are convergent with the Vedāntic identification of *upādāna* and *nimitta* in Brahman, and with the Trika view of the world as internal to *cit*. This supports T11 (ontology of phenomenology: the divine is not absent from the material but expressed in it).

**Knowledge by identity in *amor Dei intellectualis*.** E5p36 articulates a structural identity: *Mentis Amor intellectualis erga Deum est ipse Dei Amor, quo Deus se ipsum amat*. This is the closest Western articulation of *jñāna-by-identity* before Aurobindo, and it carries the same anti-objectivist force: the knower–known–knowing distinction collapses in the third kind. The Corollary's astonishing claim — *Deus, quatenus seipsum amat, homines amat* — that God's love for men *is* the same love by which the mind loves God — is structurally close to the Vaiṣṇava claim that the divine loves the *jīva* with the *jīva's* own loving-of-the-divine, both being one love.

**Anti-anthropomorphism on the divine.** The Scholium to E1p15 and the Appendix together demolish the imaginative anthropomorphism that confuses *Deus* with a Big Personal Agent. Our position should adopt this discipline. Divine forms (gods, *iṣṭa-devatās*) are real *modalities* of the divine — Kālī as a real configuration of force/dissolution/lucidity/time — not anthropomorphic agents-among-other-agents. Spinoza is right that to picture God as a man-with-a-body-and-passions is to import the *imaginatio* into the wrong register.

**The descent into singulars (E5p24).** *Quo magis res singulares intelligimus, eo magis Deum intelligimus*. The path to God is *into* singulars, not away from them. This is structurally Aurobindo's reversal of the *via negativa*: the divine is found *in* and *as* the descended manifestation, not by abstraction from it. The Upaniṣadic *yad iha tad amutra* converges here.

**Beatitude as virtue itself (E5p42).** Action and reward are not separate. Doing the adequate thing *is* the *ānanda* of doing it. This is structurally the Gītā's *niṣkāma karma*: action without attachment to fruit because the action itself, in its rooting in the divine, is its own *ānanda*. The Spinozistic *sapiens* — *vix animo movetur*, *sui et Dei et rerum æterna quadam necessitate conscius* — is recognizably the Indian *sthitaprajña* and *jīvanmukta*.

### Where Spinoza diverges

**No *līlā*, because no *svātantrya*.** Def. VII defines freedom as existing-from-the-necessity-of-one's-own-nature. Spinoza's God is *libera causa* but not free in the Trika sense to do otherwise. E1p33 — *res nullo alio modo, neque alio ordine a Deo produci potuerunt, quam productæ sunt* — closes the modal margin entirely. The user's position requires a Brahman whose self-veiling is *play*, not necessitation; Spinoza's grammar cannot say this. The freedom-to-be-otherwise — *svātantrya* as constitutive of *Cit-Śakti* — is what Spinoza's geometry forecloses by Definition. This is the divergence we keep without softening, and Della Rocca's PSR-driven reading makes the contrast philosophically sharp: Spinoza is committed to unrestricted PSR; the user's position rejects PSR at the level of divine self-determination, holding that the divine's freedom is constitutive and not derivative.

**The identification *Deus sive Natura* is too tight.** Pars IV Praefatio's *Deus seu Natura* is convertibility, not perspectival distinction. Our position holds that Brahman is *not* nature *simpliciter*; manifestation is the play of Brahman's *svātantrya*, and the difference between Saccidānanda-as-such and manifestation is the room in which *līlā* is possible. Spinoza closes that room. We must read *Deus sive Natura* with care: at the architectural level it gives us immanence; at the modal level it forecloses the freedom-to-self-veil that our position requires. Macherey's recovery of the *naturans / naturata* distinction as a temporal-grammatical doublet (active aspect and passive aspect of one process) softens the Hegelian totalization but does not give us back the *spanda*.

**Impersonalism.** What is eternal in the human mind (E5p23) is not the individual biography or the psychic being; it is the impersonal *eternum quoddam* of adequate ideas. Our position requires the persistence of the *caitya puruṣa* across embodiments as the locus of divine self-recognition, with continuity of disposition, taste, longing, deepening relation. Spinoza has no equivalent. Garrett's revival, as we have argued, narrows but does not close this gap.

**No descent.** The third kind of knowledge ascends; there is no corresponding *descent* of the divine into the finite that transforms the lower poises. Aurobindo's supramental descent — the doctrine of the supramental yoga's transformation of mind, life, body, beginning historically with the descent of 29 February 1956 — has no Spinozistic correlate. Spinoza articulates a *jñāna* that lifts; Aurobindo articulates a *vijñāna* that lifts *and* transforms.

**No registers in our sense.** Our four-minute-mile claim — that registers are non-destructively layered, that realization adds rather than substitutes — fits awkwardly with Spinoza's three-kinds-of-knowledge hierarchy, which is *substitutive*: the third corrects the second corrects the first. What Spinoza does *not* offer is a structure in which all three kinds are *simultaneously* operative, each at its own register, each true at its own register, and the whole transparent to itself. That is what we have called aperspectival-integral; Spinoza articulates the third kind but not the through-all.

**No teleology.** The Appendix's attack on *causæ finales* is the place where Spinoza is most decisively not an Aurobindonian. There is no *parā prakṛti*, no Self-disclosing *Saccidānanda* directing the cosmos toward an end. Only efficient causes coursing through the modal ladder. Our position requires evolutionary supramental cosmology; Spinoza forbids it categorically. (Garrett's recovery of teleology at the modal level via the *conatus* is genuine but does not extend to cosmic teleology.)

### Where Garrett's revival narrows the gap

The most important softening is at impersonalism. The *conatus* is not yet *caitya puruṣa* — no trans-incarnational continuity, no deepening evolutionary relation, no personal-loving capacity — but it is a real principle of individuation that resists collapse into substance. The finite mode has its own actual essence, striving, real existence; Spinoza is not the dissolver-of-individuals he was made into.

Garrett also softens modal monism by showing necessity is *internal*. The comparison with our position becomes one of *kind* of self-expression — necessitated or playful — rather than of *whether* there is room for the finite at all. The third kind of knowledge, read with Garrett's emphasis on real individuation, becomes the place where the finite mode *recognizes itself as* a determinate expression of the divine attributes — closer to Trika *pratyabhijñā* than the Hegelian reading allowed.

What Garrett does not give: freedom-to-be-otherwise. *Svātantrya* in the strong sense is not in Spinoza on any reading. *Līlā* requires that the divine *could* have played otherwise, unsayable in Spinoza's grammar. The divergence we keep.

### The user's verdict

Spinoza is a structural ancestor of the supramental's *comprehending poise*. The *intellectus infinitus*, parallelism (E2p7), *amor Dei intellectualis* (E5p36) — the cleanest Western articulations of "the divine experiences all registers at once, non-destructively." Substance / attribute / mode, read with Deleuze and Garrett, is a model of one-Real-said-in-many-senses supporting T1 / T4 / T11.

But the divergences are decisive: necessitarianism closes *līlā*; impersonalism (E5p23) forecloses the psychic being; absence of descent leaves lower poises untransformed; absence of teleology forbids evolutionary cosmology. Not soft disagreements; structural reasons we cannot rest in Spinoza.

Spinoza gives us the architecture of comprehension; for the freedom of *līlā*, the persistence of the psychic being, the descent of the supramental, and evolutionary teleology, we go to Aurobindo. With the contemporary scholarship Spinoza becomes a real interlocutor rather than a dissolved-into-Hegel foil.

Setup for what follows: Prop. 33 is the structural place where Leibniz, fifty years later, inserts the modal margin — not yet *līlā*, but *compossibility*. Whether Leibniz reopens the divine's freedom enough is the question of the next reader.

### Concrete reading sequence for the *Ethica*

For our project, read in this order:

**1. Pars I (De Deo) — essential, in full.** The Definitions, Axioms, and Propositions 5, 11, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 25, 29 (with the *naturans / naturata* scholium), 33, and the Appendix. Spend time here. The architecture is the architecture; if you do not have Pars I in your bones, the rest of the *Ethica* will read as application of doctrines you have not internalized. The Latin matters for the technical primitives — *substantia*, *attributum*, *modus*, *causa immanens*, *libera causa*, *natura naturans / naturata*. Read these in Latin even if you read the rest in English. Allow yourself two or three weeks on Pars I; it is dense, and the geometric form requires habituation.

**2. Pars V (De Potentia Intellectus) — essential, in full.** Especially Propositions 14–16 (the love begins), 22–24 (the eternal mind, knowing singulars), and the central Proposition 36 with its Corollary and Scholium. This is the salvific apex. *Amor Dei intellectualis* is what the whole *Ethica* is for. The closing Proposition 42 with its scholium-ending aphorism — *omnia præclara tam difficilia, quam rara sunt* — is where Spinoza's geometric demonstration closes back into the lived register from which the TIE began.

**3. Pars II Prop. 7 with Corollary, and Prop. 11 Corollary — essential, focused.** The parallelism doctrine and the human-mind-as-part-of-God's-infinite-intellect doctrine. Read these as the bridge from the metaphysics of Pars I to the salvific epistemology of Pars V. The *modus extensionis et idea illius modi una eademque est res, sed duobus modis expressa* of the scholium is the formula of dual-aspect monism that Bertrand Russell, more than two centuries later, will rediscover under that name.

**4. Pars II Prop. 40 Schol. 2 — essential, focused.** The three kinds of knowledge. This is the epistemic structure that Pars V works through. The Aurobindonian mapping is approximate but instructive: *imaginatio* is roughly *manas* in *vāsanā*-saturated mode; *ratio* is roughly *buddhi* discursive; *scientia intuitiva* approaches Higher Mind / Illumined Mind / possibly Intuitive Mind, but does not reach the Supramental's threefold *comprehending-apprehending-projecting* status.

**5. Pars III (De origine et natura affectuum), focused on Propositions 6, 7, 9 — important, focused.** The *conatus* doctrine is what Garrett's revival turns on. *Unaquæque res, quantum in se est, in suo esse perseverare conatur* (E3p6); *Conatus, quo unaquæque res in suo esse perseverare conatur, nihil est præter ipsius rei actualem essentiam* (E3p7). Read these carefully if you want to engage Garrett. The remainder of Pars III — the catalogue of affects, with its definitions of *lætitia*, *tristitia*, *amor*, *odium*, *cupiditas*, etc. — is useful but not load-bearing for our project.

**6. Pars IV (De servitute humana, seu de affectuum viribus) — Praefatio especially — useful, focused.** The *Deus seu Natura* line is in the Praefatio. The doctrine of human bondage to passive affects organizes the part. Skim the propositions; read the Preface and the Appendix. The model of the wise life given here is the practical-philosophical companion to the metaphysics of Pars I and the salvific apex of Pars V.

**7. Tractatus theologico-politicus, Chapters I, IV, XIV — supplementary, on theological method, prophecy, and the foundations of religion.** Useful for understanding Spinoza's hermeneutic. Chapter I (on prophecy as imagination), Chapter IV (on the divine law), Chapter XIV (on the universal religion as love of God and neighbor stripped of dogma) are the philosophical core of the TTP for our project. The political chapters (XVI–XX) are a separate read, more relevant to the political register of the user's thesis (matsya-nyāya, the Atharvaveda formulae, the Aurobindonian collective egoism diagnosis) than to the metaphysical engagement here.

**Edwin Curley, *The Collected Works of Spinoza*** (Princeton, 1985–2016) is the standard translation; cross-check against **Samuel Shirley, *Complete Works*** (Hackett, 2002) for technical primitives.

### A final discipline

Spinoza wrote in Latin, in geometric form, with technical primitives used with iron consistency. The most common error is to substitute familiar English for them — *substantia* as "stuff," *modus* as "thing," *amor* as ordinary affective love, *libertas* as voluntarist freedom. The *Ethica* is, like a Sanskrit *śāstra*, technical vocabulary that *is* the system.

The geometric form is not optional; it is the philosophy. Read in sequence; allow the scaffolding to do its work. The *Ethica* is *sūtra* literature; reading out of order is reading a different book.

For our project: hold convergences and divergences simultaneously. Spinoza is the early-modern Western articulation of one Real said in many senses, comprehended aperspectivally, immanent in its expression, known in identity in the third kind — without the freedom, personhood, descent, or evolutionary teleology the user's thesis requires. Convergence exact at the architectural level; divergence exact at the lived-personal level.

*Omnia præclara tam difficilia, quam rara sunt.*
