# 14 — Martin Heidegger (1889–1976): A Deep Reader

*A reader's companion for engaging Heidegger from the standpoint of the user's thesis. The aim is not to convert Heidegger into the user's idiom, nor to canonize him; it is to read him with patience, to keep the key citations visible, and to see precisely where the diagnostic power of his thought confirms the user's anti-totalizing instincts and where its closures must be broken.*

*A note on translations. Heidegger asked his readers to listen to the German itself; he was unusually emphatic that the philosophical force of his sentences lives in the words he chose, and that translations — even careful ones — already begin to mediate. The standard published English versions (Macquarrie & Robinson 1962 and Stambaugh 1996 for *Sein und Zeit*; *Pathmarks* and Krell's *Basic Writings* for the *Wegmarken* essays; Stambaugh for *Identität und Differenz*; Emad/Maly and Rojcewicz/Vallega-Neu for the *Beiträge*; Capuzzi for "The Anaximander Fragment"; Hofstadter for the *Vorträge und Aufsätze* pieces and the "Letter on Humanism"; Anderson/Freund and Bret Davis for *Gelassenheit*) have all done indispensable work — and have all, in places, smoothed Heidegger into the very metaphysical idiom he was trying to break. So in this reader the German term comes first; the English follows; and where a standard rendering reverses or flattens what Heidegger is doing — *zuhanden* as "ready-to-hand," *Vorlaufen* as "anticipation," *Sein-zum-Tode* as "being-toward-death," *primordial* and *fundamental* as the catch-all glosses for *ursprünglich* and *eigentlich* — the departure is noted in place. The bracketed Greek and Sanskrit transliterations are mine.*

*GA = Gesamtausgabe (Klostermann complete edition). The Heidegger volumes consulted in primary form for this reader are GA 2 (Sein und Zeit), GA 5 (Holzwege), GA 9 (Wegmarken — including the Brief über den Humanismus, Vom Wesen der Wahrheit, Was ist Metaphysik?), GA 11 (Identität und Differenz), GA 12 (Unterwegs zur Sprache), GA 13 (Aus der Erfahrung des Denkens — including the Gelassenheit pieces), GA 16 (the Self-Assertion address), GA 54 (Parmenides), GA 65 (Beiträge zur Philosophie [Vom Ereignis]).*

## Reader's Orientation

### Bio in brief

Martin Heidegger was born on 26 September 1889 in Meßkirch, a small town in the Swabian uplands at the edge of the Black Forest, into a Catholic sexton's family of modest means. The provincial Swabian world — the bell-tower, the fields, the woodpaths, the dialect — never left his thinking. He prepared for the priesthood at the Jesuit gymnasium in Konstanz and the archiepiscopal seminary in Freiburg, broke with formal Catholic theology, took up Husserlian phenomenology under Edmund Husserl at Freiburg, and habilitated in 1915 with a study of Duns Scotus's doctrine of categories and meaning. He taught at Marburg from 1923 (where his electrifying lectures drew Hannah Arendt, Hans Jonas, Hans-Georg Gadamer, Karl Löwith, Leo Strauss), returned to Freiburg in 1928 as Husserl's successor, and there delivered, in a single decade, the work that made him the most consequential continental philosopher of the twentieth century.

He published *Sein und Zeit* in 1927 — the existential analytic of *Dasein*, the question of Being reopened, the temporalization of human existence. In 1929 came *Was ist Metaphysik?* and *Kant und das Problem der Metaphysik*. Then came what cannot be elided in any honest reading: in April 1933, weeks after the Nazi seizure of power, he was elected Rector of Freiburg University and joined the NSDAP. His *Rektoratsrede*, the *Selbstbehauptung der deutschen Universität*, conscripted his philosophy of authentic decision into the rhetoric of the new regime. He resigned the rectorate within ten months, but his party membership was never renounced; the *Schwarze Hefte* (Black Notebooks), published only in the 2010s, contain antisemitic passages that no philosophical apologetics can scrub. After 1945 the French occupation banned him from teaching; he was reinstated in 1949 and re-emerged with a quieter, slower, more poetically inflected late work — the lectures and essays gathered as *Holzwege* (1950), *Vorträge und Aufsätze* (1954), *Was heißt Denken?* (1954), *Der Satz vom Grund* (1957), *Identität und Differenz* (1957), *Unterwegs zur Sprache* (1959), *Gelassenheit* (1959), *Wegmarken* (1967), *Zur Sache des Denkens* (1969). The vast *Beiträge zur Philosophie (Vom Ereignis)*, written 1936–1938, was held back during his lifetime and published only in 1989 (GA 65). He died in Meßkirch on 26 May 1976 and is buried beside his parents in the village churchyard, with the Black Forest path he loved running past it.

### The arc — what to expect

A caution about the arc. The popular periodization "early Heidegger / late Heidegger / Kehre" is a later-academic frame; Heidegger himself, when he spoke of the *Kehre*, did not mean a biographical turn from one Heidegger to another. He meant a *turn in the matter* — a turn in the question of Being itself — to which his thinking was responding. In the *Brief über den Humanismus* he says plainly that the third Division of *Sein und Zeit* (the move from Dasein's temporality to *Time and Being*) was held back because "*thinking failed*" — the language of metaphysics inherited from Plato and Aristotle was inadequate to what he was trying to think. The *Kehre*, on his own self-presentation, is not a change of mind; it is the matter (*die Sache*) itself turning, and the thinker following it. We will keep this in view rather than packing him into the textbook three-phase frame.

That said, there is a genuine progression in his vocabulary. *Sein und Zeit* (1927) approaches the *Seinsfrage* — the question of Being — through the existential analytic of Dasein, the entity that we ourselves are, the one for whom Being is at issue. Through the 1930s — the Hölderlin lectures, the work-of-art essay, the *Beiträge zur Philosophie* — the question is approached from the side of Being itself: the destinal sendings (*Geschick*) by which Being has un-concealed itself differently in different historical epochs. From the late 1940s — the *Brief*, *Gelassenheit*, *Identität und Differenz*, the *Andenken* meditations on Parmenides and Anaximander — the central word becomes *Ereignis*, the appropriative event in which Being and the human come into the relation that is proper to each, and *Geviert*, the four-gathering (earth, sky, mortals, divinities) of dwelling.

The continuity beneath the turn is the *Seinsfrage* itself. The early book makes Dasein the privileged site for asking; the later work humbles Dasein into one *Lichtung* (clearing) within an event that is no longer human-centered. What remains constant from the first lecture course to the last is the diagnosis: Western metaphysics has *forgotten* the *ontologische Differenz*, the difference between *Sein* and *Seiendes* (Being and beings); it has reduced Being to the most general property of beings or to a highest being (the *causa sui*); and this forgetting has produced both technological *Gestell* (enframing) and the death of the metaphysical god, leaving the human *unbehaust* — unhoused — in the very world its categories have built.

### Why the user reads Heidegger

The user reads Heidegger for three principal reasons:

First, **Heidegger is the West's most rigorous anti-totalization counterpoint to Hegel**. Where Hegel completes metaphysics by gathering Being into the self-mediated Concept, Heidegger refuses the gathering. *Being is not a being.* No system, no Absolute, no synthesis — and yet not relativism, because the question of Being remains binding. This refusal of closure is structurally close to what the user defends against the Hegelian temptation: that there is no single grammar that can absorb every other grammar.

Second, **the Bhartṛhari–Heidegger language thread is real**. "Die Sprache ist das Haus des Seins" — language is the house of Being. Speech is not an instrumental medium for thought but the precinct in which Being shows itself. This is structurally close to Bhartṛhari's *Vākyapadīya*: *śabda-tattva*, word-reality, as the very fabric of cognition; *paśyantī* speech as the seeing-speech in which meaning has not yet split into syllabic articulation. Heidegger never read Bhartṛhari; the structural homology is independent and significant.

Third, **the user's polemic against grammar finds in Heidegger an ally and a limit**. The polemic — that Hegel ontologizes grammar, that "we are not rid of God because we still believe in grammar" (Nietzsche), that postmodernism compounds the error — gets serious traction from Heidegger's distinction between Being and beings, his suspicion of the copula, his rejection of subjectivity-philosophy. But Heidegger himself remains caught by *another* grammar: the destinal grammar of the history of Being, which the user reads as an epistemic-conditioning closure that Aurobindo's open evolutionary ontology breaks through.

### Glossary (German terms preserved untranslated where they matter)

- **Sein** — Being. The verb, the *that-it-is*, never an entity.
- **Seiendes** — being / a being / entity. Anything that *is*. Heidegger's life work: keep these two from collapsing into each other.
- **Ontologische Differenz** — the ontological difference. Being is not a being.
- **Dasein** — being-there, being-the-there. Heidegger's name for the entity we ourselves are, the one for whom Being is an issue. *Not* "human being" or "consciousness" — those are derivative. Dasein names the *site* where Being can be questioned at all.
- **In-der-Welt-sein** — being-in-the-world. A unitary phenomenon (the hyphens matter): not a subject *and* a world later joined, but world-involvement as Dasein's basic structure.
- **Sorge** — care. The unifying ontological structure of Dasein: ahead-of-itself-already-in (a world)-as-being-alongside (entities). Not psychological worry; the structural fact that Dasein's Being is at issue for it.
- **Geworfenheit** — thrownness. Dasein finds itself already delivered over to a world it did not choose, with a past it did not author.
- **Verfallenheit** — fallenness. Dasein's tendency to absorb itself in the public, the *Man* (the "they"), in chatter (*Gerede*), curiosity (*Neugier*), ambiguity (*Zweideutigkeit*).
- **Eigentlichkeit / Uneigentlichkeit** — authenticity / inauthenticity. *Eigen* means "own"; authenticity is owning one's existence as one's own. Inauthenticity is not a moral failing; it is the everyday default.
- **Sein-zum-Tode** — being-toward-death. Death as Dasein's ownmost, non-relational, certain, indefinite, not-to-be-outstripped possibility, the one that individuates absolutely.
- **Ereignis** — event of appropriation. The mutual coming-into-their-own of Being and humanity. Late Heidegger's pivotal term, untranslatable. *Eignen* (to own, to be proper to) is the root.
- **Lichtung** — clearing. The open space in the forest, the place where light gathers; the site at which entities show up as the entities they are. Not "light" (Heidegger insists) but *opening*.
- **Gelassenheit** — releasement, letting-be. The non-willful, non-calculative comportment that lets Being be what it is.
- **Gestell** — enframing. The essence of modern technology: the way Being is unconcealed today as standing-reserve (*Bestand*) for ordering and disposal.
- **Andenken** — commemorative thinking, mindful remembering. Thinking that recollects what has been forgotten — above all, Being.
- **das Geviert** — usually "the fourfold." But the German *Ge-viert* is built like *Ge-stell* and *Ge-birge*: the *Ge-* prefix names a *gathering*, a *holding-together*, of what stands under the root (*viert*, four). *Geviert* is a *four-gathering* — earth, sky, mortals, divinities held in mutual mirroring as the simple oneness of dwelling. "Fourfold" loses the gathering; we will keep saying *Geviert* / four-gathering.

Hold these. Heidegger's German is not ornamental — the words *do* the philosophical work. We will keep returning to the German.

## Part I — *Sein und Zeit* (1927)

### Introduction §§1–8: The question of Being

The book opens — characteristically — with a quotation from Plato (*Sophist* 244a) on the perplexity of those who think they know what "being" means. Heidegger's first move is to insist that this perplexity has been *forgotten*. Not solved; forgotten. The forgetting is itself the symptom.

> "This question has today been forgotten, even though our time considers itself progressive in once again affirming 'metaphysics'." (§1)

Three classical "prejudices" supposedly justify the dismissal of the question: that "Being" is the most universal concept and so needs no definition; that it is indefinable; that it is self-evident. Heidegger spends §1 dispatching all three. Universality does not equal clarity (Aristotle already saw this); indefinability of *the highest genus* does not free us from asking what its sense is; the supposed self-evidence of every "the X *is* Y" is precisely what conceals the obscurity. The forgetting is sustained by the appearance of obviousness.

§2 develops the *formal structure of every question* and applies it to the question of Being. This is one of the densest, most underrated passages in twentieth-century philosophy:

> "Every inquiry is a seeking. Every seeking is guided beforehand by what is sought. Inquiry is a knowing search for an entity, both with regard to the fact that it is and with regard to its Being as it is. Any inquiry, as an inquiry about something, has that which is asked about. In what is asked about there lies also what is to be ascertained, what the inquiry is really after." (§2)

The structure is tripartite: every question has a *Gefragtes* (that which is asked-about), a *Befragtes* (that which is interrogated), and an *Erfragtes* (that which is to be found out). For the question of Being:

> "Being is what is asked about. That which is to be interrogated is beings themselves. They are to be questioned with respect to their Being. What is to be ascertained, the sense of Being, calls for its own conceptuality." (§2)

This is Heidegger's first crucial move. Asking about Being requires interrogating *some entity* about its Being — but which entity? Not just any. The proper interlocutor is the entity that already has *some* understanding of Being, however dim, just by being the kind of entity it is — namely, *us*: Dasein. Hence §§4–5: the *ontic priority* of the question (we already exist with an inchoate understanding of Being) and the *ontological priority* (Dasein is the only entity to whom Being is an issue, *worum es geht*).

§6 announces the famous program of the *Destruktion* of the history of ontology. "Destruction" here is not nihilist demolition but a careful unbuilding — going back through the deposits of metaphysical concepts (substance, subject, object, *res cogitans*, *res extensa*, the present-at-hand) to liberate the question of Being from the sediment that has buried it. The list of those to be unbuilt is the canon of Western philosophy: Aristotle, the Schoolmen, Descartes, Kant, Hegel.

§7 — the most important methodological section in the book — declares phenomenology to be *not* one method among others but ontology itself:

> "Ontology and phenomenology are not two distinct philosophical disciplines among others. These titles characterize philosophy itself with regard to its object and its way of treating that object. Philosophy is universal phenomenological ontology." (§7C)

Phenomenology is here detached from Husserlian transcendental subjectivity and reattached to the Greek *phainesthai*, to show itself, to come into the open. The phenomenon, for Heidegger, is *das Sich-an-ihm-selbst-zeigende* (that which shows itself in itself); phenomenology is the *logos* (discourse) that lets what shows itself be seen from itself in the very way it shows itself. The combination is not technical but quasi-tautological: letting what appears be seen.

A note for the user's purposes. The Gefragtes/Befragtes/Erfragtes structure is *exactly* the structure that the user's directive question (T5: what is one to aim at?) demands. The asked-about is the lived condition of the seeker; the interrogated is the standpoints (the darśanas, the registers); the to-be-ascertained is the diaphanous through-all. This formal homology is real. But where Heidegger keeps the answer formal (the *sense* of Being, *Sinn des Seins*), the user takes the answer to be substantive — Saccidānanda's progressive self-disclosure. The structural permission Heidegger gives, the user claims; the closure he keeps (the answer must remain formal because Being is not a being and so cannot be substantively articulated) the user breaks, on the grounds that the Indian realisms — Madhva, Bhāskara, Aurobindo — never accepted that the only legitimate ontological discourse is purely formal.

### Division 1, §§9–13: Dasein and being-in-the-world

§9 names the protagonist:

> "The 'essence' of Dasein lies in its existence. That Being which is an issue for this entity in its very Being is in each case mine."

Two formulae of the entire book. *Existenz* — not the medieval *existentia* opposed to *essentia*, but the active mode of Dasein's having-to-be. *Jemeinigkeit* — mineness — but not in the sense of psychological possession; rather, the structural fact that Dasein's Being is in each case its own to take up or fail to take up. The categories appropriate to Dasein are not "categories" in the Aristotelian sense (*was-Bestimmungen*, what-determinations) but *Existenzialien* — existentials, modes of how-to-be.

A crucial methodological warning in §§10–11: Dasein is not to be analyzed by the methods of anthropology, psychology, or biology. Those sciences presuppose an ontological understanding of human being that has already been worked out (by philosophy) and *just borrowed* — usually Cartesian (substance with attributes), or Aristotelian (rational animal), or biological (organism). Heidegger wants to begin again with the existential analytic before any regional science.

§12 introduces *In-der-Welt-sein*. The hyphens are absolute. There is no *erste Trennung* between subject and object that subsequent epistemology has to bridge. Dasein is *already* in a world. The Cartesian problem of the external world is, for Heidegger, a pseudo-problem produced by an ontology that has split what is unsplit. He writes (§13) that the proof of an external world cannot be given because the demand itself is malformed: Dasein *is* world-involvement, and what would need proving is not the existence of the external world but the existence of a worldless subject — which has never actually existed.

This single move dissolves a great deal of Western epistemology. It is also the move that makes Heidegger structurally close to the Indian realist traditions the user defends: Nyāya never doubted the world; Bhāskara, Madhva, Aurobindo all proceed from a world-already-given. The user's polemic against correlationism (Meillassoux) reads Heidegger here as one of the few Western thinkers who *refuses* the Kantian containment — though, as we will see, Heidegger then re-imposes his own form of containment via the destinal history of Being.

### §§14–24: World and worldhood; *Zuhandenheit*

The single most influential analysis in twentieth-century philosophy is here: the analysis of *Zeug* (equipment, gear), the workshop, *Zuhandenheit*. The standard English "ready-to-hand" — Macquarrie's coinage — is one of the places where the translation flattens the German. *Zuhanden* literally says *to-hand*, *under-hand*, the mode in which a tool *is encountered in the very swinging-of-it*. It is not a property of the object ("readiness") but the very mode of its showing-up *in use*. We will keep the German alongside the standard English. Heidegger's example is the hammer.

> "Hammering itself discloses the hammer's specific *Handlichkeit*. The kind of Being equipment possesses, the way it manifests itself in its own right, we call *Zuhandenheit*." (§15)

The carpenter does not first stare at the hammer as a *vorhanden* object — *Vorhandenheit*, the standard English "presence-at-hand" or "objective presence," literally *before-hand*, lying-there-in-front, the mode of an object that has been wrenched out of use and set up as a contemplated *thing* — and then decide to wield it. The hammer is encountered *as* hammer in the very act of hammering, within a *Bewandtnisganzheit* (a totality of involvement-relations, of *with-this-it-has-its-bearing*-relations): hammer-for-driving-nails-for-fastening-the-board-for-building-the-house-for-sheltering-Dasein. The world is this referential totality of *Bedeutsamkeit* (significance, meaning-fulness), not the sum of objects.

When the hammer breaks, the equipment *leuchtet auf* — lights up — into *Auffälligkeit*, *Aufdringlichkeit*, *Aufsässigkeit* (conspicuousness, obtrusiveness, obstinacy) — and at *that* moment we get the *vorhanden* mode, the bare object that science and traditional epistemology took as primary. The Cartesian *res extensa* is, for Heidegger, an ontologically *derivative* mode that has been illegitimately privileged. The order of disclosure is *zuhanden* first, *vorhanden* second; the order of the philosophical tradition has been the reverse.

§§19–21 contain the polemic against Descartes. Descartes set up the world as *res extensa* — extension as the substance of corporeal nature — and thereby (a) presupposed the ontology of substance he should have interrogated, (b) made of mathematical knowing the only legitimate knowing of nature, and (c) bequeathed the modern subject-object split. Heidegger's diagnosis is decisive: this is not how we *encounter* world; it is a theoretical reduction that has forgotten its own provenance.

For the user, this is one of the most useful pages in twentieth-century philosophy. The user's polemic against the postmodern reflex (Hegelian and post-Kantian) of treating cognition as the primary mode of world-relation — and thereby replicating the correlationist closure — finds in Heidegger §§14–24 a forceful prior critique. Where Heidegger then directs the analysis (toward *Sorge* and *Sein-zum-Tode*) is one path; the user's path runs toward the Indic seam where ontology and praxis are already non-different (Mīmāṃsā's *codanā*-ontology, Yoga's somato-psychic praxis).

### §§25–38: Mitsein, das Man, fallenness, mood, understanding

§25 asks the question of *Wer*: who is Dasein? Heidegger refuses the trivial answer ("each of us, the I, the subject") because that answer has been determined by the Cartesian metaphysics he has just dismantled. The "who" is to be found *phenomenologically*, in everyday behavior. And what we find is not the autonomous I of the philosophers but *das Man* — the "they," the anonymous public, the "one says," "one does." Dasein is, in its average everydayness, *das Man-selbst*, the "they-self."

> "The self of everyday Dasein is the they-self, which we distinguish from the authentic self, the self taken up as one's own." (§27)

The *Mitsein* — being-with — is not a contingent addition to a primary solitary Dasein. We are with others ontologically, all the way down. Even when alone, we are *with*, because being-with is structural. The "they" is a mode of this being-with: the leveling-down (*Einebnung*), the average (*Durchschnittlichkeit*), the public interpretedness (*Öffentliches Ausgelegtsein*) in which we always already find ourselves.

§§29–34 give the famous analysis of the existential structures of disclosedness (*Erschlossenheit*): *Befindlichkeit* (state-of-mind, attunement, mood), *Verstehen* (understanding), *Rede* (discourse). Mood is not a psychological epiphenomenon but the way Dasein finds itself *thrown* (*geworfen*) into a world whose being-as-such-and-such matters to it. Understanding is not cognitive grasp but the projective working-out of possibilities (*Entwurf*); we understand always *as* something, always already with a *Vorhabe*, *Vorsicht*, *Vorgriff* — fore-having, fore-sight, fore-grasp (the famous "hermeneutic circle"). Discourse is the articulation of intelligibility — language is its worldly expression but is not identical with it.

§§35–38 then describe the *Verfallen* — fallenness — into idle talk (*Gerede*), curiosity (*Neugier*), and ambiguity (*Zweideutigkeit*). Fallenness is not a "fall" in the Christian sense (Heidegger insists, against early readers); it is Dasein's structural tendency to flee its ownmost Being-able-to-be by absorption in the world of the *Man*. It is the everyday default; *Eigentlichkeit* (authenticity) is the modification of fallenness, not its abolition.

### §§39–44: Care (*Sorge*) as the Being of Dasein

§39 announces the question of the unity of Dasein's structures, and §§40–42 stage the analysis through *Angst*. Anxiety, distinguished from fear (which has a determinate worldly object), discloses the *Unheimlichkeit* — the un-homeliness — of being-in-the-world: the world as such, in its sheer worldhood, becomes uncanny. The *Wovor* of anxiety is being-in-the-world as such; the *Worum* is Dasein's Being-able-to-be. Anxiety strips away the comforting sedimentation of the *Man* and confronts Dasein with itself.

The unifying structural formula is announced in §41 — and the German hyphen-string is what does the work; the English smooths it out:

> "*Sich-vorweg-schon-sein-in-(der-Welt-) als Sein-bei (innerweltlich begegnendem Seienden)* — ahead-of-itself-already-being-in-(the-world) as being-alongside (beings encountered within the world). This Being gives content to the title *Sorge* (care)."

(Stambaugh renders *Sein-bei* as "being-together-with," Macquarrie as "being-alongside." The German *bei* is the *bei* of *beim Hammer* — *at* the hammer, *by* the hammer, in the workshop sense Heidegger drew out in §15. We will keep "alongside" with the German visible.)

Three structural moments fused: *Existenzialität* (the *sich-vorweg* — ahead-of-itself — projective-future moment), *Faktizität* (the *schon-sein-in* — already-in, *Geworfenheit* — past-having-been moment), *Verfallensein* (the *Sein-bei* — alongside, absorbed in beings — present moment). *Sorge* is the *Einheit* (unity). It is not a psychological feeling; it is the structural form of an entity for whom its Being is at issue, who is always projecting, always thrown, always among.

§43 — Dasein, worldhood, and reality — clarifies that "reality" (*Realität*) is one ontological character among others, one *kind* of Being (the Being of the present-at-hand), not Being as such. The "scandal of philosophy" (Kant) of having no proof of the external world is dissolved: the demand for proof presupposes a worldless subject who has never existed. There is no scandal because there is no skeptical position from which the demand could intelligibly be raised.

§44, *Dasein, Erschlossenheit, und Wahrheit* (Dasein, disclosedness, and truth), gives Heidegger's celebrated retrieval of *aletheia* as *Un-Verborgenheit* — *un-concealment*. Heidegger insists on the alpha-privative: *a-letheia*, the *not-being-concealed*, the *being-wrested-out-of-hiddenness*. Truth as *Übereinstimmung* (correspondence, *adaequatio rei et intellectus*) is a *derivative* phenomenon; the more *ursprünglich* (originary) truth is the *Erschlossenheit* (disclosedness) in which entities can show up as the entities they are. Dasein is "in the truth" (*in der Wahrheit*) insofar as it is disclosive, and "in the untruth" (*in der Unwahrheit*) insofar as it is closed off. *Wahrheit* and *Unwahrheit* are *gleichursprünglich* — equi-originary, equally rooted at the source.

> "The Being-true of discourse, as showing something, has the character of un-concealing: it takes what one is talking about out of hiddenness and lets it be seen as unhidden." (§7B)

This single move, truth as unconcealment prior to truth as correspondence, is one of the most important things twentieth-century philosophy gave the rest of its century. The user's reading: yes, *darśana* (literally "seeing") is closer to *aletheia* than to *adaequatio*; the Indic philosophies of *pramāṇa* (means of valid cognition) already operate in a register that does not reduce truth to propositional correspondence. But Heidegger's truth-as-unconcealment is structurally receptive, Dasein lets entities show, and this passivity, when generalized, becomes the late Heidegger's *Gelassenheit*. The user's thesis, drawing on Aurobindo, refuses the equation of disclosure with passivity: *līlā* is generative, not just receptive; the supramental is projective, not merely clearing.

### Division 2, §§45–53: Being-toward-death

Division 1 analyzed Dasein's structure in its everyday averageness. Division 2 asks: what is the *whole* of this entity, given that it is always still on its way? What does Dasein's Being-a-whole look like?

§§45–46 stage the problem. Dasein is constitutively *not yet*; as long as it exists, there is something outstanding. Death seems to complete it — but only by taking it away. So: how can the *Ganzsein* (Being-whole) of Dasein be made phenomenologically accessible *without* requiring its actual ending?

Heidegger's answer: by analyzing death not as an event-in-the-world but as a *Möglichkeit des Seins* — a *possibility of Being*. §§47–49 dispense with mediating analyses (the death of others, biological death, theological eschatology). What we need is the existential analysis of *Sein-zum-Tode* — literally *being-toward-death*, though even the standard rendering loses what the *zum* (*to-the-, toward-the-*) is doing: *Sein-zum-Tode* names a structural directedness, a *being-stretched-out-into-death-as-one's-own-coming*, that runs through Dasein's existence at every moment, not only at its end.

§50's formula:

> "Death, as the end of Dasein, is Dasein's *eigenste, unbezügliche, gewisse und als solche unbestimmte, unüberholbare Möglichkeit* — Dasein's ownmost, non-relational, certain, and as such indefinite, not-to-be-outstripped possibility."

Five existential characters: *eigenste* (ownmost — it cannot be transferred to another), *unbezüglich* (non-relational — it isolates Dasein from all involvements), *gewiss* (certain — no Dasein escapes it), *unbestimmt* (indefinite — the *when* is unfixed), and *unüberholbar* (not-to-be-outstripped — no possibility outflanks it). The crucial verb is *Vorlaufen in den Tod*: *running-forward into* death. The standard English "anticipation" — Stambaugh's smoothing, Macquarrie's too — is too cognitive, too detached; *Vorlaufen* is a *running-forward-into*, a forward-leaning *towards*, a comportment of bodily-directed running rather than mental forecasting. To *run forward into* death is *not* to bring it about, but to take it on as one's ownmost possibility — and this is the existential comportment that lets Dasein wrest itself from the *Man*-self. *Das Man* says *man stirbt* — *one* dies — meaning, no one in particular, sometime, not now. *Vorlaufen* says: *ich sterbe* — *I* die, this is mine, this is the possibility that individuates me utterly.

§§51–52 contrast everyday *Sein-zum-Tode*, which evades death by chattering about it (the *Man*'s "*man stirbt*"), with authentic *Sein-zum-Tode* — which faces death as *eigenste*, ownmost. §53 gives the existential project: *Vorlaufen* — running-forward-into — is the authentic (*eigentlich* — "owning") comportment, the one that frees Dasein for its own existence.

The German phrasing matters:

> "In *Vorlaufen* into this non-relational possibility, Dasein becomes free for its ownmost *Seinkönnen* (potentiality-for-Being). *Vorlaufen* discloses to existence, as its outermost possibility, *Selbstaufgabe* (self-relinquishment), and thus shatters any rigid clinging to whatever existence has been reached." (§53)

The user's reading. Anticipation of death as individuating is, in the user's idiom, structurally close to the Upaniṣadic *yo vā etad akṣaraṃ gārgi aviditvāsmāl lokāt praiti sa kṛpaṇaḥ* (Bṛhadāranyaka III.8.10): "He who departs from this world without knowing the imperishable is a wretch." Death as the radically individuating limit is taken seriously. But Heidegger's death is *terminus*: an ownmost possibility that has nothing beyond it, no continuance, no beyond, no rebirth. The Indic traditions in which the user is rooted refuse this closure on independent metaphysical grounds (the *jīva*, *liṅga-śarīra*, *karma*-vāsanā continuity, Aurobindo's evolutionary teleology). The user does not reject Heidegger's existential analysis; the user *brackets* its ontological closure. The phenomenology of being-toward-finitude is real and individuating; the further claim that there is *nothing* beyond is a metaphysical thesis Heidegger himself elsewhere abjures.

### §§54–60: Conscience, guilt, resoluteness

§§54–60 give Heidegger's most consequential — and most controversial — analysis: how does Dasein attest to its ownmost *eigentliches Seinkönnen*? (Standard English "authentic potentiality-for-Being." But hold the German: *eigentlich* is built from *eigen* — "own." *Eigentlichkeit* is *ownness*, the *taking-of-one's-existence-as-one's-own*. The English "authenticity" carries connotations — sincerity, non-imitation, romantic self-expression — that Heidegger does not intend. We will keep saying *eigentlich* / "own" alongside the smoothed "authentic.") The attestation comes through *Gewissen* — conscience.

Conscience for Heidegger is not the moral conscience of the philosophical tradition (Kantian *Pflicht*, Christian *syneidesis*). It is an existential structure: the call (*Ruf*) of Dasein to itself, from itself, summoning itself out of *Man*-self lostness back to its ownmost ability-to-be. The call is silent (*lautlos*) — it says nothing in propositional form — and it summons Dasein to *Schuld*, guilt, but in a peculiar sense: not moral debt, but the existential structure of being-the-null-ground (*Grundsein einer Nichtigkeit*) of one's projective Being. Dasein is "guilty" because it is the entity that is its own ground without grounding itself; thrown, it has not posited its thrownness; projecting, it always projects on the basis of possibilities not all chosen.

§60 introduces *Entschlossenheit*. The standard English "resoluteness" smuggles in heroic-voluntarist connotations Heidegger explicitly rejects. The German is built on *ent-schlossen* — *un-closed*, the *lifting of closure* — and Heidegger insists that this lifting-of-closure is the proper sense, against the everyday sense of *Entschluss* as a willful "decision." *Entschlossenheit* is the disclosive comportment in which Dasein owns its *Geworfenheit* (thrownness), projects upon its ownmost possibility, and takes up its existence as its own. Heidegger writes that it is *the most extreme possibility of disclosedness*, not a forceful self-imposition. *Vorlaufende Entschlossenheit* — running-forward-resolute-openness, standard "anticipatory resoluteness" — is the unifying *eigentlich* comportment: *Vorlaufen* into death + disclosive owning = the form of authentic *Sorge*.

Two cautions. First, the rhetoric of resoluteness in §§54–60 was later co-opted, including by Heidegger himself, into the rhetoric of decision and authenticity that bled into political voluntarism in 1933–34. This is not in *Sein und Zeit*'s own argument, but the rhetorical proximity is real and must be acknowledged. Second, *Entschlossenheit* is empty of content in *Sein und Zeit*: it does not tell Dasein what to do, only that it should do what it does as its own. The book deliberately stops short of any ethical filling-in — and the lack of content is what makes it both philosophically powerful and politically dangerous.

The user's reading. The empty resoluteness — the lack of any directive content — is structurally *the same problem* the user diagnoses in T5 (the directive question: what is one to aim at?). The user's answer is the substantive one Heidegger refuses: niṣkāma karma, surrender from fullness, the lawful unfolding of *sākṣi-caitanya*. Heidegger's silence here is one of the deepest reasons the user's position must depart from Heidegger toward Aurobindo: phenomenology of authenticity without a directive substantive aim is half a philosophy.

### §§61–66: Temporality

The book's deepest move — and the one that most clearly *announces the turn* later to come — is the temporalization of care. The unity of *Sorge*, Heidegger argues, is *Zeitlichkeit* — temporality. Existentiality (ahead-of-itself) is rooted in the future (*Zukunft* — etymologically, *Zu-kunft*, the coming-toward); facticity (already-in) in the having-been (*Gewesenheit*); fallenness (alongside) in the present (*Gegenwart*). But these three temporal "ekstases" are not the linear past-present-future of vulgar time. They are the *coming-toward-itself-in-having-been-as-making-present* of Dasein.

> "Temporality temporalizes itself, in an equiprimordial way, in the ecstases of future, having-been, and present." (§65)

The unity *zeitigt* (Heidegger uses the verb actively, as if temporality "ripens" or "matures" itself; English "temporalizes" loses some of this). Temporality is not a thing but the very *zeitigen* — the active happening — of Dasein's structural unity.

*Eigentliche* (authentic, *own*) temporality has three names worth keeping in German. *Vorlaufen* (running-forward-into) is the future-comportment. *Wiederholung* — usually "repetition" or "retrieval" — is *holding-back-again*: the active fetching-back of having-been possibilities so that they can be projected anew. (The standard "repetition" loses the *holen* — the *fetching* — at the root.) And *Augenblick* — usually "the moment" — is literally *Augen-Blick*, the *glance-of-the-eye*, the gathered present that sees and is gathered in one stroke. Heidegger's Japanese readers later noticed the structural parallel with the Buddhist *kṣaṇa*, the indivisible momentary unit of arising-perishing — a parallel real and independent. *Uneigentliche* temporality has the corresponding inauthentic forms: *Gewärtigen* (awaiting, expecting), *Vergessen* (forgetting), and *Gegenwärtigen* in the mode of distraction (making-present that scatters rather than gathers).

The book breaks off after Division 2. The planned Division 3, "*Zeit und Sein*" (Time and Being), was never published as part of *Sein und Zeit*; Heidegger replaced it eventually with a 1962 lecture under that title. The promised move — from Dasein's *Zeitlichkeit* to the *Temporalität des Seins selbst*, the temporality of Being itself — was the rock the project foundered on. Heidegger himself later said (in the *Brief über den Humanismus*) that the third Division was withheld because *das Denken versagte* — *thinking failed*; the language of metaphysics inherited from Plato and Aristotle was inadequate to what he was trying to think. The *Kehre*, again on his own self-presentation, is the matter (*die Sache*) turning at the point where this language failed — not a biographical Heidegger-2 succeeding Heidegger-1, but a turn within the question of Being itself, which the thinker must follow.

The user's reading of *Sein und Zeit* in summary. The book is one of the four or five greatest works of philosophy in the twentieth century. Its phenomenology of everyday Dasein, its dismantling of the Cartesian subject-object split, its retrieval of *alētheia*, its analysis of being-toward-death, its temporalization of care — these stand. The user takes them seriously and uses them. What the user does *not* take is the implicit closure: that the existential analytic of Dasein is the proper *and only* path to the question of Being, that Dasein's finitude is metaphysically terminal, that resoluteness without content is sufficient. The user reads *Sein und Zeit* alongside the *Bhagavad Gītā*, *Bṛhadāranyaka*, the *Vivekacūḍāmaṇi*, and Aurobindo's *Life Divine* — and finds that the existential analytic *describes* a register, not the totality.

## Part II — *Brief über den Humanismus* (1947; in *Wegmarken*, GA 9, pp. 313–364)

In the autumn of 1946 Jean Beaufret, a young French philosopher who would become Heidegger's principal interlocutor in France, wrote a letter posing three questions, the first of which was: "Comment redonner un sens au mot 'Humanisme'?" — How can the word "humanism" be given meaning again? Sartre had just delivered the lecture *L'existentialisme est un humanisme*, claiming Heidegger as a fellow existentialist. Heidegger's reply, the *Brief über den 'Humanismus'*, was both a response to Beaufret and a public dissociation from Sartre. It is also the text in which Heidegger himself speaks most directly about the *Kehre* — and it is worth noting that he does *not* describe the *Kehre* as a change in his thinking. He describes it as the turning *of the question itself* at the point where the language of *Sein und Zeit* could not carry the third Division through. The published *Brief* therefore is not a "later Heidegger" replacing the "early"; it is the same *Seinsfrage* now spoken in a vocabulary — *Lichtung*, *Wahrheit des Seins*, *Sprache als Haus des Seins* — that has been wrested from the metaphysical idiom in which the early book was still partly enclosed.

The opening sentences of the letter are now canonical:

> "Thinking accomplishes the relation of Being to the essence of the human being. It does not make or cause the relation. Thinking only offers it to Being as that which has been handed over to thinking itself from Being. This offering consists in the fact that, in thinking, Being comes to language. Language is the house of Being. In its home the human being dwells. Those who think and those who create with words are the guardians of this home."

Read this with care, because every word does work. *Vollbringen*: not "produce," not "perform," but *to bring to fulfilment* — to *bring through to* completion. Thinking does not *cause* the relation between Being and human essence; that relation is already underway. Thinking *brings the relation through* by offering the relation back to Being — and this offering happens *as* thinking-becoming-language. *Sprache* — language — is then declared *Haus des Seins*: the house, the dwelling, the precinct in which Being takes up residence. Humans dwell in this house. Poets and thinkers are the *Wächter* — guardians, watchmen — of the house.

The image is precise. A house is not a tool; it is a *site* of dwelling. Language is not the instrument by which thought is communicated (the Aristotelian-Lockean view); it is the place where Being shows itself to humans and humans receive Being. Speech is ontologically constitutive, not instrumental.

Heidegger continues:

> "Their guardianship accomplishes the manifestation of Being insofar as they bring this manifestation to language through their saying, and preserve it in language."

Manifestation (*Offenbarkeit*) — opening, openness — is *brought to* language and *preserved in* language. Not generated by language; not reducible to language. The temporal structure: Being shows itself *and then* — through poets and thinkers — that showing is captured, *aufbewahren* (preserved, kept safe), in language for transmission.

The user's reading. The structural homology with Bhartṛhari's *Vākyapadīya* is real. Bhartṛhari's *śabda-tattva* — speech-as-reality — holds that *vāk* is not derivative of cognition but is cognition's own constitutive substance: there is no thought-object that is not already articulated in *paśyantī* (the seeing-speech, the pre-articulate vision-of-meaning). Heidegger's "language is the house of Being" is structurally close. But the differences must be marked. For Bhartṛhari, *śabda-tattva* is *Brahman*: speech-reality is the absolute, identical with the divine. Heidegger keeps Being agnostic of any divine substantiation. For Bhartṛhari, *paśyantī* is a real ontological level disclosed in *yogic* praxis (Patañjali's commentary explicitly links it to *samādhi*). Heidegger has no such praxis: his "guarding" is *Denken* (thinking) and *Dichten* (poetry), and remains in the precinct of textual hermeneutics. So: real seam, but Heidegger's version is incomplete from the user's standpoint. Aurobindo's reading of the Veda — *vāk* as a generative *Word* descending from supramental levels (*vāc*, *parā vāk*, the four levels of speech) — provides the substantive completion Heidegger leaves open.

Heidegger then turns to the diagnostic of "humanism." Sartre's existentialism, in placing the human at the center, replicates the very metaphysics Heidegger has been laboring to overcome: the metaphysics of subjectivity, in which the human is the measure, the foundation, the ground. Heidegger's reply is not anti-humanist in the moral sense; it is the diagnosis that *every* humanism so far — Roman *humanitas*, Renaissance *studia humanitatis*, Marxist humanism, Christian humanism, Sartrean existentialism — has placed the human within an already-decided metaphysical determination of the human's essence. Each humanism presupposes a metaphysics it has not interrogated.

> "Every humanism is either grounded in a metaphysics or makes itself the ground of one. Every determination of the essence of the human being that already presupposes an interpretation of beings without raising the question of the truth of Being is metaphysical. Insofar as the human is grasped as *animal rationale*, which now means as the acting being, thinking cannot leap into a more originary experience of the human essence."

The phrase *die Wahrheit des Seins*, the truth of Being, is the centerpiece of the post-*Kehre* vocabulary. The shift from *Sein und Zeit*'s "*Sinn des Seins*" (sense of Being) to "*Wahrheit des Seins*" is not cosmetic; it marks the move from a hermeneutic-existential framing, in which Dasein interrogates, to a destinal framing, in which Being unconceals itself in epochs and Dasein is the site of receiving.

The famous re-determination of the essence of the human follows:

> "The human is not the lord of beings. The human is the shepherd of Being."

Not master, but *shepherd*. The pastoral metaphor is deliberately Christian-monastic in resonance and deliberately anti-Promethean. Humanity's dignity (*Würde*) lies in being *called by Being* to guard its truth. This is decisively *not* anti-humanism in the sense of denigrating the human; it elevates the human to a higher and more demanding role than any humanism has so far named — the role of the one through whom Being gets to come to language.

Then comes the diagnosis of metaphysics:

> "Metaphysics indeed represents beings in their Being, and so it also thinks the Being of beings. But it does not think the difference between the two. Metaphysics does not raise the question of the truth of Being itself."

The *ontologische Differenz* — the ontological difference between Being and beings — is what metaphysics has *forgotten*. It thinks Being only *as* a property of beings (the most general property, or the highest being) and never thinks Being *as such*. The history of Western philosophy from Plato to Nietzsche, on Heidegger's diagnosis, is the history of this forgetting; even Nietzsche's "will to power" is, on Heidegger's reading, a final metaphysical determination of beings as a whole, not a step beyond metaphysics.

The third major motif of the *Brief* is the rejection of subjectivity-philosophy. Sartre had said: existence precedes essence. Heidegger replies that this formula, while it inverts the medieval *essentia precedes existentia*, *keeps the metaphysical pair intact*. The real task is to question *both* terms — both the *essentia/existentia* distinction and the *subject/object* polarity that frames them. The user's polemic against ontologized grammar takes Heidegger's analysis here as a friendly precursor: Sartre keeps the grammar of *essentia/existentia* and merely flips its order; Heidegger asks whether the grammar itself is on loan from a metaphysics already in trouble.

> "The proposition 'the human ek-sists' does not answer the question whether the human really is or is not. It answers the question of the human essence."

*Ek-sistenz* — with the hyphen — is Heidegger's neologism: standing-out into the openness of Being. The human, qua Dasein, *ek-sists*: stands out into *Lichtung*, the clearing, in which beings can show themselves as the beings they are. The human is not a *substantia* with attributes; it is a *standing-out into*. This is Heidegger's most direct refusal of the substance-metaphysics that runs from Aristotle through Descartes to Husserl.

The *Brief* closes with a remarkable passage on thinking and acting:

> "Thinking acts insofar as it thinks. This action is presumably the simplest and at the same time the highest, because it concerns the relation of Being to the human being."

Thinking is itself a kind of acting — *the highest* — because it accomplishes (*vollbringt*) the relation of Being to humanity. This is one of the lines that has been read as Heidegger's quietism, his withdrawal from political-ethical action. The user's reading: there is real truth here — thinking *is* a mode of action, and *Denken* in its *Andenken* mode (commemorative thinking) is a form of work. But to make thinking *the highest* form of acting is to inscribe a hierarchy that the user's commitment to *karma-yoga* refuses. *Niṣkāma karma* — desireless action — is at least co-equal with thinking; the *Bhagavad Gītā*'s rejection of mere thinking-as-action (Arjuna's would-be renunciation in chapter 1) is exactly what Aurobindo retrieves against Heideggerian quietism. Action without ego-arrogation is not below thinking; it is thinking's somatic completion.

A final point on the *Brief*. Heidegger here speaks of *die Lichtung des Seins* — the clearing of Being — for the first time as the central image of the post-*Kehre* work. The clearing is the open space in which beings can show themselves; it is *not* a metaphor for cognition, *not* the human mind, *not* light in the optical sense. It is the *open-ness* itself, prior to any seer or seen. The human ek-sists into this clearing as the entity who can *attend to* the open as such. The user reads *Lichtung* as Heidegger's strongest term for the *aperspectival condition* (Gebser) — the diaphanous through-all that the user's thesis names. But again: Heidegger's *Lichtung* is structurally *receptive* (the clearing is *given*, Dasein attends), and the user's aperspectival is *generative* (the diaphanous is the *active* through-flow of all registers, what Aurobindo calls the supramental's *projecting* poise).

## Part III — *Identität und Differenz* (1957; GA 11, pp. 27–80)

In February 1957, Heidegger delivered two short lectures — one at Freiburg ("Der Satz der Identität"), one at Todtnauberg ("Die onto-theo-logische Verfassung der Metaphysik") — that were published together as *Identität und Differenz*. The slim volume contains, in compressed form, the late Heidegger's most consequential single argument: that all metaphysics from Anaximander to Nietzsche has had the same structural form, which Heidegger calls *Onto-Theo-Logik* — the conjoint thinking of beings as a totality (*onto-*) and beings as grounded in a highest being (*theo-*) under the unifying principle of grounding (*-logik*).

### The principle of identity

The first lecture takes up Parmenides' formula usually rendered "for thinking and Being are the same" and asks what *the same* means here. Not identity in the trivial sense of A=A, but belonging-together. Thinking and Being are *zusammengehörig*, they belong together not because one reduces to the other but because they are mutually called into the *Ereignis*, the event of appropriation. This is the first major appearance in Heidegger's published work of *Ereignis* as the central term of his late thinking, though it had been worked out in the unpublished *Beiträge* twenty years earlier.

> "The principle of identity now says that selfsameness includes togetherness, the uniting into unity."

Identity is not the bare A=A of formal logic. It is *Selbigkeit*, selfsameness, which is itself a gathering (*Versammlung*), a belonging-together. The Parmenidean terms for Being and thinking are the same not because they collapse into one another but because they belong to the same *Ereignis*. The *Ereignis* is the third term that lets the two be the same without being one.

This is structurally important for the user. The *Selbigkeit*-not-*Einheit* distinction maps onto exactly the kind of move the user's thesis makes against Hegelian totalization: the registers of the darśanas, the modes of the divine, the levels of speech (Bhartṛhari's four), are *zusammengehörig* — belong-together — but do not collapse into a single Hegelian Concept. *Selbigkeit* without *Einheit* is one of Heidegger's gifts to the user's anti-totalizing project.

### The onto-theological constitution of metaphysics

The second lecture is the major one. Heidegger asks: how has metaphysics, throughout its history, thought Being? And his answer is sharper and more diagnostic than anything in *Sein und Zeit*. Metaphysics has thought Being in a doubled way: as the most general (*koinotaton*), that which is common to all beings, and as the highest (*hypsiston*), the supreme being that grounds all others. Aristotle's *Metaphysics* contains both moves: ontology in the book on being qua being, and theology in the book on the unmoved mover, the divine.

> "Metaphysics thinks of beings as such in the manner of grounding representation. In its essence, metaphysics is therefore onto-theo-logy."

The unity of *onto-* and *theo-* is *-logik*: the reason-giving, ground-providing, *Begründung*. Metaphysics is the discourse in which beings are accounted for *both* by being grouped under their commonality (the *most general*) *and* by being traced back to a highest cause (the *supreme*). The two are not separate disciplines that happen to coexist; they are structurally *the same* move — the move of grounding-representation.

The diagnostic punchline:

> "Perdurance yields and bestows Being as generative ground, and that ground in turn demands its own grounding, causation by the most originary matter. This is the cause as *causa sui*."

*Austrag* — perdurance, carrying-out, the difference *as* difference — gives Being *as* the generative ground, but then this ground itself, viewed from the perspective of what it grounds, requires *its own* grounding-cause — the *causa sui*, the self-causing cause, the metaphysical god of philosophy. The metaphysical god is not the god of religion; it is the supreme being that the *ontological* logic of grounding requires in order to close itself.

Then comes the famous passage:

> "To this god the human can neither pray nor sacrifice. Before the *causa sui*, the human can neither fall to the knees in awe nor play music and dance."

This is one of the great sentences of twentieth-century thought. Read it slowly. The metaphysical god — the *causa sui* of Aquinas, Leibniz, the rational theology of the moderns — is a *god you cannot pray to*. You cannot fall to your knees in *Scheu* (awe, reverence, the German has a religious tonality lost in English); you cannot *musizieren und tanzen* — play music and dance — before this god. The metaphysical god is, in the strict sense, *liturgically dead*: it does not call forth worship, gratitude, or joy. Heidegger's diagnosis is that the *causa sui* and the *real* divine have nothing to do with one another — and that Western philosophy's confusion of the two is the source of both onto-theology *and* its inevitable Nietzschean obituary ("God is dead" — but the god who died is the *causa sui*, not whatever divine the religions point toward).

> "Accordingly, godless thinking, which must abandon the god of philosophy, the god as *causa sui*, is perhaps closer to the divine God."

Read this with care. *Gott-loses Denken* — godless thinking — is *not* atheist thinking; it is thinking that has *let go of* the philosophical god, the *causa sui*. And Heidegger says this thinking is *perhaps closer to* the *divine* God (*dem göttlichen Gott*) than the metaphysical theology that produced and then mourned the *causa sui*. The conditional *vielleicht* — perhaps — is honest: Heidegger is not making a claim *about* the divine God; he is clearing the metaphysical underbrush so that the divine God could, perhaps, again be heard.

The user's reading. This is one of the most directly useful passages in the entire late Heidegger corpus for the user's thesis. The user's commitment to a real divine — Krishna, Kālī, the Iṣṭa-deva, the Mother — is *exactly the kind of divine* Heidegger says philosophy has lost the right to even discuss as long as it operates in the *causa sui* register. The user's polemic against ontologized grammar is precisely a polemic against the conditions under which the *causa sui* has hegemonic status. Bhakti from fullness (T10), the divine perceived in lived encounter (T11), the divine forms as real powers (Forms section of POSITION_BRIEF) — none of this is the *causa sui*, and none of it is touched by the death-of-god critique that targets the *causa sui*. Heidegger gives the user a clean philosophical defense of the right *not* to identify the divine of devotion with the metaphysical highest being.

### The step back

The methodological correlate of the diagnosis is what Heidegger calls *der Schritt zurück* — the step back. The step back is not regress, not nostalgia, not return-to-origin in the historicist sense. It is the step *back out of metaphysics into the essence of metaphysics* — that is, into what metaphysics has *forgotten* in order to be metaphysics. We do not step *forward* beyond metaphysics; we step *back* into the un-thought of metaphysics, which is the *ontological difference* itself, the *Austrag* of Being and beings.

> "The step back goes out of metaphysics into the essence of metaphysics."

This is methodologically important. Heidegger is *not* offering a *new* metaphysics; he is offering a way of *attending* to what metaphysics has covered over. The step back does not deliver a new system; it inaugurates a different *Denken* — a different mode of thinking, slower, more poetic, more attentive, less assertoric.

The user's reading of the step back. The step back is structurally analogous to the *vivarta-vāda* move *in reverse*: where vivarta-vāda steps back from the empirical to the absolute, the step back here steps back from the metaphysics-of-grounding to its un-grounded clearing. It is also analogous to Aurobindo's *involution* (the descent of Saccidānanda into matter, life, mind) read in reverse as a *sādhana*: stepping back through mind toward Overmind toward Supermind. The structural homology is real. The difference: Heidegger's step back stops at *Lichtung* (the clearing), which has no positive content; the user's step back continues into the supramental, which is Saccidānanda — substantively positive, generative, evolutionary. Heidegger's step back is, in Aurobindo's terms, *complete only up to the level of the Overmind* — and even there only as a *clearing* not as a *positive disclosure*.

### Difference as difference

The deepest move of *Identität und Differenz* is the thinking of *Differenz als Differenz* — difference as difference. Metaphysics thinks the *between* of Being and beings, but only by making each into a being-like thing and then asking how they relate. The proper thinking of difference would think the *between* itself — *Austrag*, the carrying-out, the perdurance — without converting it into a third thing.

> "Perdurance is not an afterthought concept. It is the matter of thinking."

Difference *as* difference — *die Differenz als Differenz* — is what metaphysics has never thought because it has always thought difference as difference *between* two beings (or between Being-as-a-being and beings). To think difference as difference is to refuse the conversion of difference into a relation between *re-* lata.

This is Deleuze's connection to Heidegger that often goes unstated. Deleuze's univocity of being (*Difference and Repetition*) takes up exactly this thread: Being is said in a single sense of all that is said, but that of which it is said *differs*. Difference is primary; identity is the surface effect of difference's repeated production. The user's thesis explicitly takes Deleuze's univocity-with-difference as one of its auxiliary integrations; reading Heidegger here, the user finds that Deleuze's *univocity* and Heidegger's *Selbigkeit-not-Einheit* are saying nearly the same thing in different idioms. Both refuse Hegelian totalization without falling into pluralism's relativism.

The user's break with Heidegger on difference. The *Austrag* in Heidegger remains *formal* — it is never given substantive content beyond the formula that Being and beings differ. The user, following Aurobindo, reads the supramental as *substantive Austrag*: the active, generative, projecting differentiation in which the One *is said* in many senses without ceasing to be One and without the senses ceasing to be many. The user's move is not against Heidegger but past him: through the formal opening Heidegger preserves, into the substantive disclosure Heidegger does not deliver.

## Part IV — *Beiträge zur Philosophie (Vom Ereignis)* (1936–38; GA 65, published 1989)

The *Beiträge zur Philosophie (Vom Ereignis)* — "Contributions to Philosophy (Of the Event)" — is the manuscript Heidegger wrote between 1936 and 1938 and held back from publication, to be released only as Volume 65 of the *Gesamtausgabe* in 1989, on the centenary of his birth. It is, by common assent of the secondary literature, his second major work after *Sein und Zeit*, and the place where the *Kehre* is *enacted*, not merely diagnosed.

The book is organized as a *Fuge* — a fugue — in six "joinings" (*Fügungen*): *Anklang* (echo, resonance), *Zuspiel* (interplay, the play-toward), *Sprung* (leap), *Gründung* (grounding), *die Zukünftigen* (the ones-to-come), *der letzte Gott* (the last god). The form is musical-architectural rather than systematic-deductive: each *Fügung* is a meditative variation on the same matter — *Ereignis* — from a different *fuge* (joint, hinge).

### Ereignis: not "event" in the ordinary sense

The pivotal term is *Ereignis*. Standard German for "event," but Heidegger's reading is the etymological one. *Ereignis* is built from *eignen* — to be proper to, to come into one's own — and the root carries forms like *eigen* (own), *zueignen* (to dedicate, appropriate to oneself), *enteignen* (to expropriate). Heidegger reads *Er-eignis* as *the event of mutual coming-into-one's-own*: the event in which Being and Dasein come into the relation that is proper to each, by being given over to each other. The relation is not between two pre-given terms; the *terms* are constituted *in* the relation.

> "The event is the self-instituting middle into which all essential relations of beings must be swung back."

The *sich selbst stiftende Mitte* — the self-instituting middle — does not lie *between* two pre-given relata; it *constitutes* the relata as the relata they are. This is the structural move that takes Heidegger past *Sein und Zeit*. There, Dasein was the privileged interlocutor of the question of Being. Here, Dasein is itself only insofar as it is *zugeeignet* — appropriated, given over — to Being, and Being is itself only insofar as it *ereignet* — events itself toward — Dasein. The two *belong-together* in the *Ereignis*, neither prior, neither sufficient.

The book's most cited single sentence:

> "Being essences as the *Ereignis*."

*Wesen* used as a verb — *west*, "essences" — is Heidegger's signature post-*Kehre* move. Being is no longer a noun referring to a property of beings; Being *happens, unfolds, comes-to-presence*. And what *Sein* does, in its essencing, is *Ereignis*: the appropriative event. Being is not a thing that *causes* events; Being *is* the eventing in which beings and Dasein come into their own.

### The other beginning

The *Beiträge* announces what Heidegger calls *der andere Anfang*, the other beginning. The first beginning, Heidegger says, was the Greek beginning, Anaximander, Heraclitus, Parmenides, the pre-Socratics, in whose thinking Being once briefly came to language as *physis*, *aletheia*, *moira*, and *logos*. That beginning was almost immediately covered over by the metaphysics of Plato and Aristotle, which set Western thought on the path of *Seinsvergessenheit* (forgetting of Being) that reaches its consummation in modern technology and the *Gestell*. The other beginning is not a return to the Greek beginning; it is a new commencement that learns from the first beginning what was unthought there and what must now be thought.

> "The undertaking of the *Contributions* is the preparation of the passage from the first to the other beginning."

The other beginning is *vorbereitet* — prepared — but not *brought about* — by the *Beiträge*. Heidegger insists the book is preparatory, in a way that recalls John the Baptist preparing the way: a thinker can ready the soil but cannot make the event come. The other beginning will *event itself* (*sich ereignen*) when it does, or will not.

### The fugue, the leap, the last god

The six joinings form an architectural rather than logical sequence. *Anklang* (echo): the *Ereignis* sounds in our age as a distant echo, audible only to thinking that has been chastened by the *Gestell*. *Zuspiel* (interplay): the dialogue between first and other beginning. *Sprung* (leap): the *Sprung* into the *Ereignis* itself — a leap because the passage is not gradual, not deducible, not preparable in the manner of a rational argument. *Gründung* (grounding): the *Da-sein* (now hyphenated and reread) as the *site* in which *Ereignis* takes place, the founding of the *there* of Being. *Die Zukünftigen* — the ones to come, the future ones, those who can think the *Ereignis*. *Der letzte Gott* — the last god.

The "last god" is a phrase that has caused enormous interpretive controversy. Heidegger writes:

> "The last god, the wholly Other over against the gods that have been, especially the Christian one."

The *letzte Gott* is *not* the metaphysical god, *not* the Christian God of dogmatic theology, *not* a return to paganism. It is the *passing* (*Vorbeigang*) in which the divine — *not philosophy's, not the church's* — would touch us in a way for which we have no name. The user's reading: this is structurally close to the *iṣṭa-devatā* tradition — the chosen deity, the personal divine that meets the seeker as utterly Other and yet utterly intimate, *not* the metaphysical highest being and *not* the abstract impersonal Absolute. Heidegger's *letzte Gott* is, however, *purely formal*: he refuses to give it any substantive content beyond the formal predicate of being the *wholly Other*. The user's traditions — Vaiṣṇava, Śākta, Śaiva — fill in the substantive content Heidegger keeps blank, and the user's thesis defends the right of those traditions to do so against the post-Nietzschean European reflex that any substantive divine is by definition a metaphysical *causa sui*.

### A long passage

> "The final question is that of the *Ereignis* as the historicity of Beyng. With its working-through, being-historical thinking is brought to its beginning. It is no body of doctrine and no 'teaching'; it is the founding of *Da-sein* as the moment-site for the contestability of the truth of Beyng."

Several things to note. First, *Seyn* — Heidegger uses the archaic spelling to mark a distinction from the metaphysically-determined *Sein*. Second, *Augenblicks-Ort* — moment-site — recalls the *Augenblick* of *Sein und Zeit* §65 (authentic temporality's gathered present), now spatialized: Da-sein is the *site* at which Being's truth can become contested, *erstreitbar*, fought-out. Third, the *Geschichtlichkeit des Seyns* — the historicity of Beyng — is the *destinal sending* (*Geschick*) of Being in the epochs of Western metaphysics. Heidegger's claim is that we now stand at the *end* of one such epoch — the technological-metaphysical — and the question is whether *another* epoch, the *other beginning*, can be prepared.

### What the user takes and what the user breaks

What the user takes from the *Beiträge*:

1. The *Ereignis* as *the self-instituting middle*. Structurally, this is the right shape for the relation between divine and individual that the user defends — neither reducing divine to individual (subjectivism) nor individual to divine (acosmism), but holding both in a *zugeeignet* relation that constitutes each as such.

2. The diagnostic of the *Gestell* as the consummation of *Seinsvergessenheit*. The user's polemic against the postmodern reflex of treating cognition as the only legitimate world-relation finds in Heidegger here a powerful prior critique.

3. The *fugue* form as a refusal of system. The *Beiträge* does not aim at deduction; it sounds the same matter from multiple sides. This is methodologically close to the user's own commitment to *standpoint discipline*: bound speech, seeker speech, knower speech, ritual speech, polemical speech, limit speech are not interchangeable. Heidegger's fugue is *Standpunktsdiziplin* in compositional form.

4. The *letzte Gott* as the wholly Other against onto-theology. The user's right to a real, lived, personally-encountered divine that is not the metaphysical *causa sui* gets philosophical cover from Heidegger's refusal of philosophy's god.

What the user breaks:

1. The *exclusivity* of the German-Greek axis. Heidegger's "first beginning" is the Greek pre-Socratics; his "other beginning" is the German future. There is no place in his historicity-of-Beyng for the Indic traditions. Even when Heidegger engaged Asian thinking late in life (the Tezuka dialogue), the engagement remained tangential to the destinal narrative. The user's thesis runs through the Veda, Bhartṛhari, Madhva, Caitanya, Aurobindo — and treats those as *equiprimordial* sources of the question, not as outside the destinal narrative but as *another beginning that already happened* in another precinct of the human.

2. The *purely formal* character of the *letzte Gott*. Heidegger refuses substantive content; the user, on independent traditional grounds, fills in substantive content (Krishna, Kālī, the Mother) — and defends the right to do so against the European post-Nietzschean reflex.

3. The *quietism* of preparation. Heidegger's stance is fundamentally one of *waiting* (*Warten*) — for the *Ereignis* to come, for the other beginning to dawn, for the divine to touch. The user's stance is fundamentally one of *practiced surrender* — *prapatti* — which is *not* waiting and *not* doing in the willful sense, but the active continuance of *niṣkāma karma* in the disposition of *bhakti*. The phenomenology is close; the disposition diverges.

## Part V — *Holzwege* / *Wegmarken* essays (GA 5, GA 9)

The two collections, *Holzwege* (1950, "forest paths," from the German proverb that *Holzwege* are paths that fade into the woods and end in clearings nobody planned) and *Wegmarken* (1967, "way-markers"), gather Heidegger's most-read essays of the post-*Kehre* decades. Among the major pieces are "Der Spruch des Anaximander" (1946), "Hegels Begriff der Erfahrung" (1942/43), "Nietzsches Wort 'Gott ist tot'" (1943), "Der Ursprung des Kunstwerkes" (1935/36), and "Wozu Dichter?" (1946), in *Holzwege*; "Was ist Metaphysik?" (1929) with its 1949 introduction "Der Rückgang in den Grund der Metaphysik," "Vom Wesen der Wahrheit" (1930/1943), "Brief über den 'Humanismus'" (1947), and "Vom Wesen des Grundes" (1929), in *Wegmarken*; and, just outside those collections, the 1953 lecture "Die Frage nach der Technik." We will engage the essays most directly relevant to the user's project.

### "Der Spruch des Anaximander" (1946; GA 5, pp. 321–373)

Heidegger's reading of Anaximander's surviving fragment, the oldest piece of philosophical Greek we possess, is the densest meditation in the Heidegger corpus on what the first beginning sounded like. Heidegger treats the fragment in its Greek wording, then renders it into an aggressively non-classical German that tries to hear presencing and fittingness before later metaphysics hardens them into moral or juridical terms.

The English (Hofstadter) is necessarily looser: "along the lines of usage; for they let order and thereby also reck belong to one another, in the surmounting of disorder, according to the assignment of what unfolds time, by way of time." The crucial terms are *chreon* (Heidegger hears *Brauch*, usage), *dike* (*Fug*, fittingness or joinedness), *tisis* (*Ruch*, reckoning), and *adikia* (*Un-Fug*, dis-jointing).

What Heidegger reads in the fragment is the *first* — the most originary — saying of the relation between Being and beings: beings are *fugged* (jointed) into and out of presence according to the *Brauch* (the usage, the way) of Being itself; their presencing and their absencing are not events that happen to them but the very mode in which Being grants and withdraws. The *adikia* — the *Un-Fug*, the dis-joining — is not moral injustice but the structural fact that beings are out of joint with respect to Being whenever they assert themselves as if they were the ground of their own presence.

> "Presencing does not occur without the surmounting of dis-jointure fitting itself in accord with usage."

The user's reading. The fragment, on Heidegger's interpretation, is structurally close to the Vedic *ṛta* — cosmic order — and to the Daoist *dao* — the way. The Anaximandrian *Brauch*, the Vedic *ṛta*, the Daoist *dao*: each names the *way of way-making* in which beings come to presence and pass out of presence according to a structural usage that is not itself a being. Heidegger's reading of Anaximander is one of the rare places in his corpus where the cross-cultural homology with Indic and Chinese thought becomes structurally visible — though Heidegger himself does not draw the cross-cultural lines. The user does.

### "Nietzsches Wort 'Gott ist tot'" (1943; GA 5, pp. 209–267)

Heidegger's reading of Nietzsche's celebrated proclamation is, like the *Identität und Differenz* discussion, a diagnostic. Nietzsche's "God is dead" — pronounced through the *Tollen Mensch* (madman) of *The Gay Science* §125 — is read by Heidegger *not* as the announcement of cultural atheism, *not* as a thesis about religious belief, but as the announcement of the collapse of the *supersensuous world* — Plato's true world, the moral-metaphysical superstructure that has organized Western life for two and a half millennia.

> "The pronouncement 'God is dead' means: the supersensuous world is without effective power. It bestows no life."

The "God" who has died is the metaphysical God — the *causa sui*, the supersensuous absolute — whose function was to organize the sensuous-supersensuous hierarchy of Platonism-Christianity. Nietzsche's proclamation is the inevitable consequence of the metaphysical history: the supersensuous world, having been progressively emptied (Cartesian doubt, Kantian noumenal restriction, Hegelian secularization), has now lost its organizing power. *Was bleibt*, what remains? Nihilism — but nihilism, on Heidegger's reading, is not the *opposite* of the metaphysical project; it is its *consummation*. The metaphysical project always already contained nihilism as its inner telos.

Heidegger's deeper claim is that Nietzsche himself remained metaphysical: the doctrine of *will to power* and the *eternal return* are, for Heidegger, the final metaphysical determinations of beings as a whole — values for the *will*. Nietzsche thus *completes* metaphysics rather than overcoming it. Only *another beginning* — Heidegger's own project — can step out of the metaphysical history.

The user's reading. Heidegger's reading of Nietzsche is partially right and partially captures Nietzsche too tightly into Heidegger's own destinal narrative. The reading of "God is dead" as targeting the *causa sui* is correct and confirms the user's defense of a non-metaphysical divine (T7). The reading of *eternal return* as a metaphysical determination of beings as values is, however, contested by Deleuze's reading (which the user explicitly endorses): the eternal return is the return of *difference*, not the same; it is anti-metaphysical in Deleuze's sense, not metaphysical in Heidegger's. The user holds Deleuze's reading against Heidegger's here. Both readings can be true at different levels: Nietzsche the polemicist *did* sometimes write as if "will to power" were a metaphysical thesis; Nietzsche the affirmer wrote of return as repetition of difference. The thesis selects the second, with Deleuze.

### "Der Ursprung des Kunstwerkes" (1935/36; GA 5, pp. 1–74)

This essay is indispensable for the user's project because it is where Heidegger shows, in the densest way, how truth can happen without taking the form of correct representation. The famous thesis is that the artwork does not first exist and then express a meaning; rather, "the work-being of the work" consists in the happening of truth. The Greek temple, van Gogh's peasant shoes, the tragedy, and the poem are not illustrations. They are cases in which a world is set up and earth is set forth, so that beings can appear otherwise than they do within ordinary utility.

Heidegger's most important distinction in the essay is between *world* and *earth*. World is the open whole of significance, practice, orientation, and historical intelligibility in which a people dwells. Earth is what withdraws from every disclosure even while supporting it: stone, color, sound, material density, the self-secluding side of presence. A genuine work holds these in *strife* (*Streit*). It opens a world only by letting earth resist full conceptual capture. That is why art matters to the user's thesis. The work preserves a non-totalized disclosure. It makes something manifest, but it does not dissolve material opacity into concept.

This also clarifies why Heidegger's account of art is stronger than a mere aesthetics of taste. The work is not a pleasing object for a subject; it is a site in which truth happens historically. The user's use of aesthetic disinterestedness, from Kant through Schopenhauer to Abhinavagupta, can take this directly. Art is a privileged register because it discloses without collapsing what it discloses into utility, dogma, or system. The work's truth-content is therefore one of Heidegger's closest approaches to the user's thesis that different standpoints disclose the Real without being absorbed into a total grammar.

### "Vom Wesen der Wahrheit" (1930/1943; in *Wegmarken*, GA 9, pp. 177–202)

The 1930 lecture, revised through the 1940s, is Heidegger's most important shorter essay on *aletheia*. The thesis: the essence of truth is *Freiheit* — freedom — and the essence of freedom is the *Sein-lassen des Seienden* — letting beings be.

> "The essence of truth is freedom. The essence of truth reveals itself as freedom. It is the ek-sistent, revealing letting-be of beings."

Read this carefully. *Sein-lassen* is *not* indifference (it is not English "let alone") and *not* permissiveness; it is *letting-into-the-open-where-beings-can-show-themselves-as-the-beings-they-are*. Truth is *freedom-as-Sein-lassen* because only an entity that can *let beings be* — that does not impose its categories on them, does not reduce them to standing-reserve, does not annex them to the *Gestell* — is in a position for truth-as-unconcealment to occur. Truth requires *receptive freedom*.

The essay's deeper move is the famous claim that truth's essence includes *un-truth* — *Un-Wahrheit* — as its co-original: truth is always also concealment, always also *Verborgenheit*, always partial. Every unconcealment of *one* aspect of beings is the concealment of *another*. This is not skepticism; it is the structural recognition that disclosure is always perspectival, partial, conditioned by what is *not* shown.

The user's reading. *Sein-lassen* — letting-be — is structurally close to *upekṣā* in Buddhist psychology and to the disinterestedness of the user's *niṣkāma karma* / Kantian *interesseloses Wohlgefallen* / Schopenhauerean Sabbath-of-will / Abhinavagupta *rasāsvāda*. The user's auxiliary integration explicitly groups these. Heidegger's *Sein-lassen* is one of the strongest Western articulations of the *aesthetic-disinterested* condition that the user reads as the universalization of the right human comportment toward the world. The truth-includes-untruth thesis is also taken on board: the user's commitment to *standpoint discipline* and to *register-thesis* (T1) is precisely the recognition that any disclosure is always partial, always co-original with concealment, never total.

### *Parmenides* (winter semester 1942/43; GA 54)

The *Parmenides* lectures, delivered while the Wehrmacht was still in Stalingrad, are Heidegger's most sustained reading of the goddess of truth in Parmenides' fragment B 1, and his most polemical treatment of what he calls the Roman translation of Greek words: *aletheia* into *veritas*, *physis* into *natura*, *logos* into *ratio*, *polis* into *res publica*. The thesis is that the Roman re-coining is not innocent translation but a metaphysical re-determination: the Greek word that named unconcealment (*Un-Verborgenheit*) was replaced by the Latin word that named correspondence (*adaequatio*), and with that replacement the whole subsequent grammar of Western metaphysics was set on its course. Heidegger's blunt formulation is that the Roman translation transfers the essence of truth from the clearing of Being into the correctness of representation.

A briefer key passage:

> "With this translation of the Greek names into Latin, something more than a transfer is at work, as people still today believe. With this translation, the first step in the concealment and de-realization of the originary is carried out." (GA 54, §3)

The user's reading. This is one of the most useful Heidegger passages for the user's polemic against ontologized grammar. Heidegger is here saying, in his own idiom, exactly what the user's thesis says: the *grammar* in which a thought is cast does ontological work, and a translation that switches grammars is not neutral. The user's polemic against the post-Kantian / Hegelian / postmodern reflex of reading every philosophical claim *through* the substance-attribute / subject-predicate / signifier-signified grammars is structurally identical with Heidegger's diagnosis of the Latin re-coining of the Greek. There is, however, a divergence: Heidegger diagnoses the Latin betrayal but stops short of considering the *Sanskrit* alternative. The Sanskrit philosophical vocabulary — *sat*, *cit*, *ānanda*, *jñāna*, *darśana*, *vāc*, *śabda*, *brahman*, *ātman*, *prakṛti*, *puruṣa*, *guṇa* — has its *own* grammar, neither Greek nor Latin, and its categories are not collapsible to either. Heidegger never raised the question whether his own diagnosis applies *also* to the Greek-as-against-Sanskrit, and the user's thesis raises it on his behalf: if Latin distorted Greek, what should be said of any European philosophical reading of Sanskrit conducted in Latin or German categories? The Tezuka dialogue worried this question for Japanese; the question for Sanskrit was never seriously joined.

A second strand of the *Parmenides* lectures, the meditation on *polis* as *Statt* (place, site) and on the uncanny (*das Unheimliche*) as the proper home of historical Dasein, is the place where the lecture course's political context becomes most legible. Heidegger reads the *polis* not as a political community in the Roman *res publica* sense but as the site in which the destinings of Being are decided for a people. The proximity of this rhetoric to the destinal-historical narrative of the *Schwarze Hefte* and the 1933 *Rektoratsrede* is uncomfortable and must be flagged. The *Parmenides* lectures are philosophically rich and politically compromised in the same passages.

### "Die Frage nach der Technik" (1953; in *Vorträge und Aufsätze*)

If one major Heidegger essay had to be added for the user's present project beyond the texts already central here, it would be "The Question Concerning Technology." This is where *Gestell* becomes fully explicit. Heidegger's thesis is not that technology is a collection of tools and machines, nor primarily that it is dangerous because it can be used badly. The essence of modern technology is a mode of revealing in which beings appear only as *Bestand*, standing-reserve, resources on call for extraction, storage, optimization, and use.

That is why the famous formula matters: the essence of technology is nothing technological. It is ontological. A hydroelectric plant, a coal field, a forest, a river, a worker, a population, even language itself, come to show up within enframing as inventories awaiting ordering. Heidegger's critique therefore reaches far beyond gadgets. It is a diagnosis of a civilizational stance. The user's critique of modern epistemic and political closure can use this directly. What Heidegger calls *Gestell* is one of the sharpest Western analyses of how one register of disclosure tries to annex all the others.

The essay's last movement also matters. Heidegger does not answer technology with primitivism. He says that where danger is, there grows the saving power also. The point is that once enframing is recognized as a mode of revealing rather than as fate in the vulgar sense, another relation to revealing becomes thinkable again. Art, meditative thinking, and non-appropriative attention reappear here as counter-movements. For the user's thesis, this is where Heidegger comes closest to a practical civilizational diagnosis of why the non-totalizing registers matter now, not just in textual exegesis but in a world governed by optimization, extraction, and managed visibility.

### "Was ist Metaphysik?" (1929) and its 1949 introduction (in *Wegmarken*, GA 9, pp. 103–122 and pp. 365–383)

The 1929 inaugural lecture at Freiburg is the place where Heidegger first poses the question of *the nothing* — *das Nichts* — as the proper subject of metaphysics. The famous formula:

> "The nothing itself nihilates."

This sentence drove Carnap to apoplexy (his 1932 essay "Überwindung der Metaphysik durch logische Analyse der Sprache" cited it as paradigmatic of metaphysics-as-nonsense). The sentence is, however, a serious philosophical claim once read in context. Heidegger's *Nichts* is not the negation of being in the propositional sense; it is the *Nichten* (nihilation, no-thinging) that *holds beings out into the open* by being the abyssal background against which beings show up *as* beings. *Angst* — anxiety, as in *Sein und Zeit* §40 — is the disposition in which the nothing is encountered: *all beings* slip away, what remains is the sheer *that-it-is* of being-in-the-world, and this is the *Nichts* by which beings are first held into the clearing.

The 1949 introduction — "Der Rückgang in den Grund der Metaphysik," the going-back into the ground of metaphysics — adds the vocabulary of the post-*Kehre*: the *Lichtung*, the destinal sendings of Being, the relation between metaphysics and the question of Being. The introduction is one of the clearest summary statements of Heidegger's late position.

> "Metaphysics is in every respect the first thing of philosophy. It does not reach into what is first for thinking. Metaphysics is overcome in the question of the truth of Being."

*Überwinden* — overcoming — is Heidegger's word for the relation his thinking has to metaphysics: not abolition, not refutation, but *getting through* and *getting past*. (The English "overcome" loses the *-winden*, twist, that the German preserves; *Verwindung* — twisting-into, getting-over — is Heidegger's later word for the same relation, with the connotation of *recovering from* an illness rather than defeating an enemy.)

The user's reading of these essays as a group. The *Holzwege* and *Wegmarken* essays are the laboratory in which Heidegger's late vocabulary gets its precision: *Lichtung*, *Gestell*, *Ereignis*, *Andenken*, *Sein-lassen*, *Verwindung*, *Geviert*. The user takes most of this vocabulary on board as a usable Western parallel for the structural moves the Indic traditions make in their own idiom. The user breaks with the implicit destinal narrative — that all this is happening to *us* (the German-Greek "we") in *our* history — and reads the same structural moves as occurring trans-culturally, trans-historically, wherever the registers of the Real are seriously engaged.

## Part VI — Heidegger and the Indian / Asian

The question of Heidegger's relation to Asian thinking is delicate. He read no Sanskrit, no Pali, no Chinese, no Japanese; what he knew of Asian thought he received through the German Indological and Sinological literature, through translations (often loose), and through encounters with individual Asian interlocutors — most famously with the Japanese aestheticist Tezuka Tomio, whose 1954 visit to Heidegger's Freiburg study became the basis for Heidegger's published "Aus einem Gespräch von der Sprache: Zwischen einem Japaner und einem Fragenden" (in *Unterwegs zur Sprache*, 1959). The dialogue is fictional in form (Heidegger reconstructs and reshapes the conversation) but the subject is real: how, if at all, can a thinking that has emerged from the German-Greek tradition meet a thinking that has emerged from the Sino-Japanese?

### The Tezuka dialogue

The dialogue's central tension is the question of whether *Iki* — the Japanese aesthetic-existential category — can be "translated" into the conceptual vocabulary of Western metaphysics. Heidegger's *Fragender* (Questioner) and the *Japaner* (Japanese) circle the question without resolving it. The *Japaner* admits that earlier attempts (in particular Kuki Shūzō's *Iki no kōzō*, on which he was an indirect commentator) had failed because they tried to express *Iki* through European aesthetics — Hegelian, Schopenhauerian, Husserlian — and thereby submitted *Iki* to a foreign metaphysics. The *Fragender* responds that this is symptomatic: every East-West exchange so far has happened on Western metaphysical ground, and the Asian side has paid the price of distortion.

> "The danger of our conversation was hidden in language itself, not in what we were discussing, nor in the way we tried to say it."

The danger is that the very *language* of the conversation — German — is metaphysically pre-loaded, so that any *Iki* expressed in it has already been re-shaped by the metaphysics of subjectivity, representation, and grounding that Heidegger has spent his career criticizing. The dialogue ends without a resolution and that ending *is* its argument: real cross-cultural philosophy cannot proceed by translation; it requires both sides to listen patiently for what their respective languages have *not* been able to say, and to wait for an *Ereignis* in which the same matter could be said from both sides.

### The *Geviert* and Asian thinking

The *Geviert* — the four-gathering of *Erde*, *Himmel*, *die Sterblichen*, *die Göttlichen* (earth, sky, mortals, divinities) — is Heidegger's late image of dwelling. In "Das Ding" and "Bauen Wohnen Denken" (both in *Vorträge und Aufsätze* 1954), he describes the way a thing — a jug, a bridge, a house — *versammelt* (gathers) the four. The peasant jug pours wine that comes from earth (the grape, the soil), under sky (the rain, the sun), for mortals (whose festival the wine joins), in honor of the divinities (whose presence is felt at the festival's gathering). The thing (*das Ding*) is not an object for representation; it is the *Versammlung* of the four. Heidegger explicitly contrasts *Ding* (thing, in its old German sense of an *assembly*, a *gathering-place*) with *Gegenstand* (object, *standing-against* a representing subject). The smoothed English "thing" loses this; we will keep both senses live.

The *Geviert* has been read by some commentators (Reinhard May, *Heideggers verborgene Quellen*, 1989) as drawing — possibly silently, possibly via Schopenhauer-Buber-Buddhist influences — on East Asian conceptions of correlative cosmology. The user's view: the structural homology is real and probably independent of any direct influence. The *Geviert* is closer to the Vedic *catuṣṭaya* — the four-fold structures of Vedic ritual cosmology, the four directions, the four āśramas, the four Vedas — than to anything in earlier Western metaphysics. The Tantric mandala is structurally even closer: a center (the practitioner, the *bindu*), surrounded by directional powers (earth, sky, ancestors, gods), gathered in the *yantra* as the *Geviert* is gathered in the thing.

### The *Gelassenheit* dialogue

Heidegger's 1959 *Gelassenheit* contains, in addition to the address proper, the dialogue "*Zur Erörterung der Gelassenheit. Aus einem Feldweggespräch über das Denken*" — a three-way conversation between a Teacher, a Scholar, and a Scientist on a country path. Released through the country-path (the *Feldweg*) form, the conversation moves toward *Gelassenheit* — releasement — as the proper comportment of thinking that is no longer willful (not *willing*) but is also no longer passive (not *not-willing*). It is the *third* of these — neither willing nor not-willing — that *Gelassenheit* names.

> "Releasement lies, if this word is permissible here, outside the distinction between activity and passivity."

The phrase that follows in the same dialogue — that *Gelassenheit* is *die Wachheit für die Gegnet*, "wakefulness for the open-region" — is the key. The *Gegnet* is Heidegger's revival of an old form of *Gegend* (region, area), used to name the open expanse in which things can be released to their own being. The Anderson/Freund translation renders it "that-which-regions"; the German verb-form *gegnen* (to region, to come-to-meet) is built into the noun, so the *Gegnet* is the *regioning* of the open, not a region in the sense of a bounded territory. *Gelassenheit* is the wakeful-attentive disposition that lets the *Gegnet* be the *Gegnet* and lets things in it be themselves.

The user's reading. *Gelassenheit*-as-*neither-willing-nor-not-willing* is structurally exact for *niṣkāma karma* — desireless action — as the user reads it. The *Bhagavad Gītā*'s *yoga-sthaḥ kuru karmāṇi* (II.48) — "established in yoga, perform actions" — is *neither* a willful seizure of action *nor* a renunciative withdrawal from action; it is the third comportment in which action proceeds *through* the actor without ego-arrogation. Heidegger's *Gelassenheit* is one of the strongest Western articulations of this third comportment.

But — and this is the user's principal critical move on Heidegger's late thought — Heidegger's *Gelassenheit* remains *insufficiently generative*. The *Gegnet* is the *open*, and *Gelassenheit* lets the open be open; what *Gelassenheit* does not do is *generate*. It does not produce; it does not project (in the sense of Aurobindonian *projecting* poise of Supermind); it does not act in the sense of *līlā*, divine play. The user's quarrel is precise: Heidegger's *Gelassenheit* is the *clearing's* disposition, but the clearing is only one moment of the *involution-evolution-transformation* arc Aurobindo names. *Sein-lassen* without subsequent *līlā* is half a phenomenology of action; *Gelassenheit* is the receptive moment, but the active, evolutionary, generative moment that completes it is what Aurobindo calls *the supramental's projecting*.

### Heidegger and Aurobindo / Bhartṛhari

It must be said clearly: Heidegger never read Aurobindo, never read Bhartṛhari, never read the Veda in any serious sense. His engagement with Indian philosophy was thin; what he knew of Buddhism came through D.T. Suzuki and the *Kyoto School*; what he knew of Daoism came through Richard Wilhelm's translations and his unfinished collaboration with the Chinese scholar Paul Hsiao on a translation of the *Daodejing* in 1946. There is no evidence that he read the *Vākyapadīya* or the *Life Divine*.

But the *structural* homologies are real:

- "Die Sprache ist das Haus des Seins" ↔ Bhartṛhari's *śabda-tattva* as the substance of cognition.
- *Lichtung* as the open-clearing in which beings show ↔ Aurobindo's *Lord of the Worlds* as the witness-clearing in which the cosmic process unfolds.
- *Gelassenheit* as neither-willing-nor-not-willing ↔ *niṣkāma karma* as neither willful-action nor renunciative-non-action.
- *Geviert* as the four-fold gathering of dwelling ↔ Vedic and Tantric four-fold cosmologies.
- *Ereignis* as the self-instituting middle in which Being and Dasein come into their own ↔ Aurobindo's *Real-Idea*, the supramental's poise in which the One projects multiplicity without ceasing to be One.
- *Onto-theo-logy* as the metaphysical confusion of Being and highest-being ↔ the user's diagnosis of ontologized grammar from Hegel to postmodernism.

These homologies are independent. Heidegger arrived at his vocabulary by working back through Greek, Latin, German philosophical history; Bhartṛhari, the Vedic seers, Aurobindo arrived at theirs through the Sanskrit tradition. That two such different routes converge on structurally similar formulations is *evidence* — evidence that the matter being thought is *real*, not the artifact of one tradition's grammar.

The user's stance toward this convergence: it is a confirmation of T11 (ontology of phenomenology) and T4 (all darśanas ultimately true). The convergence does *not*, however, mean that Heidegger and Aurobindo are saying the *same* thing. They are saying *structurally homologous* things from *different registers*. Heidegger speaks from the register of philosophical-poetic thinking after the closure of Western metaphysics; Aurobindo speaks from the register of *yogic-experiential* knowledge of the supramental level itself. Heidegger has read the structure off the philosophical history; Aurobindo has *seen* the structure in *darśana* (literally: seeing) and *anubhava* (direct experience).

## Part VII — Reading notes: where Heidegger confirms, where he diverges

This final part records, in working form, the user's accounts and accounts-against of Heidegger. The intended readership is the user — and any future seeker who will use this reader as a working notebook for the active philosophical task.

### Where Heidegger confirms the user's thesis

**1. The ontological difference as a permanent prophylactic against ontologized grammar.** The thesis that *Being is not a being* — and the corresponding refusal to allow Being to be reduced either to a property of beings or to a highest being — is structurally identical with the user's refusal to allow the divine to be ontologized into a metaphysical *causa sui*. T7 (divine play through willful self-ignorance), T9 (divine purpose: unity-in-apparent-multiplicity), T11 (ontology of phenomenology) all rely on a divine that is *not* a thing among things and *not* the supreme thing. Heidegger's *ontologische Differenz* is a philosophical scaffolding that lets this stand without surrendering to either materialist reduction or onto-theological capture.

**2. The diagnostic of metaphysics as onto-theo-logy.** The diagnosis in *Identität und Differenz* — that the entire history of Western metaphysics has thought beings as a totality grounded in a highest being, and that this *causa sui* is a god one can neither pray to nor dance before — gives the user a non-Indian philosophical defense of the right to a divine that is not the philosophical highest being. Krishna, Kālī, the Mother, the Iṣṭa-deva are not the *causa sui*. The user's bhakti from fullness (T10) presupposes a divine that is encountered, not deduced; called upon, not concluded to. Heidegger's diagnosis exempts this divine from the death-of-god critique.

**3. *Lichtung* as the user's "aperspectival condition."** The clearing is the open in which beings show themselves *as* the beings they are. It is not light, not consciousness, not subject. It is the *open* itself, prior to any seer or seen. The user's commitment to the diaphanous through-all of registers (Gebser's aperspectival, Aurobindo's supramental's *comprehending* poise) is structurally close to Heidegger's *Lichtung*. Heidegger gives a Western philosophical name for what the user has otherwise to import from Gebser and Aurobindo.

**4. The dismantling of the Cartesian subject-object split.** *Sein und Zeit* §§13–24's analysis of *In-der-Welt-sein* and the ready-to-hand dissolves a great deal of correlationist closure before correlationism gets named. Meillassoux's diagnosis is that post-Kantian thought cannot think the *ancestral* (the in-itself prior to all thinking); Heidegger, by refusing the worldless subject as a starting point, refuses one of the two pillars on which correlationism rests. Indian realism (Nyāya, Mīmāṃsā, Madhva, Bhāskara) never fell into the correlationist trap; Heidegger is one of the few Western thinkers who at least began to climb back out.

**5. *Selbigkeit* without *Einheit* — sameness that is not unity.** Identity as belonging-together rather than collapse-into-one is exactly the structural shape of T1 (darśana register thesis) and T4 (all darśanas ultimately true). The registers belong together — they speak of the same Real — without collapsing into a single grammar. Heidegger's *Selbigkeit*-not-*Einheit* is one of the cleanest Western statements of this structure.

**6. *Gelassenheit* as neither willing nor not-willing.** This is Heidegger's strongest statement of the third comportment of action — neither willful seizure nor passive withdrawal — that the *Bhagavad Gītā*'s *karma-yoga* and Aurobindo's triple transformation also articulate. The user's *niṣkāma karma* and Heidegger's *Gelassenheit* are structurally close; reading them together strengthens both.

**7. Truth as *alētheia*, including untruth.** Truth-as-unconcealment (rather than truth-as-correspondence) is structurally close to *darśana*-as-seeing and to the Indian *pramāṇa*-traditions' richer epistemic vocabulary. The thesis that every disclosure is co-original with concealment supports the user's standpoint discipline (methodological principle 3) and the *register*-thesis (T1).

### Where Heidegger diverges from — and is broken by — the user's thesis

**1. Epistemic-conditioning closure.** This is the principal break. Heidegger's destinal narrative — that Being has been sent to *us* (the German-Greek "we") in *our* metaphysical history, that *we* await the *Ereignis*, that the *other beginning* will dawn for *us* — is, on the user's reading, a closure. It limits the question of Being to the historical situation of post-Greek European thought and treats the *forgetting of Being* as if it were the universal human condition. Aurobindo's open evolutionary ontology rejects this. The Vedic seers were *not* in *Seinsvergessenheit*; the Upaniṣadic *ṛṣis* were not waiting for an *Ereignis* that had not yet come; Bhartṛhari was not in the metaphysical forgetting Heidegger diagnoses. The user's polemic, hard-stated: Heidegger's "history of Being" is the *European subject's* history, projected onto the human condition, and is itself a final form of correlationist closure that pretends to overcome correlationism.

**2. No positive divine.** Heidegger's *letzte Gott* is *purely formal*: the wholly Other against the metaphysical god, refused all substantive content. The user's traditions — Vaiṣṇava, Śākta, Śaiva, Aurobindonian — fill in substantive content (Krishna, Kālī, the Mother, the *Iṣṭa-deva*) and defend the right to do so against the post-Nietzschean European reflex that any substantive divine is by definition a metaphysical *causa sui*. Heidegger's formal placeholder is preferable to the metaphysical highest-being, but the user goes further: the divine is *not only* the formal placeholder; it is *also* the lived, encountered, named, danced-before, prayed-to divine that Heidegger says philosophy can no longer touch. The user holds that philosophy *can* touch the divine, but only when philosophy is not metaphysics — only when the philosophical comportment is itself a comportment of *bhakti* (devotion) in the right disposition (*pūrṇatva-icchā*).

**3. Ungenerative passivity.** Heidegger's *Sein-lassen*, *Gelassenheit*, *Andenken*, *Warten* — letting-be, releasement, commemorative thinking, waiting — are receptive comportments. They are appropriate as *one moment* of philosophical-spiritual life: the moment of opening-the-clearing. But they are insufficient on their own. The user's *līlā* — divine play — is *generative*: the cosmos is not only *opened* (Lichtung) but also *projected* (Aurobindo's projecting Supermind); the actor is not only *attentive* (Gelassenheit) but also *engaged* (niṣkāma karma); the divine is not only *Other* (letzte Gott) but also *intimate* (the Iṣṭa-deva). Heidegger's quietism — and it is a quietism, however philosophically defended — is, on the user's reading, half a phenomenology.

**4. The German-Greek axis as the only "first beginning."** The user's commitment to the trans-cultural reality of philosophical insight — that the Veda, the Upaniṣads, the Mahābhārata, Madhva, Caitanya, Aurobindo, but also the Daoist classics, the Buddhist literatures, the Sufi corpus — *all* speak truly, each from its own register — is structurally inconsistent with Heidegger's German-Greek monopoly on the destinal sending of Being. The user respects Heidegger's hesitation about cross-cultural translation (the Tezuka dialogue's danger-of-language) but rejects the implicit conclusion that *only* the German-Greek can think the question of Being. This is a closure that must be broken on independent grounds.

**5. The *Schwarze Hefte* and the political-ethical question.** It must be noted, without resort to apologetics, that the same philosophical machinery that produced Heidegger's most powerful insights also failed him politically. The 27 May 1933 Rectorial Address — *Die Selbstbehauptung der deutschen Universität* (GA 16, pp. 107–117) — opens with the Greek *Wesen der Wissenschaft* and within minutes binds science and university to the *Volk* and to the *geistige Welt eines Volkes*; the rhetoric of *Wesen*, *Wissen*, *Selbstbehauptung*, and the threefold service to *Arbeit / Wehr / Wissen* (labor, defense, knowledge) is *Sein und Zeit*'s vocabulary of authenticity and resoluteness conscripted for the Führer state. The address is short — read it once in German (eight pages) and notice how the existential-ontological language of 1927 is now drawing the Volk into the place that Dasein had occupied in 1927. The antisemitic passages in the *Schwarze Hefte* (especially GA 95–96, the notebooks of 1938–41) are continuous with the same destinal-historical narrative, in which the "*Weltjudentum*" is pictured as a calculative-rootless force opposed to the rootedness of German Dasein. These passages are not extrinsic to his philosophy; they emerge from a place where the destinal-historical narrative could become a vehicle for ethno-nationalist self-mythology. The user's commitment to *matsya-nyāya* (prevention of predation) as the political telos of the state, and the Vivekanandan / Aurobindonian commitment to a different human being, runs against any philosophical narrative in which the destiny of Being is the destiny of *one* people. This is not a reason to discard Heidegger; it is a reason to read him with the same standpoint discipline he himself recommends — knowing which of his speeches were *bound* speech, which *seeker*, which *polemical*, which *political*, which *thinking*. Not all are interchangeable, and not all are equally trustworthy. The *Rektoratsrede* is *bound* and *political* speech, organized for a regime; the post-1945 *Gelassenheit* is *seeker* speech of a chastened thinker; the *Beiträge* and *Identität und Differenz* are *thinking* speech of the highest order. The user's reading reads each register as the register it is, and refuses both the apologetic (which dismisses the *Schwarze Hefte* as private and irrelevant) and the inverse-totalitarian (which uses the *Schwarze Hefte* to discredit the rest). The *Beiträge*'s diagnosis of *Gestell* survives even when the *Rektoratsrede*'s rhetoric is repudiated; the *Identität und Differenz* analysis of *causa sui* stands even when the *Schwarze Hefte*'s antisemitism is named for what it is.

### Reading sequence (recommended)

A reader new to Heidegger, with the user's thesis as orienting frame, should proceed as follows.

**First**, read *Sein und Zeit* Introduction §§1–8 carefully — the formal structure of the question, the ontological priority of Dasein, the methodological declaration that philosophy *is* phenomenological ontology. Set aside Division 1's analyses of equipment and *das Man* on a first reading; skim them, return later. Then go directly to Division 2, especially §§45–53 on being-toward-death and §§54–60 on conscience and resoluteness. The reader's first encounter with Heidegger should be with *Dasein* and *Sein-zum-Tode*, not with the workshop.

**Second**, read the *Brief über den Humanismus* in full. It is Heidegger at his clearest on the question of Being post-*Kehre*, the relation of language to Being, and the rejection of subjectivity-philosophy. It is the bridge between the early and late Heidegger.

**Third**, read *Identität und Differenz* — both lectures, very short. The *Onto-Theo-Logik* diagnosis and the *causa sui* passage are unmissable.

**Fourth**, read the *Holzwege* and *Wegmarken* essays selectively: "Vom Wesen der Wahrheit," "Was ist Metaphysik?" with the 1949 introduction, "Nietzsches Wort 'Gott ist tot,'" "Der Spruch des Anaximander."

**Fifth**, read *Gelassenheit* — both the address and the country-path dialogue. This is the doorway to the user's reading of the third comportment.

**Sixth**, only after the above, attempt the *Beiträge zur Philosophie*. The book is forbidding even for Germanists; for the user's purposes, the *Vorblick* (preview) and the sections on *Ereignis* and *der letzte Gott* are the centers of gravity.

**Seventh** — and this is non-Heideggerian advice — read Aurobindo's *Life Divine* I.16 (the triple status of Supermind), *The Synthesis of Yoga* on the integral comportment of action, and Bhartṛhari's [*Vākyapadīya* I](cite://bhartrhari/vakyapadiya/I) (with the commentary in mind), in order to see what the substantive completion of Heidegger's formal openings would look like in another register. Then return to *Sein und Zeit* §§39–66 and the *Brief* with that completion in view, and notice what Heidegger leaves open and what closes prematurely.

### Closing remark

Heidegger is, for the user, a Western interlocutor of the first rank — neither to be venerated nor to be discarded, but read with patience, in his own German where possible, with the constant standpoint-disciplinary question: from which register is this said, and to whom does it speak? The greatest gift of Heidegger to the user is the philosophical permission to refuse onto-theology while insisting on the question of Being; the limit of Heidegger, for the user, is the closure of that question into the German-Greek destinal narrative, the formal-empty *letzte Gott*, the receptive-only *Gelassenheit*, and the absence of substantive evolutionary disclosure. Aurobindo is the user's principal completion of what Heidegger leaves rightly open; Bhartṛhari, Madhva, Caitanya, Abhinavagupta are the auxiliary completions. The user's thesis takes from Heidegger what is given, declines what is closed prematurely, and reads beyond him into the substantive disclosure that the user's traditions — and the user's own seeking — point toward.

The *Holzwege* — the forest paths that fade into clearings — is the right image. A reader walks them not to arrive somewhere already named on a map, but to come to a clearing — a *Lichtung* — where the trees have given way and the open shows. What the open *contains* is not Heidegger's question; the user's question is exactly what the open contains, and the user's traditions are the answers Heidegger refused to attempt.
