# Engagement: Vallabha’s *Aṇu-Bhāṣya* on the *Brahma-Sūtras*

## 1. What the text is

Vallabha’s *Aṇu-Bhāṣya* is a compact commentary on the *Brahma-Sūtras*, traditionally placed in the late fifteenth to early sixteenth century and belonging to the Śuddhādvaita / Puṣṭimārga world. In the 1921 Mumbai print used here, the volume has several layers: front matter and indices; a running `pratisūtraṃ śrīmad-aṇubhāṣya-tātparyam` synopsis (roughly lines 108-3999 of the pure-Sanskrit file); the full *Aṇu-Bhāṣya* itself (roughly lines 4004-13566); and appended kārikās / lists thereafter. The commentary follows the four-adhyāya, four-pāda architecture of the *Brahma-Sūtras*; the printed edition also announces a later ṭīkā by Śrīdharaśarman, title read from OCR as *Vāṭabodhinī* [OCR?].

Cross-engagement: at the substrate structure axis the two texts choose differently. Akalanka ([primary text](cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/1.34-35)) commits to `many-coordinate-reals`; this section commits to `one-self-standing`.

## 2. Sampling and scope statement

Engaged at structural depth: the full *Aṇu-Bhāṣya* section of the pure-Sanskrit file, from the first-pāda colophon at lines 4003-4004 through the fourth-adhyāya discussion of liberated embodiment and enjoyment (to roughly line 13566). Engaged at close-reading depth: selected passages from 1.2, 2.1, 3.2, 3.3, 4.2, and 4.4, with full analytic treatment given to `lokavat tu līlākaivalyam` (2.1.33). Used for orientation but not translated continuously: the earlier `tātparya` synopsis (lines 108-3999). Not engaged in substance: the appended kārikās and indices after line 13566.

Cross-engagement: Abhinavagupta works the manifestation status axis in the metaphysical register too, but the value is `expressive-manifestation`, not `real-transformation` ([primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16)).

## 3. Structural walk-through

- **Adhyāya 1: samanvaya** (full bhāṣya begins at lines 4003-4004; the synopsis opens at lines 111-262). The opening pāda argues for Brahman as both intelligent and material cause, handles the ineffability objection, and insists that `ānandamaya` is Brahman rather than jīva or prakṛti; the second pāda turns ambiguous upāsanā-texts back into brahma-vākyas rather than jīva-upāsanā (lines 4018-4075). The third pāda shifts to Brahman as support / container (`āyatana`) and keeps doubtful terms from being reassigned to Sāṅkhya categories (lines 4670-4721); the fourth continues that anti-misreading work and ends with `etena sarve vyākhyātā vyākhyātāḥ` (lines 6027-6033). Polemically, this adhyāya is less about external schools than about preventing Vedāntic language from collapsing into non-Brahman referents.

Cross-engagement: Adi Sankara lands on `non-otherness` for the identity relation axis ([primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1)); this section lands on `self-expression-or-appearance`.

- **Adhyāya 2: avirodha** (lines 6040-7374). The first pāda rejects the Sāṅkhya appeal to Kapila-smṛti, refuses the claim that Brahman cannot be world-cause, defends creation without ulterior lack, and explains inequality through karma rather than divine cruelty; `lokavat tu līlākaivalyam` sits here (lines 6271-6288). The second pāda broadens the attack to atomists, Buddhists, and other external constructions; Vallabha even remarks that some forms of `māyāvāda` survive only in confused minds after these refutations (lines 6355-6359). The third and fourth pādas then repair internal doctrinal tensions about creation-texts, ākāśa, prāṇa, the senses, and name-and-form. This adhyāya is the text’s main anti-Sāṅkhya / anti-Buddhist / anti-atomist engine.

- **Adhyāya 3: sādhana** (lines 7376-11515). The third adhyāya asks what sort of embodied or post-embodied subject is actually competent for Brahman-attainment. Its first pāda treats rebirth-processes, subtle accompaniment, and the production of a fit body for further experience or realization (lines 7392-7450). The second pāda is philosophically denser: dream is called `māyāmātram eva svapnam` (lines 7893-7900), the obscuration of the jīva’s divine properties is explained through Bhagavat’s will and the jīva’s orientation to enjoyment (lines 7914-7928), and the contradictory predicates of Brahman are sorted without surrendering Brahman’s fullness (lines 8028-8045). The third pāda shifts toward bhakti and upāsanā architecture: it treats multiplicity of practices, `adhikārika` beings (lines 9594-9618), and the decisive distinction between `akṣara`-knowledge and the higher orientation to `puruṣottama` (lines 9620-9675). The fourth pāda is the most programmatic: it debates whether karma is subordinate to knowledge or vice versa, rejects a merely ritual construal of Brahman-knowledge, and repeatedly elevates bhakti and Bhagavat-relation above bare `akṣara`-jñāna (lines 10473-10618, 11240-11511). This is where the text’s constructive thesis becomes unmistakably sectarian rather than merely anti-rival.

- **Adhyāya 4: phala** (lines 11519-13566). The first pāda discusses repetition of practice, the persistence or destruction of karma, and distinct arrangements for `maryādā` and `puṣṭi` cases (lines 11563-11981). The second treats the fate of the subtle body and senses; Vallabha resists simple annihilation and argues instead for transformation into an alaukika condition suitable to divine enjoyment (lines 11991-12135). The third discusses post-mortem routes, especially `arcirādi`, while distinguishing knowledge-route and bhakti-route attainments (lines 12441-12944). The fourth culminates in a debate over body, no body, or both in liberation; Vallabha’s discussion makes clear that his final horizon is participation in `puruṣottama`-līlā rather than impersonal extinction.

## 4. Load-bearing passages — engaged at depth

### Passage 1

Cross-engagement: Akshapada Gautama's reading at [primary text](cite://aksapada/nyaya-sutra/1.1.1) fills the epistemic authority axis with `plural-pramana-realism`. The present section fills it with `scripture-dominant`.

**Locus:** 1.2.1, `sarvatra prasiddhopadeśāt` (pure-Sanskrit file, lines 4066-4075).

Cross-engagement: Haridas Chaudhuri works the motive force axis in the soteriological register too, but the value is `bliss-or-delight`, not `devotion` ([primary text](cite://chaudhuri/integral-yoga/1)).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `atha khalv ityādi brahmavākyam eva. kutaḥ? sarvatra prasiddhopadeśāt ... upadeśa eva yukto mananarūpo na tu kācit siddhasya jīvasyopāsanā.`

**Close English:** “The passage beginning ‘atha khalu’ is itself a sentence about Brahman. Why? Because there is instruction there concerning what is everywhere well known. What is fitting there is precisely an instruction, of the form of reflection, not some upāsanā of an already established individual self.”

**Engagement:** This is a good example of Vallabha’s first-adhyāya method. He refuses to let a Chāndogya passage such as `sarvaṃ khalvidaṃ brahma` become merely a subordinate meditative prescription built on a deified jīva. The key move is grammatical and hermeneutic at once: `upadeśa` is taken not as an injunction to fabricate a meditation-object, but as a declarative or reflective disclosure of Brahman.

If the doubtful passages are downgraded to jīva-upāsanā, the first adhyāya loses its claim to `samanvaya`. Vallabha instead uses them to expand Brahman-reference.

### Passage 2

**Locus:** 2.1.33, `lokavat tu līlākaivalyam` (pure-Sanskrit file, lines 6271-6279).

Cross-engagement: Anandabodha ([primary text](cite://sarvajnatman/samksepa-sariraka/1.319-352)) and this section take opposite turns on the practice-path axis — `knowledge-discipline` against `devotion-and-grace`.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `lokavat tu līlākaivalyam. loke līlāyāṃ hi na kiṃcit prayojanam asti. līlāyā eva prayojanatvāt.`

**Close English:** “But, as in the world, it is sheer play. For in play in the world there is no ulterior purpose at all; play itself is what has the status of purpose.”

**Engagement:** This is one of the most useful loci in the text. Vallabha is answering the old objection that if Brahman creates, Brahman must lack something and therefore act for a purpose. He refuses the premise. Creation need not be need-driven production; it can be `līlā`, and `līlā` is not a disguised teleology but the suspension of that demand.

The second sentence is decisive. Vallabha does not say that play has some hidden extrinsic end. He says `līlāyā eva prayojanatvāt`: play itself is the `prayojana`. That makes divine manifestation intelligible without reducing it either to moral pedagogy or to a fall from fullness. It is a compact but important world-affirming theistic answer to a problem that later anti-Māyāvāda realists keep worrying.

**Pāṇinian-rich treatment:** `lokavat` = `loke vat`, “as in the world,” an indeclinable comparative formation. `tu` marks the siddhānta against the preceding pūrvapakṣa. `līlākaivalyam` admits Vallabha’s own gloss `līlā eva kaivalyam iti vā`: either “mere play” or “play alone.” The principal sentence of the commentary is `loke līlāyāṃ hi na kiṃcit prayojanam asti.` Here `asti` is the principal verb; `prayojanam` is its nominative predicate; `kiṃcit` qualifies `prayojanam`; `loke` and `līlāyām` are locatives, the second being the stricter adhikaraṇa-locative, “in the case of play.” The next sentence, `līlāyā eva prayojanatvāt`, uses pañcamī in a hetu-sense: “because play itself has the status of purpose.” `prayojanatva` is an abstract `-tva` formation from `prayojana`; Vallabha’s point is not merely causal but categorial. He is redefining what counts as explanation in divine action.

### Passage 3

**Locus:** 3.2.3, dream-discussion (pure-Sanskrit file, lines 7893-7900).

Cross-engagement: same soteriological register, different commitments at the soteric end axis. Alfred North Whitehead: `not-soteriological`, [primary text](cite://whitehead/process-and-reality/1.2). Here: `loving-participation`.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `śākhāniyamābhāvāc ca māyāmātram eva svapna iti na tatkṛtaguṇadoṣasambandhaḥ.`

**Close English:** “And because there is no rule of scriptural determination here, dream is merely māyā; therefore there is no connection with merits and faults produced by it.”

**Engagement:** The important point is not simply that dream is false. Vallabha’s `māyāmātram` is functioning in a narrower, pragmatic way: dream-acts do not generate binding moral consequences because the dream-scene is only a shadow-appearance, not the full register of karmically effective action. He immediately speaks of it as `praticchāyārūpa` elsewhere in the same stretch.

This shows a realism that still uses `māyā` without reducing the world to `asat`. The dream-case is non-binding because it is a special case; the waking world remains Bhagavat’s produced order.

### Passage 4

**Locus:** 3.2.5, `parābhidhyānāt tu tirohitaṃ` (pure-Sanskrit file, lines 7914-7928).

Cross-engagement: same metaphysical register, different commitments at the causation model axis. Baladeva Vidyabhushana: `unchanged-ground-with-changing-power`, [primary text](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1). Here: `real-transformation`.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `asya jīvasyaiśvaryādi tirohitam. tatra hetoḥ parābhidhyānāt [OCR?]; parasya bhagavato 'bhito dhyānaṃ svasyet asya ca sarvato bhogecchā. tasmād īśvarecchayā jīvasya bhagavaddharmatirobhāvaḥ.`

**Close English:** “The lordship and the rest belonging to this jīva are concealed. The cause of that is ‘parābhidhyāna’ [OCR?]: on the one side, orientation toward the supreme Bhagavat, and on this being’s own side, desire for enjoyment in every direction. Therefore, by the Lord’s will, there is a concealment of the jīva’s divine property.”

**Engagement:** This is one of the most philosophically important passages in the whole text. Vallabha explains bondage not as the arising of an independent non-being, but as a divinely governed concealment (`tirobhāva`) of powers that in some sense belong to the jīva as Bhagavat’s part. The line between metaphysics and soteriology is thin here: the jīva is bound because divine qualities are hidden, and they are hidden within a structure ordered by Bhagavat’s will.

This is not yet the Vivaraṇa thesis that Brahman is both `āśraya` and `viṣaya` of `avidyā`; Vallabha is not doing that technical theory. But it is a strong theistic self-obscuration model. If one wants a primary-source precedent for positive concealment rather than mere privation, this passage is considerably more useful than the reputation-summary “Vallabha is just a realist.”

### Passage 5

**Locus:** 3.3.33, `akṣaradhiyāṃ tv avarodhaḥ...` (pure-Sanskrit file, lines 9633-9653).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `tena jñānamārge 'kṣaraviṣayakāṇy eva jñānāni nirūpyante, puruṣottamaviṣayakāṇi neti niścīyate ... akṣarād api cottamaḥ ... bhaktyā mām abhijānāti.`

**Close English:** “Accordingly, in the path of knowledge, only cognitions whose object is the imperishable are being taught, not cognitions whose object is Puruṣottama: this is what is determined ... ‘higher even than the imperishable’ ... ‘by bhakti he comes to know me.’”

**Engagement:** Vallabha here states explicitly what elsewhere is only implied. `Akṣara`-knowledge is real, scripturally grounded, and salvifically relevant; but it is not yet the same as the consummation centered on `puruṣottama`. Bhakti is not one optional ornament on top of an already complete impersonal realization; it is the route to a distinct and higher object.

This is exactly where Vallabha resists easy synthesis. He is not simply a general anti-Māyāvādin realist. He is reorganizing the hierarchy of ends inside Vedānta itself.

### Passage 6

**Locus:** 4.4.1, liberation and enjoyment (pure-Sanskrit file, lines 12966-12969).

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `so 'śnute sarvān kāmān saha brahmaṇā vipaścitā ... sa ca na vigrahaṃ vinā saṃbhavatīti śrutibalād eva tathā mantavyam.`

**Close English:** “He enjoys all desires together with Brahman the all-knowing ... and that cannot occur without form; therefore, precisely on the strength of scripture, it must be understood that way.”

**Engagement:** Vallabha’s liberation doctrine is not embarrassed about enjoyment-language. He takes `so 'śnute sarvān kāmān` seriously and presses it against bodyless-release models. If enjoyment with Brahman is scripturally asserted, then some form adequate to that enjoyment must be admitted.

The larger consequence is clear in the whole fourth adhyāya: mokṣa is not mere cessation. It is a transformed and divinely conferred participation, sometimes argued as alaukika embodiment, in `puruṣottama`’s own sphere and play.

## 5. The text’s relation to the position

Vallabha belongs on the project’s map as a parallel realism, closer in strategic effect to Nimbārka than to the Vivaraṇa defense of Advaita. He does not continue the Bhartṛprapañca → Bhāskara → Madhva line directly; nor does he sit comfortably in the Padmapāda / Prakāśātman / Vidyāraṇya / Madhusūdana lineage. He is one of the strong anti-Māyāvāda pressures internal to Vedānta that insist that the world, divine form, and liberated enjoyment cannot be treated as merely provisional appearances. Relative to Yāmuna–Rāmānuja–Vedānta Deśika, Vallabha shares the anti-impersonal thrust but departs sharply in his `akṣara` / `puruṣottama` hierarchy and his Puṣṭimārga-specific soteriology.

By name, he refutes Kapila / Sāṅkhya, Yoga, atomists, Buddhists, and other rival constructors of cosmology or liberation. By anonymous `pūrvapakṣa`, he targets a broad range of claims: that doubtful Vedāntic passages concern jīva-upāsanā rather than Brahman; that Brahman cannot create without need; that mokṣa must mean sheer disappearance of body, action, and enjoyment; and that `akṣara`-knowledge is already final. His constructive thesis is that Brahman is the real cause, the world is a real divine manifestation, bondage is concealment rather than sheer nullity, and bhakti to `puruṣottama` outruns bare `akṣara`-jñāna.

For **T2**, the text is very useful. Vallabha’s use of `māyā` in the dream-discussion does not collapse `mithyā` into `asat`; it marks a special, non-binding appearance within a larger real order. More importantly, the world is argued throughout as Brahman’s produced reality, and 2.1.33 gives a compact primary-source formula for manifestation as `līlā` rather than defect. For **T9**, the text is stronger still: it is a direct Vaiṣṇava pressure against any reading of non-duality that erases divine form, relation, or manifest enjoyment.

For **T7**, the fit is partial and important but not exact. Vallabha’s `tirobhāva` passage gives a robust account of positive concealment under divine will; that supports a realist account of obscuration better than a merely privative one. But he is not offering the Vivaraṇa theory of `avidyā` as `bhāvarūpa`, nor the thesis that Brahman is both `āśraya` and `viṣaya` of `avidyā`. So the text helps the position at the level of ontological self-veiling, but not as direct textual support for the full Vivaraṇa-Madhusūdana line.

For **T11**, the text is relevant through its treatment of waking, dream, sleep, obscuration, and liberated experience. It offers an ontology of states and manifestation, but not an evolutionary ontology in the Aurobindonian sense.

The main resistance is straightforward. Vallabha does not preserve all non-dual moves the position may wish to retain. He sharply differentiates `akṣara` from `puruṣottama`, `maryādā` from `puṣṭi`, and knowledge-route from bhakti-consummation. That makes him excellent for anti-Mayavada pressure, but not available for a flattening synthesis in which all final ends become simply interchangeable.

## 6. What the user can productively take

First, this text gives the position a strong primary-source basis for saying that realist Vedānta is not merely a late reaction against Śaṅkara but a fully argued internal option. The two best citations for that are the `sarvatra prasiddhopadeśāt` passage for hermeneutics and `lokavat tu līlākaivalyam` for world-manifestation without lack.

Second, the text sharpens the project’s anti-Mayavada archive by giving a theistic account of obscuration. The `tirobhāva` passage is not the Vivaraṇa doctrine, but it is a serious primary text for the claim that concealment is positively structured and divinely governed rather than reducible to blank absence.

Third, Vallabha forces a distinction the position must keep clear: anti-illusionism is not enough. His constructive thesis is `puruṣottama`-centered and culminates in bhakti and divine enjoyment. If the position cites him for realism, it should also register that he narrows the final end more sharply than a broad many-registers synthesis does.

Fourth, the fourth adhyāya materially changes the corpus. It blocks any easy description of liberation in Vedānta as sheer de-individuation. Vallabha insists that scriptural enjoyment-language must be taken seriously, and that some transformed form of participation remains. That is a useful pressure point wherever the project wants to argue that non-duality need not mean world-denial or the extinction of relation.
