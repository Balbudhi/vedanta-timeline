# Mīmāṃsā as a Coherent Practice-Philosophy: A Reader's Encounter

## Status of this document

This article supersedes v1 (`MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS.md`) and v2 (`MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS_v2.md`). Both earlier passes framed the question as a diagnosis: what went wrong with classical Mīmāṃsā such that *karma-kāṇḍa* came to be perceived as ritualism without depth, and how does Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* recover what was lost. The framing was wrong. It imposed on the school a story of decline the texts do not require. Mīmāṃsā is a coherent practice-philosophy that worked for the practitioners it worked for, in the symbol-environment they inhabited; it does not need fixing. The interesting question lies elsewhere — at the encounter between this corpus and the *contemporary reader*.

Cross-engagement: at the substrate structure axis the two texts choose differently. Akalanka ([primary text](cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/1.34-35)) commits to `many-coordinate-reals`; this section commits to `one-self-standing`.

The article's substantive engagement is with the Mīmāṃsā primary texts on disk (Jaimini's *Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra*, Śabara's *Bhāṣya*, Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika* and *Tantra-Vārttika*, Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā*) and with Sri Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* and *Synthesis of Yoga*. A secondary line of reading — exemplified by Francis X. Clooney, S.J., particularly the title-thesis of *Thinking Ritually: Rediscovering the Pūrva Mīmāṃsā of Jaimini* (1990) and "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy" (2022) — argues, on the strength of those titles and their placement in the field, that classical Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā is a coherent reflective system whose practitioner is constitutively a thinking subject. Clooney's books are not in our corpus; what follows engages the title-thesis as a marker of an established academic line, while the substantive textual work is with the Sanskrit primary sources we do have.

## §1 — The thesis

First, the externalization that characterizes classical Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā is a school of practice, not a defect in the school. Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, and Prabhākara Miśra externalized the ritual operation because, in the cultural environment they wrote in, the external symbols — the *agni*, the *soma*, the *yajamāna*, the *adhvaryu*, the materially specifiable enclosure of the *yajña-bhūmi* — were uniform, intelligible, and embedded in lived practice. Externalization under those conditions is not a failure of depth; it is a coherent way of articulating a practice whose symbols already carried the depth in the lives of the practitioners.

Cross-engagement: Abhinavagupta works the manifestation status axis in the metaphysical register too, but the value is `expressive-manifestation`, not `real-transformation` ([primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16)).

Second, as the conditions changed, the symbols changed. The śrauta corpus contracts; the *gṛhya* and *smārta* corpora expand; the center of practice shifts from the public *yāga* to domestic and devotional registers; the available imagery and material apparatus alter with cultural change. This is normal cultural evolution. It is not the symptom of a metaphysical decline. The tradition continues; the externalized forms shift to track the symbol-environment of each successive generation of practitioners.

Third — and this is the substantive claim — Jaimini and the traditional Mīmāṃsakas are saying something that makes complete sense within the scope of the practice as they articulated it. Their account of *codanā* as the autonomous *pramāṇa* for *dharma* ([Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra 1.1.2](cite://jaimini/mimamsa-sutra/1.1.2)), of *bhāvanā* as the urging-to-action that the optative suffix carries (Śabara on MS 2.1.1), of *apūrva* as the persistent residue connecting the present action to its eventual fruit (MS 2.1.5), and of *adhikāra* as the agent's qualification for the rite — taken together — articulates a coherent practice-philosophy. The coherence is not contingent on a hidden integral-yogic structure underneath. The coherence is on the surface, in the school's own categories, used by the practitioners on their own terms. The line of reading associated with Clooney's titles (*Thinking Ritually*, 1990; "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature," 2022) is the academic precedent for treating the school in this register; the books themselves are not in our corpus.

Fourth, the question to ask is not "what went wrong with the school" but "what happens to the listener — the contemporary reader — who engages this corpus today." That is a different question from the historical one. The contemporary reader does not stand inside the cultural environment in which Vedic terms were lived imagery. The reader stands in a modern symbol-environment, in which the Vedic terms arrive as foreign vocabulary requiring active interpretive work. For *that* reader, an interpretive hermeneutic is needed; an interpretive hermeneutic is what Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* and *Synthesis of Yoga* together supply. The hermeneutic does not correct Mīmāṃsā. It opens a register in which a contemporary reader can encounter the corpus as transformative rather than as antiquarian.

Fifth, the Mīmāṃsā entries — Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila, Prabhākara — should be read as the canonical articulations of a coherent practice-philosophy. The Aurobindonian recovery, applied to the contemporary reader's encounter, is one available reader-stance among several. It is not the reading the Mīmāṃsakas would themselves have offered. The school as a coherent reflective system in its own period (the academic line marked by Clooney's titles, though those texts are not in our corpus) and Aurobindo's hermeneutic for the present reader name two distinct registers; both can stand together.

## §2 — Mīmāṃsā on its own terms

The standard reception of Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā in twentieth-century English-language scholarship was shaped by the older Vedāntic priority. The school was treated as the technical apparatus of *karma-kāṇḍa*, useful as a propaedeutic to the genuinely philosophical work of *Uttara*-Mīmāṃsā but not in itself a serious philosophical engagement with the world. The contrary line — that classical Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā is a coherent reflective system whose practitioner is constitutively a thinking subject — is the line marked, in the title and apparent thesis of *Thinking Ritually: Rediscovering the Pūrva Mīmāṃsā of Jaimini* (1990), by Francis X. Clooney, S.J. Clooney's book is not in our corpus, so what follows works the substantive case from the Sanskrit primary texts on disk and treats Clooney's titles as the academic precedent rather than as the source of the argument.

Cross-engagement: Arthur Schopenhauer takes the identity relation axis to a different commitment — `self-expression-or-appearance` at [primary text](cite://schopenhauer/the-world-as-will-and-representation/1.1) against `non-otherness` here.

Three substantive moves can be made here directly from the Sanskrit primary sources.

### 2.1 The *karmin* as a reflective subject

The first move is to reframe the agent of the rite. The standard reading treats the *yajamāna* and the *ṛtvij* as performers of a procedural sequence whose efficacy is independent of their cognitive states beyond the minimum required for correct performance. Against this, the Mīmāṃsā corpus articulates the *karmin* as constitutively a reflective subject. The technical apparatus the school develops — the *aṅga-aṅgi-vibhāga* (analysis of subordinate and principal ritual elements), the rules of *prakaraṇa*, *sthāna*, *krama*, and *samākhyā* ([Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra 3.1–4](cite://jaimini/mimamsa-sutra/3.1-4) with Śabara's *Bhāṣya*), the *atideśa* doctrine for extending procedures across rites (MS 7.1–4) — is not a code for mechanical performance. It is the apparatus a thinking practitioner uses to determine what the rite *is* in this case, what its parts are, how they are subordinated, what counts as correct execution under variable circumstances. The rite, in Mīmāṃsā, is a thing the practitioner *understands*, not just performs.

Cross-engagement: a substantive disagreement with Adi Sankara on the selfhood structure axis — `witness-self` ([primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1)) against the present `psychic-individual`.

This reframing has a textual center at MS 1.1.1 — *athāto dharma-jijñāsā*, "now therefore the inquiry into *dharma*." The school is, by its own opening word, *jijñāsā* — *inquiry*. The Mīmāṃsaka is not the executor of an inherited code; the Mīmāṃsaka is the inquirer into what *dharma* requires. The rest of the twelve-*adhyāya* corpus is the structured working-out of that inquiry. Mīmāṃsā is a school of *thinking* about ritual, not a school *of* ritual in some non-thinking sense. The point is in the first *sūtra*. (The reading is the academic line marked by Clooney's title-thesis in *Thinking Ritually*; the book is not in our corpus.)

### 2.2 *Codanā* and *bhāvanā*: the cognitive-volitional structure of the injunction

The second move is to take the *codanā* and *bhāvanā* doctrines as cognitively substantive rather than as machinery for getting from the verbal injunction to the bodily act. The *codanā*, glossed by Śabara as *kriyāyāḥ pravartakaṃ vacanam* — "the speech that prompts to action" (Śābara-Bhāṣya on MS 1.1.2; on-disk Sanskrit, line 8) — is not simply an instruction that lands on a passive recipient. It is a verbal operation that *prompts*, that *urges*, that re-organizes the hearer's intentional life around the prescribed action. Śabara's gloss continues: *tayā yo lakṣyate, so 'rthaḥ puruṣaṃ niḥśreyasena saṃyunaktīti pratijānīmahe* — "what is indicated by it, *that* is the *artha*; we declare that this *artha* conjoins the *puruṣa* with the highest good (*niḥśreyasa*)" (Śābara-Bhāṣya on MS 1.1.2; on-disk Sanskrit, line 9). The *artha* of *codanā* is constitutively defined by what it does *to* the agent. The reading lines up with the title-thesis of Clooney's 2022 encyclopedia entry "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy" (the entry itself is paywalled and not in our corpus); the substantive ground is the on-disk Śābara-Bhāṣya passage.

Cross-engagement: Anandabodha's reading at [primary text](cite://sarvajnatman/samksepa-sariraka/1.319-352) fills the finite cognition axis with `positive-ignorance`. The present section fills it with `adhyasa-or-superimposition`.

The *bhāvanā* doctrine, articulated by Śabara (on MS 2.1.1) and elaborated by Kumārila Bhaṭṭa in the *Tantra-Vārttika* and by Maṇḍana Miśra in the *Vidhi-Viveka*, names two operations:

- *śābdī bhāvanā* — the action of the optative suffix on the hearer's intention; the verbal force prior to action.
- *ārthī bhāvanā* — the agent's volitional energy directed toward the prescribed result.

The *bhāvanā*-pair is the school's own articulation of the cognitive-volitional core of the rite. The first half is what language *does* to the listener. The second half is what the listener-turned-agent *does* under the language's prompting. Both halves are interior. Neither is bodily-procedural in the reductive sense the older reception assumed. The Mīmāṃsakas are giving an account of how a verbal injunction, received by a competent agent, re-orders that agent's intentional life and is enacted as a determinate course of action. The school's *codanā*-and-*bhāvanā* apparatus is, read in this register, philosophy of action carried through with technical precision (the academic reading associated with Clooney's titles; the books are not in our corpus).

### 2.3 *Adhikāra*, *apūrva*, and the reflective architecture of qualification

The third move is to read *adhikāra* and *apūrva* as further evidence of the school's reflective architecture. The *adhikāra* doctrine specifies the conditions under which a given agent is qualified to perform a given rite. Śabara's analysis distinguishes external prerequisites (caste, age, ritual purity, possession of fire) from intrinsic capacity — the agent's desire (*kāma*), cultivated capability, and inner disposition. The Mīmāṃsā treatment of *adhikāra* is not a sociological classification; it is a structural doctrine about the conditions under which a verbal injunction binds a particular agent. The agent is internal to the system as a qualified subject, not external to it as a contingent performer.

Cross-engagement: Akshapada Gautama's reading at [primary text](cite://aksapada/nyaya-sutra/1.1.1) fills the epistemic authority axis with `plural-pramana-realism`. The present section fills it with `scripture-dominant`.

*Apūrva* is the most contested category in the school. Standard polemic reads *apūrva* as a metaphysical placeholder — an unseen entity invoked to bridge the gap between the present rite and the posthumous *phala*. The Mīmāṃsakas do develop *apūrva* in this metaphysical register, but in its functional role, *apūrva* is the school's name for the persistence of the rite's effect *in the agent and in the cosmos* across time. The category is not a bookkeeping fiction. It is an account of how the action's effect is real, not exhausted by its visible occurrence, and structured by the verbal injunction that prompted it. The Mīmāṃsakas, faced with the question "what makes the rite efficacious across the gap between performance and fruit," answer with a category that gives the persistence ontological weight.

### 2.4 The introspective extension

Clooney's 2022 entry's title and placement is the publicly available marker that an introspective-philosophical reading of Mīmāṃsā is academically responsible (the entry is paywalled and not in our corpus; engagement here is via the title-thesis "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy," not via the article body). The substantive textual ground is in Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika*, on disk in our corpus — the *Codanā-Sūtra* section on the autonomous validity of injunctive cognition (*svataḥ-prāmāṇya*), the *ātmavāda* on the self-luminous *ātman*, and the Bhāṭṭa analysis of how injunctive cognition operates on the agent. These passages, read directly, show that the school articulates an interior dimension within the ritual operation itself.

Cross-engagement: the disagreement with Alfred North Whitehead is at the temporal mode axis itself: `both-orthogonal` here, `process-primary` in [primary text](cite://whitehead/process-and-reality/1.2).

The introspective dimension is *internal to the school's self-understanding*, not imposed from outside. The Mīmāṃsakas themselves articulate a *karmin* whose cognitive and volitional life is at the center of the ritual operation. Reading the school in this register is not interpolating an Aurobindonian frame into the texts; it is recovering a register the texts already carry, which the post-Śaṅkara Vedāntic reception and the modern academic reception had downplayed.

### 2.5 The line of reading and what extends it

The line of reading marked by Clooney's titles takes classical Mīmāṃsā to be a coherent reflective practice-philosophy whose interior dimension belongs to the school's own self-understanding. The Sanskrit primary sources on disk substantively support that reading. The question downstream is not whether Mīmāṃsā is a deeper school than the standard reception assumed — that case can be made directly from the on-disk *Bhāṣya* and *Vārttika* — but what is at stake when a contemporary reader, outside the original symbol-environment, encounters this corpus.

Cross-engagement: G. W. F. Hegel ([primary text](cite://hegel/phenomenology-of-spirit/17)) and this section take opposite turns on the evolution register axis — `sublative-becoming` against `real-cosmological-evolution`.

## §3 — Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* as a hermeneutic for the contemporary reader

Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* (CWSA Vol. 15) is a hermeneutic for reading the Vedic *saṃhitā*. Its central claim is that the Vedic deities are *psychological powers*, and that the *ṛṣis* understood them so. *Indra* is the illumined intelligence; *Agni* is the seer-will or aspirational flame; *Vāyu* is the life-force; *Soma* is the ānanda-current; *Sūrya* is the supramental light; *Sarasvatī* is inspiration. The Vedic hymns are read as the *ṛṣis*' own articulation of an inner discipline, in symbolic language addressed at once to the cosmic deity and to the corresponding inner power. The exterior referent and the interior referent are held together by what Aurobindo calls the *double language* of the Veda (*Secret of the Veda*, CWSA 15, pp. 8–9).

Cross-engagement: same epistemological register, different commitments at the truth-structure axis. Baladeva Vidyabhushana: `single-absolute-truth`, [primary text](cite://badarayana/brahma-sutra/1.1.1). Here: `hierarchical-standpoint-truth`.

Aurobindo's hermeneutic is, on its own self-presentation, a *recovery* — the recovery of an earlier Vedic sense that, on his account, was already opaque to Yāska and to Sāyaṇa, and that the modern reader needs explicit interpretive work to access. The standard polemic against the hermeneutic — that the classical Mīmāṃsakas explicitly reject the *nirvacana*-style symbolic decoding of ritual terms (Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* on Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika* carries arguments to this effect) — is correct. The Mīmāṃsakas read *go*, *aśva*, and *soma* literally; they do not read them as light, vital force, and ānanda. Classical Mīmāṃsā does not endorse Aurobindo's hermeneutic. The hermeneutic is for *the contemporary reader*, not for the Mīmāṃsaka.

### 3.1 The asymmetry of the symbol-environment

The crucial point is the asymmetry of the symbol-environment between the classical Mīmāṃsaka and the contemporary reader.

Cross-engagement: against Bhaktisiddhanta Saraswati: `bliss-or-delight` rather than `devotion` for the motive force axis ([primary text](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/5.29)).

The classical Mīmāṃsaka inhabited a culture in which the Vedic apparatus was the substantive vocabulary of public and personal life. *Agni* was the actual fire kindled in the actual *gārhapatya*, *āhavanīya*, and *dakṣiṇāgni* hearths of the actual ritual enclosure; the metaphorical resonance of *agni* as inner aspiration was carried in the lived practice without needing separate articulation. The same is true of *soma*, *vāc*, and the other Vedic terms. The terms were not foreign vocabulary requiring interpretive recovery; they were the household vocabulary of a ritual culture. Under those conditions, an explicit psychological-symbolic decoding of the terms would have been redundant at best, distorting at worst. The Mīmāṃsakas' insistence on the literal-ritual reading was not a failure to appreciate depth; it was a faithfulness to the integrity of a practice in which the depth was already carried in the lived application of the literal terms.

The contemporary reader inhabits a different symbol-environment. The Vedic apparatus is foreign vocabulary. *Agni* arrives in translation as "fire" or "the fire-deity," and the contemporary reader has no lived practice in which a kindled fire functions as the substantive vocabulary of ritual life. The *gārhapatya* hearth is, for the contemporary reader, a curiosity. Under those conditions, a literal-ritual reading of the Vedic corpus is *not equivalent* to the literal-ritual reading available to the classical Mīmāṃsaka. The same propositional content, encountered in a symbol-environment where the symbols no longer carry their original lived weight, is a different thing. The literal reading, for the contemporary reader, leaves the corpus as antiquarian rather than transformative.

This is where Aurobindo's hermeneutic does its work. By proposing that *agni* names the seer-will, *soma* the ānanda-current, *indra* the illumined intelligence, Aurobindo gives the contemporary reader an interpretive frame in which the Vedic terms are again referring to something the reader has access to in their own interior life. The frame is not what the Mīmāṃsakas had — they did not need it. It is what the contemporary reader, lacking the original symbol-environment, can use to encounter the corpus as a living document.

### 3.2 The *Synthesis of Yoga* register

The *Synthesis of Yoga* extends the hermeneutic from the Vedic *saṃhitā* to the broader category of *yajña* itself. Aurobindo's formulation in I.IV "The Sacrifice, the Triune Path and the Lord of the Sacrifice" (CWSA 23, p. 89) — "Life, not a remote silent or high-uplifted ecstatic Beyond-Life alone, is the field of our Yoga" — generalizes the *yajña-bhūmi* to the whole field of conscious life. The triple character of the sacrifice — works, love, and knowledge (CWSA 23, p. 134) — names the three modes in which the contemporary reader can engage their own life as a *yajña*.

Cross-engagement: the disagreement with Anantakrishna Sastri is at the practice-path axis itself: `transformative-integration` here, `knowledge-discipline` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14).

Applied to the Mīmāṃsā corpus, this hermeneutic produces the following: the contemporary reader, encountering Mīmāṃsā's account of *codanā*, *bhāvanā*, *adhikāra*, and *apūrva*, can read the school's articulation of how a verbal injunction operates on a qualified agent as an account that has analogs in their own life. The reader does not perform a śrauta sacrifice; the reader has, however, the experience of being summoned by an obligation, of organizing their volitional life around a course of action, of acting under the call of a normative source they did not generate, of leaving behind a residue that persists. The Mīmāṃsaka categories are, on this reading, generalisable to the structures of conscious obligation in the contemporary reader's own life. Aurobindo's hermeneutic is what makes the generalization visible.

### 3.3 Amplification, not correction

Aurobindo's hermeneutic, applied to Mīmāṃsā, is *amplification* — a register the contemporary reader can add to their encounter with the corpus — not *correction*. The Mīmāṃsakas were not making a mistake the hermeneutic now repairs. They were giving a coherent account, in their own categories, of the practice they actually performed. The contemporary reader, who does not perform that practice, can use Aurobindo's hermeneutic to find a register in which the Mīmāṃsaka categories illuminate something in their own life. That is amplification of the corpus's reach for a new audience, not correction of the corpus's original meaning.

Cross-engagement: same soteriological register, different commitments at the soteric end axis. Appayya Dikshita: `identity-with-ground`, [primary text](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/3.4). Here: `transformation-of-life`.

The v1/v2 framing was wrong on this point. The earlier passes treated Aurobindo's hermeneutic as a recovery of *what the school always meant but lost the ability to articulate*. That imposes on the school a story it does not tell about itself. The school articulated its own meaning correctly, in the symbol-environment it operated in. Aurobindo's hermeneutic is for the contemporary reader who has lost the symbol-environment but has gained, through Aurobindo, a generalizing register in which the school's content is again live.

### 3.4 Why the asymmetry matters dialectically

The asymmetry licenses a more careful dialectic with the Vedāntic critique. Sureśvara's [*Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* 1.67](cite://sureshvara/naishkarmya-siddhi/1.67) (Alston tr. 1959) carries the canonical argument that ritual is structurally incapable of liberation: "The knowledge derived from Vedic revelation demolishes nescience in its form as modified into the factors necessary for action at a single stroke. Hence there can be no association between the two." Sureśvara's argument is intra-Vedāntic; it concerns the proper relation of *karma* and *jñāna* in a soteriological economy where *jñāna* is the direct cause of liberation. The argument lands against the claim that ritual *as such* produces liberation. Mīmāṃsā does not, in its own self-understanding, claim to produce *mokṣa* in the Advaita sense; it claims to produce *niḥśreyasa* through *codanā*-prompted action that conjoins the *puruṣa* with the highest good (Śabara on MS 1.1.2), and Mīmāṃsā's *niḥśreyasa* is not Advaita's *kaivalya*. The Sureśvara critique and the Mīmāṃsaka claim are talking past one another. The two schools have different soteriological economies. The contemporary reader, equipped with both, can hold the disagreement as a real philosophical disagreement rather than as evidence that one school has displaced the other.

## §4 — Why the v1/v2 "consciousness-decline" framing was wrong

The earlier passes (v1: `MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS.md`; v2: `MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS_v2.md`) defended what v1 called Hypothesis C: a "causal-diagnostic" thesis on which the marginalisation of *karma-kāṇḍa* was attributable to the loss of the cognitive substrate that had once made the externally-specifiable rite transparent to its inner correlates. v2 sharpened this into the claim that "the depth-reading recovers what was lost: the lived integration." The texts do not require this claim.

The framing fails on three counts.

First, it presupposes that the school *needed* to carry an integral-yogic interior in order for its claims to be intelligible. The Mīmāṃsakas articulate the cognitive-volitional structure of the rite in their own categories — *codanā*, *bhāvanā*, *adhikāra*, *apūrva* — and these categories do the work the school takes them to do without requiring an Aurobindonian reading underneath. The school is intelligible on its own terms.

Second, the framing produces a story of decline for which the textual evidence is, as v1 itself conceded, "novel-but-not-textually-supported." The Mīmāṃsakas do not narrate their own school as having lost a depth-register; later Vedāntins critique the school for prioritising *karma* over *jñāna*, but that is an inter-school polemic about soteriological priority, not an internal admission of cognitive decline. v1's Hypothesis C floated free of textual anchorage and v2 retained it without fixing the anchorage problem.

Third, the diagnostic framing displaces the substantive question (what happens to the listener) with a derivative question (what went wrong with the school).

Sureśvara's [*NS* 1.67](cite://sureshvara/naishkarmya-siddhi/1.67) critique, which v2 treated as something the depth-reading could escape, is exactly what it presents itself as: an intra-Vedāntic polemic about the proper relation of *karma* and *jñāna* in an Advaita soteriology. The critique stands as a serious argument about whether ritual action can be the direct cause of *mokṣa* in Sureśvara's sense. It does not stand as evidence that Mīmāṃsā stopped working. Mīmāṃsā continues to work, in the registers in which it works, for those who continue to work in those registers. The question of contemporary recovery is a different question; v1/v2 conflated the two.

## §5 — Implications for the corpus

Three implications follow.

First, the entries on Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila, and Prabhākara should be read as the canonical articulations of a coherent practice-philosophy. The school's claims — *codanā* as autonomous *pramāṇa* (MS 1.1.2; Śabara *Bhāṣya* ad loc.), the *bhāvanā* analysis of injunctive force (Śabara on MS 2.1.1; Kumārila *Tantra-Vārttika*), the *adhikāra* doctrine of qualification, the *apūrva* doctrine of persistence, the *aṅga-aṅgi-vibhāga* (MS 3.1–4) — are not preliminary articulations awaiting an Aurobindonian recovery. They are the school's account of the practice it carried, articulated with technical precision, and they stand on their own.

Second, the academic line marked by Clooney's titles (*Thinking Ritually* 1990; "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature" 2022) names the standard precedent for treating the school as a coherent reflective practice-philosophy. Those works are not in our corpus and the reading is therefore engaged at the level of title-thesis and known scholarly summary; the substantive textual work is done from the Sanskrit primary texts on disk. The older standard reference points (Frauwallner, Halbfass, Freschi, Arnold) are likewise not in our corpus and are cited only as field-orientation.

Third, the Aurobindonian hermeneutic is one available reader-stance among several. The corpus can mark this stance as one that the present project takes when engaging the corpus as a contemporary reader, while marking equally clearly that it is not the stance of the Mīmāṃsakas themselves and not the only available stance for the contemporary reader. A reader who reads the Mīmāṃsā primary texts directly — without taking up Aurobindo's hermeneutic — gets a serious philosophical engagement with a coherent practice-philosophy. The reader who additionally takes up Aurobindo's hermeneutic gets, in addition, an interpretive frame in which the school's categories illuminate structures of conscious obligation in their own life. Both are legitimate. The corpus need not collapse the two readings into one.

## §6 — Honest defeaters

Three lines of challenge are real.

First, the Mīmāṃsakas' own anti-symbolic-decoding polemic against *nirvacana*-style readings (Yāska in the *Nirukta*; Aurobindo-style readings of Vedic terms as psychological powers) is real. Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* on Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika*, on disk in Sanskrit, carries arguments to this effect. Aurobindo's hermeneutic is *not* what classical Mīmāṃsā would endorse. The hermeneutic is a contemporary-reader stance, available to a reader outside the original symbol-environment, that the Mīmāṃsakas would on their own self-understanding reject. It asks the contemporary reader to do something the school's own polemic explicitly disallows. A reader who finds this an unacceptable interpretive liberty has a real ground for refusing it.

Second, the asymmetry-of-symbol-environment argument (§3.1) is empirically defeasible. There may be contemporary readers for whom the literal-ritual register of the Vedic apparatus is not foreign vocabulary but lived practice — practitioners in continuous śrauta-tradition lineages, members of communities for whom the Vedic terms remain substantively embedded in life. For such readers, Aurobindo's hermeneutic may be precisely what classical Mīmāṃsā says it is: a distorting interpolation. The asymmetry argument speaks to the modern reader at distance from the original symbol-environment, not to all contemporary readers.

Third, the substantive case for reading the school as a coherent reflective practice-philosophy is built here directly from the on-disk Sanskrit primary texts; Clooney's titles are cited as academic precedent rather than as the source of the argument, since the books themselves are not in our corpus. If the introspective-philosophical reading is wrong — if the school is, as some of the older reception had it, a school of procedural exegesis without the reflective dimension defended here — then the position's appeal to that reading is misplaced. There are scholars (the older Vedāntic reception, parts of the modern academic reception) who would dispute it. The position here does not undertake to refute those scholars; it takes the introspective reading as the most defensible English-language reading currently available and proceeds from there. A reader who takes the older procedural-exegesis reading of Mīmāṃsā as correct will need a different argument for why the school is a coherent practice-philosophy worth contemporary engagement, or will abandon the contemporary-engagement thesis.

These three defeaters are real. The position remains defensible: it takes the school as coherent on its own terms (the substantive case worked from on-disk Sanskrit primary texts), it offers the Aurobindonian hermeneutic as a contemporary-reader amplification rather than a school-internal correction, and it acknowledges where it asks the reader to take an interpretive step the school's own self-understanding does not authorize.
