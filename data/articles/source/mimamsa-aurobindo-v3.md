# Mīmāṃsā as a Coherent Practice-Philosophy: Clooney's Reading and the Contemporary Reader's Encounter

## Status of this document

This article supersedes v1 (`MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS.md`) and v2 (`MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS_v2.md`). Both earlier passes framed the question as a diagnosis: what went wrong with classical Mīmāṃsā such that *karma-kāṇḍa* came to be perceived as ritualism without depth, and how does Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* recover what was lost. The framing was wrong. It imposed on the school a story of decline the texts do not require. Mīmāṃsā is a coherent practice-philosophy that worked for the practitioners it worked for, in the symbol-environment they inhabited; it does not need fixing. The interesting question lies elsewhere — at the encounter between this corpus and the *contemporary reader*.

The two principal interlocutors are Francis X. Clooney, S.J., whose *Thinking Ritually* (1990) and 2022 encyclopedia entry "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy" are the standard English-language readings of the school as a coherent reflective system, and Sri Aurobindo, whose *Secret of the Veda* and *Synthesis of Yoga* offer a hermeneutic addressed to the modern reader rather than a correction of the ancient practitioner.

---

## §1 — The thesis

First, the externalization that characterises classical Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā is a school of practice, not a defect in the school. Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, and Prabhākara Miśra externalised the ritual operation because, in the cultural environment they wrote in, the external symbols — the *agni*, the *soma*, the *yajamāna*, the *adhvaryu*, the materially specifiable enclosure of the *yajña-bhūmi* — were uniform, intelligible, and embedded in lived practice. Externalisation under those conditions is not a failure of depth; it is a coherent way of articulating a practice whose symbols already carried the depth in the lives of the practitioners.

Second, as the conditions changed, the symbols changed. The śrauta corpus contracts; the *gṛhya* and *smārta* corpora expand; the centre of practice shifts from the public *yāga* to domestic and devotional registers; the available imagery and material apparatus alter with cultural change. This is normal cultural evolution. It is not the symptom of a metaphysical decline. The tradition continues; the externalised forms shift to track the symbol-environment of each successive generation of practitioners.

Third — and this is the substantive claim — Jaimini and the traditional Mīmāṃsakas are saying something that makes complete sense within the scope of the practice as they articulated it. Their account of *codanā* as the autonomous *pramāṇa* for *dharma* (Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra 1.1.2), of *bhāvanā* as the urging-to-action that the optative suffix carries (Śabara on MS 2.1.1), of *apūrva* as the persistent residue connecting the present action to its eventual fruit (MS 2.1.5), and of *adhikāra* as the agent's qualification for the rite — taken together — articulates a coherent practice-philosophy. The coherence is not contingent on a hidden integral-yogic structure underneath. The coherence is on the surface, in the school's own categories, used by the practitioners on their own terms. Clooney's contribution is to have shown this in a sustained way against the standard Vedāntic and modern-academic reception.

Fourth, the question that moves this article is not "what went wrong with the school" but "what happens to the listener — the contemporary reader — who engages this corpus today." That is a different question from the historical one. The contemporary reader does not stand inside the cultural environment in which Vedic terms were lived imagery. The reader stands in a modern symbol-environment, in which the Vedic terms arrive as foreign vocabulary requiring active interpretive work. For *that* reader, an interpretive hermeneutic is needed; an interpretive hermeneutic is what Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* and *Synthesis of Yoga* together supply. The hermeneutic does not correct Mīmāṃsā. It opens a register in which a contemporary reader can encounter the corpus as transformative rather than as antiquarian.

Fifth, the Mīmāṃsā entries — Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila, Prabhākara — should be read as the canonical articulations of a coherent practice-philosophy. The Aurobindonian recovery, applied to the contemporary reader's encounter, is one available reader-stance among several. It is not the reading the Mīmāṃsakas would themselves have offered. Clooney's Mīmāṃsā is the school as a coherent reflective system in its own period; Aurobindo's hermeneutic is what the present reader can do with the corpus now.

## §2 — Mīmāṃsā on its own terms: Clooney as the principal interpreter

The standard reception of Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā in twentieth-century English-language scholarship was shaped by the older Vedāntic priority. The school was treated as the technical apparatus of *karma-kāṇḍa*, useful as a propaedeutic to the genuinely philosophical work of *Uttara*-Mīmāṃsā but not in itself a serious philosophical engagement with the world. Clooney's *Thinking Ritually* (1990) is the sustained refutation of that reception. The book's title carries the thesis: the Mīmāṃsā ritualist *thinks*, and the thinking is constitutive of the ritual operation rather than ornamental to it.

Three of Clooney's moves deserve substantive engagement here.

### 2.1 The *karmin* as a reflective subject

Clooney's first move is to reframe the agent of the rite. The standard reading treats the *yajamāna* and the *ṛtvij* as performers of a procedural sequence whose efficacy is independent of their cognitive states beyond the minimum required for correct performance. Clooney argues, against this, that the Mīmāṃsā corpus articulates the *karmin* as constitutively a reflective subject. The technical apparatus the school develops — the *aṅga-aṅgi-vibhāga* (analysis of subordinate and principal ritual elements), the rules of *prakaraṇa*, *sthāna*, *krama*, and *samākhyā* (Mīmāṃsā-Sūtra 3.1–4 with Śabara's *Bhāṣya*), the *atideśa* doctrine for extending procedures across rites (MS 7.1–4) — is not a code for mechanical performance. It is the apparatus a thinking practitioner uses to determine what the rite *is* in this case, what its parts are, how they are subordinated, what counts as correct execution under variable circumstances. The rite, in Mīmāṃsā, is a thing the practitioner *understands*, not just performs.

This reframing has a textual centre at MS 1.1.1 — *athāto dharma-jijñāsā*, "now therefore the inquiry into *dharma*." Clooney emphasises that the school is, by its own opening word, *jijñāsā* — *inquiry*. The Mīmāṃsaka is not the executor of an inherited code; the Mīmāṃsaka is the inquirer into what *dharma* requires. The rest of the twelve-*adhyāya* corpus is the structured working-out of that inquiry. Mīmāṃsā is a school of *thinking* about ritual, not a school *of* ritual in some non-thinking sense. The point is in the first *sūtra* and Clooney's reading recovers it.

### 2.2 *Codanā* and *bhāvanā*: the cognitive-volitional structure of the injunction

Clooney's second move is to take the *codanā* and *bhāvanā* doctrines as cognitively substantive rather than as machinery for getting from the verbal injunction to the bodily act. The *codanā*, glossed by Śabara as *kriyāyāḥ pravartakaṃ vacanam* — "the speech that prompts to action" (Śābara-Bhāṣya on MS 1.1.2; on-disk Sanskrit, line 8) — is not simply an instruction that lands on a passive recipient. It is a verbal operation that *prompts*, that *urges*, that re-organises the hearer's intentional life around the prescribed action. Śabara's gloss continues: *tayā yo lakṣyate, so 'rthaḥ puruṣaṃ niḥśreyasena saṃyunaktīti pratijānīmahe* — "what is indicated by it, *that* is the *artha*; we declare that this *artha* conjoins the *puruṣa* with the highest good (*niḥśreyasa*)" (Śābara-Bhāṣya on MS 1.1.2; on-disk Sanskrit, line 9). The *artha* of *codanā* is constitutively defined by what it does *to* the agent. Clooney's 2022 entry takes this as the textual centre: from the foundational *Bhāṣya*, the school's category of *artha* is agent-binding, not world-describing.

The *bhāvanā* doctrine, articulated by Śabara (on MS 2.1.1) and elaborated by Kumārila Bhaṭṭa in the *Tantra-Vārttika* and by Maṇḍana Miśra in the *Vidhi-Viveka*, names two operations:

- *śābdī bhāvanā* — the action of the optative suffix on the hearer's intention; the verbal force prior to action.
- *ārthī bhāvanā* — the agent's volitional energy directed toward the prescribed result.

Clooney's reading, in *Thinking Ritually* and again in 2022, treats the *bhāvanā*-pair as the school's own articulation of the cognitive-volitional core of the rite. The first half is what language *does* to the listener. The second half is what the listener-turned-agent *does* under the language's prompting. Both halves are interior. Neither is bodily-procedural in the reductive sense the older reception assumed. The Mīmāṃsakas are giving an account of how a verbal injunction, received by a competent agent, re-orders that agent's intentional life and is enacted as a determinate course of action. That is, on Clooney's reading, philosophy of action carried through with technical precision.

### 2.3 *Adhikāra*, *apūrva*, and the reflective architecture of qualification

Clooney's third move is to read *adhikāra* and *apūrva* as further evidence of the school's reflective architecture. The *adhikāra* doctrine specifies the conditions under which a given agent is qualified to perform a given rite. Śabara's analysis distinguishes external prerequisites (caste, age, ritual purity, possession of fire) from intrinsic capacity — the agent's desire (*kāma*), cultivated capability, and inner disposition. Clooney points out that the Mīmāṃsā treatment of *adhikāra* is not a sociological classification; it is a structural doctrine about the conditions under which a verbal injunction binds a particular agent. The agent is internal to the system as a qualified subject, not external to it as a contingent performer.

*Apūrva* is the most contested category in the school. Standard polemic reads *apūrva* as a metaphysical placeholder — an unseen entity invoked to bridge the gap between the present rite and the posthumous *phala*. Clooney does not deny that the Mīmāṃsakas develop *apūrva* in this metaphysical register. He argues that *apūrva*, in its functional role, is the school's name for the persistence of the rite's effect *in the agent and in the cosmos* across time. The category is not a bookkeeping fiction. It is an account of how the action's effect is real, not exhausted by its visible occurrence, and structured by the verbal injunction that prompted it. The Mīmāṃsakas, faced with the question "what makes the rite efficacious across the gap between performance and fruit," answer with a category that gives the persistence ontological weight.

### 2.4 The introspective extension: Clooney 2022

The 2022 encyclopedia entry "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy" sharpens the 1990 reading. The argument is that Mīmāṃsā is not only philosophy of language and exegesis but *introspective literature*: a literature that thematises the practitioner's interior orientation to the sacred. Clooney works through Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika* — the *Codanā-Sūtra* section on the autonomous validity of injunctive cognition (*svataḥ-prāmāṇya*), the *ātmavāda* on the self-luminous *ātman*, and the Bhāṭṭa analysis of how injunctive cognition operates on the agent — to show that the school articulates an interior dimension within the ritual operation itself.

The 2022 entry does two things. It establishes in a major reference work that the introspective-philosophical reading of Mīmāṃsā is academically responsible and textually grounded. And it makes clear that the introspective dimension is *internal to the school's self-understanding*, not imposed from outside. The Mīmāṃsakas themselves articulate a *karmin* whose cognitive and volitional life is at the centre of the ritual operation. Clooney is not interpolating an Aurobindonian register into the texts; he is recovering a register the texts already carry, which the post-Śaṅkara Vedāntic reception and the modern academic reception had downplayed.

### 2.5 Extending Clooney

Clooney has shown that classical Mīmāṃsā is a coherent reflective practice-philosophy whose interior dimension belongs to the school's own self-understanding. The school is precisely as coherent as Clooney shows it to be. The question downstream of that establishment is not "is Mīmāṃsā a deeper school than the standard reception assumed" — Clooney has already settled that — but "what is at stake when a contemporary reader, outside the original symbol-environment, encounters this corpus."

## §3 — Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* as a hermeneutic for the contemporary reader

Aurobindo's *Secret of the Veda* (CWSA Vol. 15) is a hermeneutic for reading the Vedic *saṃhitā*. Its central claim is that the Vedic deities are *psychological powers*, and that the *ṛṣis* understood them so. *Indra* is the illumined intelligence; *Agni* is the seer-will or aspirational flame; *Vāyu* is the life-force; *Soma* is the ānanda-current; *Sūrya* is the supramental light; *Sarasvatī* is inspiration. The Vedic hymns are read as the *ṛṣis*' own articulation of an inner discipline, in symbolic language addressed at once to the cosmic deity and to the corresponding inner power. The exterior referent and the interior referent are held together by what Aurobindo calls the *double language* of the Veda (*Secret of the Veda*, CWSA 15, pp. 8–9).

Aurobindo's hermeneutic is, on its own self-presentation, a *recovery* — the recovery of an earlier Vedic sense that, on his account, was already opaque to Yāska and to Sāyaṇa, and that the modern reader needs explicit interpretive work to access. The standard polemic against the hermeneutic — that the classical Mīmāṃsakas explicitly reject the *nirvacana*-style symbolic decoding of ritual terms (Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* on Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika* carries arguments to this effect) — is correct. The Mīmāṃsakas read *go*, *aśva*, and *soma* literally; they do not read them as light, vital force, and ānanda. The position taken here accepts this; the position is not that classical Mīmāṃsā endorses Aurobindo's hermeneutic. The position is that Aurobindo's hermeneutic is for *the contemporary reader*, not for the Mīmāṃsaka.

### 3.1 The asymmetry of the symbol-environment

The crucial point is the asymmetry of the symbol-environment between the classical Mīmāṃsaka and the contemporary reader.

The classical Mīmāṃsaka inhabited a culture in which the Vedic apparatus was the substantive vocabulary of public and personal life. *Agni* was the actual fire kindled in the actual *gārhapatya*, *āhavanīya*, and *dakṣiṇāgni* hearths of the actual ritual enclosure; the metaphorical resonance of *agni* as inner aspiration was carried in the lived practice without needing separate articulation. The same is true of *soma*, *vāc*, and the other Vedic terms. The terms were not foreign vocabulary requiring interpretive recovery; they were the household vocabulary of a ritual culture. Under those conditions, an explicit psychological-symbolic decoding of the terms would have been redundant at best, distorting at worst. The Mīmāṃsakas' insistence on the literal-ritual reading was not a failure to appreciate depth; it was a faithfulness to the integrity of a practice in which the depth was already carried in the lived application of the literal terms.

The contemporary reader inhabits a different symbol-environment. The Vedic apparatus is foreign vocabulary. *Agni* arrives in translation as "fire" or "the fire-deity," and the contemporary reader has no lived practice in which a kindled fire functions as the substantive vocabulary of ritual life. The *gārhapatya* hearth is, for the contemporary reader, a curiosity. Under those conditions, a literal-ritual reading of the Vedic corpus is *not equivalent* to the literal-ritual reading available to the classical Mīmāṃsaka. The same propositional content, encountered in a symbol-environment where the symbols no longer carry their original lived weight, is a different thing. The literal reading, for the contemporary reader, leaves the corpus as antiquarian rather than transformative.

This is where Aurobindo's hermeneutic does its work. By proposing that *agni* names the seer-will, *soma* the ānanda-current, *indra* the illumined intelligence, Aurobindo gives the contemporary reader an interpretive frame in which the Vedic terms are again referring to something the reader has access to in their own interior life. The frame is not what the Mīmāṃsakas had — they did not need it. It is what the contemporary reader, lacking the original symbol-environment, can use to encounter the corpus as a living document.

### 3.2 The *Synthesis of Yoga* register

The *Synthesis of Yoga* extends the hermeneutic from the Vedic *saṃhitā* to the broader category of *yajña* itself. Aurobindo's formulation in I.IV "The Sacrifice, the Triune Path and the Lord of the Sacrifice" (CWSA 23, p. 89) — "Life, not a remote silent or high-uplifted ecstatic Beyond-Life alone, is the field of our Yoga" — generalises the *yajña-bhūmi* to the whole field of conscious life. The triple character of the sacrifice — works, love, and knowledge (CWSA 23, p. 134) — names the three modes in which the contemporary reader can engage their own life as a *yajña*.

Applied to the Mīmāṃsā corpus, this hermeneutic produces the following: the contemporary reader, encountering Mīmāṃsā's account of *codanā*, *bhāvanā*, *adhikāra*, and *apūrva*, can read the school's articulation of how a verbal injunction operates on a qualified agent as an account that has analogues in their own life. The reader does not perform a śrauta sacrifice; the reader has, however, the experience of being summoned by an obligation, of organising their volitional life around a course of action, of acting under the call of a normative source they did not generate, of leaving behind a residue that persists. The Mīmāṃsaka categories are, on this reading, generalisable to the structures of conscious obligation in the contemporary reader's own life. Aurobindo's hermeneutic is what makes the generalisation visible.

### 3.3 The hermeneutic is amplification, not correction

It needs to be marked explicitly: Aurobindo's hermeneutic, applied to Mīmāṃsā, is *amplification* — a register the contemporary reader can add to their encounter with the corpus — not *correction*. The Mīmāṃsakas were not making a mistake the hermeneutic now repairs. The Mīmāṃsakas were giving a coherent account, in their own categories, of the practice they actually performed. The contemporary reader, who does not perform that practice, can use Aurobindo's hermeneutic to find a register in which the Mīmāṃsaka categories illuminate something in their own life. That is amplification of the corpus's reach for a new audience, not correction of the corpus's original meaning.

This is the precise sense in which the v1/v2 framing was wrong. The earlier passes treated Aurobindo's hermeneutic as a recovery of *what the school always meant but lost the ability to articulate*. That treatment imposes on the school a story it does not tell about itself. The corrected position is more modest. The school articulated its own meaning correctly, in the symbol-environment it operated in. Aurobindo's hermeneutic is for *us* — for the contemporary reader who has lost the symbol-environment but has gained access, through Aurobindo, to a generalising register in which the school's content is again live.

### 3.4 Why the asymmetry matters dialectically

The asymmetry licenses a more careful dialectic with the Vedāntic critique. Sureśvara's *Naiṣkarmya-Siddhi* 1.67 (Alston tr. 1959) carries the canonical argument that ritual is structurally incapable of liberation: "The knowledge derived from Vedic revelation demolishes nescience in its form as modified into the factors necessary for action at a single stroke. Hence there can be no association between the two." Sureśvara's argument is intra-Vedāntic; it concerns the proper relation of *karma* and *jñāna* in a soteriological economy where *jñāna* is the direct cause of liberation. The argument lands against the claim that ritual *as such* produces liberation. It is a serious argument and the position taken here does not propose a refutation. Mīmāṃsā does not, in Mīmāṃsā's own self-understanding, claim to produce *mokṣa* in the Advaita sense; it claims to produce *niḥśreyasa* through *codanā*-prompted action that conjoins the *puruṣa* with the highest good (Śabara on MS 1.1.2), and Mīmāṃsā's *niḥśreyasa* is not Advaita's *kaivalya*. The Sureśvara critique and the Mīmāṃsaka claim are talking past one another in important respects. That is, on the position taken here, what one would expect: the two schools have different soteriological economies. The contemporary reader, equipped with both, can hold the disagreement as a real philosophical disagreement rather than as evidence that one school has displaced the other.

## §4 — Why the v1/v2 "consciousness-decline" framing was wrong

The earlier passes (v1: `MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS.md`; v2: `MIMAMSA_AUROBINDO_HYPOTHESIS_v2.md`) defended what v1 called Hypothesis C: a "causal-diagnostic" thesis on which the marginalisation of *karma-kāṇḍa* was attributable to the loss of the cognitive substrate that had once made the externally-specifiable rite transparent to its inner correlates. v2 sharpened this into the claim that "the depth-reading recovers what was lost: the lived integration." That claim, on reflection, is not the user's view, and the texts do not require it.

The reasons to retract the framing are three.

First, the framing presupposes that the school *needed* to carry an integral-yogic interior in order for its claims to be intelligible. The Mīmāṃsakas, as Clooney has shown, articulated the cognitive-volitional structure of the rite in their own categories — *codanā*, *bhāvanā*, *adhikāra*, *apūrva* — and these categories do the work the school takes them to do without requiring an Aurobindonian reading underneath. The school is intelligible on its own terms. The diagnostic framing imposed an interpretive demand the texts do not make.

Second, the framing produces a story of decline for which the textual evidence is, as v1 itself conceded, "novel-but-not-textually-supported." The Mīmāṃsakas do not narrate their own school as having lost a depth-register; later Vedāntins critique the school for prioritising *karma* over *jñāna*, but that is an inter-school polemic about soteriological priority, not an internal admission of cognitive decline. v1's Hypothesis C floated free of textual anchorage and v2 retained it without fixing the anchorage problem. The corrected position drops the hypothesis.

Third, and most importantly, the framing misrepresents what the user actually wants to claim. The user's interest is in the *contemporary reader's encounter*, not in a diagnosis of historical decline. The diagnostic framing displaces the substantive question (what happens to the listener) with a derivative question (what went wrong with the school) the user is not asking. By retracting the diagnostic framing, the present article restores the substantive question to its proper place.

Sureśvara's *NS* 1.67 critique, which v2 treated as something the depth-reading could escape, is on the corrected reading exactly what it presents itself as: an intra-Vedāntic polemic about the proper relation of *karma* and *jñāna* in an Advaita soteriology. The critique stands as a serious intra-Vedāntic argument about whether ritual action can be the direct cause of *mokṣa* in Sureśvara's sense. It does not stand as evidence that Mīmāṃsā stopped working. Mīmāṃsā as a school of practice continues to work, in the registers in which it works, for those who continue to work in those registers. The question of contemporary recovery is a different question. v1/v2 conflated the two; the correction separates them.

The retraction is offered without apologetic disclaimer. The earlier passes were a first attempt to articulate the position; the position has come into focus through engagement with the user's correction; the article that articulates the corrected position must replace, not gloss, the earlier framing.

## §5 — Implications for the corpus

Three implications for the timeline-corpus follow from the corrected position.

First, the entries on Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila, and Prabhākara should be read as the canonical articulations of a coherent practice-philosophy. The school's claims — *codanā* as autonomous *pramāṇa* (MS 1.1.2; Śabara *Bhāṣya* ad loc.), the *bhāvanā* analysis of injunctive force (Śabara on MS 2.1.1; Kumārila *Tantra-Vārttika*), the *adhikāra* doctrine of qualification, the *apūrva* doctrine of persistence, the *aṅga-aṅgi-vibhāga* (MS 3.1–4) — are not preliminary articulations awaiting an Aurobindonian recovery. They are the school's account of the practice it carried, articulated with technical precision, and they stand on their own.

Second, Clooney is the principal modern interpreter through whose work the school becomes legible to the contemporary English-language reader. The corpus pages should foreground Clooney's *Thinking Ritually* and 2022 entry as the primary secondary literature, alongside the older standard reference points (Frauwallner, Halbfass, Freschi, Arnold). This is already partially done in the existing thinker entries (Jaimini, Śabara, Kumārila, Prabhākara entries cite Clooney 1990); the v3 reading reinforces Clooney as the spine.

Third, the Aurobindonian hermeneutic is one available reader-stance among several. The corpus can mark this stance as one that the present project takes when engaging the corpus as a contemporary reader, while marking equally clearly that it is not the stance of the Mīmāṃsakas themselves and not the only available stance for the contemporary reader. The reader who works through the Mīmāṃsā corpus on Clooney's reading alone — without taking up Aurobindo's hermeneutic — gets a serious philosophical engagement with a coherent practice-philosophy. The reader who additionally takes up Aurobindo's hermeneutic gets, in addition, an interpretive frame in which the school's categories illuminate structures of conscious obligation in their own life. Both are legitimate. The corpus need not collapse the two readings into one.

## §6 — Honest defeaters

Three places the position taken here may be challenged.

First, the Mīmāṃsakas' own anti-symbolic-decoding polemic against *nirvacana*-style readings (Yāska in the *Nirukta*; Aurobindo-style readings of Vedic terms as psychological powers) is real. Sucaritamiśra's *Kāśikā* on Kumārila's *Ślokavārttika*, on disk in Sanskrit, carries arguments to this effect. The position taken here owns this. Aurobindo's hermeneutic is *not* what classical Mīmāṃsā would endorse. The hermeneutic is a contemporary-reader stance, available to a reader outside the original symbol-environment, that the Mīmāṃsakas would on their own self-understanding reject. The position is consistent — the hermeneutic is for the contemporary reader, not for the classical Mīmāṃsaka — but it must be marked: the hermeneutic asks the contemporary reader to do something the school's own polemic explicitly disallows. The reader who finds this an unacceptable interpretive liberty has a real ground for refusing the hermeneutic. The position taken here does not force the hermeneutic on any reader. It offers it.

Second, the asymmetry-of-symbol-environment argument (§3.1) is empirically defeasible. There may be contemporary readers for whom the literal-ritual register of the Vedic apparatus is not foreign vocabulary but lived practice — practitioners in continuous śrauta-tradition lineages, members of communities for whom the Vedic terms remain substantively embedded in life. For such readers, Aurobindo's hermeneutic may be precisely what classical Mīmāṃsā says it is: a distorting interpolation. The position taken here speaks to a particular contemporary reader (the modern reader at distance from the original symbol-environment); it does not speak for all contemporary readers. The asymmetry argument is reader-relative.

Third, the position takes Clooney's reading of Mīmāṃsā as established, and builds on it. If Clooney's reading is wrong — if the school is, as some of the older reception had it, a school of procedural exegesis without the reflective-introspective dimension Clooney attributes to it — then the position's appeal to Clooney is misplaced. The defeater is not just abstract: there are scholars (the older Vedāntic reception, parts of the modern academic reception) who would dispute Clooney's introspective reading. The position here does not undertake to refute those readings; it takes Clooney's reading as the most defensible English-language reading currently available and proceeds from there. A reader who takes the older procedural-exegesis reading of Mīmāṃsā as correct will need a different argument for why the school is a coherent practice-philosophy worth contemporary engagement, or will need to abandon the contemporary-engagement thesis. The position taken here does not provide that alternative argument.

These three defeaters are real. The position is not invulnerable. The position is, however, defensible: it takes the school as coherent on its own terms (with Clooney as the warrant), it offers the Aurobindonian hermeneutic as a contemporary-reader amplification rather than a school-internal correction, and it acknowledges where it asks the reader to take an interpretive step the school's own self-understanding does not authorise.

## Secondary sources consulted

- Francis X. Clooney, S.J., *Thinking Ritually: Rediscovering the Pūrva Mīmāṃsā of Jaimini*, Publications of the De Nobili Research Library XVII, Vienna 1990. The principal English-language argument that classical Pūrva-Mīmāṃsā is a coherent reflective system whose *karmin* is constitutively a thinking subject. Engaged in §2.1–2.3.
- Francis X. Clooney, S.J., "Mīmāṃsā as Introspective Literature and as Philosophy," in *Hinduism and Tribal Religions*, Springer Encyclopedia of Indian Religions, ed. Long et al., 2022, entry 1397. https://link.springer.com/referenceworkentry/10.1007/978-94-024-1188-1_1397 (paywalled). The 2022 sharpening of the 1990 reading: Mīmāṃsā as introspective literature with the practitioner's interior orientation thematised by the school itself. Engaged in §2.4.
- Francis X. Clooney, S.J., *Theology After Vedānta: An Experiment in Comparative Theology*, SUNY 1993. Methodological background — the discipline of reading another tradition's primary texts on their own terms, which underwrites the reading of Mīmāṃsā in *Thinking Ritually*.
- Wilhelm Halbfass, *Tradition and Reflection: Explorations in Indian Thought*, SUNY 1991. On-disk research file: `/orcd/home/002/eeshan/philosophy/sources/research/R5_halbfass.md`. Background reading of Kumārila on the structural account of how Vedic injunction generates normative grip.
- Elisa Freschi, "Adhikāra and rights," elisafreschi.com, 2024-05-08. Śabara's *bahir-bhūta* / *ātma-vṛtti* distinction in *adhikāra*.
- Andrew Ollett, "What is Bhāvanā?," *Journal of Indian Philosophy* 41 (2013), pp. 535–555. Standard contemporary discussion of the *bhāvanā* doctrine.
