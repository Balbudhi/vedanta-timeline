## Reader's Orientation

### Why Nietzsche and why now

Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900) is, for the user's thesis, the indispensable Western diagnostician, the philosopher who identified the structural failures of post-Platonic metaphysics and yet remained inside the horizon whose closure he was diagnosing. He performed the most thorough Western critique of the "view from nowhere"; he showed that the grammatical subject is a fiction back-projected from language and moral demand; he articulated, in *Geburt der Tragödie*, a phenomenology of dissolution in the *Ur-Eine* under the *Schleier der Māyā* that comes closer to a *līlā* ontology than any other Western text before Aurobindo. And he refused to fill the cleared space with anything other than a "philosophy of the future" whose central figure is the legislator-philosopher, a refusal that is the precise point of structural divergence: where Nietzsche reads the dissolution of the metaphysical subject as licensing imperious value-creation, the thesis reads the same dissolution as licensing total surrender from fullness, *niṣkāma karma* without ego-arrogation.

This document is a reader's instrument: faithful English translations, selective retention of indispensable German terms inline, and explication clarifying both what Nietzsche says and where his text stands relative to the thesis's commitments.

### Biographical arc — the single thread

Nietzsche's published life-work runs 1872–1888, Nachlass to the Turin collapse of 3 January 1889. The 1872 *Geburt der Tragödie* answers Schopenhauer-Wagnerianly: tragedy provides *metaphysischer Trost*. The middle works (*Menschliches* 1878–80, *Morgenröthe* 1881, *Fröhliche Wissenschaft* 1882–87) shed the apparatus and install positivist-genealogical hygiene. The mature works (*Zarathustra* 1883–85, *Jenseits* 1886, *Genealogie* 1887, *Götzen-Dämmerung* and *Antichrist* and *Ecce Homo* 1888) build the late doctrines: *Wille zur Macht*, *ewige Wiederkunft*, *Übermensch*, *Umwerthung aller Werthe*.

For the thesis, the most useful early Nietzsche is the most metaphysically committed, the 1872 text whose Schopenhauerian scaffolding he later disowned. *Geburt der Tragödie* §1, §16/§17, §24 articulates the closest pre-Aurobindonian Western statement of *līlā* ontology. The mature Nietzsche keeps the phenomenology of dissolution and the will-to-power dynamism but loses the *Ānanda*-character of the *Ur-Eine*. The thesis can recover the early ontology, retain the late phenomenology, and complete what Nietzsche did not, articulating with Aurobindo the *Saccidānanda* ground that the *Ur-Eine* pointed toward but could not sustain.

### The *Wille zur Macht* caveat (read first; restated in Part VI)

*Der Wille zur Macht* (1901, expanded 1906) is a posthumous editorial fiction by Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche and Peter Gast, drawn from notebooks 1883–1888 according to a plan Nietzsche abandoned. The *Kritische Studienausgabe* (KSA), Colli/Montinari, 1980 (rev. 1988), publishes the Nachlass in original notebook order; Montinari's *Nietzsche lesen* (1982) demonstrates the WzM arrangement is editorial fiction. **Citation discipline:** always KSA volume + notebook + fragment, *KSA 12, 7[60]*, never "*WzM §481*"; cross-reference WzM only when engaging Heidegger.

### Untranslated terms — glossary

- ***Wille zur Macht***. *Wille* is not a faculty but a structural character of the living, closer to *Śakti* than to *voluntas*. Deleuze de-psychologizes: pure differential force.
- ***ewige Wiederkunft***. Cosmological-existential thought-experiment, *Fröhliche Wissenschaft* §341 (1882), elaborated in *Zarathustra* III. Exoteric (same world recurs) vs. esoteric Deleuzean (return of difference); the latter is structurally compatible with Aurobindonian evolutionary cosmology.
- ***Übermensch***. The figure of *Zarathustra*'s Vorrede; phenomenological-existential, not biological-eugenic.
- ***Ressentiment***. The systematic reactive structure of *Genealogie* I.
- ***Schein***. Appearance not opposed to a true world behind it (*Götzen-Dämmerung*, "Wie die 'wahre Welt' endlich zur Fabel wurde").
- ***Wahrheit***. Itself a value with a history, produced by the ascetic ideal.
- ***Schleier der Māyā***. Sanskrit term in *Geburt der Tragödie* §1, via Schopenhauer and the early German Indologists. The structure described is what the *darśana* tradition has been mapping.
- ***Apollinisch / Dionysisch***. The two *Kunsttriebe* of *Geburt der Tragödie*, individuation and dissolution.
- ***Ur-Eine***. "Primordial-One." Schopenhauer's *Wille* renamed under *principium individuationis*. The closest Nietzsche comes to *Saccidānanda* in its *Ānandamaya* aspect.

### How to use this document

Structured by text, not theme. Each Part works through structurally decisive passages with German verbatim, English, and explication. Part VII gives compressed reading notes, where Nietzsche confirms, where he challenges, the bilateral-vs-tripartite asymmetry, and a recommended reading sequence by KSA citation. The bias is toward depth in the German: where Kaufmann, Hollingdale, or Del Caro smooth philosophically load-bearing terms, this document foregrounds the original.

## Part I — *Die Geburt der Tragödie* (1872; reissued 1886)

*KSA 1, 9–156. English: Kaufmann (Vintage, 1967); Golffing (Doubleday, 1956). Citations by GT § and KSA page.*

*Geburt der Tragödie* is Nietzsche's first major work and, for the thesis, the most useful, though he later distanced himself from its Schopenhauerian apparatus. The early Nietzsche, with the most metaphysical scaffolding, articulates the position closest to the thesis's ontology; the mature Nietzsche, having shed it, keeps the phenomenology but loses the ontological completion. This Part works through §1 (the *Kunsttriebe*; *Schleier der Māyā*), §16/§17 (Schopenhauer's music-metaphysics; *Ur-Eine*; *metaphysischer Trost*), §24 (*künstlerisches Spiel des Willens mit sich selbst*, closest Western articulation of *līlā* prior to Aurobindo), and the 1886 *Versuch einer Selbstkritik*.

### §1 — Die zwei Kunsttriebe und der Schleier der Māyā

**KSA 1, 25–30.**

§1 announces the architectural thesis: art-development is bound to the duality of the Apollinian and Dionysian *Kunsttriebe*, whose periodic reconciliation engenders tragedy.



*KSA 1, 25 (GT §1; orthography per Colli/Montinari)*

#### English

> "We shall have gained much for the science of aesthetics when we have arrived not only at the logical insight, but at the immediate certainty of intuition, that the continued development of art is bound up with the duality of the Apollinian and the Dionysian, in a manner similar to how generation depends on the duality of the sexes, amidst perpetual strife and only periodically intervening reconciliation."

**Translation note.** *Anschauung* preserves the Kantian register: sensible intuition, the receptive grasp prior to conceptual articulation. Generation requires the tension of opposed drives; periodic reconciliation, not permanent fusion.

#### Explication

The opening does three things. (a) It announces a *dualism of drives*, not of substances, not two worlds like Plato's, but two modes of artistic production. (b) It refuses logical insight alone: phenomenological recognition is required. (c) The relation between the drives is *Kampf and Versöhnung*, periodic strife and reconciliation, never Hegelian *Aufhebung*. Tragedy holds the tension without dissolving difference; structurally this is closer to Caitanya's *acintya-bhedābheda* than to Hegel.



*KSA 1, 25–26 (GT §1)*

#### English

> "These names we borrow from the Greeks, who disclose to the discerning person the profound secret doctrines of their art-intuition not in concepts, but in the vividly clear figures of their world of gods. With their two art-deities, Apollo and Dionysus, is linked our recognition that in the Greek world there exists a tremendous opposition, in origin and aims, between the art of the sculptor, the Apollinian, and the non-visual art of music, the Dionysian; these two so different drives run side by side, mostly in open conflict with each other, and mutually stimulating each other to ever new and more powerful births, in order to perpetuate in them the struggle of that opposition which the common word 'art' only apparently bridges over, until, through a metaphysical miracle-act of the Hellenic 'will', they appear paired with each other, and in this pairing finally engender the Attic tragedy."

#### Explication

Greek philosophical-aesthetic insight is figural, not propositional. The gods are not symbols *of* concepts; they *are* the concepts in their proper mode. For the thesis, this is structurally homologous to its position on divine forms, *real powers and modalities of the divine*, the way the structure of reality figures itself forth in human apprehension. The "metaphysical miracle-act" is the moment when structural opposition produces, through its tension, a higher form, structurally similar to Aurobindo's supramental descent: lower planes are not abolished but brought into a new poising.



*KSA 1, 26–27 (GT §1)*

#### English

> "The beautiful semblance [*schöne Schein*] of the dream-worlds, in whose production every human being is a complete artist, is the prerequisite of all plastic art and also, as we shall see, of one important half of poetry. We take delight in the immediate comprehension of figure; all forms speak to us; there is nothing indifferent or superfluous. [...] This joyous necessity of dream-experience has been likewise expressed by the Greeks in their Apollo: Apollo, as the god of all plastic forces, is at the same time the soothsaying god."

#### Explication

The Apollinian is the dream-mode. *Schöner Schein* is appearance-considered-as-such, formed, bounded, image-presenting, not deceptive appearance. Apollo is the *principium individuationis*: the principle by which the One Will appears as many individuated phenomena.

For the thesis, the Apollinian as *principium individuationis* is structurally T7 (divine play through willful self-ignorance): *māyā* as a positive ontological function, the divine's self-differentiation into individual forms, *bhāvarūpa avidyā* in aesthetic vocabulary. The early Nietzsche holds the symmetry of the two drives; the mature Nietzsche tends to read the Apollinian as the Dionysian's self-representation. The thesis is closer to the early position.



*KSA 1, 29–30 (GT §1; Nietzsche's spelling: "Maja", not "Māyā", Schopenhauerian transliteration)*

#### English

> "Under the spell of the Dionysian, not only is the bond between human and human sealed again: also estranged, hostile or subjugated nature celebrates its reconciliation festival again with its lost son, the human being. Freely the earth proffers her gifts, and peacefully the predators of the rocks and desert draw near. The chariot of Dionysus is heaped with flowers and wreaths: panthers and tigers walk under his yoke. Transform Beethoven's jubilee hymn to 'Joy' into a painting, and do not restrain your imagination when the millions sink shuddering into the dust: thus can one approach the Dionysian. Now the slave is a free man, now all the rigid, hostile demarcations that necessity, caprice or 'impudent fashion' have established between human beings are shattered. Now, in the gospel of universal harmony, everyone feels himself not only united, reconciled, merged with his neighbor, but *one*, as if the *Schleier der Māyā* had been torn and now fluttered only in rags before the mysterious primordial-One [*Ur-Einen*]."

**Translation note.** *Ur-Einen* must not be smoothed away; Kaufmann's "Primordial Unity" is acceptable but weaker than "primordial-One." Nietzsche writes *Maja* (Schopenhauerian transliteration). Schopenhauer uses the term as exact synonym for *principium individuationis*, i.e., for what Nietzsche has just called the Apollinian.

#### Explication — the structural center for the thesis

The passage's structure: under Dionysian ecstasy, the boundaries between humans break down; the human-nature bond is restored; the result is not mere union but *oneness* (*eins*), figured as the tearing of the *Schleier der Māyā* and a brief glimpse of the *Ur-Eine*.

Three commitments must be marked.

**First**, this is *samādhi*-phenomenology, an altered state in which ordinary individuation gives way and underlying unity is disclosed. The structure is what Kashmir Shaivism calls *pūrṇāhantā*, what Advaita calls *nirvikalpa samādhi*.

**Second**, the *Schleier der Māyā* reference is deliberate. Nietzsche could have used *Schleier der Erscheinung*; he chose *Māyā* because German metaphysical vocabulary after Hegel was too saturated with idealist commitments to carry the weight. T7 holds that *māyā* is the divine's self-veiling as part of its own play (*bhāvarūpa avidyā*). Nietzsche reads the *Schleier* as a veil to be *torn* (closer to Schopenhauer's *Verneinung*); Aurobindo and the thesis read its function as itself divine, to be seen-through-as-divine, not torn-and-discarded.

**Third**, the *Ur-Eine*. The mature Nietzsche replaces it with *Wille zur Macht* and removes the *Eine*: only differential play of forces. For the thesis, this is a loss: the *Ur-Eine*'s closeness to *Saccidānanda* in its *Ānandamaya* aspect is what the mature Nietzsche moves away from. The thesis recovers the early *Ur-Eine*, deepens it with *Sat* and *Cit* (Aurobindo's contribution), and reads the mature *Wille zur Macht* as the dynamic face of what *Ur-Eine* named more fully.

The most important asymmetry is bilateral-vs-tripartite: GT operates with two drives; Aurobindo's supermind operates with three poises (comprehending, apprehending, projecting). GT maps cleanly onto apprehending and projecting; the *comprehending* poise has no precise GT analog. Part VII develops this.

### §16 — Schopenhauers Substrat: Musik als unmittelbare Sprache des Willens

**KSA 1, 100–108.**

§16 makes the Schopenhauerian metaphysics explicit: music alone among the arts bypasses the *principium individuationis* and gives the Will directly. Music is Dionysian art *par excellence*.



*KSA 1, 103–104 (GT §16)*



*KSA 1, 108 (GT §16)*

#### English

> "Apollo stands before me as the transfiguring genius of the *principium individuationis*, through whom alone redemption in the world of semblance is truly to be attained, while under the mystical jubilation-cry of Dionysus the spell of individuation is shattered and the way to the Mothers of Being, to the innermost core of things, lies open. [...] Music has a different character and origin from all the other arts, because it is not, like all the others, a copy of the appearance, but immediately a copy of the Will itself, and therefore stands to everything physical in the world as the metaphysical, to every appearance as the thing-in-itself."

> "From the essence of art as it is commonly comprehended under the single category of semblance and beauty, the tragic cannot be honestly derived: only out of the spirit of music do we understand a joy in the annihilation of the individual. For in the individual instances of such annihilation only the eternal phenomenon of Dionysian art is made clear to us, the art that expresses the Will in its omnipotence behind the *principium individuationis*, eternal life beyond all appearance and in spite of all annihilation. The metaphysical joy in the tragic is a translation of the instinctively unconscious Dionysian wisdom into the language of the image: the hero, the highest manifestation of the Will, is for our pleasure negated because he is, after all, only appearance, and the eternal life of the Will is not touched by his annihilation. 'We believe in eternal life,' tragedy cries; while music is the immediate idea of this life."

#### Explication

Music bypasses the *principium individuationis* and presents the Will directly; listening at depth is the Dionysian state, *jenseit aller Erscheinung und trotz aller Vernichtung*.

Three points for the thesis. **First**, this Dionysian state is structurally *samādhi*, what *Yoga-Sūtra* describes as *asamprajñāta*, what Kashmir Shaivism calls the experience of *Bhairava*. Convergence is structural, not philological. **Second**, Nietzsche already modifies Schopenhauer's pessimism: tragedy gives *metaphysische Freude*, the seed of *amor fati*. The structure is *Saccidānanda* in its *Ānandamaya* aspect, *Ānanda* not as absence of *duḥkha* but as the deeper character that includes both. **Third**, music's privileged status converges with the Tantric *nāda-Brahman*: sound is the least-individuated sensory modality.

The 1886 *Versuch* repudiates the Schopenhauerian *frame* (Will vs. Vorstellung, thing-in-itself vs. appearance) but the phenomenology of dissolution survives. The thesis can hold the phenomenology without the dualism: appearance is the mode in which the divine appears to itself; the *Ur-Eine* is not behind the world but is the world in its non-individuated aspect. This is Aurobindo's position, Brahman *as* the world in its dynamic-static non-difference, not behind it.

### §24 — *Künstlerisches Spiel des Willens*: the eternal play of the Will with itself

**KSA 1, 151–154.**

§24 is the conceptual culmination of *Geburt der Tragödie*: even the ugly and disharmonious are a *künstlerisches Spiel* that the Will plays with itself in the eternal fullness of its joy, the closest pre-Aurobindonian Western articulation of *līlā*.



*KSA 1, 152 (GT §24)*


*KSA 1, 152 (GT §24)*

#### Companion passage from §17 (the earlier articulation of the *metaphysischer Trost*)


*KSA 1, 109 (GT §17)*

#### English

> "Here now it becomes necessary to take a bold leap into a metaphysics of art, by repeating the earlier proposition that existence and the world appear justified only as an aesthetic phenomenon: in which sense the tragic myth in particular must convince us that *even the ugly and the disharmonious are an artistic play that the Will, in the eternal fullness of its joy, plays with itself*."

> "The pleasure the tragic myth produces has the same homeland as the joyful sensation of dissonance in music. The Dionysian, with its primal joy [*Urlust*] perceived even at and through pain, is the common birth-womb of music and of the tragic myth."

> (§17:) "Dionysian art too wants to convince us of the eternal joy of existence, only we are to seek this joy not in the appearances but behind the appearances. We are to recognize how all that comes to be must be ready for sorrowful downfall; we are forced to gaze into the terrors of individual existence, and yet we are not to be paralyzed: a *metaphysical comfort* tears us momentarily out of the bustle of changing figures. We really are, in brief moments, the primordial Being itself, and we feel its untameable lust for existence and joy in existence; the strife, the agony, the destruction of appearances now strikes us as necessary [...]. We are pierced by the raging sting of these agonies at the very moment when we have become, as it were, one with the immeasurable primordial joy in existence, and when we sense, in Dionysian rapture, the indestructibility and eternity of this joy. Despite fear and pity, we are the happily-living, not as individuals, but as the one Living Being, with whose generative joy we are fused."

**Translation note.** *Urlust selbst am Schmerz percipirt*, *Lust* is not the absence of *Schmerz* but what is perceived through and including pain. *Spiel* is the German term that does the structural work *līlā* does in Sanskrit. The Will plays *with itself*: ground-level self-play, in which individual suffering is an aspect.

#### Explication — the closest Western articulation of *līlā*

The argument: (1) Tragedy presents the dissolution of the individual. (2) The individual is an individuation of the Will; when it perishes, what perishes is the individuation, not the source. (3) The Will experiences the totality as its own self-play, not cruelty, not masochism, but *Spiel*: structured, joyful, self-displaying activity producing and dissolving its own forms. (4) At the Will's level, *Schmerz* and *Lust* coexist as constitutive moments of a single dynamism, *Urlust*.

This is *structurally* the *līlā* doctrine. The Kālī image is most precise: her dance *is* Śiva's *Ānanda* in its destructive aspect, Śiva does not suffer the dance; Śiva *is* the dance. Nietzsche reaches this in 1872, two generations before Aurobindo's *Life Divine*, without direct contact with the Indian texts. Independent traditions reaching the same structure suggest the structure is a feature of the real (T4).

What §24 *gives*: a fully Western articulation of *līlā*, with language (*metaphysischer Trost*, *künstlerisches Spiel*, *Urlust selbst am Schmerz percipirt*); recognition through aesthetic experience rather than propositional argument; the constitutive co-belonging of *Schmerz* and *Lust* at the ground.

What §24 *lacks*: (a) the Ur-Eine's *consciousness*, the *Cit* aspect of *Saccidānanda*; (b) the individual's sustainable participation, the Dionysian moment is *consumed*, not held as the *vijñāna* state holds both individual and ground simultaneously; (c) the personal dimension, Nietzsche's Ur-Eine is impersonal, leaving no place for *caitya puruṣa* (T11). These gaps are filled by Aurobindo's *Cit*-aspect, the *vijñāna* vs. *jñāna*-only distinction, and the psychic-being doctrine.

#### Four commentators on §24 (compressed)

**Klossowski** (1969) reads the eternal return into §24: each moment of pain is the Will's *Lust* in self-production; Turin was a Dionysian dissolution. Closest Western interpretation, but the danger is what Aurobindo's tripartite supermind avoids, dissolution of ego need not dissolve the psychic being.

**Deleuze** (1962) de-Schopenhauerizes the *Trost*: affirmation of becoming, not consolation. The Ur-Eine becomes pure productive force. Most thesis-compatible, but loses the *Ānanda*-character.

**Heidegger** (1936–37 lectures) reads §24 as *Gegenbewegung* against Plato, aesthetic answer without transcendent realm. Useful against McGilchrist-emissary reductions of *līlā* to coping.

**Kaufmann** (1950) reads §24 as *Lebensbejahung* but treats the Ur-Eine as metaphor. For the thesis, a partial reading.

### The "Versuch einer Selbstkritik" (1886): what is repudiated, what survives

**KSA 1, 11–22.**

The 1886 preface repudiates the Schopenhauerian metaphysics (Ur-Eine as thing-in-itself), the Wagnerian aesthetics, the romantic tone, and the Christian-redemptive framing. What survives: the *problem* of the Greek tragic experience; the Apollinian/Dionysian opposition as art-drives (kept through *Götzen-Dämmerung* and *Ecce Homo*); the phenomenology of Dionysian intoxication; the diagnosis that "*Die Philosophen haben das Dionysische verloren*", which runs through to *Genealogie* III.

#### Key passage


*KSA 1, 14 (Versuch §2)*

> "What I then wanted to see 'with the eye of an artist': science under the optic of the artist, but art under the optic of *life*."

This is the methodological statement of mature Nietzsche. Life > art > science: science as terminal optic is the ascetic ideal in modern dress. For the thesis, this triple-ordering is homologous to Aurobindo's planar hierarchy, supramental ground, overmental-mental aesthetic disclosure, rational-empirical mediation. Take the early ontology where Nietzsche abandoned it; take the methodological mature Nietzsche where his diagnosis sharpened.

## Part II — *Jenseits von Gut und Böse* (1886)

*KSA 5, 9–243. English: Kaufmann (Vintage, 1966); Hollingdale (Penguin, 1973).*

*Jenseits von Gut und Böse* is the most systematic of Nietzsche's mature works, the agenda for "the philosophers of the future", those who come after the death of God and after the recognition that the will-to-truth itself was an expression of the ascetic ideal. This Part works through the *Vorrede*/§4 (perspectivism, *Unwahrheit als Lebensbedingung*), §6 (every philosophy as confession), §16 (the cogito), §22 (physics as interpretation), §211 (the philosopher of the future, the structural divergence point), and §259 (*Wille zur Macht*).

### Vorrede §1 / Erstes Hauptstück §4 — Wahrheit als Weib; Unwahrheit als Lebensbedingung

**KSA 5, 11–18.**



*KSA 5, 11 (Vorrede §1)*



*KSA 5, 18 (Erstes Hauptstück §4)*

#### English

> "Suppose truth is a woman, what then? Is the suspicion not well-founded that all philosophers, insofar as they were dogmatists, have understood little about women? That the gruesome earnestness, the awkward importunity with which they have hitherto been wont to approach truth were inept and improper means for winning over precisely a female?"

> "The *falseness* of a judgment is for us not yet an objection to a judgment; in this respect our new language sounds perhaps strangest. The question is, to what extent it is life-promoting, life-preserving, species-preserving, perhaps even species-cultivating; and we are fundamentally inclined to claim that the falsest judgments (to which the synthetic judgments *a priori* belong) are the most indispensable to us, that without granting the validity of the logical fictions, without measuring reality against the purely invented world of the unconditional and self-identical, without a constant falsification of the world through number, the human being could not live, that the renunciation of false judgments would be a renunciation of life, a negation of life. *To acknowledge untruth as a condition of life*: that, to be sure, means resisting accustomed value-feelings in a dangerous fashion; and a philosophy that ventures this thereby places itself, on this account alone, beyond good and evil."

#### Explication

**First**, the woman-truth metaphor is methodological. The dogmatic philosopher's *Zudringlichkeit* is the structural form of *pramāṇa-consciousness* demanding cognitive mastery. Aurobindo's *jñāna by identity* and Bhattacharyya's *svarūpa-jñāna* articulate what Nietzsche's metaphor signals.

**Second**, *Unwahrheit als Lebensbedingung* is stronger than postmodern anti-realism. Organisms cannot live without certain falsifications (self-identical objects, stable causation, mathematizable regularity); the falsification is life-promoting. This aligns with T7: *māyā* as *bhāvarūpa avidyā*, the positive veiling-as-individuation. The individuated organism requires the veil. Nietzsche's claim is the secularized version of the divine's willful self-veiling enabling the play of individual life.

**Third**, the philosophy that ventures this places itself *jenseits von Gut und Böse*, not nihilism but a space where values are recognized as vital functions. Nietzsche's "beyond" has no positive ground; the philosopher creates values by legislative fiat. The thesis grounds ethics differently: the divine's lawful *līlā* unfolds; the individual participates, does not legislate. Divergence returns in §211.

### §6 — Every philosophy is the personal confession of its author

**KSA 5, 19–20.**



*KSA 5, 19–20 (§6)*

#### English

> "Gradually it has become clear to me what every great philosophy so far has been: namely, the personal confession of its author and a kind of involuntary and imperceptible *mémoires*; equally, that the moral (or immoral) intentions in every philosophy constituted the real germ from which the whole plant has always grown. Indeed one does well (and wisely), to explain how a philosopher's most remote metaphysical claims actually came about, always to ask first: what morality does it (does *he* —) aim at? I do not therefore believe that a 'drive to knowledge' is the father of philosophy, but rather that another drive has, here as elsewhere, employed knowledge (and *Verkenntniss*, misknowing!) only as a tool. Whoever examines the basic drives of the human in order to discover how far they may have played their inspiring game precisely here as inspiring geniuses (or daemons and kobolds —) will find that all of them have at some point practiced philosophy, and that each one of them would very much like to represent itself as the ultimate purpose of existence and as the legitimate lord of all the other drives. For every drive is domineering [*herrschsüchtig*]: and as such it attempts to philosophize."

**Translation note.** *Verkenntniss*, Hollingdale's "misconception" is too weak; the active character of *knowing-otherwise* must be preserved. The drive uses both *Erkenntniss* and *Verkenntniss* as tools. *Herrschsüchtig*, *Sucht* is addiction; the drive is *addicted* to dominance.

#### Explication

§6's claim: every great philosophy is the personal confession of its author. The metaphysics is the bloom; the germ is the moral intention. This is genetic deconstruction without genetic fallacy, not "the philosophy is false because the drive produced it" but "to understand the philosophy one must understand the drive."

For the thesis, §6 is the structural form of the anti-Hegel polemic: Hegel's *Logik* is the systematizing drive in its purest form, the drive that "would very much like to represent itself as the ultimate purpose of existence." Reflexively, §6 applies to the thesis itself but not destructively: (a) the thesis is *adhikāra*-bound (T12), not universalizing-systematic; (b) it distinguishes *pramāṇa-caitanya* (epistemic-bullying) from *sākṣi-caitanya* (witness without dominance). §6 catches every philosophy operating from the former; the thesis claims to operate from the latter. The *sākṣi*-vs-*pramāṇa* distinction is where Indian epistemology supplies what Western philosophy after Nietzsche lacks.

### §16–§17 — *es denkt*: the deconstruction of the cogito

**KSA 5, 29–31.**

§16 attacks "immediate certainty" as *contradictio in adjecto* and dissects the assumptions hidden in "I think." §17 supplies *es denkt* and shows even the *es* is too much.



*KSA 5, 29–30 (§16)*



*KSA 5, 30–31 (§17)*

#### English

> (§16:) "There are still harmless self-observers who believe that there are 'immediate certainties', for example, 'I think', or, as was the superstition of Schopenhauer, 'I will', as if here cognition got its object pure and naked, as a 'thing-in-itself', with no falsification on the side of either subject or object. But that 'immediate certainty', like 'absolute knowledge' and 'thing-in-itself', involves a *contradictio in adjecto*, I shall repeat a hundred times. [...] The philosopher must say to himself: 'When I dissect the process expressed in the proposition "I think," I obtain a series of reckless assertions whose justification is difficult, perhaps impossible, for example, that it is *I* who thinks, that there must be something that thinks, that thinking is an activity and effect on the part of a being conceived as cause, that there is an "I", and finally that it is already established what is to be designated as thinking, that *I know* what thinking is.'"

> (§17:) "As regards the superstitions of the logicians: I shall not weary of underlining one small short fact, which these superstitious folk concede unwillingly, namely, that a thought comes when *it* will, and not when *I* will; so that it is a falsification of the facts to say: the subject 'I' is the condition of the predicate 'think'. *It thinks* [*es denkt*]: but that this 'it' is precisely that old famous 'I' is, mildly put, only an assumption, an assertion, and above all no 'immediate certainty'. In the end, even with this 'it thinks' too much has been done: already this 'it' contains an interpretation of the process and does not belong to the process itself. One infers here according to the grammatical habit: 'Thinking is an activity; to every activity belongs one who is active; therefore —'."

#### Explication

§16 deconstructs the Cartesian cogito and subject-predicate metaphysics from Aristotle through Hegel. (1) *Cogito ergo sum* claims immediate certainty. (2) Immediate certainty is *contradictio in adjecto*. (3) Dissecting "I think" exposes unjustified assumptions: that there is an "I," that the "I" is agent, that thinking requires an agent, that we know what thinking is. (4) Even *es denkt* is too much, the *Subject + Verb* habit projects an actor behind every action.

For the anti-Hegel polemic, §16 is the precise weapon: Hegel's *Logik* assumes grammar tracks ontology; §16 short-circuits this at the source. Cartesian cogito and Hegelian *Logik* are products of the same *grammatische Gewohnheit*.

What §16 lacks is the positive remainder. Nietzsche dissolves the ego *downward* into drives and stops. The thesis: after the linguistic ego is dissolved, the *sākṣi* remains, and beneath it the *caitya puruṣa*, experiential findings of the contemplative tradition. The Indian tradition dissolves the ego *upward*. Nietzsche's deconstruction is not wrong; it is incomplete.

### §22 — *es ist auch nur Interpretation*: physics as text

**KSA 5, 36–38.**



*KSA 5, 37 (§22)*

#### English

> "[...] but, as I said, that is interpretation, not text; and someone could come along who, with the opposite intention and interpretive art, from the same nature and with regard to the same phenomena, would understand precisely the tyrannical-ruthless and inexorable enforcement of power-claims, an interpreter who would set the exceptionlessness and unconditionedness in all 'will to power' before your eyes so vividly that almost every word, even the word 'tyranny', would appear unusable or already as a weakening and mitigating metaphor, as too-human [...]. Supposing that this too is only interpretation, and you will be eager enough to raise that objection, well, so much the better."

(Note: the famous "no facts, only interpretations" formula does *not* appear in JGB §22; it is in the Nachlass, KSA 12, 7[60] and KSA 13, 14[184], discussed in Part VI. JGB §22's phrasing is the milder *das ist Interpretation, nicht Text*, applied specifically to physics-as-interpretation.)

#### Explication

§22 extends §16's grammatical critique to natural science. The categories of Newtonian mechanics are interpretive impositions; nature does not "obey" laws, the laws are how the interpretive apparatus organizes regularities. §22 is not self-refuting: Nietzsche anticipates the reflexive objection and answers *um so besser*. The position is reflexively stable; what it precludes is *all is interpretation, but my position is exempt*.

For the thesis, §22 aligns with no-super-perspective and precludes scientism. The thesis's own status: (a) *Saccidānanda* is at a different register, accessible through *jñāna by identity* rather than inferential interpretation; (b) Bhattacharyya's literal/symbolic distinction, metaphysical claims about the absolute are symbolic pointers, not text-claims; (c) the thesis's propositions are perspectival approximations, consistent with §22 rather than refuted by it.

### §211 — Philosophen sind Befehlende und Gesetzgeber

**KSA 5, 144–146.**

§211 is the structural divergence point. Nietzsche here specifies the figure of "the philosopher of the future", and the specification is *imperatival*, *legislative*, *creative-by-fiat*. The thesis's *vijñāna* state, by contrast, is *surrender from fullness*, *niṣkāma karma*, *witnessing without arrogation*. These are structurally opposed.




*KSA 5, 144–146 (§211)*

#### English

> "I insist that one finally cease confusing philosophical workers and scientific people in general with philosophers, that one give precisely here 'to each his own', and not too much to the former, not too little to the latter. [...] Those philosophical workers, after the noble model of Kant and Hegel, have to establish some great fact of valuation, that is, of former value-positings, value-creations that became dominant and for a time were called 'truths', and press them into formulas [...]"

> "But the genuine philosophers are *commanders and legislators*: they say 'thus it *shall be*!'; they first determine the Whither and the Why of the human, and have at their disposal the preliminary labor of all philosophical workers, of all those who have overpowered the past, they reach with creative hand toward the future, and everything that is and was becomes for them a means, an instrument, a hammer. Their 'knowing' is *creating*, their creating is a legislation, their will to truth is, *Wille zur Macht*."

#### Explication — the structural divergence

§211 distinguishes philosophical workers (Kant, Hegel) from genuine philosophers (of the future). The workers organize; the genuine create, and their creation is *legislation*: *so soll es sein*. Their will-to-truth is *Wille zur Macht*.

This is the imperatival philosopher, structurally opposed to the thesis's surrender-philosopher. T10 forbids the legislator-stance; T8 names this as *pramāṇa-bullying*. The §211 philosopher is the most powerful form of *pramāṇa-caitanya*: grasping reality with creative hand. The *vijñāna* state surrenders from fullness, witnesses rather than legislates, allows divine *līlā* to unfold rather than imposing.

Two qualifications soften but do not abolish the divergence. **First**, Deleuze drains the imperatival-egoistic content from *Wille zur Macht*: the philosopher is a node through which differential force operates, closer to the thesis's view of the individual as instrument of *līlā*. **Second**, Nietzsche's claim that the philosopher *creates values* (articulates a vision of *Wohin und Wozu*) need not be legislative-imperatival: T11 articulates the divine threading through experience without imposing; *adhikāra* (T12) means the vision is offered to those for whom it is fitting. Even with these qualifications, §211's rhetoric is opposed to the thesis: Nietzsche's philosopher seizes; the thesis's surrenders. The user's project takes a stand here, not absorption.

### §259 — Life is *Wille zur Macht*

**KSA 5, 207–208.**



*KSA 5, 207–208 (§259)*

#### English

> "Life itself is essentially appropriation, injury, overpowering of the alien and weaker, suppression, hardness, imposition of one's own forms, incorporation, and at the very least, at the mildest, exploitation, but why should one always use precisely such words, into which a slanderous intention has been imprinted from of old? [...] Even that body [...] must itself, if it is a living and not a moribund body, do toward other bodies all that from which its individuals refrain among themselves: it will have to be incarnate *Wille zur Macht*, it will want to grow, to spread, to draw to itself, to gain ascendancy, not out of any morality or immorality, but because it *lives*, and because life *is* precisely *Wille zur Macht*."

#### Explication

*Leben ist Wille zur Macht*: not driven by, but *is*. Every living thing is *Wille zur Macht* incarnate.

Structural overlap with *Śakti* (Śiva considered *as activity*): both name the productive force that is the character of all becoming. But *Śakti* is intrinsically conscious, *Śiva's* power, and Śiva is consciousness. *Wille zur Macht* is non-conscious in §259. Aurobindo's *Tapas-śakti* fuses what §259 leaves separate: the dynamism and the consciousness that is its inner aspect. Read *Wille zur Macht* as an incomplete articulation of *Tapas-śakti*; Nietzsche's atheism is the refusal to name what he is in fact describing.

A second difference: §259 is non-teleological; Aurobindo's *Tapas-śakti* operates within evolutionary cosmology. The thesis: Aurobindonian telos is not a pre-given destination but the *immanent direction* of divine self-disclosure, which can be participated in or resisted. This preserves Nietzsche's anti-teleological insight (no externally imposed goal) and Aurobindo's (the dynamism has direction by what it intrinsically is).

## Part III — *Zur Genealogie der Moral* (1887)

*KSA 5, 245–412. English: Kaufmann and Hollingdale (Vintage, 1967).*

The three treatises pursue genealogies of slave morality (I), bad conscience (II), and the ascetic ideal (III). Four passages are structurally decisive: **GM I.13** (the *Blitz*, no-doer-behind-the-deed); **GM II.12** (*Überwältigen-Herrwerden-Neu-Interpretiren*); **GM III.12** (perspectivism); **GM III.24–27** (self-overcoming of the ascetic ideal).

### GM I.13 — *kein Sein hinter dem Thun*

**KSA 5, 279.**

GM I culminates in §13's deconstruction of the moral subject, set up by the lambs and the birds of prey: the moral demand requires the fiction of a free subject behind the action.



*KSA 5, 279 (GM I §13)*

#### English

> "... but there is no such substratum; there is no 'being' behind doing, effecting, becoming; 'the doer' is merely a fiction added to the doing, the doing is everything. The folk in effect double the doing, when they see the lightning flash, that is a doing-doing: the same event posited once as cause and then once more as effect. Natural scientists do no better when they say 'force moves, force causes', and the like, our entire science, despite all its coolness and freedom from affect, still stands under the seduction of language and has not got rid of the changelings foisted upon it, the 'subjects', the atom, for example, is such a changeling, a doing-doing ... How much misunderstanding lies in our innocent talk of 'cause' and 'effect'!"

The *Blitz*-argument: when lightning flashes, there is one event, the flashing. Folk grammar projects two: *the lightning* (subject) which *flashes* (predicate). The flashing *is* the lightning. Extended to the moral case: there is no "strong one" behind the "strong action"; demanding that the strong refrain presupposes a doer who could have chosen otherwise. The moral demand makes sense only as a strategic move by the weak, *Ressentiment* mobilized into a moral system.

#### Explication — the *niṣkāma karma* parallel

GM I.13 articulates by genealogical route what the Gītā articulates by spiritual-instructive route: no doer behind the deed. *Niṣkāma karma* is the recognition that the ego-doer is a fiction. Direction differs, Nietzsche dissolves the doer *downward* into drives; the Gītā dissolves it *upward* into the witness, but the deconstruction is the same.

GM I.13's structural lack: after *das Thun ist Alles*, what is the witness? Nietzsche denies any. Classical Sāṃkhya resolves: action belongs to *prakṛti*, witnessing to *puruṣa*. The witness is not vulnerable to GM I.13 because it is not a substance back-projected from grammar but the *condition* under which experience can occur (Yoga's *vṛtti/sākṣi*).

The lambs/birds figure makes two claims at once: (a) the doer is a fiction; (b) slave morality is reactive moralization. The thesis accepts (a) without (b). *Dharma*, *adhikāra*, *ahiṃsā* give an ethical structure that is neither slave nor master morality, bird and lamb both participate in lawful *līlā* according to their natures.

### GM II.12 — *Überwältigen, Herrwerden, Neu-Interpretiren*

**KSA 5, 313–315.**

§12 is a methodological digression articulating the historical method behind the genealogical project: distinguish *origin* from *current function*; recognize that all becoming is interpretive force-imposition.




*KSA 5, 313–314 (GM II §12)*

#### English

> "The origin of a thing and its eventual utility, its actual employment and place in a system of purposes, lie *toto coelo* apart; something already in existence, somehow come about, is again and again reinterpreted by a superior power according to new aims, taken into possession anew, reshaped and redirected toward a new utility; all happening in the organic world is an *overpowering, a mastering*, and again all overpowering and mastering is a *fresh interpretation*, an arrangement, through which the previous 'meaning' and 'purpose' is necessarily obscured or altogether extinguished."

> "But all purposes, all utilities are only signs that a *Wille zur Macht* has become master of something less powerful and has imprinted upon it from itself the meaning of a function; and the entire history of a 'thing', an organ, a practice can in this way be a continuous chain of signs of ever new interpretations and arrangements, whose causes themselves need not be interconnected, they merely succeed and replace one another."

#### Explication

GM II.12 establishes three claims governing the entire genealogical project.

(1) **The genetic-functional gap.** Origin and current function are *toto coelo* apart. What punishment is *for* now (correction, deterrence) has nothing to do with what punishment *was* historically. Assuming current function reveals origin is the fundamental genealogical fallacy.

(2) **All becoming is force-imposition.** *Alles Geschehen in der organischen Welt ist ein Überwältigen, Herrwerden*. The world contains processes in which stronger forces master weaker and impose new meanings.

(3) **All mastering is interpretation.** *Alles Überwältigen und Herrwerden ist ein Neu-Interpretiren*. There is no fixed essence resisting reinterpretation; the essence *is* the current interpretive imposition.

For the thesis, this is the methodology required to handle T2, the self-correcting trajectory toward realism (Bhartṛprapañca → Bhāskara → Madhva → Caitanya → Aurobindo), without the false narrative of "Vedānta as degeneration from Śaṅkara." The post-Śaṅkara tradition is a series of *fresh interpretations* taking over the apparatus and reshaping it.

GM II.12 also challenges the thesis: if every interpretation is force-imposition, the *darśana* registers themselves are force-impositions. Response on two levels. **Concession**: every articulation, including the thesis's own, is perspectival force-imposition (consistent with §22). **Distinction**: between force-impositions aiming at domination and those aiming at recognition. *Pramāṇa-caitanya*'s imposition is dominative; *sākṣi-caitanya*'s is recognitive. Nietzsche reads all force-imposition through mastery; the *darśana* tradition treats grasping as a limited mode transcended in *niṣkāma karma*. GM II.12 catches the grasping mode; it does not refute the non-grasping mode.

### GM III.12 — *es giebt nur ein perspektivisches Sehen*

**KSA 5, 364–365.**

GM III.12 is the philosophically decisive perspectivism passage, appearing in the third treatise's argument that science, the modern will-to-truth, is itself a manifestation of the ascetic ideal. §12 turns this argument against the figure of the "pure, will-less, painless, timeless subject of knowledge" presupposed by Kantian transcendentalism and post-Kantian positivism. It is Nietzsche's most rigorous statement of his positive theory of objectivity.



*KSA 5, 364–365 (GM III §12)*

#### English

> "Let us be on our guard, my dear philosophers, from now on better against the dangerous old conceptual fable that posits a 'pure, will-less, painless, timeless subject of knowledge'; let us guard ourselves against the tentacles of such contradictory concepts as 'pure reason', 'absolute spirituality', 'knowledge in itself':, here it is always demanded that we think an eye that cannot possibly be thought, an eye toward which no direction of gaze is to be ascribed, in which the active and interpreting forces, through which seeing first becomes a *seeing-something*, are to be suppressed; here, then, an absurdity and non-concept of an eye is always demanded. *There is only a perspectival seeing, only a perspectival 'knowing'; and the more affects we allow to speak about a thing, the more eyes, different eyes, we know how to bring to bear on the same thing, the more complete will be our 'concept' of that thing, our 'objectivity'.* But to eliminate the will entirely, to detach the affects altogether, supposing we could: how then? would that not be to *castrate* the intellect?"

**Translation note.** *Sehen-Etwas*, "seeing-something"; the seeing is constitutively transitive, directed at a something. *Mehr Augen einzusetzen*, "to bring more eyes to bear" or "to deploy more eyes"; Nietzsche's *einsetzen* is active, deliberate (Hollingdale: "bring to bear"; Kaufmann: "know how to use"; the active "deploy" is closer). *Intellekt kastrieren*, Nietzsche's deliberately violent image; both translators keep it.

#### The four-move analysis

GM III.12 has four philosophical moves, each crucial for the thesis.

**Move 1, Deconstruction of the will-less subject.** The "pure, will-less, painless, timeless subject of knowledge" is *Widersinn und Unbegriff*, absurdity and non-concept. Every eye is directed, sees from somewhere with some interest. The Kantian transcendental subject is not a purified knower but an impossible eye.

The *sākṣi* is not vulnerable to Move 1. It is not an eye that sees from nowhere; it is the *condition of seeing as such*. Nietzsche's target is the empirical-transcendental subject *occupying* a view-from-nowhere position. The *sākṣi* does not occupy a position. Bhattacharyya's *Subject as Freedom*: the subject is not an object; it is what makes objects objects-for-something. Nietzsche, working only with the post-Kantian empirical-transcendental subject, has no place for this structural condition.

**Move 2, *es gibt nur ein perspektivisches Sehen*.** Perspectival seeing is *the only kind of seeing there is*. Anti-skeptical: perspective is the *form* of knowledge, not its disqualification. The thesis aligns strongly: every register, every *darśana*, every articulation is from-a-position.

**Move 3, objectivity as multiplicity.** Objectivity is not subtracting perspectives but adding them, *je mehr Augen, verschiedne Augen*. Structurally homologous to T4: each register is a real "eye"; more eyes give more complete recognition. *Anekāntavāda* and *syādvāda* articulate this in Indian register: from this side, in this respect, X; from that side, in that respect, Y; truth is the conjunction.

**Move 4, the ascetic paradox.** Eliminating the will would *castrate* the intellect. The will is the *condition* of knowing.

For the thesis, Move 4 has a delicate application. Vedāntic *vairāgya* is not elimination of affect but dis-identification of the witness from the affects. The witness *sees* affects without being driven by them, *liberation* of intellect, not castration. *Vṛtti* is *seen by* the *sākṣi*; the *sākṣi* is not a *vṛtti*. Nietzsche's target, the will-less subject of pure reason, would be a *vṛtti*-free subject; this is what he rightly says is impossible. The Vedāntic *sākṣi* is *vṛtti*-witnessing, structurally different.

#### Klossowski / Deleuze / Heidegger on GM III.12

**Klossowski** reads "more eyes" through the eternal return: each perspective is a simulacrum, a role played by the impersonal force; close to *līlā*, but with no positive ground.

**Deleuze** reads "more eyes" as differential production: the concept is a multiplicity, not a container. Closest to Aurobindo's *comprehending supermind*, the differential ground from which perspectives arise. Most thesis-compatible reading, with the caveat that Deleuze loses the personal dimension.

**Heidegger** reads perspectivism as still within metaphysics of subjectivity: "more eyes" remain a subject's eyes; perspectivism is the radicalization of subjectivism, not its overcoming. The thesis must take this seriously, the "more eyes" approach can degenerate into consumerist perspective-multiplication. Response: *vijñāna* is not another perspective but the structural ground holding all in non-destructive coexistence. Whether this constitutes genuine overcoming of subjectivity is the deepest unresolved question with Heidegger; Part VII returns.

### GM III.24 — Modern exhaustion and the ascetic ideal

**KSA 5, 395–399.**

GM III.24 is the diagnosis. The modern human is *exhausted*: not exhausted *by* truth but exhausted by a *reality* in which they no longer hear meaning. This is the structural exhaustion of the ascetic ideal in its scientific form.


*KSA 5, 395–399 (GM III §24)*

> "I show here at first only the most immediate consequence: the modern human is exhausted when he believes, in truth —; he is without force when he has 'known'., But here he deceives himself. He is not exhausted *in* truth: he is exhausted by a reality in which he no longer hears any meaning, in which he has ceased to be a member."

#### Explication

The modern human won the truth, but the truth pursued by the ascetic ideal was the truth of *what is the case in the disenchanted world*, stripped of meaning and belonging. The ascetic ideal succeeded; the cost of success is the exhaustion of the one who succeeds.

This maps onto McGilchrist's left-hemisphere usurpation: instrumental abstraction, parts-without-whole, mathematizable regularities-without-meaning. Gebser's deficient mental, Aurobindo's rational-mental plane held as terminal: same diagnosis in different vocabularies. *Erschöpft an einer Wirklichkeit, an der er keinen Sinn mehr hört*: the recovery is *Sinn-Hören*, *brahma-jñāna* (knowledge of Brahman as meaning-ground), Aurobindo's psychic discrimination, Gebser's *verition*.

### GM III.27 — *Selbstaufhebung des asketischen Ideals*

**KSA 5, 409–411.**

The will-to-truth, engine of the ascetic ideal, turns against the ascetic ideal itself. The genealogical method is will-to-truth turned back on its own origin.



*KSA 5, 409–411 (GM III §27)*

#### English

> "Rather: this saying-no and willing-no that comes at the end into science's hand, what does it mean, if there is a No and no Yes underneath it? With this, science loses its faith in itself: that is the last thing it achieves. [...] It, the *will to truth* gets a problem: *whence* does the will to truth come? [...] All great things perish through themselves, through an act of self-overcoming: so the law of life wills it, the law of necessary 'self-overcoming', the essence of life, it always finally comes to the lawgiver himself: 'patere legem, quam ipse tulisti' (endure the law that you yourself enacted)."

#### Explication

The will-to-truth, having served as engine of the ascetic ideal, turns against itself, asking *whence the will to truth*? Asked from within the will-to-truth, the question exposes it as a value with a history. The science that destroyed every faith must destroy its own faith.

Structurally this is the same moment Aurobindo describes when the rational-mental, reaching its terminus, forces transition to the supramental. The *self-overcoming* is not replacement by another ideal but recognition that the entire ideal-structure of the rational-mental plane is incomplete. Nietzsche names the opening; he does not name the successor. The thesis names what fills it: the integral-aperspectival, the supramental, the *vijñāna* state.

*Patere legem, quam ipse tulisti*, endure the law you yourself enacted, applies to Nietzsche's philosopher; it does not apply to the thesis's surrendered one, who has not enacted any law. Nietzsche's philosopher is the legislator who endures his own law; the thesis's surrender-figure participates in a lawful unfolding that is not the participant's law. Both refuse the metaphysical-transcendent legislator; Nietzsche fills the space with the immanent legislator, the thesis with the surrendered participant in divine *līlā*.

## Part IV — *Götzen-Dämmerung* (1888)

*KSA 6, 55–161. English base: Walter Kaufmann (Viking, 1954); R. J. Hollingdale (Penguin, 1968); Duncan Large (Oxford, 1998).*

*Götzen-Dämmerung, oder: Wie man mit dem Hammer philosophirt* (Twilight of the Idols, or How to Philosophize with a Hammer) was written in the summer of 1888 and published in early 1889, immediately before Nietzsche's collapse in Turin. It is the most compressed and combative of the late works, short sections, polemical edge, and aphoristic concentration of the doctrines worked out in *Jenseits* and *Genealogie*. For the user's thesis, two sections are decisive: "Die 'Vernunft' in der Philosophie" §5 (the grammar passage that Part I of the thesis foregrounds as the locus classicus of the anti-Hegel polemic), and "Wie die 'wahre Welt' endlich zur Fabel wurde" (the famous six-stage history of the abolition of the true world). The genealogical method developed in *GM* is here applied as method, not just as practice.

### "Die 'Vernunft' in der Philosophie" §5 — *wir werden Gott nicht los, weil wir noch an die Grammatik glauben*

**KSA 6, 77–78.**



*KSA 6, 77–78 (Götzen-Dämmerung, "Die 'Vernunft' in der Philosophie" §5)*

#### English

> "Language belongs in its origin to the period of the most rudimentary form of psychology: we land in a crude fetishism when we make conscious to ourselves the basic presuppositions of the metaphysics of language, in plain words: of reason. It sees doers and doings everywhere; it believes in will as cause in general; it believes in the 'I', in the I as Being, in the I as substance, and it projects the belief in the I-substance onto all things, it is only thereby that it creates the concept 'thing' ... Being is everywhere thought-in, smuggled-in, as cause; it is only out of the conception 'I' that there follows, as derivative, the concept 'Being' ... At the beginning stands the great calamitous error that the will is something that effects, that will is a faculty ... Today we know that it is only a word ... Much later, in a world a thousandfold more enlightened, the security, the subjective certainty in the handling of the categories of reason came to philosophers' consciousness with surprise: they concluded that these could not have come from the empirical, for the whole of empirical reality stood in contradiction to them. Where do they come from, then?, And in India as in Greece they made the same false move: 'we must have been at home in a higher world once before (— instead of in a much lower one: which would have been the truth!), we must have been divine, for we have reason!' ... In fact, nothing has had a more naïve power of persuasion than the error concerning Being, as for example it was formulated by the Eleatics: it has every word for itself, every sentence we speak!, Even the opponents of the Eleatics still succumbed to the seduction of their concept of Being: Democritus among others, when he invented his atom ... 'Reason' in language: oh what an old deceptive female! **I fear we are not getting rid of God because we still believe in grammar** ..."

#### Explication — the structural anti-Hegel passage

This is the locus classicus for the user's anti-Hegel polemic. Three moves.

(1) **Grammar is fossilized psychology.** Language was structured in the period of the most rudimentary psychology, when everything is read as agent-action. To philosophize in this language is to use a tool whose categories were forged for a primitive task. Grammar, on Nietzsche's claim, is *fetishism*: it treats linguistic categories (subject, predicate, copula) as ontological categories (substance, attribute, being).

(2) **The "I" is the source of "Being."** The conception "I" is the original from which "Being" is derived: *aus der Conception 'Ich' folgt erst, als abgeleitet, der Begriff 'Sein'*. Nietzsche reverses the standard story: we have self-as-substance and project it as Being onto everything else.

(3) **The Eleatic error.** The fundamental *Verhängniss* was the Eleatic move: *Being* as basic ontological category, generated by the grammatical fetishism of subject-substance. Even the atomists succumbed: their atoms are *Wechselbälge*, grammatical subjects masquerading as physical entities.

Conclusion: *Wir werden Gott nicht los, weil wir noch an die Grammatik glauben.* God here is not just the theological deity; God is the *grammatical structure of subject-substance* generalized to its limit. The death of God requires recognition that grammar is *not* ontology.

For the thesis, the polemical force is the structural critique of Hegel. Hegel's *Logik* is grammar systematized; the dialectical movement *Sein → Nichts → Werden → Daseyn* assumes conceptual articulation tracks ontological structure. The *Logik* is the most sustained commitment to grammar-as-ontology in the Western canon; §5 is its most concentrated demolition.

The thesis goes one step further. Grammar is not arbitrary; it reflects something real at a *limited* register, empirical individuation under *principium individuationis*. Subject-predicate structure is appropriate there and misleading only when extrapolated to the absolute. Consistent with GM III.12: grammar is one optic, useful at its level, mistaken when totalized.

#### Nietzsche's "in Indien wie in Griechenland"

The parenthetical refers to the Eleatic move and its Indian parallel, the *Sat* of Sanskrit, *ekam evādvitīyaṃ*, *Tat tvam asi*. Nietzsche treats Indian *Sat*-philosophy as the same grammatical fetishism as Greek *to on*. The Sanskrit *Sat* is grammatically present participle of *as-* and does carry substantive-of-being structure. But the *Sat* of *Saccidānanda* is not opposed to *Asat* as parallel to *to on* / *to mē on*; it is the being-aspect of a triple-aspected absolute whose other aspects are *Cit* and *Ānanda*, not properties but co-constitutive aspects of one absolute. The triple structure short-circuits Eleatic substance-metaphysics. Aurobindo gives the precise structure that escapes Nietzsche's critique; the thesis grants the point against Eleatic *to on* and Hegelian *Sein* and rejects its application to *Saccidānanda*.

#### The hammer image

The subtitle, *Wie man mit dem Hammer philosophirt*, is often misread as destructive. Nietzsche's preface describes the hammer as *Stimmgabel* (tuning fork): tap the idol; if hollow, hollow. Diagnostic, not destructive. The thesis's *darśana*-discrimination is structurally similar, testing each concept against its register. Nietzsche's hammer is wielded broadly, assuming all metaphysics is equally idolatrous; the *darśana* version is more discriminating, allowing some to stand within their proper level while exposing over-extension of others.

### Genealogy as method

*Götzen-Dämmerung*'s "Streifzüge" applies the genealogical method to figures (Rousseau, Carlyle, Emerson, Darwin, Wagner) and concepts (free will, altruism, "improvement"): take the current self-presentation; trace its psychological-historical origin; expose the gap.

The method is a permanent acquisition. Every concept in circulation, including the thesis's own (*Saccidānanda*, *vijñāna*, *bhakti*, *līlā*, *adhikāra*), has a genealogy. The thesis's structural-identity claims (Aurobindo's supermind ≈ Gebser's aperspectival-integral ≈ Deleuze's univocity ≈ McGilchrist's right-hemisphere knowing) must be defensible *genealogically*, not merely asserted. T2's self-correcting trajectory is itself a genealogical claim. The thesis cannot exempt itself from the scrutiny it applies to others.

### *Die fröhliche Wissenschaft* §341 — "The Greatest Weight"

Before *Zarathustra* turns eternal return into myth, *The Gay Science* §341 gives the doctrine its first canonical formulation as a thought-experiment. A demon tells you that this life, exactly as you now live it, will have to be lived again, innumerable times, with nothing new in it. The question is not first whether the doctrine is cosmologically true. The question is whether you can hear it without collapse. Nietzsche's own phrase for the demon's announcement is "das grösste Schwergewicht," the greatest weight: the weight of a recurrence that strips away every consolation of final escape, progress, or compensatory afterlife.

This section matters because it clarifies the order of dependence in Nietzsche. The existential test comes first. *Zarathustra*'s later images of gateway, circle, and convalescence elaborate what §341 has already posed in naked form: can this life be affirmed so completely that its exact recurrence would be welcome rather than intolerable? For the user's project this makes the doctrine more usable. Even if literal recurrence is rejected at the cosmological level, the ethical force of §341 remains fully intact. It is the strongest Western test for whether action has been purified of recoil, resentment, and the wish to have been elsewhere.

Read this against *niṣkāma karma*. The point is not resignation. It is purified affirmation. An act done without ego-claim is exactly the kind of act that could be willed again. An act done from domination, self-protection, or hidden revenge immediately fails the test. §341 therefore belongs beside GT §24 and the later Zarathustra passages: it is the bridge between Nietzsche's early tragic metaphysics and his later ethics of affirmation.

## Part V — *Also sprach Zarathustra* (1883–1885)

*KSA 4. English: Walter Kaufmann (Viking, 1954); Adrian Del Caro (Cambridge UP, 2006). Citations by part and section title.*

*Also sprach Zarathustra. Ein Buch für Alle und Keinen* is Nietzsche's most stylized work and the one whose interpretive challenges are greatest. It is composed in four parts (I, II, III published 1883–1884; IV privately printed 1885) in the form of a quasi-biblical prose-poem. The protagonist Zarathustra descends from his mountain solitude after ten years to teach the *Übermensch*, encounters various human types representing the *letzter Mensch* and other modern psychological structures, suffers and learns and re-articulates his teaching, and finally affirms, in Part III, the philosophical heart of the work, the doctrine of the *ewige Wiederkunft des Gleichen*.

For the user's thesis, four passages are structurally decisive: the *Drei Verwandlungen* (Three Metamorphoses) at the opening of Part I, which articulates the developmental sequence from camel to lion to child; *Vom Gesicht und Räthsel* (On the Vision and the Riddle) in Part III, with its image of the gateway *Augenblick* and the question whether all things must already have been; *Der Genesende* (The Convalescent), also in Part III, with the animals' formula of the eternal return and Zarathustra's response; and the elaborated doctrine of *ewige Wiederkunft* across these passages, read against Deleuze's interpretation as the return of difference.

This Part takes the four passages in turn.

### *Die drei Verwandlungen* — Camel, Lion, Child

**KSA 4, 29–31. Z I, "Die drei Verwandlungen".**

Zarathustra's opening pedagogical speech describes three metamorphoses of the spirit. The camel is the spirit of *bearing*: it loads itself with the heaviest weights, the inherited values, the *Du-sollst* (Thou-shalt), the moral obligations, and bears them into the desert. The lion is the spirit of *negation*: in the desert, the camel becomes a lion who challenges the great dragon "*Du-sollst*" and asserts the *Ich-will* (I-will), the lion creates *freedom*, but cannot create *new values*. Only the child can create new values. The child is the spirit of *innocence*, *forgetting*, *new beginning*, the *aus sich rollendes Rad* (self-rolling wheel), the *heiliges Ja* (holy Yes-saying).




*KSA 4, 29–31 (Z I, "Von den drei Verwandlungen")*

#### English

> "But in the loneliest desert the second metamorphosis occurs: here the spirit becomes a lion, freedom it would seize as prey, and be master in its own desert. [...] *Creating new values*, the lion cannot do that either: but to create itself freedom for new creating, that the lion's power can do."

> "But tell me, my brothers, what is the child still able to do that the lion is not able to do? Why must the predatory lion also become a child? Innocence is the child, and forgetting, a new beginning, a play, a self-rolling wheel, a first movement, a holy Yes-saying."

#### Explication

The three metamorphoses are a developmental sequence, but not, despite occasional Anglo-American readings, a Hegelian dialectical sequence. They are three *modes* of relation to value: bearing, negating, creating. The camel cannot create because it is loaded with already-existing values. The lion cannot create because its mode is negation, *Ich will*, but always *against* the *Du sollst*. Only the child can create, because the child has *forgotten* the inherited values and so is not in reactive relation to them; the child plays, and the play *is* the new beginning.

For the thesis, the sequence relates structurally to *darśana* developmental schemes. Camel ≈ disciple internalizing the tradition's burdens (*śravaṇa, manana*). Lion ≈ *neti-neti* practitioner negating every limited identification. Child ≈ *jīvanmukta* (liberated-while-living) living in spontaneous recognition. Aurobindo's supramental being similarly involves a *forgetting* of burdened mental constructions, as *sahaja* spontaneity, not ignorance, and a new beginning without mediation of *Du sollst*.

But a structural divergence at the lion. Nietzsche's lion creates *freedom* by negating *Du sollst*, political-existential, reactive, *standing-against*. The Vedāntic *neti-neti* creates *liberation*, ontological, recognitive, *seeing-through* without standing-against. This shows in the child: Nietzsche's child is post-negation as the lion's sublimation; the *jīvanmukta* is post-negation by *recognition* of misidentification, not by negation of *Du sollst*. The *jīvanmukta*'s play is not creation-by-fiat (cf. §211) but spontaneous expression of what was always the case. The thesis's child is structurally closer to Aurobindo's *caitya puruṣa*, innocent (*anāgrahin*), playful (*līlā*-participating), Yes-saying (integral affirmation). The overlap with Nietzsche's child is significant; the genealogy differs.

### *Vom Gesicht und Räthsel* — The Gateway *Augenblick*

**KSA 4, 197–202. Z III, "Vom Gesicht und Räthsel".**

Part III is the philosophical core of *Zarathustra*. *Vom Gesicht und Räthsel* contains the first explicit articulation of the eternal return, in the form of a vision Zarathustra recounts to the sailors on board ship. The vision is the gateway *Augenblick*: two paths, one stretching back into eternity, one stretching forward into eternity, meet at a gateway named "*Augenblick*" (Moment). Zarathustra puts to the dwarf (the *Geist der Schwere*, the Spirit of Heaviness) the question: must not all things that can run, have already run this lane?




*KSA 4, 198–200 (Z III, "Vom Gesicht und Räthsel")*

#### English (Del Caro)

> "'Behold this gateway, dwarf!' I continued, 'it has two faces. Two paths come together here; no one has yet gone them to their end. This long lane back: it lasts an eternity. And that long lane out there, that is another eternity. They contradict each other, these paths; they butt their heads against each other:, and it is here, at this gateway, that they come together. The name of the gateway is written above: "Moment".'"

> "'Must not what *can* run from all things have already once run through this lane? Must not what *can* happen from all things have already once happened, been done, run past? [...] And this slow spider that creeps in the moonlight, and this moonlight itself, and you and I in the gateway, whispering together, whispering of eternal things, must we not all have already been here?'"

#### Explication

The gateway is the *Augenblick*. Two infinite lanes meet. If time is infinite and configurations are finite, every configuration must have recurred, including this moment, this spider, this conversation.

The argument is existential, not cosmological-scientific: *can you affirm this moment so completely that you can will its eternal return?* An *Affirmationskriterium*: *amor fati* in strongest form.

Two readings. **Exoteric**: same world recurs identically. **Esoteric (Deleuze)**: what returns is *difference*, the productive force of differential becoming. Zarathustra distances himself from the animals' simplification.

For the thesis, the Deleuzean reading is structurally compatible with Aurobindonian cosmology: literal identical recurrence would conflict with genuine novelty. The literal version remains as the *existential test*. The *līlā* tradition articulates the same: each iteration is the same (same *Saccidānanda*) and different (novel). The gateway is the *vijñāna*-moment where static and dynamic aspects of *Saccidānanda* are held simultaneously.

### *Der Genesende* — The Convalescent

**KSA 4, 270–277.**

The climactic confrontation with eternal return. Zarathustra summons the abyss-thought, confronts it, and is convalescing when his animals (eagle and serpent) recite the doctrine in simplified form.



*KSA 4, 272–273 (Z III, "Der Genesende")*



*KSA 4, 276 (Z III, "Der Genesende")*

#### English

> "'All things go, all things come back; eternally rolls the wheel of being. All things die, all things blossom again, eternally runs the year of being. All things break, all things are joined anew; eternally builds itself the same house of being. All things part, all things greet each other again; eternally remains faithful to itself the ring of being.'"

> "'I come again, with this sun, with this earth, with this eagle, with this serpent, *not* to a new life or a better life or a similar life:, I come back eternally to this same, selfsame life, in the greatest things and in the smallest, to teach again the eternal recurrence of all things.'"

#### Explication

The animals recite the doctrine as sing-song: the wheel, the year, the house, the ring of being. Zarathustra calls them *Schalks-Narren und Drehorgeln*, they have made the doctrine into a tune.

Zarathustra's own affirmation, *ich komme ewig wieder zu diesem gleichen und selbigen Leben*, with emphasis on *im Grössten und auch im Kleinsten*, is the strong literal formula, presented as his own articulation, not the animals' simplification. The Deleuzean reading must therefore work harder: it reads *gleichen und selbigen* as affirmation of *this* life as worthy of being lived again, the existential test in strongest *amor fati* form.

For the thesis, three readings of the relation to Aurobindonian evolutionary cosmology. **Way 1**: existential thought-experiment, not cosmological doctrine. **Way 2**: different levels, each *manvantara* a novel supramental disclosure; the same divine returns in different configurations. **Way 3**: Deleuzean return-of-difference. The thesis synthesizes 2 and 3: the divine returns *as differential play*, each iteration novel within the eternal pattern.

### The eternal return: doctrinal status and the thesis

The doctrine has three dimensions. (1) The *cosmological* claim (literal recurrence), taken literally, is incompatible with the thesis's evolutionary commitment: cyclic time yes, literal identity of cycles no. (2) The *existential* test (could one affirm this moment so completely that one would will its eternal return?) is fully compatible: *amor fati* as the negative form of *līlā*-affirmation, converging with *niṣkāma karma*. (3) The *ontological* structure (relation of *Werden* and *Sein*) is articulated in the Nachlass at KSA 13, 14[152], *dem Werden den Charakter des Seins aufprägen*, and treated in Part VI. The supramental is the level at which becoming and being are non-different; the eternal return is Nietzsche's cosmological-imagistic formulation of what *Saccidānanda* (intrinsically static-and-dynamic) articulates ontologically.

### The closing dance: Zarathustra's seven seals

Part III's final section, "Die sieben Siegel (oder: das Ja- und Amen-Lied)," is the most affirmative passage in *Zarathustra*. Each of the seven sections ends with the refrain: *denn ich liebe dich, oh Ewigkeit!* (for I love you, O eternity!). The refrain is the *amor fati* in lyrical form. Eternity is loved, and "this same, selfsame life" is loved with it.

For the thesis, this refrain is structurally homologous to *bhakti*: the divine loved in its eternal *and* temporal aspects, in its identity-with-itself *and* its self-multiplying play. The Vaiṣṇava *premā parā-bhakti*, supreme devotion that is constitutive love, articulates the same in Indian register. Zarathustra's *ich liebe dich, oh Ewigkeit* is, in compressed form, *brahmāsvāda* (the savor of Brahman), love of the eternal that *is* the eternal's self-savoring through the lover. This is also where Nietzsche's atheism is most pressed: the eternity loved is not a personal divine but the eternity of becoming itself. The thesis's reading: Nietzsche's *Ewigkeit* is structurally the eternity-aspect of *Saccidānanda*; his refusal to name it personally is consistent with his rejection of the metaphysical-theological God, but the structural function it plays is what the thesis names as *Saccidānanda* in its *Sat*-aspect, loved through *Ānanda*-participation.

## Part VI — *Wille zur Macht* (Nachlass) — with editorial caveat

### The editorial caveat (read first; restated)

*Der Wille zur Macht. Versuch einer Umwerthung aller Werthe* (1901; expanded 1906) is a posthumous editorial assembly by Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche and Peter Gast, drawn from Nietzsche's notebooks of 1883–1888. The "WzM" arrangement is *not* Nietzsche's; the "Hauptwerk" status assigned to it is a forgery. Elisabeth was an active anti-Semite and later a Nazi sympathizer; her editorial program was ideologically motivated; she altered, suppressed, and re-ordered fragments to produce a "great work" that served purposes Nietzsche himself rejected (see *JGB* §251, *Ecce Homo*'s "Warum ich so weise bin" §3, the late letters to Overbeck).

The authoritative edition is the *Kritische Studienausgabe* (KSA), Colli/Montinari, de Gruyter / dtv, 1980 (rev. 1988), which publishes the Nachlass in original notebook order without the WzM frame. **All citations in this Part are by KSA volume + notebook + fragment number**, *KSA 12, 7[60]*, never by *"WzM §481."* Cross-reference WzM section numbers parenthetically only when engaging Heidegger or older Kaufmann translations whose argument depends on the WzM arrangement.

Why use the Nachlass at all? Three reasons. (a) The philosophical content is genuinely Nietzsche's. (b) Heidegger's two-volume *Nietzsche* (1961) is built substantially on these materials; engaging Heidegger requires them. (c) Deleuze's *Nietzsche et la philosophie* (1962) reads them as philosophically central. The fragments are unavoidable; they must be used with the editorial caveat foregrounded and with KSA citations.

### KSA 12, 7[60] — *Tatsachen gibt es nicht, nur Interpretationen*

**KSA 12, 315. Nachlass 1886–87. Cf. WzM §481.**

This is the fragment most commonly misattributed to *Jenseits* §22. The §22 argument is more careful: physics gives interpretation, not text; and even this claim is interpretation (cf. Part II). The 7[60] fragment states the perspectivism thesis more starkly.



*KSA 12, 315 (Fragment 7[60])*

#### English

> "Against positivism, which halts at phenomena, 'there are only facts', I would say: no, facts are precisely what there is not, only interpretations. We cannot establish any fact 'in itself': perhaps it is folly to want such a thing. 'Everything is subjective,' you say; but even this is interpretation; the 'subject' is nothing given but something additionally invented, posited behind., Is it in the end necessary still to posit an interpreter behind the interpretation? Already that is fiction, hypothesis. So far as the word 'knowledge' makes any sense at all, the world is knowable: but it is interpretable otherwise, it has no sense behind it, but *countless senses*., 'Perspectivism.' Our needs are what interpret the world; our drives and their pros and cons. Every drive is a kind of desire for dominion; each has its perspective, which it would like to impose as a norm on all the other drives."

#### Explication — four moves

**(1)** Against positivism's "only facts": no, only interpretations. Not idealism (the world is mind-dependent) but perspectivism: there is no fact that is not already an interpretation by a perspective.

**(2)** "Everything is subjective" is also an interpretation. The subject is not a given but a fiction posited behind the process. (Parallel to GM I.13 and JGB §16.)

**(3)** The world has *unzählige Sinne*, countless senses. Not one true sense; not no sense; an inexhaustible plurality.

**(4)** Our needs, drives, and their pro-and-contra are what interpret. Every drive is *Herrschsucht*, desire for dominion, with its own perspective seeking to impose itself as norm.

For the thesis, the philosophically decisive phrase is *unzählige Sinne*. This is the strongest available Western articulation of what the *darśana* tradition calls *anekāntavāda*, many-sided-ness of the real. Each *darśana* register is a real "sense" of the absolute; the absolute has not "one true sense behind it" (which would be the Vedāntic-Śaṅkarian over-totalization the thesis rejects via T2) but countless senses, each a genuine register, none reducible to another.

But the fragment also presents the methodologically problematic move: *unsere Bedürfnisse*, our needs, are what interpret. The plurality of senses is grounded in the plurality of biological-psychological needs. This is naturalistic-reductive in a way that the thesis cannot accept. The thesis's plurality is grounded in the divine's *svātantrya*-driven self-differentiation, not in competing biological drives. Nietzsche grounds perspectivism in biology; the thesis grounds it in ontology.

The Jain *anekāntavāda* parallel is structurally important: Jain philosophy explicitly denies that the plurality of senses is reducible to subjective-pragmatic needs; the plurality is *in the object*, in the many-aspected nature of the real. *Syādvāda*, qualified assertion, articulates how each predication is true *from a side, in a respect*: not relativism, not single-sense, but real plurality of real aspects. The thesis is closer to *anekāntavāda* than to KSA 12, 7[60]'s naturalistic ground; but Nietzsche's fragment is the strongest *demolition* of the one-sense view that *anekāntavāda* presupposes for its dialectical purchase.

### KSA 12, 9[35] — Interpretation as form of *Wille zur Macht*

**KSA 12, 351–352. Nachlass 1887. Cf. WzM §556.**



*KSA 12, 351–352 (Fragment 9[35])*

#### English

> "Interpretation itself, as a form of *Wille zur Macht*, has existence in the organic, not as 'knowledge' but as schematizing, as imposing as much regularity and form as our practical needs demand. [...] There is no 'knowing'; only a perspectivizing and approximating; no 'knowledge in itself', no unconditional knowing. Knowing itself is already a form of *Herrschaft* (mastery, dominion)."

#### Explication

9[35] extends 7[60] by identifying interpretation with *Wille zur Macht* in the organic. *Schematisiren*, schematizing, is Kant's term for how categories are applied to intuition; Nietzsche biologizes it. Schema-imposition is what organisms do to survive. *Erkennen ist bereits eine Form der Herrschaft*, knowing is already a form of dominion.

For the thesis, this is the strongest Nietzschean articulation of what T8 identifies as *pramāṇa-caitanya*. *Pramāṇa-consciousness* is the cognitive-bullying mode that demands mastery: to know is to dominate. Nietzsche correctly identifies this as the structural character of organic-biological knowing. The thesis adds: there is also *sākṣi-caitanya*, witness consciousness, which is *not* a form of mastery, which knows by *being permeable* to what is, rather than by imposing form. Nietzsche has no analogous mode in his philosophical apparatus; he treats organic knowing as exhaustive of knowing.

Aurobindo's distinction between *mental knowing* (which works by separation, schematization, conceptual imposition, and which is what Nietzsche correctly diagnoses) and *supramental knowing* (which works by identity, by being-the-known) is the precise technical articulation of what the thesis must add to 9[35]. The thesis grants 9[35]'s diagnosis at the mental-organic level and articulates the supramental level as structurally different.

### KSA 13, 14[152] — *Dem Werden den Charakter des Seins aufprägen*

**KSA 13, 374. Nachlass 1888. Cf. WzM §617.**



*KSA 13, 374 (Fragment 14[152])* **[VERIFY KSA location.]**

#### English

> "To stamp becoming with the character of being, that is the highest *Wille zur Macht*. [...] That everything recurs is the most extreme approximation of a world of becoming to a world of being: the height of contemplation."

#### Explication

14[152] explicitly bridges *ewige Wiederkunft* and *Wille zur Macht*, and *Werden* and *Sein*. *Dem Werden den Charakter des Seins aufprägen* is structurally what Aurobindo names as the *comprehending supermind*: the poise that holds becoming (*Śakti*, process) under the aspect of being (*Sat*, the timeless ground).

The eternal return is Nietzsche's cosmological formulation: recurrence stamps becoming with being. Aurobindo's articulation differs: becoming *is* being in its dynamic aspect, not because it recurs but because *Saccidānanda* is intrinsically static-and-dynamic, the constitutive non-difference of *Sat* and *Cit-Śakti*. Prigogine on irreversible becoming and Whitehead's process metaphysics provide independent Western analogs. For the thesis, eternal return is Nietzsche's *cosmological-imagistic* approximation of what *Saccidānanda* articulates ontologically.

### KSA 13, 14[184] — Second version of *Tatsachen gibt es nicht*

**KSA 13, 360–361. Nachlass 1888. Cf. WzM §715.**



*KSA 13, 360 (Fragment 14[184])*

#### English

> "No, facts are precisely what there is not, only interpretations. We cannot establish any fact 'in itself': perhaps it is folly to want such a thing. 'Everything is subjective,' you say; but that too is already interpretation. The 'subject' is nothing given, but something additionally invented, posited behind., Is it finally necessary still to posit an interpreter behind the interpretation? This condensed version ends with the one-word summary "*Perspektivismus.*" Cite it together with 7[60] as a sustained late-notebook position, not an isolated remark.

### Heidegger, Deleuze, Klossowski (compressed)

**Heidegger** (*Nietzsche*, 1961) reads the Nachlass as Nietzsche's *completion* of metaphysics: *Wille zur Macht* = *essentia*; *ewige Wiederkunft* = *existentia*; together "the last metaphysics." Perspectivism (KSA 12, 7[60]) is still within the metaphysics of subjectivity, the interpreting drive is a subject, even if biological. Nietzsche radicalizes Kant rather than overcoming him. For the thesis: if Heidegger is right, Nietzsche's perspectivism does not escape correlationist closure. The thesis goes beyond via Nyāya's *paratah-prāmāṇya*, Aurobindo's knowledge by identity, and Bhattacharyya's literal/symbolic distinction.

**Deleuze** (*Nietzsche et la philosophie*, 1962; *Différence et répétition*, 1968) reads the opposite: Nietzsche overcomes metaphysics by de-substantializing force. *Wille zur Macht* is pure differential force, difference in itself. Eternal return brings back difference, not the Same. Most thesis-compatible: *return of difference* matches Aurobindonian evolutionary cosmology; differential production matches divine *līlā*. Cost: Deleuze loses the personal dimension. There is no *caitya puruṣa* in Deleuze. The thesis takes the structural ontology and adds the personal dimension via the psychic-being doctrine.

**Klossowski** (*Nietzsche et le cercle vicieux*, 1969) reads the eternal return as lived experience: dissolution of "Ich" and replacement by *all the names of history* (the Turin note). The philosopher plays all roles, maps directly onto *līlā*. Danger: no positive ground; the eternal return is "vicious" precisely because foundationless. Klossowski reads Turin as the philosopher *literally becoming* the eternal return without stabilizing ground. The failure-mode the *vijñāna* state avoids: ego-dissolution does not require psychic-being dissolution.

### The Aurobindo connection

Nietzsche gives the critique: no perspective among perspectives becomes the view from nowhere. Aurobindo gives the positive ontology: the comprehending supermind is not another perspective but the through-all in which perspectives arise as expressions. *Jñāna by identity* names that shift.

## Part VII — Reading Notes

### Where Nietzsche confirms

**(a) Perspectivism as anti-super-perspective.** GM III.12, KSA 12, 7[60], KSA 13, 14[184]. Any claim of "the one true sense" is *Begriffs-Fabelei* positing an impossible eye. GM III.12 gives formula: *je mehr Augen, verschiedne Augen wir uns für dieselbe Sache einzusetzen wissen, um so vollständiger wird unser "Begriff" dieser Sache, unsre "Objektivität" sein*. Objectivity is *richer* perspective, not its elimination.

**(b) Eternal return as return-of-difference.** Z III "Der Genesende"; KSA 13, 14[152]. Deleuze's reading, affirmation of difference itself, is structurally compatible with Aurobindonian evolutionary cosmology: cyclic time yes, identity of cycles no. Each *manvantara* a novel supramental disclosure under the eternal pattern.

**(c) The *Schleier der Maja* and the *Ur-Eine*.** GT §1, §16, §17, §24. Closest Western articulation of *līlā*: the Will's *künstlerisches Spiel mit sich selbst* (§24); *Urlust selbst am Schmerz percipirt* (§17); *glücklich-Lebendigen, nicht als Individuen, sondern als das eine Lebendige* (§17).

**(d) The deconstruction of subject-substance grammar.** *Götzen-Dämmerung* §5, JGB §16, GM I.13. The most concentrated Western critique of the grammatical fetishism culminating in Hegel's *Logik*: *Ich fürchte, wir werden Gott nicht los, weil wir noch an die Grammatik glauben*.

**(e) The self-overcoming of the ascetic ideal.** GM III.27. The will-to-truth turns against itself; *all great things perish through self-overcoming*. Nietzsche names the opening; the thesis names what fills it (the integral-aperspectival, the supramental, the *vijñāna* state).

**(f) The genealogical method.** Apply to thesis.

### Where Nietzsche challenges

**(g) Impersonalism.** GT §16's *Ur-Eine*, *Wille zur Macht* in §259, the eternal return, all non-personal. No Nietzschean analog to *caitya puruṣa* or Vaiṣṇava *jīva* theology. Response: the ego-as-grammatical-projection is correctly dissolved (GM I.13); what remains is the psychic being, not a fictional substantive subject but the center of experiential coherence surviving ego-dissolution. Aurobindo's *ahaṃkāra* / *caitya puruṣa* distinction gives the technical resource.

**(h) The master-philosopher / legislator-stance.** JGB §211; the *Übermensch*. Structurally opposed to the surrender-philosopher; T10 and T8 forbid the legislator-stance. Take the Deleuzean de-personalization of *Wille zur Macht* (drains imperatival content) and reject §211's rhetoric. The thesis takes a stand *against* Nietzsche here.

**(i) Naturalistic-biological reduction of perspectivism.** KSA 12, 7[60] grounds plurality in biological-psychological needs. The thesis grounds plurality in the divine's *svātantrya*-driven self-differentiation. Response via Jain *anekāntavāda*: plurality in the object, ontological before epistemological.

**(j) Non-teleological *Wille zur Macht*.** §259: life grows because it lives, not toward any goal. Correct against externally-imposed goals; but the dynamism has internal direction, what Aurobindo names supramental unfolding.

### The bilateral-vs-tripartite asymmetry

The most important structural point about the GT engagement.

*Geburt der Tragödie*'s ontology is **bilateral**: two *Kunsttriebe* (Apollinian / Dionysian), with periodic reconciliation in tragedy. The structure is *two-and-their-relation*. Aurobindo's supramental ontology is **tripartite**: comprehending / apprehending / projecting, three *poises* of the same supermind, not three different things. Comprehending: the supramental holds all simultaneously, in the through-all of all registers. Apprehending: focuses on a particular while still holding awareness of the all. Projecting: issues forth in the actual individual form.

The mapping:

| GT structure | Supramental structure |
|---|---|
| Apollinian (*principium individuationis*) | Apprehending + Projecting |
| Dionysian (*Ur-Eine*, dissolution) | Comprehending — *partially* |
| Tragedy as periodic reconciliation | Sustained simultaneity of all three poises |

The asymmetry: GT's Dionysian is the *dissolution* of individuation into the *Ur-Eine*. The Aurobindonian comprehending poise is *not* dissolution but *non-destructive simultaneous holding* of individuation and the through-all. The Dionysian moment is *temporary* (one falls into the Ur-Eine, then returns); the comprehending poise is *sustained* (the supramental being lives continuously in the through-all without losing the individual center).

GT's bilateral structure is what one would expect from a Schopenhauerian framework, where the *Ur-Eine* is the thing-in-itself behind the appearance. The Aurobindonian tripartite structure requires rejecting the appearance/ground dualism: one divine, three poises, all three equally divine and constitutive. The mature Nietzsche, having shed the Schopenhauerian dualism, moves toward a single dynamic differential force (with eternal return as cosmological structure), but does not articulate the tripartite structure. The Aurobindonian threefoldness has no precise analog in Nietzsche. The conceptual gap is real and must be filled by Aurobindo, not by Nietzsche.
