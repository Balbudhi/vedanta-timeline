# K. C. Bhattacharyya, the Kantian critique, and the Jaina-realist alternative to Hegelian sublation
*Reader's Orientation; I. KCB on Hegel; II. KCB and evolutionary metaphysics; III. Aurobindo on Kant; IV. Aurobindo and perspectivist objection; V. The Jain doctrine and its Vedāntic reception; VI. Being and becoming as orthogonal; VII. What KCB does not give; VIII. Reading notes.*
This essay is written for a very specific philosophical pressure-point.
The pressure-point is not merely "Hegel versus Kant," nor merely "KCB versus Aurobindo."
It is the user's insistence that being and becoming should not be collapsed.
Being, on this view, names identity.
Becoming names modality.
The two are not enemies.
But neither are they convertible.
The temptation resisted here is a familiar one.
Once one grants that reality is dynamic, differentiated, and expressive, one is strongly tempted to say that the ultimate must be becoming itself, or at least that being must be understood only as self-becoming.
That is the Hegelian temptation in its purest form.
It appears in the opening of the *Logic*, where pure being, pure nothing, and becoming are made to pass into one another.
It reappears in more spiritualized forms wherever manifestation, evolution, or self-expression is taken to be the privileged grammar of the Absolute.
The user's refusal is different.
The refusal is not a retreat into static substance.
It is not the claim that becoming is unreal.
It is the claim that becoming does not have the right to define being.
The question, then, is whether there is a serious philosophical defense of that refusal.
K. C. Bhattacharyya is one of the best places to look.
He is not useful because he offers a ready-made doctrine in the user's exact terms.
He does not.
He is useful because he gives a powerful critique of Hegelian totalization, a deep Kantian suspicion of the illicit objectification of what exceeds object-thought, and a remarkable recovery of the Jaina doctrine of *anekānta* as a realism of alternative truths.
That last point matters most.
If KCB can recover the Jain doctrine without accepting contradiction as truth, then he gives a conceptual space in which plurality, identity, and mode can all be real without being forced into Hegelian sublation.
The dramatis personae are four.
First, KCB himself: transcendental philosopher, Vedāntic thinker, critic of totalization, philosopher of the subject as freedom.
Second, Hegel: the thinker for whom the Real is not only self-identical but self-developing, and whose dialectic constantly presses toward higher unities.
Third, Aurobindo: not a Hegelian in any simple sense, but a philosopher of real involution and evolution, of Supermind, and of the transformation of nature.
Fourth, the Jaina tradition: above all Umāsvāti, Kundakunda, Akalaṅka, and the later theory of *syādvāda*, because this is where the grammar of persistence-and-mode is philosophically explicit.
The article's thesis is deliberately narrow.
KCB gives a strong explicit critique of Hegelian sublation.
KCB does not give an explicit printed critique of Aurobindo in the materials on disk.
The anti-Aurobindo section must therefore be a structural reconstruction.
Aurobindo, for his part, does not substantially engage Kant by name in the primary materials on disk.
His answer to Kant must likewise be reconstructed from his own account of Mind, Supermind, and knowledge by identity.
The central positive claim defended here is the user's own.
Being and becoming are orthogonal.
Being concerns identity.
Becoming concerns modality.
The Hegelian error is to collapse them, as if the modality of transition could generate identity as such, or as if identity were intrinsically incomplete unless it dialectically became itself.
The Jain doctrine gives the cleanest Indian formal analogue.
A real has *dravya*, persistent identity.
That same real has *paryāya*, modal transformation.
Origination, cessation, and persistence are co-instantiated without requiring that identity be abolished into becoming or that becoming be dismissed as mere illusion. [cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/guna-paryayavat-dravyam] [cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/tad-bhavah-parinamah]
Kant adds the decisive negative clarification.
At *KrV* A598/B626, existence is not treated as a real predicate added to a concept.
Modality does not generate essence.
Applied here, the point is simple.
Becoming, taken modally, cannot by itself yield being, taken identitatively.
That is why the user's refusal is not merely psychological resistance to Hegel.
It is a serious categorial objection.
The plan is straightforward.
First, KCB on Hegel.
Second, KCB's implicit critique of evolutionary metaphysics, including Aurobindo.
Third, Aurobindo's likely answer to Kantian limits.
Fourth, the Jain doctrine and the Vedāntic objections to it.
Fifth, the orthogonality argument stated as clearly as possible.
Finally, a note on what KCB does not by himself deliver, and where the user's position goes further.
## I. KCB on Hegel
KCB's relation to Hegel is serious enough that caricature is useless.
He is not a routine anti-Hegelian.
He learns from Hegel.
But he learns from him in order to refuse what Hegel most wants to secure: the right of dialectic to totalize the Real.
The most accessible explicit statement of the issue comes through the line preserved in George B. Burch's reconstruction of Bhattacharyya.
Hegelian logic, KCB says, "does not admit the indefinite but holds instead that dialectic necessity is creative" (*Studies in Philosophy* II, p. 211, quoted in Burch).
This is already an exact diagnosis.
The problem is not movement.
The problem is the claim that movement is self-authorizing.
Dialectic, in Hegel, is not merely a method of exposing tensions in thought.
It becomes the very productivity of reality.
KCB denies that this productivity can be closed in principle.
Hence the second line.
The inability actually to create new categories is an "implicit admission of the indefinite" (*Studies* II, p. 231, quoted in Burch).
The indefinite is the point.
If dialectic must always already know the space of possible determinations it can generate, then it is not creative in the strong ontological sense Hegel needs.
If it does not already know that space, then the indefinite remains irreducible to dialectical necessity.
The anti-Hegelian force is not that transition is unreal.
It is that the very fact of transition does not license closure.
This same thought appears in an earlier and sharper line.
Dialectical thought, KCB says, "necessary as it is, has the alternative of absolute scepticism beyond it" (*Studies* I, p. 53, quoted in Burch).
The sentence is unusually important.
It does not reject dialectic.
It deprives it of ultimacy.
The possibility of a beyond that dialectic cannot absorb means that the movement from one determination to another is not the final authority on what reality must be.
At this point the connection with the user's position becomes visible.
If Hegel says that pure being, because indeterminate, is indistinguishable from pure nothing and therefore passes into becoming, KCB's style of objection is not mainly textual but logical.
Why should the instability of an indeterminate concept legislate the ontological priority of becoming?
Why should the dialectical poverty of a concept of pure being entail that being itself is essentially self-becoming?
That is exactly the sort of move the user hears as the overreach of ontologized grammar.
KCB's most direct anti-Hegelian argument, however, comes in the Jaina essay in *Studies in Philosophy* I.
There he defines the Jaina theory of *anekānta* as "a form of realism which not only asserts a plurality of determinate truths but also takes each truth to be an indetermination of alternative truths" (*Studies* I, p. 331).
This sounds at first glance like a celebration of plurality alone.
It is stronger than that.
Each determinate truth remains bordered by alternative truths that are not reducible to it.
The determinate never becomes sovereign.
There is always an alternative space not canceled by the truth that happens to be in view.
Hegel's dialectic is almost the inverse.
For Hegel, a determination carries within itself the tension that necessitates its supersession.
The alternative is internalized as contradiction and then aufgehoben.
For KCB, by contrast, alternatives are not always internal contradictions awaiting resolution.
They may be genuinely alternative truth-spaces.
That is why his language of "alternative truths" matters so much (*Studies* I, p. 342).
It means that not every multiplicity is a mere unfinished unity.
The anti-Hegelian point becomes explicit a few pages later.
KCB argues that the Hegelian absolute identity must become "at once a relation and a term" (*Studies* I, p. 334).
That is the key sentence for the user's objection.
If the absolute is sheer identity, it is a term.
If it must generate all relations out of itself, it becomes relation.
If it is both in the same breath, then identity has been loaded with a relational or modal function that does not belong to identity as such.
This is exactly the kind of collapse the user wants to resist.
To say that the absolute is real as identity is one thing.
To say that it must therefore also be the dynamic relation by which all differences mediate themselves is another.
KCB's complaint is that Hegel illicitly conflates the two demands.
The force of the complaint can be made even sharper.
A relation is not just one more term.
Nor is a term just a frozen relation.
Once identity is forced to do the work of relation, and relation the work of identity, the logical registers have been crossed.
That is why KCB's critique is not merely a preference for plurality over unity.
It is a protest against mislocated functions.
The late doctrine of alternative absolutes confirms the same orientation.
KCB will say that the absolute is either truth or freedom or value, but not all at once in one reflective act (*Studies* II, p. 143, via Burch).
This line is often cited as if it were just pluralism.
It is more radical and more relevant here.
The reflective demand to hold all absolutes simultaneously in a single mastered synthesis is precisely what KCB denies.
He does not deny that truth, freedom, and value are all absolute.
He denies that reflection has the right to collapse them into one captured identity.
This matters because Hegel's strongest claim is always that the higher unity is not abstract but concrete.
He will insist that he does not erase difference.
He preserves it as a moment.
KCB's counterpoint is that moment-language may itself be the problem.
To preserve a truth as a moment is already to have subordinated it.
To call it an alternative truth is to refuse that subordination.
This is why the user's instinct is better stated in KCB's terms as a critique of totalization than as a blanket critique of movement.
KCB is not a philosopher of dead stasis.
He is a philosopher of irreducible alternatives, of the indefinite, and of distinct logical functions that do not all collapse into one highest articulation.
The phrase "ontologized grammar" can now be given philosophical content.
The complaint is not that Hegel uses grammar.
Every philosopher does.
The complaint is that Hegel promotes the transition-patterns of thought into necessary structures of reality.
The poverty of an indeterminate concept of being becomes an ontological story about the insufficiency of being.
The mediation of opposed predicates in reflection becomes the ontological destiny of the Absolute.
The speculative proposition becomes a cosmological engine.
KCB's resistance is not philological.
It is categorial.
Relation is one thing.
Term is another.
Alternative truths are one thing.
Moments of a higher identity are another.
The indefinite is one thing.
Dialectical closure is another.
That is the deepest KCBian support for the user's refusal to let being be swallowed by becoming.
## II. KCB and Evolutionary Metaphysics
Here one has to begin with a negative result.
KCB does not name Aurobindo in the published philosophical works on disk.
The existing KCB reader on the site already states plainly that KCB and Aurobindo never engaged each other in print.
Nothing in the searches through the KCB primary notes and indices overturned that conclusion.
That means the present section cannot honestly be written as a chapter in a historical debate.
It must be written as a structural reconstruction.
Still, the reconstruction is not arbitrary.
KCB's architecture is sufficiently determinate that one can see where it would resist an Aurobindonian evolutionary metaphysics.
The first point is methodological.
KCB's philosophy begins from reflective discrimination, not from cosmic history.
What matters for him is the deepening of subjectivity through release from objectivity.
The subject is "free from the object" and the modes of subjectivity are the modes of freeing oneself from objectivity (*The Subject as Freedom*, pp. 23, 29–31, 194–196).
That movement is not an evolutionary ascent of the cosmos.
It is a phenomenological and spiritual inwardization.
The direction matters.
Aurobindo's grand story is that consciousness has involved itself into matter and evolves upward through life and mind toward Supermind.
KCB's grand story, if one may call it that, is almost inverse in emphasis.
It is the discovery that what appears first as body, image, thought, feeling, and introspection progressively reveals a freedom from objective determination.
The load-bearing motion is from objectivization to release, not from matter to divinized nature.
Second, KCB's critique of Hegel already tells against any attempt to privilege becoming as ultimate grammar.
Aurobindo is not Hegel.
He does not say that all determinations are moments of Concept.
He does, however, give becoming an enormous systematic dignity.
The world-process is not merely contingent appearance.
It is the real unfolding of an involved consciousness.
If one reads this from a KCBian standpoint, the obvious question is: why should real process enjoy that privilege?
Why should becoming be metaphysically primary rather than one valid register among others?
KCB's doctrine of alternative absolutes makes the pressure sharper.
Truth, freedom, and value do not collapse into one reflective whole.
Each has its own absolute demand.
An evolutionary metaphysics tends, by contrast, to re-order these around a single cosmic telos.
Truth becomes the truth of the process.
Freedom becomes the liberated participation in the process.
Value becomes the consummated expression of the process.
From a KCBian perspective, that is already suspicious.
It risks converting alternatives into phases of one trajectory.
Third, KCB's realism in the register of knowing cuts against strong constructivist or voluntarist readings of cosmic becoming.
The particular act of knowing, he says, discovers and does not construct the object known (*Studies* II, p. 129, via Burch).
That sentence is not an anti-Aurobindo sentence.
But it does mean that not every philosophical function should be read through productive emergence.
There is a receptive dimension irreducible to self-projection.
Any total evolutionary metaphysics has to explain why receptivity is not derivative illusion.
KCB keeps that question open.
Fourth, the KCBian method of *adhyāropa-apavāda* is structurally at odds with the Aurobindonian doctrine of supramental descent.
In KCB, philosophical work often proceeds by allowing a position to stand, extracting what truth is there, and then canceling its absolutization.
The movement is subtractive as much as additive.
It is a movement of de-objectification.
Aurobindo's central late doctrine, by contrast, is not cancellation but manifestation.
Nature is not only to be seen through.
It is to be transformed.
Again, this is not a direct controversy.
It is a difference in philosophical instinct.
KCB distrusts totalizing syntheses because they misplace logical functions.
Aurobindo seeks a higher integration in which lower truths are preserved and surpassed.
Fifth, KCB's recovery of the Jaina doctrine implies that no one register should be treated as exhaustive.
This is the strongest implicit anti-Aurobindo point.
Aurobindo famously treats Mind, Overmind, and Supermind as a graded hierarchy culminating in truth-consciousness.
The hierarchy is not merely epistemic.
It is ontological.
That gives the system power.
It also introduces exactly the kind of privilege the user wants to question.
Why should Supermind, rather than being one real register among others, be the integrating register that normatively subsumes the rest?
Why is manifestation toward that telos the truth of all other registers?
KCB does not ask those questions of Aurobindo by name.
But his philosophical architecture makes them unavoidable.
One can therefore state the structural critique like this.
Aurobindo gives a metaphysics of real becoming.
KCB gives a logic of irreducible alternative absolutes.
Where Aurobindo sees the higher truth in an integrating ascent, KCB is more likely to ask whether integration has already distorted the alternatives it claims to preserve.
That is why the user's position can use KCB as a prophylactic against turning real-world panentheism into a teleology of becoming.
KCB does not force one back into world-denial.
He does force one to justify why becoming should rule.
The Medhananda notes on Aurobindo help clarify the pressure from the other side.
Medhananda reconstructs Aurobindo as a thinker for whom the world is a real manifestation of Saccidānanda and individuation arises by "exclusive concentration," a real self-limitation of the Divine.
That is philosophically strong.
It avoids reducing the world to illusion and the individual to a mere error.
But from the present standpoint the question remains.
Why should the reality of self-limitation imply that self-recovery through evolutionary manifestation is the privileged metaphysical arc?
The user's position accepts the reality of world and individuality.
It does not thereby concede that becoming outranks being.
## III. Aurobindo on Kant
One must be plain here too.
The primary Aurobindo materials on disk do not contain a substantial explicit engagement with Kant.
The search results turned up only an incidental mention in a cultural essay, not a worked philosophical dispute.
So the right question is not "What did Aurobindo say about Kant by name?"
The right question is: what would Aurobindo's architecture say to a Kantian limitation of speculative reason?
The answer begins with Aurobindo's distinction between Mind and Supermind.
Mind is not false.
It is real, necessary, and powerful.
But it is also dividing, indirect, and derivative.
It knows by separation.
It builds by distinction.
It operates through partial viewpoints.
That is why *The Life Divine* insists that one has not understood the problem if one merely refines mentality.
There must be a different mode of knowledge.
Supermind is that mode.
In Aurobindo's language it is truth-consciousness, knowledge by identity, not just concept.
The local philosophical use of this doctrine against Kant is fairly clear.
Kant's categories and antinomies map the powers and limits of discursive mind.
Aurobindo would say: granted.
But why infer that these are the limits of consciousness as such?
Why should the structure of separative thought legislate the ultimate possibility of knowledge?
That is where the *Life Divine* chapters on the classical metaphysical categories matter.
Aurobindo says that Brahman, Purusha, Ishwara, Maya, Prakriti, and Shakti are "the fundamental aspects in which we see and experience the omnipresent Reality" (*The Life Divine*, II.I.2, p. 336).
The sentence is instructive.
They present themselves to conceptual cognition as aspects.
Conceptual cognition is real.
But it is aspectual.
It is not the whole of knowledge.
Similarly, Aurobindo says that "the Knowledge is there concealed in the Ignorance itself; it has rather to be unveiled than acquired" (*The Life Divine*, II.I.3, p. 380).
This is not a Kant commentary.
But it is a direct rejection of the thought that the knower must remain imprisoned within the conditions of discursive finitude.
The limits of mentality are transitional, not final.
The same point appears more practically in *The Synthesis of Yoga*.
The object of yoga is to raise the human being from the consciousness of ordinary mind into a free self-knowledge, free will, and free delight of being (*The Synthesis of Yoga*, Part IV, p. 783).
And the transition from mind to supermind is "a change and conversion of the whole consciousness" (p. 825).
The implication is unmistakable.
Kant describes a certain type of knower.
Aurobindo proposes that the knower itself can be transformed.
From this standpoint, the thing-in-itself problem is not solved by better inference.
It is dissolved by a different ontological condition of knowledge.
The noumenal is not an unreachable beyond.
It is what becomes present when knowledge ceases to be only external and separative.
This is why Aurobindo's answer to Kant is both strong and dangerous.
It is strong because it refuses the easy universalization of mental limits.
It is dangerous because it can sound like a promissory note unless one grants the possibility of supra-mental consciousness.
KCB would probably agree with Aurobindo against a crude object-bound epistemology.
KCB too insists on knowing without objectifying thought.
But the agreement would end quickly.
KCB's non-objectifying knowledge is a deepened introspective and spiritual realization of the subject as freedom.
Aurobindo's is a transformed cosmic consciousness whose fullest form is supramental and world-affirming.
Both refuse Kant's confinement of knowledge to discursive object-cognition.
They refuse it in very different ways.
There is one more point worth stressing.
Aurobindo's system is not simply anti-Kantian exuberance.
It preserves something of the Kantian warning.
The warning is that mind should not mistake its own conceptual forms for ultimate possession of reality.
Aurobindo accepts that.
He radicalizes it.
Mind is not sovereign.
But his conclusion is the opposite of Kant's restraint.
Kant says: therefore speculative reason must recognize its limit.
Aurobindo says: therefore consciousness must exceed mind.
## IV. Did Aurobindo Ever Answer This Sort of Perspectivist Critique?
There is, again, no direct printed answer to KCB on disk.
There is also no substantial direct engagement with Jaina *anekāntavāda* in the Aurobindo primary materials inspected.
So one has to reconstruct the nearest Aurobindonian reply from within his own system.
That reply would begin by conceding more than Hegel does.
Aurobindo is not a philosopher of a merely universal Geist for which the individual is a transient moment.
He insists that the individual is an eternal poise of the Divine.
He repeatedly says that the first silent realization is not the end.
The active Brahman, the personal Divine, the cosmic self, and the individual soul all remain real.
That already puts him at some distance from the standard Hegelian picture.
*The Life Divine* is full of passages whose function is to resist one-sided absolutizations.
The classic metaphysical categories are all real disclosures.
The silent and active Brahman are both real.
The individual and the universal are both real.
The personal and impersonal Divine are both real.
From one angle this looks close to the user's perspectival instinct.
Why not say that Aurobindo is already an *anekāntic* thinker in everything but name?
Because he refuses to stop there.
For Aurobindo, partial realizations are true, but they are not co-ordinate alternatives in the KCBian sense.
They are stages or poises whose truth is grounded in a higher integral consciousness.
That integral consciousness is not an empty logical unity.
It is Supermind.
So the Aurobindonian answer to a KCB-style perspectivism would probably be this.
Yes, there are many valid standpoints.
Yes, the silent Brahman, the dynamic Divine, world-affirmation, world-transcendence, personal devotion, and impersonal realization are all genuine.
But their coexistence is not best understood as irreducible alternative absolutes.
It is best understood as a hierarchy of real poises held together in truth-consciousness.
That is the precise place where the user's position diverges.
The user accepts the plurality.
The user doubts the right of the integrating hierarchy.
Aurobindo would likely press the point hardest through the doctrine of knowledge by identity.
If the supramental knows reality not piecemeal but integrally, then the different registers need not remain merely external alternatives.
Identity and becoming, silence and action, transcendence and manifestation can all be held non-destructively in a higher poise.
The user will hear the difficulty immediately.
Once "higher poise" is invoked, is not the old privilege of synthesis back again?
Aurobindo would say no, because the higher poise does not destroy the lower realities.
It fulfills them.
The user will reply that this is precisely the Hegelian word in new dress.
What is called fulfillment may still be subordination.
At that point the dispute becomes genuinely sharp.
Aurobindo's best case is that his synthesis is not conceptual violence but ontological simultaneity.
The user's best case is that even ontological simultaneity should not be presumed to erase the independence of logical registers.
The difference can be stated in one sentence.
Aurobindo grants plurality but privileges integration.
The user's position grants plurality and denies that integration is metaphysically mandatory.
That is why KCB is so useful here.
He gives a principled reason not to infer from plurality to synthesis.
## V. The Jain Thing — Anekāntavāda and the Vedāntic Reception
The Jaina material is not a decorative appendix.
It is the conceptual hinge of the whole issue.
In its simplest form, *anekāntavāda* says that reality is many-sided and that a single, unqualified predication never exhausts a thing.
The correlating doctrine of *syādvāda* says that predication must be standpoint-conditioned.
*Syāt* does not mean "maybe" in the weak modern sense.
It means: from a certain valid standpoint, under a certain aspect, in a certain respect.
That is why the doctrine is not relativism.
It is disciplined aspectual realism.
The underlying ontology is crucial.
A substance has qualities and modes.
It persists, and it changes.
Umāsvāti's formal grammar is exactly this co-belonging of persistence and transformation. [cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/guna-paryayavat-dravyam] [cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/tad-bhavah-parinamah]
This already supplies something the Hegelian opening does not.
It tells you how one and the same real can endure as itself while entering different modal states, without requiring that persistence and change be dialectically generated out of each other.
The famous sevenfold predication is one way of formalizing this aspectual discipline.
From one standpoint the thing is.
From another it is not.
From another it is indescribable.
And so on through the *saptabhaṅgī*.
The doctrine is often mocked as if it simply said everything is both true and false.
That is not its best philosophical form.
Its best form is a warning that the same predicate should not be universalized across respects.
KCB's reconstruction seizes precisely this point.
He does not recover Jainism as contradiction-tolerance.
He recovers it as realism of alternative truths.
That is why he can say that each truth is bordered by indeterminate alternatives and that the modes of truth are "alternative truths" (*Studies* I, pp. 331, 342).
But the Hindu schools did object, and their objections matter.
Śaṅkara, in *Brahma-sūtra-bhāṣya* 2.2.33–36, attacks the Jaina position on two fronts. [cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.2.33-36]
First, opposed predicates cannot coherently inhere in one *dharmin* in the same respect.
Second, the Jaina doctrine of the soul's contraction and expansion with the body makes the self mutable in unacceptable ways.
The site glossary summarizes the first point well.
Advaita rejects the conjunction of contrary predicates in one locus because `sat` and `asat` cannot be instantiated together in that manner. [cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.2.33-36]
Rāmānuja's objection in the *Śrī-bhāṣya* on the same sūtras is similar in structure. [cite://ramanuja/shri-bhasya/2.2.33-36]
He too denies that one *vastu* can bear opposed determinations without incoherence.
He therefore distinguishes his own Brahman-world relation, fixed through *śarīra-śarīrin*, from what he sees as oscillating predication.
These objections are serious.
They should not be brushed aside as mere sectarian impatience.
If *syādvāda* is taken to mean that the same predicate is both true and false in the same respect, the criticism lands.
The doctrine would corrode determinate knowledge.
But that is not the only way to read what the Jain grammar is trying to protect.
The strongest recovery, and KCB's recovery, is different.
What needs protection is not contradiction.
It is alternative validity.
A thing has identity and mode.
A standpoint may validly track one without thereby exhausting the other.
Predication is then disciplined by respect, not dissolved into arbitrariness.
That is why KCB can retrieve a realist lesson from Jainism after Śaṅkara.
He is not accepting the loose thesis that a thing is simply both A and not-A without qualification.
He is accepting that reality can sustain a plurality of determinate disclosures none of which automatically sublates the rest.
This is also the place to be cautious about Madhva.
Madhva is not a Jaina.
His realism is theological and scripturally anchored in a very different way.
Still, there is a structural affinity worth noting.
Madhva is far more resistant than Advaita to the flattening of plurality into a merely lower truth.
The real distinctions are real.
That does not make Madhva an *anekāntavādin*.
It does mean that the Jaina instinct toward ontological plurality finds more sympathetic air there than in strong monistic Advaita.
The user's position can therefore use the Jaina tradition in a precise way.
Not as a license for "everything is true."
Not as a rejection of metaphysical seriousness.
But as a rigorous grammar of aspect, persistence, and mode.
It is this grammar that makes the orthogonality argument more than a mood.
## VI. The Orthogonality Argument
The user's reformulation can now be stated as a formal argument.
Being names identity.
Becoming names modality.
Identity answers the question: what is this, as itself?
Modality answers the question: in what state, respect, phase, or mode does it occur?
These are different questions.
Nothing requires them to collapse.
The first support for this claim is Kantian.
At *KrV* A598/B626, Kant argues that existence does not add a real predicate to the concept of a thing.
Whether one says "the possible hundred thalers" or "the actual hundred thalers," the concept is not enriched by actuality as a new determining content.
The philosophical use of this point here is not the existence of God.
It is the status of modality.
Modal marks do not constitute identity.
They qualify, position, or actualize what is already determinable as this rather than that.
Applied to the present dispute, becoming is modal.
It concerns transition, alteration, manifestation, emergence, phase, realization.
All of that may be real.
None of it, merely as such, yields identity.
One does not get what a thing is merely from the fact that it becomes.
The second support is KCB's anti-Hegelian line.
If the Hegelian absolute identity must become "at once a relation and a term," then identity has been made to do relational work that belongs elsewhere (*Studies* I, p. 334).
The user's formulation is simply a cleaner way of making the same complaint.
Identity is one register.
Modality is another.
If one says that being truly is only as becoming, one has already loaded being with modality.
If one says that becoming is the self-truth of being, one has loaded modality with identity.
In either case a category-crossing has occurred.
The third support is the Jain ontology of *dravya* and *paryāya*.
A substance persists.
Its modes alter.
Origination and cessation are real, but so is persistence.
This is not a static metaphysics pretending that change is unreal.
Nor is it a process-metaphysics pretending that identity is an afterthought.
It is exactly the kind of co-reality the user wants.
One might object that Hegel can easily reply.
Does he not say that a higher unity preserves difference?
Could he not therefore preserve identity and modality together?
The answer is that Hegel can preserve them only by redescribing them as moments of a higher self-developing identity.
That is precisely what the user refuses.
The issue is not whether a higher unity can mention both.
The issue is whether identity and modality need a higher unity in order to be real at all.
The user's answer is no.
They are orthogonal.
Orthogonal does not mean unrelated.
It means irreducible.
The same real may be identifiable and modally variable.
A standpoint may foreground identity.
Another may foreground becoming.
Neither thereby destroys the other.
What is illegitimate is the inference that one axis must be generated out of the other.
This also explains why the user's position need not "privilege being over becoming."
That was never the point.
To deny Hegelian sublation is not to reinstall static substance as king.
It is to deny that becoming has an exclusive metaphysical franchise.
Becoming is real.
It may even be cosmologically central in some domains.
But its reality is modal.
It tells us how reality unfolds, manifests, changes, or discloses.
It does not by itself tell us what reality is as itself.
Here the KCBian language of alternative truths becomes especially helpful.
Identity-truth and becoming-truth may both be real.
They need not be identical truths.
They may be alternative disclosures belonging to different registers.
If so, the compulsion to sublate them disappears.
One can now see why the user reached for the Jain analogy.
The Jain move is not merely "many views."
It is the refusal to let one mode of predication devour the others.
The Hegelian error, from this standpoint, is not that Hegel values becoming.
It is that he mistakes one logic of disclosure for the logic of reality as such.
The Aurobindonian pressure is subtler.
Aurobindo would say that the higher consciousness can hold identity and becoming together without violence.
The user's reply is that even then one should not assume that the fact of a higher simultaneity abolishes the irreducibility of the two axes.
One may grant ontological simultaneity and still deny logical collapse.
That is the cleanest and strongest form of the position.
Being and becoming are different irreducible registers of reality.
Being is identity.
Becoming is modality.
Neither outranks the other by definition.
Neither generates the other by necessity.
Sublation is not the structure of the absolute any more than it is the structure of every empirical truth.
## VII. What KCB Does Not Deliver
It would be a mistake to make KCB carry more than he can.
He is indispensable here.
He is not sufficient.
His positive culmination is not a full world-affirming panentheist metaphysics.
It is the subject as freedom, the inwardizing trajectory of self-knowledge, and the doctrine of alternative absolutes.
He gives a formidable critique of Hegelian totalization.
He gives a powerful retrieval of Jain alternative truth.
He does not give a robust doctrine of the world's positive reality in Brahman of the sort the user's position wants.
To be sure, KCB can say that the world is "real in the reality of Brahman" even if unreal apart from Brahman (*Studies* I, p. 43).
That is important.
But it is not yet a fully developed realist panentheism.
Nor is it a doctrine of divine self-limitation, manifestation, and cosmic play comparable to Aurobindo's or to the user's fuller view.
This is why the user's position goes beyond KCB in two directions at once.
It uses KCB negatively against totalizing sublation.
It then supplements him positively with a stronger account of real manifestation.
That positive supplement may borrow from Aurobindo's world-realism, from the broader realist Vedāntic line, and from the doctrine of *bhāvarūpa* self-veiling.
But the supplement must not undo the KCBian safeguard.
If it does, one simply returns to a new privilege of becoming.
The proper balance is therefore delicate.
KCB is necessary because he blocks the slide from plurality to synthesis.
Aurobindo is useful because he blocks the slide from transcendental critique to world-denial.
The user's own position arises in the gap.
The world is real.
Individuality is real.
Manifestation is real.
But none of this requires that becoming be the metaphysical master-term.
## VIII. Reading Notes
For KCB, begin with four loci.
First, *The Subject as Freedom*, especially pp. 17, 23, 29–31, and 194–196, for the doctrine of subjectivity as freedom from object.
Second, *Studies in Philosophy* I, p. 43, for the line that the world is real in the reality of Brahman.
Third, *Studies in Philosophy* I, pp. 331–343, for the Jaina essay and the anti-Hegel lines about alternative truths and relation versus term.
Fourth, *Studies in Philosophy* II, pp. 127–143, for the alternative absolutes.
For Aurobindo, the best sequence for this question is selective rather than exhaustive.
Read *The Life Divine* Book One, Chapters XIV–XVI on Supermind.
Then Book Two, Part I, Chapters II–III on the complementary metaphysical categories and the unveiling of knowledge in ignorance.
Then *The Synthesis of Yoga*, Part IV, Chapters XIX–XXV, where Supermind is treated not as metaphor but as a transformed instrumentality of knowledge.
Read them with one question in mind.
Is Aurobindo describing a higher integration that preserves irreducible truths, or is he still privileging synthesis?
For the Jain material, start with the site's own glossary entries on `anekānta-vāda` and `syād-vāda`.
Then read the Umāsvāti citations on *dravya* and *paryāya*. [cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/guna-paryayavat-dravyam] [cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/tad-bhavah-parinamah]
Only after that should one turn to Śaṅkara and Rāmānuja on *Brahma-sūtra* 2.2.33–36.
Otherwise one risks reading the Jain position only through its adversaries.
The philosophical conclusion of this essay is modest but firm.
KCB gives a real defense of the refusal to let Hegelian sublation legislate reality.
The Jain doctrine gives the ontological grammar in which identity and modality can both be real.
Kant supplies the negative point that modality does not create essence.
Aurobindo supplies the strongest rival, because he defends real becoming without collapsing the world into illusion.
The user's position lives where these four pressures meet.
It accepts world-reality and individuation.
It rejects the privilege of becoming.
It treats being and becoming as orthogonal.
And it therefore refuses to assume that the absolute, merely because it is ultimate, must be the dialectical sublation of identity into self-becoming.
