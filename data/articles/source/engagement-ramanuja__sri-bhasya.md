# Engagement: Rāmānuja’s Śrī-Bhāṣya

## 1. What the text is

*Śrī-Bhāṣya* (the *Śārīraka-Mīmāṃsā-Bhāṣya*) is Rāmānuja’s major commentary on Bādarāyaṇa’s *Brahma-Sūtra*, traditionally placed in the late eleventh or early twelfth century and canonical for the Viśiṣṭādvaita lineage. The on-disk text opens by invoking Bodhāyana’s extensive *vṛtti* and presenting itself as a compressed restatement in that line (p. 1), so it is self-consciously a layered commentary. Structurally it follows the *Brahma-Sūtra*’s four *adhyāyas*, each with four *pādas*: adhyāya 1 fixes the Upaniṣadic referent as Brahman; adhyāya 2 defends that thesis against rival systems; adhyāya 3 treats upāsanā, transmigration, and discipline; adhyāya 4 treats death, archirādi-gati, liberation, and non-return.

## 2. Sampling and scope statement

Engaged at depth: roughly a quarter of the file, concentrated in the opening of adhyāya 1 (especially [BS 1.1.1](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/1.1.1) and the *Ānandamayādhikaraṇa*), the anti-Sāṅkhya/Yoga opening of adhyāya 2, the first *pāda* of adhyāya 3 on transmigration and karmic residue, and the liberation arc of adhyāya 4, with quoted close reading of six loci. Structural summary only: the remaining *pādas* of adhyāyas 1-4, tracked through their headings and sampled arguments. Flagged but not fully engaged: many micro-disputes over combining *vidyās* in adhyāya 3.3, several route-order disputes in adhyāya 4.3, and a number of opponent-specific sub-arguments in adhyāya 2.2.

## 3. Structural walk-through

- **Adhyāya 1, pāda 1** opens with the *Jijñāsādhikaraṇa* and runs through *Janmādy*, *Śāstrayonitva*, *Samanvaya*, *Īkṣati*, and *Ānandamaya* (opening p. 1). Its burden is lexical and exegetical: “Brahman” is the Lord with auspicious qualities, and the disputed Upaniṣadic loci must refer to that Brahman, not to pradhāna, jīva, or an indeterminate absolute.

- **Adhyāya 1, pāda 2** begins with *Sarvatraprasiddhyadhikaraṇa* (p. 111). Subjects such as the eater, the inner ruler, and Vaiśvānara are identified with Brahman rather than finite deity, fire, or jīva.

- **Adhyāya 1, pāda 3** opens with *Dyubhvādyadhikaraṇa* (p. 131) and proceeds through *Bhūmā*, *Akṣara*, *Dahara*, *Devatā*, and related topics. The repeated move is the same: no merely cosmological or merely individualistic reading is allowed to displace Brahman.

- **Adhyāya 1, pāda 4** opens with *Ānumānikādhikaraṇa* (p. 161) and directly attacks the Sāṅkhya pradhāna, then closes with *Etena sarvaṃ vyākhyātam* (p. 184). Here the thesis becomes explicit: Brahman is both material and efficient cause.

- **Adhyāya 2, pāda 1** begins with *Smṛtyadhikaraṇa* (p. 185). The whole second adhyāya is framed as defence of the first: Kapila, Yoga, and the claim that a blissful Brahman cannot cause a changing world are all taken up here.

- **Adhyāya 2, pāda 2** opens with *Racanānupapattyadhikaraṇa* (p. 215). This is the main opponent-catalogue: Sāṅkhya, atomism, Bauddha, Jaina, and Pāśupata positions are refuted; the Pāñcarātra is then defended as Vāsudeva-centered Brahma-śāstra.

- **Adhyāya 2, pāda 3** begins with *Viyadadhikaraṇa* (p. 243). After the public polemic, the text returns to ontology: space, self, cognition, and the jīva as *aṃśa*.

- **Adhyāya 2, pāda 4** opens with *Prāṇotpattyadhikaraṇa* (p. 258/261 in the OCR). It handles the origin, measure, rank, and naming of prāṇa and the organs.

- **Adhyāya 3, pāda 1** opens with *Tadantarapratipatti* (p. 268). It asks what accompanies the departing jīva, how karmic remainder operates, and how descent and rebirth occur.

- **Adhyāya 3, pāda 2** opens with *Tadbhāvādhikaraṇa* (OCR around p. 280). It studies the states of the soul and Brahman’s relation to them, distinguishing the finite self’s conditioned modes from the Lord.

- **Adhyāya 3, pāda 3** opens with *Sarvavedāntapratyayādhikaraṇa* (p. 293). The issue is when distinct Upaniṣadic *vidyās* may be combined and when they must remain separate.

- **Adhyāya 3, pāda 4** opens with *Puruṣārthādhikaraṇa* (p. 327). It discusses śama-dama, āśrama-dharmas, *mauna*, and the timing of worldly and liberating fruits; liberating knowledge is cultivated upāsanā, not bare sentence-cognition.

- **Adhyāya 4, pāda 1** opens with *Āvṛttyadhikaraṇa* (p. 344). It defines Vedāntic “knowing” as repeated contemplative continuity, then treats ātmatvopāsanā, symbolic meditation, posture, and persistence until death.

- **Adhyāya 4, pāda 2** opens with *Vāgadhikaraṇa* (p. 352). Speech enters mind, mind enters prāṇa, and prāṇa with the jīva enters the elements; the text then asks how far knower and non-knower share the same departure.

- **Adhyāya 4, pāda 3** begins with a damaged OCR heading, but the content clearly opens the *archirādi* sequence (p. 360). Subsequent adhikaraṇas decide route-order and distinguish which upāsakas are led where.

- **Adhyāya 4, pāda 4** opens with *Saṃpadyāvirbhāva* (p. 367) and ends with *Anāvṛttiḥ śabdāt* (p. 376). Liberation is manifestation of the self in Brahman, but without the Lord’s unique cosmic governance.

## 4. Load-bearing passages — engaged at depth

**1. [BS 1.1.1](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/1.1.1), *Jijñāsādhikaraṇa*, pp. 1-2**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `brahmaṇo jijñāsā brahmajijñāsā ... brahmaśabdena ca svabhāvato nirasta-nikhila-doṣo 'navadhikātiśayā[saṅkhyeya?]-kalyāṇa-guṇa-gaṇaḥ puruṣo[ttama?] 'bhidhīyate ... ataḥ sarveśvara eva jijñāsākarmatāṃ brahma ... jñātum icchā jijñāsā; icchāyā iṣyamāṇa-prādhānyād iṣyamāṇaṃ jñānam iha vidhīyate.`

**Close English:** “`brahmajijñāsā` is the desire to know Brahman. The genitive in `brahmaṇaḥ` is objective. By the word ‘Brahman’ there is denoted the Supreme Person, intrinsically devoid of every defect and possessed of an unsurpassed [countless?] host of auspicious qualities. Therefore Brahman, that is, the Lord, is what stands as the object of inquiry. `Jijñāsā` is the wish to know; because in a wish what is wanted is primary, it is the knowledge-to-be-attained that is here enjoined.”

The first move is grammatical and decisive. Rāmānuja does not begin by asking what kind of absolute Brahman might be; he begins by fixing the compound. `Brahma-jijñāsā` is read as a *tatpuruṣa* with objective genitive: *brahmaṇaḥ jijñāsā*. Brahman is the object sought, not the hidden subject spontaneously disclosing itself by a single sentence. This grammatical choice is already a soteriological choice.

The lexical loading of `brahma` is equally decisive. The term is not left open to a nirguṇa minimum; it names the Lord as free from defect and full of auspicious qualities.

Pāṇinian-rich note. `Brahmajijñāsā = brahmaṇaḥ jijñāsā`: a genitive *tatpuruṣa*. `Brahmaṇaḥ` is glossed as `karmaṇi ṣaṣṭhī`, so Brahman is the object of inquiry. In `brahmaśabdena ... puruṣottamo 'bhidhīyate`, `brahmaśabdena` is karaṇa, `puruṣottamaḥ` the nominative subject of the passive, and the compound quality-clusters specify what the word “Brahman” denotes.

**2. [BS 1.1.13](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/1.1.13), *Ānandamayādhikaraṇa*, p. 87**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `ānandamayo 'bhyāsāt. ānandamayaḥ paramātmā ... vijñānamayo hi jīvaḥ; na buddhimātram ... vijñānamayāj jīvād anyas tad-antaraḥ paramātmā ānandamayaḥ.`

**Close English:** “`Ānandamaya` [means] the Supreme Self, because of repeated emphasis. For the `vijñānamaya` is the jīva, not mere intellect. Distinct from that `vijñānamaya` jīva, as the inner one, is the Supreme Self, the `ānandamaya`.”

The argument turns on ordered interiority. The *vijñānamaya* is the finite conscious self; the *ānandamaya* is “other than” that jīva and interior to it. So the Taittirīya’s sequence does not collapse into a featureless self; it stratifies embodiment, jīva, and the indwelling Lord.

**3. [BS 2.1.2](cite://mandana/brahma-siddhi/2.1.2), *Yogapratyuktyadhikaraṇa*, pp. 187-188**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `etena kāpila-smṛti-nirākaraṇena yoga-smṛtir api pratyuktā ... abrahyātmaka-pradhāna-kāraṇa-vādān animitta-kāraṇa-mātreśvarābhyupagamāc ca ... yoga-smṛtir api ... bhrānti-mūleti na tayā vedāntopabṛṃhaṇaṃ nyāyyam.`

**Close English:** “By this refutation of Kapila’s *smṛti*, the Yoga *smṛti* too is refuted. For it teaches a pradhāna-cause that is not Brahman, and admits an Īśvara who is only an efficient cause; therefore that Yoga *smṛti* too has error at its root, and it is not proper to use it to supplement Vedānta.”

Rāmānuja’s criterion for admissible secondary authority is stringent. Mentioning Īśvara is not enough; if causality is still assigned to a non-Brahman pradhāna, the text cannot serve as Vedāntic *upabṛṃhaṇa*.

**4. Adhyāya 3, pāda 1, *Tadantarapratipatti* and *Prāṇagati*, pp. 268-270**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `tadantarapratipattau raṃhati saṃpariṣvakta iti ... itaś ca bhūta-sūkṣma-pariṣvaktasya gamanam iti gamyate ... tam utkrāmantaṃ prāṇo 'nūtkrāmati ... gṛhītvaitāni saṃyāti vāyur gandhān ivāśayāt.`

**Close English:** “At the obtaining of another body, it moves while embraced [by subtle elements] ... and from this too it is understood that the one accompanied by subtle elements goes forth ... ‘When it departs, the vital breath departs after it’ ... ‘Taking these, the Lord goes, as the wind carries scents from their seat.’”

The jīva does not travel as a bare point. It departs with prāṇas and subtle elemental accompaniment, so rebirth is materially continuous rather than a simple reset. The absolutive `gṛhītvā` matters: it goes only after “taking these.”

**5. Adhyāya 4, pāda 1, *Ātmatvopāsanādhikaraṇa*, p. 345**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `upāsitur ātmety evopāsyam ... yathā pratyagātmanaḥ svaśarīraṃ pratyātmatvād devo 'haṃ manuṣyo 'ham ity anusandhānam; tathā pratyagātmano 'py ātmatvāt paramātmanas tasyāpy aham ity evānusandhānaṃ yuktam.`

**Close English:** “For the worshipper, Brahman is to be worshipped precisely as the self ... just as, because one’s own body stands in a self-relation to the inner self, there is the apprehension ‘I am a god,’ ‘I am a man’; so too, because the Supreme Self stands as the self of the inner self, the apprehension ‘I’ with respect to that also is appropriate.”

The logic is neither simple identity nor simple external relation. Brahman is to the jīva as the jīva is to the body: inner self to dependent mode. That is the cleanest constructive statement of Viśiṣṭādvaita in the file.

**6. Adhyāya 4, pāda 4, *Jagadvyāpāravarja* and *Anāvṛtti*, pp. 374-376**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `jagad-vyāpāraḥ ... tad-varjaṃ ... muktasyaiśvaryam ... anāvṛttiḥ śabdād anāvṛttiḥ śabdāt.`

**Close English:** “Cosmic activity [the regulation of the world’s conscious and non-conscious orders] is excluded; the liberated one’s lordly power is with that exception ... ‘No return, on the authority of scripture; no return, on the authority of scripture.’”

Here the asymmetry between mukta and Īśvara is explicit. Liberation grants bliss and sovereignty, but not the Lord’s unique governance of the whole cosmos. This is the strongest resistance-point in the text for any stronger identity-claim at the summit.

## 5. The text’s relation to the position

- On the mapped lineages, *Śrī-Bhāṣya* belongs centrally to the Yāmuna -> Rāmānuja -> Vedānta-Deśika chain. It is not part of the Bhartṛprapañca -> Bhāskara -> Madhva -> Caitanya -> Aurobindo trajectory, but it is a major adjacent realism.

- Relative to Padmapāda, Prakāśātman, Vidyāraṇya, and Madhusūdana, the text is a direct antagonist. It does not yet name those later authors, but the opening anonymous *pūrvapakṣa* already targets the package “avidyā-nivṛtti is mokṣa; sentence-born knowledge of *tat tvam asi* is sufficient; upāsanā is secondary.”

- Relative to Vallabha and Nimbārka, it is an earlier Vaiṣṇava realism with a similar anti-Māyāvāda pressure, but its distinctive grammar is the body-self relation.

- Whom it refutes by name in the inspected file: Kapila; Yoga/Hiraṇyagarbha; Akṣapāda and Kaṇāda; Saugata positions including Yogācāra and Mādhyamika; Jaina/Ārhata positions; Pāśupata and Śaiva systems. It does not simply reject the Pāñcarātra; it defends it once read as a Vāsudeva-centered exposition of Vedāntic truth.

- On T2, the text is strongly useful but not in every respect. It strongly supports the recovery of realism against world-negation, because Brahman is both upādāna and nimitta and the jagat is the Lord’s real body. But it does not help preserve an Advaitic sublation-grammar; it wants the rival ontology displaced, not rehabilitated.

- On T7, the text is mostly resistant. The anonymous *pūrvapakṣa* speaks in the register of avidyā and sentence-born knowledge, but the siddhānta does not develop a positive ontology of avidyā, still less Brahman as both its *āśraya* and *viṣaya*. The commentary’s strategy is to deny the need for that framework.

- On T11, it is a partial ally. Waking, dream, sleep, death, transit, and liberation all have determinate ontological status; but there is no involution-evolution story here.

- On T9, it is a very strong ally. The world is not to be dismissed as nullity; it is real as divine body and divine governance. The anti-Māyāvāda force of the text lies not in crude external realism, but in the claim that unity does not require the evacuation of manifestation.

- The main friction-point is adhyāya 4.4: *jagadvyāpāravarjam*. The position may wish to retain stronger non-dual language at the summit; Rāmānuja refuses that if it threatens the asymmetry between Īśvara and mukta. A second friction-point is 4.1’s insistence that liberation-relevant knowledge is reiterated upāsanā, not merely a punctiliar cognition.

## 6. What the user can productively take

This text gives the corpus a first-rank primary source for panentheistic realism inside Vedānta: Brahman as both material and efficient cause, the world as the Lord’s body, and liberation as permanent participation rather than erasure. It is especially useful wherever the project needs a rigorous Vaiṣṇava critique of world-negating readings.

It also poses a challenge that cannot be skipped. Any synthesis that wants to keep non-dual resources must answer Rāmānuja’s two strongest complaints: Vedānta cannot terminate in a qualityless referent, and liberation cannot be reduced to one-time sentence-cognition plus avidyā-talk. The gain is therefore double: strong citation-support for realism, and a sharpened burden of reply where the position wants to keep more of Advaita than Rāmānuja permits.
