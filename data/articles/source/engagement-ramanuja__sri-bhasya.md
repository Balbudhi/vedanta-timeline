# Engagement: Rāmānuja’s Śrī-Bhāṣya

## 1. What the text is

*Śrī-Bhāṣya* (more fully, the *Śārīraka-Mīmāṃsā-Bhāṣya*) is Rāmānuja’s major commentary on Bādarāyaṇa’s *Brahma-Sūtra*, traditionally dated to the late eleventh or early twelfth century and canonical for the Viśiṣṭādvaita lineage. The on-disk text opens by invoking Bodhāyana’s “extensive *vṛtti*” and presenting itself as a compressed restatement in that line, so it is already self-situated as layered commentary rather than a freestanding treatise (pure-Sanskrit file, p. 1). Structurally it follows the *Brahma-Sūtra*’s four *adhyāyas*, each with four *pādas*: adhyāya 1 secures the Upaniṣadic referent as Brahman; adhyāya 2 defends that thesis against rival systems; adhyāya 3 treats the means, states, and disciplines of upāsanā; adhyāya 4 treats death, archirādi-gati, liberation, and non-return.

## 2. Sampling and scope statement

Engaged at depth: roughly a quarter of the file, concentrated in the opening of adhyāya 1 (especially BS 1.1.1 and the *Ānandamayādhikaraṇa*, pp. 1-95), the anti-Sāṅkhya/Yoga opening of adhyāya 2 (pp. 185-214), the first *pāda* of adhyāya 3 on transmigration and karmic residue (pp. 268-275), and the liberation arc of adhyāya 4 (pp. 344-376), with quoted close reading of six loci. Structural summary only: the remaining *pādas* of adhyāyas 1-4, tracked through their actual headings and selected sampled arguments. Flagged but not fully engaged: many micro-disputes over combining *vidyās* in adhyāya 3.3, several fine-grained route-order disputes in adhyāya 4.3, and a number of opponent-specific sub-arguments in adhyāya 2.2 that I identified but did not line-by-line analyze.

## 3. Structural walk-through

- **Adhyāya 1, pāda 1** opens with the *Jijñāsādhikaraṇa* and runs through *Janmādy*, *Śāstrayonitva*, *Samanvaya*, *Īkṣati*, *Ānandamaya*, and related adhikaraṇas (opening p. 1). Its burden is lexical and exegetical: “Brahman” is the Lord endowed with auspicious qualities, and the disputed Upaniṣadic loci must be read as referring to that Brahman, not to pradhāna, the individual self, or an indeterminate absolute. The polemical architecture already includes an anonymous non-dualist *pūrvapakṣa* in the opening pages, but the main constructive work is semantic capture of the śruti-corpus.

- **Adhyāya 1, pāda 2** begins with *Sarvatraprasiddhyadhikaraṇa* (p. 111). Here Rāmānuja continues to identify difficult scriptural subjects such as the eater, the inner ruler, and Vaiśvānara with Brahman rather than with subordinate entities. Polemically this is still anti-reductionist rather than anti-systematic: the target is any reading that localizes these passages at the level of finite deity, fire, or jīva.

- **Adhyāya 1, pāda 3** opens with *Dyubhvādyadhikaraṇa* (p. 131) and proceeds through *Bhūmā*, *Akṣara*, *Dahara*, *Devatā*, and related topics. The repeated move is to refuse any merely cosmological or merely individualistic interpretation of the Upaniṣadic subject-term. In the architecture of the commentary, this pāda extends the same claim: Vedānta’s central nouns ultimately converge on one Brahman.

- **Adhyāya 1, pāda 4** opens with *Ānumānikādhikaraṇa* (p. 161) and directly attacks the Sāṅkhya pradhāna, then closes with *Etena sarvaṃ vyākhyātam* (p. 184). The constructive thesis now becomes explicit: Brahman is both material and efficient cause, and the earlier hermeneutic decisions are enough to read the whole Vedānta corpus that way. This pāda is the hinge from scriptural exegesis to overt anti-pradhāna metaphysics.

- **Adhyāya 2, pāda 1** begins with *Smṛtyadhikaraṇa* (p. 185). It frames the whole second adhyāya as defence of the first: once Brahman has been established as the Vedāntic object, every “impossibility” objection must be answered. Kapila’s *smṛti*, Yoga, and the claim that a blissful, omniscient Brahman cannot be the cause of a changing and painful world are all taken up here.

- **Adhyāya 2, pāda 2** opens with *Racanānupapattyadhikaraṇa* (p. 215). This is the most explicit catalogue of opponents in the file: Sāṅkhya, Kaṇāda’s atomism, Bauddha positions including Yogācāra and Mādhyamika, Jaina positions, and Pāśupata Śaivism are refuted; then the Pāñcarātra is defended once interpreted as Vāsudeva-centered Brahma-śāstra. The architecture is therefore discriminating, not indiscriminately exclusionary: “outside” systems are not all treated alike.

- **Adhyāya 2, pāda 3** begins with *Viyadadhikaraṇa* (p. 243). Having finished the public polemic against rival systems, the text returns to internal ontology: space, self, cognition, and the jīva as *aṃśa* are clarified. The role of this pāda is constructive consolidation after the external battles.

- **Adhyāya 2, pāda 4** opens with *Prāṇotpattyadhikaraṇa* (p. 258/261 in the OCR). It handles the origin, measure, rank, and naming of prāṇa and the organs. Polemically it answers residual confusion at the level of subtle ontology rather than named external schools.

- **Adhyāya 3, pāda 1** opens with *Tadantarapratipatti* (p. 268). The questions are now those of the moving soul: what accompanies the departing jīva, how karmic remainder operates, how descent and rebirth occur, and how quickly the process unfolds. This pāda gives the project an importantly realist account of continuity across embodiment.

- **Adhyāya 3, pāda 2** opens with *Tadbhāvādhikaraṇa* (OCR around p. 280). It studies the states of the soul and Brahman’s relation to them, repeatedly distinguishing the finite self’s conditioned modes from the unconditioned Lord. Its architectural role is to purify the object of upāsanā from confusion with the defects of saṃsāric subjectivity.

- **Adhyāya 3, pāda 3** opens with *Sarvavedāntapratyayādhikaraṇa* (p. 293). Here the issue is how far distinct Upaniṣadic *vidyās* may be combined, when they are one, and when they remain separate. This is intramural system-building: Rāmānuja refuses lazy harmonization no less than he refuses fragmentation.

- **Adhyāya 3, pāda 4** opens with *Puruṣārthādhikaraṇa* (p. 327). It discusses the necessity of śama-dama and other auxiliaries, the role of āśrama-dharmas, *mauna*, and the timing of worldly and liberating fruits. The argumentative point is that liberating knowledge is cultivated, disciplined upāsanā, not bare sentence-cognition.

- **Adhyāya 4, pāda 1** opens with *Āvṛttyadhikaraṇa* (p. 344). It defines Vedāntic “knowing” as repeated contemplative continuity, then treats ātmatvopāsanā, the limits of symbolic meditation, posture, and persistence until death. This is the direct anti-minimalist correction to any theory of liberation by one punctual cognitive event.

- **Adhyāya 4, pāda 2** opens with *Vāgadhikaraṇa* (p. 352). Speech enters mind, mind enters prāṇa, prāṇa with the jīva enters the elements; the text then examines how far knower and non-knower share the same initial departure. The architecture is transitional: soteriology is now being converted into a death-process.

- **Adhyāya 4, pāda 3** begins with a damaged OCR heading, but the content clearly opens the *archirādi* sequence (p. 360). Subsequent adhikaraṇas on Vāyu, Varuṇa, the *ātivāhikas*, and the “kārya” Brahmaloka decide route-order and distinguish which upāsakas are led where. Polemically this prevents flattening all postmortem destinies into one undifferentiated ascent.

- **Adhyāya 4, pāda 4** opens with *Saṃpadyāvirbhāva* (p. 367) and ends with *Anāvṛttiḥ śabdāt* (p. 376). Liberation is the manifestation of the self’s own nature in Brahman; it includes bliss, purity, and truth of will, but excludes the Lord’s unique cosmic governance. The final architecture is therefore intimacy without symmetry: the mukta is not reabsorbed into nothing, but neither does it become a second Īśvara.

## 4. Load-bearing passages — engaged at depth

**1. BS 1.1.1, *Jijñāsādhikaraṇa*, pp. 1-2**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `brahmaṇo jijñāsā brahmajijñāsā ... brahmaśabdena ca svabhāvato nirasta-nikhila-doṣo 'navadhikātiśayā[saṅkhyeya?]-kalyāṇa-guṇa-gaṇaḥ puruṣo[ttama?] 'bhidhīyate ... ataḥ sarveśvara eva jijñāsākarmatāṃ brahma ... jñātum icchā jijñāsā; icchāyā iṣyamāṇa-prādhānyād iṣyamāṇaṃ jñānam iha vidhīyate.`

**Close English:** “`brahmajijñāsā` is the desire to know Brahman. The genitive in `brahmaṇaḥ` is objective. By the word ‘Brahman’ there is denoted the Supreme Person, intrinsically devoid of every defect and possessed of an unsurpassed [countless?] host of auspicious qualities. Therefore Brahman, that is, the Lord, is what stands as the object of inquiry. `Jijñāsā` is the wish to know; because in a wish what is wanted is primary, it is the knowledge-to-be-attained that is here enjoined.”

The first move is grammatical and decisive. Rāmānuja does not begin by asking what kind of absolute Brahman might be; he begins by fixing the compound. `Brahma-jijñāsā` is read as a *tatpuruṣa* with objective genitive: *brahmaṇaḥ jijñāsā*. Brahman is the object sought, not the hidden subject spontaneously disclosing itself by a single sentence. This grammatical choice is already a soteriological choice.

The lexical loading of `brahma` is equally decisive. The term is not left open to a nirguṇa minimum. It names the Lord as free from defect and replete with auspicious qualities. That means the rest of the commentary proceeds under a prior semantic constraint: wherever Vedānta speaks centrally, it must be made answerable to a personal, plenary, world-causing Brahman.

Pāṇinian-rich note. `Brahmajijñāsā = brahmaṇaḥ jijñāsā`: a genitive *tatpuruṣa*. Rāmānuja explicitly glosses the genitive as `karmaṇi ṣaṣṭhī`, so `brahmaṇaḥ` is the object (*karman*) of the implied act of knowing. In `brahmaśabdena ... puruṣottamo 'bhidhīyate`, `brahmaśabdena` is karaṇa, “by the word Brahman”; `puruṣottamaḥ` is the nominative subject of the passive `abhidhīyate`; `nirasta-nikhila-doṣa` is a karmadhāraya/bahuvrīhi-like adjectival complex, and `kalyāṇa-guṇa-gaṇaḥ` is a ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa compressed into a quality-cluster. The sentence is doing semantics, not mystical exhortation: it tells us what the word picks out.

**2. BS 1.1.13, *Ānandamayādhikaraṇa*, p. 87**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `ānandamayo 'bhyāsāt. ānandamayaḥ paramātmā ... vijñānamayo hi jīvaḥ; na buddhimātram ... vijñānamayāj jīvād anyas tad-antaraḥ paramātmā ānandamayaḥ.`

**Close English:** “`Ānandamaya` [means] the Supreme Self, because of repeated emphasis. For the `vijñānamaya` is the jīva, not mere intellect. Distinct from that `vijñānamaya` jīva, as the inner one, is the Supreme Self, the `ānandamaya`.”

The important point is not simply that Rāmānuja identifies *ānandamaya* with Brahman. The argument turns on ordered interiority. The *vijñānamaya* is the finite conscious self; the *ānandamaya* is “other than” that jīva and interior to it. The Taittirīya’s sequence is therefore not collapsing inward toward a featureless self; it is stratifying embodiment, jīva, and the indwelling Lord.

This is one of the key anti-Advaitic moves of the first adhyāya. A locus that could be taken as the deepest layer of the self is instead read as revealing a distinct inner Brahman. The text’s ontology of intimacy is already present here: interiority does not entail identity.

**3. BS 2.1.2, *Yogapratyuktyadhikaraṇa*, pp. 187-188**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `etena kāpila-smṛti-nirākaraṇena yoga-smṛtir api pratyuktā ... abrahyātmaka-pradhāna-kāraṇa-vādān animitta-kāraṇa-mātreśvarābhyupagamāc ca ... yoga-smṛtir api ... bhrānti-mūleti na tayā vedāntopabṛṃhaṇaṃ nyāyyam.`

**Close English:** “By this refutation of Kapila’s *smṛti*, the Yoga *smṛti* too is refuted. For it teaches a pradhāna-cause that is not Brahman, and admits an Īśvara who is only an efficient cause; therefore that Yoga *smṛti* too has error at its root, and it is not proper to use it to supplement Vedānta.”

Rāmānuja’s criterion for admissible secondary authority is stringent. A system does not become Vedāntically serviceable merely by mentioning Īśvara or meditation. If its Īśvara is not the Brahman who is both upādāna and nimitta, and if its metaphysics leaves causality to a non-Brahman pradhāna, it cannot function as *upabṛṃhaṇa* of śruti.

This matters for the broader polemical map. The text is not anti-philosophical; it is anti-rival-authority. It will absorb what can be subordinated to Vedānta, but it refuses any parallel explanatory sovereignty.

**4. Adhyāya 3, pāda 1, *Tadantarapratipatti* and *Prāṇagati*, pp. 268-270**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `tadantarapratipattau raṃhati saṃpariṣvakta iti ... itaś ca bhūta-sūkṣma-pariṣvaktasya gamanam iti gamyate ... tam utkrāmantaṃ prāṇo 'nūtkrāmati ... gṛhītvaitāni saṃyāti vāyur gandhān ivāśayāt.`

**Close English:** “At the obtaining of another body, it moves while embraced [by subtle elements] ... and from this too it is understood that the one accompanied by subtle elements goes forth ... ‘When it departs, the vital breath departs after it’ ... ‘Taking these, the Lord goes, as the wind carries scents from their seat.’”

The jīva does not travel as a bare point. It departs with prāṇas and with subtle elemental accompaniment. Rebirth is therefore not a simple reset but a materially conditioned continuity. This is a strikingly realist account of embodiment: even between gross bodies, the self’s transit is not stripped of ontic equipment.

The absolutive `gṛhītvā` is doing real work. The main verb is `saṃyāti`: it goes, having taken these. What is carried is not illusory overlay but the basis of future embodiment. That strengthens the commentary’s refusal of any cheap opposition between metaphysics and world-process.

**5. Adhyāya 4, pāda 1, *Ātmatvopāsanādhikaraṇa*, p. 345**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `upāsitur ātmety evopāsyam ... yathā pratyagātmanaḥ svaśarīraṃ pratyātmatvād devo 'haṃ manuṣyo 'ham ity anusandhānam; tathā pratyagātmano 'py ātmatvāt paramātmanas tasyāpy aham ity evānusandhānaṃ yuktam.`

**Close English:** “For the worshipper, Brahman is to be worshipped precisely as the self ... just as, because one’s own body stands in a self-relation to the inner self, there is the apprehension ‘I am a god,’ ‘I am a man’; so too, because the Supreme Self stands as the self of the inner self, the apprehension ‘I’ with respect to that also is appropriate.”

This is one of the most characteristic passages in the file. The logic is neither simple identity nor simple external relation. Brahman is to the jīva as the jīva is to the body: inner self to dependent mode. That is why the language of “I” can be extended upward without abolishing difference.

This passage is also the cleanest statement of why Viśiṣṭādvaita is not just theism added onto Vedānta. The ontology is hierarchically reflexive: body, self, and the self of the self. The whole later debate about whether the world is real turns here on a more basic question, namely what kind of unity can hold without flattening the terms united.

**6. Adhyāya 4, pāda 4, *Jagadvyāpāravarja* and *Anāvṛtti*, pp. 374-376**

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `jagad-vyāpāraḥ ... tad-varjaṃ ... muktasyaiśvaryam ... anāvṛttiḥ śabdād anāvṛttiḥ śabdāt.`

**Close English:** “Cosmic activity [the regulation of the world’s conscious and non-conscious orders] is excluded; the liberated one’s lordly power is with that exception ... ‘No return, on the authority of scripture; no return, on the authority of scripture.’”

Here the asymmetry between mukta and Īśvara is made explicit. Liberation grants bliss, purity, and a form of sovereignty, but not the Lord’s unique governance of the whole conscious-nonconscious cosmos. The mukta is not reduced; equally, it is not promoted into a second world-Lord.

This is the strongest resistance-point in the text for any synthesis that wants a more radical identity-claim at the summit. Rāmānuja will allow intimacy, likeness, and unsurpassable beatitude; he will not allow shared *jagadvyāpāra*. But precisely because the passage ends with *anāvṛtti*, liberation is still permanent participation, not extinction.

## 5. The text’s relation to the position

- On the project’s mapped lineages, *Śrī-Bhāṣya* belongs centrally to the Yāmuna -> Rāmānuja -> Vedānta-Deśika chain. It is not part of the Bhartṛprapañca -> Bhāskara -> Madhva -> Caitanya -> Aurobindo trajectory, but it is a major adjacent realism: anti-world-negation, strongly theistic, and metaphysically serious about manifestation.

- Relative to Padmapāda, Prakāśātman, Vidyāraṇya, and Madhusūdana, the text is a direct antagonist. It does not yet name those later authors, but the opening anonymous *pūrvapakṣa* already targets the package “avidyā-nivṛtti is mokṣa; sentence-born knowledge of *tat tvam asi* is sufficient; upāsanā is secondary.”

- Relative to Vallabha and Nimbārka, the text is an earlier Vaiṣṇava realism whose basic pressure is similar: Brahman is real, the world is real, relation is real, and Māyāvāda is the target. But its distinctive grammar is the body-self relation, not Vallabha’s pure non-duality or Nimbārka’s difference-non-difference.

- Whom it refutes by name in the inspected file: Kapila; Yoga/Hiraṇyagarbha; Akṣapāda and Kaṇāda; Saugata positions including Yogācāra and Mādhyamika; Jaina/Ārhata positions; Pāśupata and Śaiva systems. It does not simply reject the Pāñcarātra; it defends it once read as a Vāsudeva-centered exposition of Vedāntic truth.

- On T2, the text is strongly useful but not in every respect. It strongly supports the recovery of realism against world-negation, because Brahman is both upādāna and nimitta and the jagat is the Lord’s real body. But it does not help preserve an Advaitic sublation-grammar; it wants the rival ontology displaced, not rehabilitated.

- On T7, the text is mostly resistant. The anonymous *pūrvapakṣa* speaks in the register of avidyā and sentence-born knowledge, but the siddhānta does not develop a positive ontology of avidyā, still less Brahman as both its *āśraya* and *viṣaya*. The commentary’s strategy is to deny the need for that framework.

- On T11, it is a partial ally. It offers a full ontology of phenomenological states: waking, dream, sleep, death, transit, and liberation all have determinate ontological status. But there is no Aurobindonian involution-evolution story here; the text is concerned with dependence, not cosmic emergence.

- On T9, it is a very strong ally. The world is not to be dismissed as nullity; it is real as divine body and divine governance. The anti-Māyāvāda force of the text lies not in crude external realism, but in the claim that unity does not require the evacuation of manifestation.

- The main friction-point is adhyāya 4.4: *jagadvyāpāravarjam*. The position may wish to retain stronger non-dual language at the summit; Rāmānuja refuses that if it threatens the asymmetry between Īśvara and mukta. A second friction-point is 4.1’s insistence that liberation-relevant knowledge is reiterated upāsanā, not merely a punctiliar cognition.

## 6. What the user can productively take

This text gives the corpus a first-rank primary source for a panentheistic realism inside Vedānta itself: Brahman as both material and efficient cause, the world as the Lord’s body, and liberation as permanent participation rather than erasure. It is especially useful wherever the project needs a rigorous Vaiṣṇava critique of world-negating readings that still preserves metaphysical depth.

Just as importantly, the text poses a real challenge that cannot be skipped. Any synthesis that wants to keep non-dual resources must answer Rāmānuja’s two strongest complaints: that Vedānta cannot terminate in a qualityless referent, and that liberation cannot be reduced to one-time sentence-cognition plus avidyā-talk. So the gain is double: strong citation-support for realism, and a sharpened burden of reply where the position wants to keep more of Advaita than Rāmānuja permits.
