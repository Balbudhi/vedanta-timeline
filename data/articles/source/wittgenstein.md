# Wittgenstein — language-games, hinges, and the limits of the sayable

*A reader's document on Ludwig Wittgenstein for the user's project. The central question is not whether Wittgenstein was "early" and then "late," as though two unrelated men had written under one name. It is whether the problem of the limits of saying remains one problem from the `Tractatus` through `Philosophical Investigations` to `On Certainty`, and whether the later work should be read as abandoning that problem or as resituating it. Primary English witnesses: Ogden's 1922 `Tractatus`, Anscombe's `Philosophical Investigations`, Anscombe-von Wright `On Certainty`. Primary German witnesses: `Logisch-philosophische Abhandlung`, `Philosophische Untersuchungen`, `Über Gewißheit`. Where the article relates Wittgenstein to the user's scope/register methodology, that relation is marked as the user's reading, not as Wittgenstein's own self-description.*

## Reader's Orientation

### The biographical arc in one page

Ludwig Wittgenstein was born in Vienna in 1889 into one of the great industrial families of the Habsburg world. He began not as a professional philosopher but as an engineer, and the engineering beginning matters. It helps explain why his writing never fully loses the feel of a man who wants to know what a sign does, what a mechanism does, what makes a structure hold, and where a device ceases to work. When he came to Cambridge in 1911 to study with Bertrand Russell, he entered analytic philosophy at the moment when the tradition thought it had at last found a method severe enough to purify philosophy into logic. Russell saw at once that this was no ordinary student. Moore saw it too. Very quickly Wittgenstein was not merely learning the new logic but pressing it to its breaking point.

He then withdrew to Norway, wrote in isolation, served in the First World War on the Eastern and Italian fronts, carried his notebooks through the war, and completed the text that became the `Logisch-philosophische Abhandlung`. The book appeared in German in 1921, and the Ogden-Ramsey English translation under the title `Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus` followed in 1922. This alone would have secured Wittgenstein a permanent place in twentieth-century philosophy. He then did something almost no major philosopher does: he largely abandoned philosophy. He worked as a village schoolteacher, worked as a gardener's assistant, and helped design the severe modernist house for his sister in Vienna. The silence was not a retirement into comfort. It was a refusal of premature authorship.

When he returned to Cambridge in 1929, he did not return as the triumphant author of a completed system. He returned as a man increasingly convinced that the earlier book had forced too much of language through one logical grid. The writings of the 1930s and 1940s are therefore not supplements to an intact `Tractatus`; they are records of a labor of self-correction. `Philosophical Remarks`, `Philosophical Grammar`, the `Blue` and `Brown Books`, the `Big Typescript`, and eventually `Philosophical Investigations` all belong to this labor. The late notes collected as `On Certainty`, written in the last years of his life and published posthumously in 1969, show the labor not ended but displaced onto a new terrain. He died in Cambridge in 1951; his most influential later works were published after his death.

This posthumous condition matters. Wittgenstein does not give us a cleanly staged public doctrine. He gives us a body of work in which the problem is often clearer than the official solution. That is one reason he matters for the user's project. He is not useful as a master who has already settled the question. He is useful as the analytic philosopher who drove the demand for logical exactness to its limit and then refused to lie when the exactness failed to govern the whole field.

### The single thread

The single thread of this article is simple to state and difficult to sustain. In the `Tractatus`, Wittgenstein seeks the limit of language from inside one ideal logical grammar: what can be said is what can be propositionally pictured, and what cannot be said is not thereby nothing, since it shows itself. In `Philosophical Investigations`, the question of "*the* grammar of language" is dissolved. Meaning is not secured by one logical form mirrored between proposition and world; meaning is use within a language-game, and language-games are plural. There is no imperial language-game above the others into which the rest can be translated without remainder. `On Certainty` adds the late structural clarification: practices of doubting, proving, testing, and asserting all turn only because some commitments are already functioning as bedrock or hinge.

That means the movement from the earlier to the later work is not from rigor to laxity. It is from one kind of rigor to another. The earlier rigor asks what form a proposition must have in order to represent the world. The later rigor asks what role an expression must have in a practice in order to count as meaningful at all.

The user's reading of Wittgenstein begins here. It takes the late plural-language-games picture as a major ally against the demand that one grammar, one register, one mode of intelligibility, or one discursive form legislate for the whole real. But it refuses the quietist temptation. The late Wittgenstein is often read as if he had shown that metaphysical ambition as such was a disease and that the only honest philosophy is therapeutic deflation. That is not the reading taken here. The reading taken here is stricter and more useful. Wittgenstein destroys a particular illusion — the illusion that grammar, merely by virtue of being grammar, mirrors ontology. He does not thereby prove that there is no real, that all forms of discourse are equal, or that philosophical thought must end in a smiling silence. He proves, rather, that every serious claim has to earn its right to be made in the language-game in which it is made. That discipline is purgative.

The user's position takes the purgation and then goes further than Wittgenstein officially goes. It adds an explicitly realist-pluralist commitment: different disciplined language-games can track different genuine aspects of one real, without being collapsed into a Hegelian super-perspective and without being dispersed into postmodern relativism. That further move is the user's move, and the article will mark it as such.

### The works that matter

Four works matter most for this article. The first is the `Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus`, the purest expression of the single-grammar ambition and also the first place where Wittgenstein says, with unforgettable compression, that what matters most cannot simply be propositionally said. The second is the transition-work of the early 1930s: `Philosophical Remarks` and the surrounding manuscripts matter not because they contain a polished doctrine but because they show Wittgenstein discovering that the earlier framework cannot handle actual linguistic life. The third is `Philosophical Investigations`, the central text for language-games, family resemblance, rule-following, and the private language argument. It is not one argument but a sustained re-education of philosophical attention. The fourth is `On Certainty`, the place where the late Wittgenstein's understanding of bedrock, world-picture, and hinge commitments becomes most explicit.

Other texts matter at the edges. `Culture and Value` gives occasional late religious and cultural remarks. The lectures on religious belief are relevant if one wants Wittgenstein on faith-language. `Remarks on the Foundations of Mathematics` belongs to the same later orbit, though it is not a central source for the present article. But the spine is still `Tractatus`, transition, `PI`, and `OC`.

### The user's polemic, stated plainly

The user's standing polemic against a large part of Western philosophy is that grammar has too often been ontologized. The form of judgment is mistaken for the structure of the real. Predication becomes ontology, dialectic becomes cosmology, and logical articulation becomes metaphysical legislation. On this polemical map Hegel is one target, but the early Wittgenstein is another. The picture theory, in its strong form, says that a proposition represents a possible state of affairs because it shares logical form with the reality it pictures. This is a more austere and analytic version of the same temptation: to take one privileged discursive form and let it dictate what counts as world.

Why, then, does Wittgenstein matter so much to the user's project? Because Wittgenstein is also the philosopher who most radically undoes this temptation from within analytic philosophy itself. The late Wittgenstein does not offer a rival ontology. He offers a discipline that blocks the imperial move. `PI 23` and `PI 43` deny that one abstract logical template can explain the multiplicity of what language does. `PI 201-242` denies that rule-following is grounded by a private rail hidden behind practice. `PI 243-315` denies that inwardness can legislate its own criteria in total privacy. `OC 94-99` and `OC 341-359` deny that inquiry can begin from nowhere and justify everything at once.

So Wittgenstein is not the destination. He is the great analytic purgative. He clears the ground of one sort of confusion. The user's project then asks what positive metaphysical and methodological work becomes possible after that clearing.

- `zeigen` / `sagen`: to show / to say.
- `Sprachspiel`: language-game, a practice-bound use of words, not a toy example alone.
- `Lebensform`: form of life, the lived background of practice within which a language-game has sense.
- `Familienähnlichkeit`: family resemblance, overlapping similarities without one common essence.
- `Regelfolgen`: rule-following.
- `Gebrauch`: use.
- `Übersicht` or `übersichtliche Darstellung`: perspicuous presentation, the late method of arranging reminders rather than deducing from axioms.
- `Weltbild`: world-picture.
- `Gewißheit`: certainty.

## Part I — `Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus`

The `Tractatus` is the analytic will to exactness in its purest and most dangerous form. It seeks to tell us what a proposition must be in order to represent the world at all. It therefore seeks the limits of sense, and it seeks them once and for all. Its severity is what makes it indispensable. If the user's project is partly a war against the imperialization of one grammar, then the `Tractatus` is one of the canonical imperial texts. If the user's project is also a defense of disciplined silence where assertion outruns its vehicle, then the `Tractatus` is one of the canonical sources of that discipline. The book matters on both sides.

### I.1 — `1-2.063`: world, fact, object, state of affairs

The opening move is famous because it is so flatly unromantic. It does not begin with consciousness, God, or substance. It begins with world. And even that world is not first given as a heap of things.

`1.1` says:

> German:
>
> "Die Welt ist die Gesamtheit der Tatsachen, nicht der Dinge."
>
> English:
>
> "The world is the totality of facts, not of things."

This sentence does at least three things at once. First, it blocks a naive substance-metaphysics: the world is not a warehouse. Second, it makes actuality primary over inventory; what matters is not just what objects there are, but what is the case. Third, it implies that the space in which the world is to be articulated is already formal. Facts are not merely there. They are what can be the case. That is why the next steps matter.

`2` says:

> "What is the case, the fact, is the existence of atomic facts."

`2.01` says:

> "An atomic fact is a combination of objects (entities, things)."

And `2.012` sharpens the logic:

> "In logic nothing is accidental..."

That is the crucial point. If a thing can occur in a state of affairs, that possibility must already belong to it. Combinability is not added from the outside; it belongs to the formal identity of the object. The ontology is therefore already a grammar. Objects are what can enter possible combinations, facts are the obtaining of such combinations, and world is the totality of such facts.

The article must resist the temptation to read this as a mere antique version of analytic atomism and leave it there. The stronger point is methodological. At the very beginning of Wittgenstein's career, the world is being disciplined by one logical space. There is one field of formal possibility. There are not many irreducible modes of articulation, not many registers of truthful discourse. There is one rigor, and all meaning must pass through it. That is why the later turn matters so much. The later Wittgenstein will not simply loosen this discipline; he will show that the discipline itself was pitched at the wrong level.

### I.2 — The picture theory

The picture theory is the first great machine built inside this ontology. `2.12` gives its cleanest formula:

> "The picture is a model of reality."

This is often repeated too quickly. It does not mean merely that propositions resemble what they describe the way portraits resemble faces. What matters is structural correspondence: the picture and the pictured must share a logical form. `2.18` makes this explicit. Without something in common between picture and reality, picturing could not happen at all.

Then `3` gives the further compression:

> "The logical picture of the facts is the thought."

A thought is not a ghostly private item later clothed in words. It is already the logical picture. Proposition, thought, picture, and possible fact belong to one articulated field.

The article needs to say plainly what is powerful in this view before criticizing it. What is powerful is that Wittgenstein is trying to explain how proposition can be answerable to world without collapsing answerability into mere causal association. A proposition is not just triggered by a fact; it is internally related to the possible situation it depicts. That is the attraction. It makes meaning strict, and it makes nonsense strict too.

The user's critique begins exactly here. This is the pure analytic form of the claim that one formal structure can legislate what counts as meaningful reality-description. The picture theory is therefore one of the great examples of what the user's methodology resists. It is not wrong because it is too rigorous. It is wrong because it assumes there is one privileged logical regime in which the world must become available if it is to be available at all. The later Wittgenstein will not deny that some language-games have strict internal form. He will deny that the whole life of meaning can be reduced to one such form.

### I.3 — Philosophy as clarification, not doctrine

The `Tractatus` is often remembered for its atomism and its mystical ending. Its middle doctrine of philosophy is no less important. Here the book tries to define philosophy itself. The decisive cluster is `4.111-4.116`.

`4.111`:

> German:
>
> "Die Philosophie ist keine der Naturwissenschaften."
>
> English:
>
> "Philosophy is not one of the natural sciences."

`4.112`:

> German:
>
> "Der Zweck der Philosophie ist die logische Klärung der Gedanken.
> Die Philosophie ist keine Lehre, sondern eine Tätigkeit."
>
> English:
>
> "The object of philosophy is the logical clarification of thoughts.
> Philosophy is not a theory but an activity."

This is a major statement. It means philosophy, on Wittgenstein's early view, does not produce a rival body of propositions standing beside chemistry or physics. It clarifies what can be meaningfully said, removes confusion, and marks limits.

`4.114` and `4.115` give the sharper edge:

> German:
>
> "Sie soll das Denkbare abgrenzen und damit das Undenkbare."
>
> "Sie wird das Unsagbare bedeuten, indem sie das Sagbare klar darstellt."
>
> English:
>
> "It should limit the thinkable and thereby the unthinkable."
>
> "It will mean the unspeakable by clearly displaying the speakable."

Already one can see the later continuity. The later Wittgenstein will no longer believe that there is one ideal logical syntax through which this limiting work is done. But the conception of philosophy as an activity of clarification rather than an accumulation of speculative theses survives. This is why it is too easy to oppose the later work to the earlier as if the earlier book were all doctrine and the later all therapy. Even in the `Tractatus`, philosophy is not one more theory.

The book tries to occupy a more difficult position. It wants to say just enough to show the limits of saying. That is why it is structurally unstable, and that instability is not accidental: it belongs to the project.

### I.4 — Saying and showing

The distinction between saying and showing is the central surviving achievement of the `Tractatus`. It is also the hardest part to state without flattening it.

The key thought is that some things cannot be propositionally represented because they are not further facts among facts. They are the enabling form of representation itself. Logical form is the most obvious example. A proposition cannot state its own representational relation as one more represented fact without circularity; it exhibits that relation in functioning as a proposition at all. That is why `4.022` matters:

> "The proposition shows its sense."

And that is why `4.12` and the surrounding propositions matter: language cannot say what it must already be structurally deploying in order to say anything.

This is where one should be very exact in relation to the user's methodology. The temptation would be to say: "This is just `neti neti` in analytic dress." That is too fast. The better claim is narrower. The `Tractatus` gives one of the strictest Western statements of the difference between propositionally articulable content and what is only exhibited by the functioning of the medium. That is a genuine structural parallel to certain Indian grammatical and apophatic disciplines. But it is still framed inside a very specific logical project. There is no `adhikara`-bound soteriology here, no developed hierarchy of discourse-levels, no yogic practice-context. The article must preserve both the parallel and the difference. The parallel matters because it shows the problem is real; the difference matters because the traditions are not to be collapsed.

This is the structural axis on which the later transformation turns. When the showing/saying distinction is reframed in `PI` as the difference between what can be stated within a language-game and what can only be shown by the functioning of the game itself — by the form of life within which the game is at home — the early discipline is not abandoned. It is relocated. The article will defend this reframing on the texts in §III.23, not assert it here as a slogan.

### I.5 — `5.6-5.632`: the limits of language and the metaphysical subject

The best-known sentence in the book may be `5.6`:

> English:
>
> "The limits of my language mean the limits of my world."

Many bad readings begin here. One bad reading makes the claim merely psychological, as if my vocabulary happened to imprison my subjective experience. Another bad reading makes it cultural relativism avant la lettre, as if different linguistic communities simply had different worlds with no further question. Neither is right. The claim belongs to the architecture of representation: what can count as world for me is bounded by the space of sense available in my language.

But then `5.62` complicates the matter:

> English:
>
> "In fact what solipsism means, is quite correct, only it cannot be said, but it shows itself."

This is one of the most important sentences in the book for the user's project, not because it secretly contains a doctrine of witness-consciousness — it does not — but because it articulates a structure. The subject is not found as one more constituent in the world it conditions.

This becomes explicit in `5.632`:

> English:
>
> "The subject does not belong to the world but it is a limit of the world."

The user's reading can legitimately say that this is structurally close to later first-person analyses in which subjectivity is not recoverable as an object among objects. KCB's "subject as freedom" is one such internal corpus analogue. But the article must not pretend that Wittgenstein is here doing Vedanta. He is not. He is articulating the paradox generated when the conditions of representation are sought as represented items. The subject recedes because the demand is malformed. The receding subject is a limit-structure, not a hidden object.

### I.6 — `6.4-7`: ethics, the mystical, silence

The final movement of the `Tractatus` is the part most readers remember and least know how to place. It begins with a startling flattening. `6.4` says:

> German:
>
> "Alle Sätze sind gleichwertig."
>
> English:
>
> "All propositions are of equal value."

Equal value here does not mean all assertions are equally important in life. It means that no proposition, merely qua proposition, can carry ethical elevation in its logical form. That is why `6.41` follows as it does:

> German:
>
> "Der Sinn der Welt muß außerhalb ihrer liegen.
> In der Welt ist alles wie es ist und geschieht alles wie es geschieht;
> es gibt in ihr keinen Wert..."
>
> English:
>
> "The sense of the world must lie outside the world.
> In the world everything is as it is and happens as it does happen.
> In it there is no value..."

This is a decisive anti-naturalist move. Facts do not add up to value. Description does not climb by accumulation into meaning.

Then `6.421` states the ethical consequence:

> German:
>
> "Es ist klar, daß sich die Ethik nicht aussprechen läßt.
> Die Ethik ist transcendental.
> (Ethik und Aesthetik sind Eins.)"
>
> English:
>
> "It is clear that ethics cannot be expressed.
> Ethics are transcendental.
> (Ethics and aesthetics are one.)"

This is not the same as saying ethics is unreal. It is the exact opposite. Ethics is too high to become one more proposition among the propositions of the world. That is why `6.44` and `6.45` are so central:

> German:
>
> "Nicht wie die Welt ist, ist das Mystische, sondern dass sie ist."
>
> English:
>
> "Not how the world is, is the mystical, but that it is."

And:

> German:
>
> "Die Anschauung der Welt sub specie aeterni ist ihre Anschauung als begrenztes Ganzes."
>
> English:
>
> "The contemplation of the world sub specie aeterni is its contemplation as a limited whole."

Then `6.522`:

> German:
>
> "Es gibt allerdings Unaussprechliches.
> Dies zeigt sich, es ist das Mystische."
>
> English:
>
> "There is indeed the inexpressible.
> This shows itself; it is the mystical."

And finally `7`:

> German:
>
> "Wovon man nicht sprechen kann, darüber muß man schweigen."
>
> English:
>
> "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent."

The article must be very disciplined here. The usual analytic reading is to treat these ending propositions either as embarrassing residue or as therapeutic performance. The usual anti-analytic mystical reading is to treat them as the triumphant revelation of a hidden ineffable doctrine. Both are too simple.

The better reading is this. The `Tractatus` has shown that proposition is tied to a certain kind of representability. Ethics, value, and the fact that the world is at all do not become representable in that way. Therefore they cannot be said in that way. But because they structure the significance of life, they are not nothing. They show themselves. The user's reading will take this as a register-honest discipline: one can acknowledge what is not propositionally sayable without either denying it or fabricating a pseudoproposition about it. That is the value of `7`. It is not positivist contempt. It is formal honesty.

### I.7 — The ladder and the self-overthrow of the book

The most important single proposition for the interpretation of the `Tractatus` is `6.54`. It is the proposition that interprets the whole book.

It says:

> German:
>
> "Meine Sätze erläutern dadurch, dass sie der,
> welcher mich versteht,
> am Ende als unsinnig erkennt,
> wenn er durch sie — auf ihnen — über sie hinausgestiegen ist.
> (Er muß sozusagen die Leiter wegwerfen,
> nachdem er auf ihr hinaufgestiegen ist.)"
>
> English:
>
> "My propositions are elucidatory in this way:
> he who understands me finally recognizes them as senseless,
> when he has climbed out through them,
> on them,
> over them.
> (He must so to speak throw away the ladder,
> after he has climbed up on it.)"

This proposition makes every lazy reading impossible. If one says the book simply lays down doctrines about logical atoms, pictures, solipsism, ethics, and the mystical, `6.54` becomes decorative nonsense. If one says the book is sheer nonsense and nothing remains, then the entire labor of the book becomes an elaborate practical joke. Neither is adequate.

The right statement is harder. The book uses propositions to bring the reader to a point at which the urge to say what can only be shown is recognized as self-defeating. That means the propositions are not doctrines in the simple way Russellian theses would be doctrines. But it does not mean the book leaves nothing behind. What it leaves behind is a transformed relation to language and limit.

This is where the Diamond-Conant resolute reading and the Hacker-style standard reading each see one side of the truth. The resolute readers are right that `6.54` has to be taken with full seriousness: nothing in the book can be cashed out as a propositionally stated thesis hiding behind the disclaimer of nonsense. The standard readers are right that the book is not merely an empty performance: a transformed relation to the limits of expression remains, and that relation is the achievement of the book. What survives is not a set of occult truths hidden behind nonsense. What survives is a discipline: the discipline of not turning formal conditions into empirical statements, of not turning value into one more fact, of not turning the limit of language into a proposition that pretends to stand outside language. This article's position, stated plainly, is closer to Hacker on what survives the climb and closer to Diamond and Conant on how the climb works.

### I.8 — What the `Tractatus` confirms and what it cannot keep

What does the `Tractatus` confirm for the user's project? It confirms that there are limits internal to proposition, that not everything real or significant is therefore propositionally stateable, that philosophy is not best understood as a rival science, and that there is a point at which one must stop forcing language to say what its mode of articulation does not permit it to say.

What can it not keep? It cannot keep the claim that there is one ideal logical grammar under which the full life of meaning can be regimented. It cannot keep the assumption that ordinary language is basically a defective surface awaiting correction by one deep logical order. It cannot keep its own severe separation between what is to be said and the multiplicity of what language actually does in life. That is why the later turn is not optional. It is demanded by the pressure generated inside the earlier project itself.

## Part II — The transition out of the picture theory

The transition matters because without it the `Investigations` can be misread as merely anti-theoretical mood. That is false. The later Wittgenstein does not wake up one day and decide to prefer ordinary language to logic. He discovers that the earlier logic had been performing imperial work it could not justify.

### II.1 — Why the earlier framework breaks

The stock example is the color-exclusion problem. If a point in the visual field cannot be both red and green all over at once, that is not just a truth-functional matter — content itself matters. The earlier apparatus had hoped to treat the logic of proposition as prior and pure, with the world's possible articulations fitting into it. But cases like these show that the articulation of sense cannot be wholly abstracted from the kinds of use and object-domain involved.

The detail is less important than the structural result. The dream of one formal inventory of all meaningful proposition begins to crack. Once that crack appears, many others follow. Ordinary language is not an imperfect copy of an ideal symbolism waiting beneath it; it is a field of heterogeneous practices. The earlier demand for a single hidden form now looks less like rigor and more like a philosophical compulsion.

### II.2 — The new method: perspicuous presentation

What replaces the earlier method is not a new theory of how all language works. That would only reproduce the same disease. What replaces it is a new way of philosophizing: the later Wittgenstein arranges reminders, gives perspicuous presentations, and asks us to look rather than to postulate.

This is not anti-intellectual modesty. It is a strategic refusal to let explanation outrun what needs clarification. In the `Tractatus`, philosophy draws limits from inside one logical form. In the later work, philosophy draws limits by bringing the use of words into view so that bewitchment loses its hold. That is a profound continuity beneath a profound change: the continuity is the concern with limit, and the change is the site at which the limit is discovered.

### II.3 — Why the transition matters for the user's project

The user's scope/register methodology needs exactly this kind of internal self-correction. It needs a major Western philosopher who discovered, not by cultural eclecticism but by philosophical pressure, that one grammar could not do all the work. Wittgenstein is that philosopher. He is therefore a far better ally for the project than a merely loose pluralist would be. A loose pluralist starts with many discourses and shrugs. Wittgenstein begins with a maximal demand for exactness and is forced to pluralize. That makes the pluralization harder and more valuable.

### II.4 — Bridge to `Philosophical Investigations`

The `Investigations` should therefore be read, not as the anti-`Tractatus`, but as the text in which the earlier book's limit-question is re-opened under conditions more faithful to the life of language. The governing question is no longer "What must proposition be in order to picture reality?" It is: "What gives an expression its life, its point, its criteria, its role, within the practices in which it is actually used?" That question does not lower the philosophical ambition. It relocates it.

## Part III — `Philosophical Investigations`

`Philosophical Investigations` is one of the few philosophical books whose greatness lies partly in making the reader worse at a familiar kind of bad philosophy. It does not chiefly advance theses. It trains the eye, interrupts compulsions, and makes a philosopher notice what he had been educated not to notice. That is why it is so easy to vulgarize. Readers often seize one slogan ("`meaning is use`"), one image ("family resemblance"), or one isolated result ("private language is impossible") and mistake the whole for the fragment.

The actual work of the book is harder. Wittgenstein is trying to break the hold of the picture that there must be one hidden essence of language, one hidden act of meaning, one hidden rail of rule-following, one hidden private object guaranteeing sensation-language, one hidden foundation from which justification starts. Against each hidden thing he sets a practice; against each philosophical occultism he sets a use; against each demand for a final sublimed explanation he sets a perspicuous arrangement of what is already before us. The article's burden is to show that this is not anti-philosophical laziness. It is a new severity.

### III.1 — `PI 1-23`: the Augustinian picture and the first turn to language-games

The `Investigations` begins by quoting Augustine. This is not antiquarian ornament; it is a diagnosis. The Augustinian picture says, in effect, that words name objects and sentences combine such names. Language is then conceived as a system of labels attached to things, with understanding modeled on the linking of word and object. Wittgenstein is not attacking Augustine as a fool. He is attacking the hold that this model still has on philosophers.

The builders' language in `PI 2` is the first decisive move. One builder calls out "`block`," "`pillar`," "`slab`," "`beam`"; the other brings the corresponding item. This is not supposed to be the primitive original of all language. It is an object of comparison. Its importance lies elsewhere: it shows that language can be fully at work inside a highly restricted practical economy without yet resembling the philosopher's picture of naming in general. Here the word is not floating above an object in contemplative naming; it is a move in an activity.

This is why `PI 7` matters. Wittgenstein introduces the term `Sprachspiel` and says of such practices that they may be called language-games. The word "game" here is not decorative diminishment. It does not mean "something unserious." It means rule-governed practice, embedded performance, activity in which the use of words is inseparable from forms of action.

Then `PI 23` opens the field:

> German:
>
> "Es gibt unzählige Arten der Verwendung alles dessen,
> was wir 'Zeichen',
> 'Worte',
> 'Sätze' nennen."
>
> English:
>
> "There are countless kinds of use of all the things we call 'signs', 'words', 'sentences'."

The examples Wittgenstein then lists matter — they are not filler. Giving orders, obeying them, describing an object, reporting an event, speculating, forming and testing a hypothesis, translating, asking, thanking, cursing, greeting, praying. This list has two effects. First, it destroys the philosopher's wish to find one essence of language by taking the declarative proposition as sovereign. Second, it shows that language is already social, practical, and multiple before any general theory arrives.

This is one of the major anchor points for the user's project. If the user's thesis says that philosophical and religious traditions often occupy distinct registers and should not be forced into one flattened contest of propositions, `PI 23` is a powerful analytic ally. But the ally must be used exactly. Wittgenstein is not saying "Every discourse is equally true." He is saying "Do not assume in advance that what you call language has one hidden essence." That is a methodological clearing. The user's further claim, that distinct disciplined discourses can each be truthful with respect to one real, goes beyond Wittgenstein. The article must say so.

The early sections also include the famous discussion of ostensive definition (`PI 27-38`) and the regress on naming. The point of those sections is not that naming is impossible but that naming presupposes a stage-setting in language — one cannot fix a name by pointing unless the role the name is to play is already understood. The lesson generalizes. Acts that look semantically primitive turn out to depend on a background of established use.

### III.2 — `PI 43`: meaning and use

The most quoted sentence of the later Wittgenstein is `PI 43`, and also one of the most abused. Wittgenstein writes:

> English:
>
> "For a large class of cases—though not for all—in which we employ the word 'meaning'
> it can be defined thus:
> the meaning of a word is its use in the language."

The hedge at the beginning is essential: "For a large class of cases—though not for all." Anyone who quotes "`the meaning of a word is its use in the language`" as if it were an exceptionless axiom has already ceased to read Wittgenstein and begun to recite a slogan.

What is the sentence actually doing? It is relocating the question of meaning. Instead of asking for the occult accompaniment of a word, the object to which it is invisibly tied, or the mental image that endows it with life, one asks: What place does this expression have? How is it taught? What counts as correct and incorrect employment? What responses does it license? What activity is it a move within? That is the shift. Meaning is not reduced to a behaviorist surface; meaning is recalled from occult depth to public role.

This matters for the user's project at two levels. At the lower level, it is a discipline against fake profundity. One does not get to take a metaphysical word and assume that because it sounds large it therefore names one hidden super-object; one has to ask what the word does. At the higher level, it supports the register-methodological point that an expression's significance is inseparable from the practice-space in which it functions. A ritual utterance, a logical proof, a devotional cry, and a phenomenological description do not all have their sense in the same way. Wittgenstein does not himself sort these by the user's register-system. But he makes that kind of discipline thinkable.

### III.3 — `PI 65-67`: family resemblance and the refusal of essence

Once language-games are plural, the philosopher immediately asks the old question again: Very well, what is common to them all? What is the essence of language as such?

Wittgenstein stages this objection in `PI 65`. He does not dodge it. He answers it directly.

`PI 65`:

> German:
>
> "Statt etwas anzugeben,
> was allem,
> was wir Sprache nennen,
> gemeinsam ist,
> sage ich,
> es ist diesen Erscheinungen gar nicht Eines gemeinsam..."
>
> English:
>
> "Instead of producing something common to all that we call language,
> I am saying that these phenomena have no one thing in common..."

Then comes the famous example of games.

`PI 66`:

> German:
>
> "Betrachte z.B. einmal die Vorgänge,
> die wir 'Spiele' nennen...
> Sag nicht:
> 'Es muß ihnen etwas gemeinsam sein...'
> sondern schau..."
>
> English:
>
> "Consider for example the proceedings that we call 'games'...
> Don't say:
> 'There must be something common...'
> but look..."

And `PI 67`:

> German:
>
> "Ich kann diese Ähnlichkeiten nicht besser charakterisieren
> als durch das Wort 'Familienähnlichkeiten'..."
>
> English:
>
> "I can think of no better expression to characterize these similarities
> than 'family resemblances'..."

Family resemblance is not the doctrine that concepts are vague because language is sloppy. Nor is it a skeptical thesis that concepts have no structure. It is a positive claim about the actual structure they often have. They are held together by overlapping strands, criss-crossing similarities, relations of extension, relations of transformation, relations of use. No one fiber runs through the whole length. The image of the thread makes this explicit later in the same discussion.

This is one of the major places where the user's project can make a carefully bounded appropriation. The article can say: family resemblance destroys the premature demand for one essential definition of `religion`, `philosophy`, `darshana`, `mysticism`, or even `language`. It therefore helps explain why traditions may be related without being reducible to one common essence articulated once and for all. But the article must again mark the boundary. Wittgenstein's point is grammatical: he is describing how our concepts are actually held together. The user's additional move, that these plural conceptual articulations may track genuinely plural but co-real aspects of one real, is not a theorem of `PI 67`. It is a further realist commitment. That commitment can be made responsibly. It just should not be smuggled in under Wittgenstein's name.

### III.4 — `PI 65-88` against essentialism, and what this means for cross-tradition reading

The anti-essentialist force of this section matters far beyond the textbook example of games. Many comparative and polemical disputes in philosophy are generated by one false demand: before I grant that a family of practices belongs together, you must tell me its one defining essence. If the essence cannot be given, the category is treated as confused or merely verbal. That is a bad demand in many domains — bad for art, religion, philosophy, and for the Indian materials the user's corpus handles. A school-term like `Vedanta`, or a field-term like `darshana`, or a lived term like `bhakti`, often works through historical layering, shared canonical reference, interlocked practices, overlapping conceptual clusters, and internal contestation, not through one fixed essence stated in one proposition. What `PI 65-88` gives is permission to stop apologizing for that fact.

But permission is not doctrine. If one stops at Wittgenstein here, one may drift into a merely taxonomic pluralism — many cases, many uses, many related phenomena, no essence, end of story. The user's project cannot stop there. It needs a realism of alternative truths, not merely a sociology of concept-use. The internal corpus resource for that realism is KCB's line that these are not merely many truths but alternative truths. The article should therefore use Wittgenstein to block essentialist compulsion, and KCB to block the slide from anti-essentialism into relativism.

Sections `PI 71-77` deepen the point by attacking the demand for "sharp boundaries." Wittgenstein observes that a concept may have sharp use within a context without having sharp boundaries simpliciter, and that the demand for boundaries before use is itself often a philosophical compulsion rather than a requirement of communication. The lesson is that intelligibility is local to a practice; boundaries are drawn for purposes, not legislated in advance.

### III.5 — `PI 143-155`: learning a series and the temptation of hidden rails

The rule-following discussion is often treated as if it begins abruptly at `PI 201`. It does not. It begins in the teaching scenes. A learner is taught to continue a series. He goes from example to continuation. He is corrected. He is trained. The whole problem appears when the philosopher asks: What guaranteed that the learner meant this continuation rather than another? What hidden item accompanied the instruction and fixed the future path?

Already one can see the later argument. The temptation is always to imagine a hidden rail. Once the rail is imagined, one then asks how the subject accesses it, interprets it, or obeys it. The rule-following sections dismantle this imagination. But they do so by returning us to practice, not by denying normativity. That difference is everything.

### III.6 — `PI 201`: the paradox

The famous paradox is stated with exceptional bluntness. `PI 201`:

> German:
>
> "Dies war unser Paradox:
> kein Kursus von Handlungen könnte durch eine Regel bestimmt werden,
> weil jeder Kursus von Handlungen mit der Regel in Übereinstimmung zu bringen ist."
>
> English:
>
> "This was our paradox:
> no course of action could be determined by a rule,
> because every course of action can be made out to accord with the rule."

The force of the paradox is easy to miss if one treats it as an undergraduate puzzle. Its target is not merely one silly picture of arithmetic learning. Its target is the philosophical hope that behind every correct application lies a hidden item that already contains the application. If every present move must be guided by a prior interpretation of the rule, then that interpretation must itself be interpreted, and so on.

This is why the section matters so much for the user's project. A great deal of philosophical metaphysics works by positing exactly such hidden rails: the meaning already there behind use, the universal already there behind instances, the occult interior already there behind criteria, the meta-language already there behind actual language. Wittgenstein's paradox is a machine for destroying such rails. But what comes after the destruction? That is where the real disagreement among interpreters begins.

### III.7 — `PI 201-202`: interpretation is not enough

Immediately after stating the paradox, Wittgenstein gives the decisive turn. There is, he says, a misunderstanding here. The argument keeps generating one interpretation after another, as if interpretation itself were what secures rule-following. But if every application had to be mediated by another interpretation, we would never arrive at an actual move.

Then `PI 202`:

> German:
>
> "Und daher ist 'der Regel folgen' eine Gepflogenheit."
>
> English:
>
> "And hence also 'obeying a rule' is a practice."

This is one of the most important sentences in modern philosophy, and one of the most misread. It does not mean that rules are merely social habits with no normativity. It means that the normativity of rule-following is not secured by a private item standing behind the practice. A practice is not the absence of normativity. A practice is the medium in which normativity exists.

That is the point. The criteria of correct continuation, correct use, correct application are not added after the practice is in place. They are inseparable from what it is to count as the practice.

This matters for the user's anti-relativist use of Wittgenstein. If one says merely that language-games are many, one has not yet blocked relativism. But if one says that each language-game is a practice with its own criteria of correctness, one has at least blocked the silly form of relativism according to which meaning and correctness are whatever anyone happens to feel. Plurality is not arbitrariness. Plurality is plurality of forms of norm-governed life.

### III.8 — `PI 217-219`: bedrock and blind obedience

Later in the same sequence Wittgenstein writes:

`PI 217`:

> German:
>
> "Wenn ich die Begründungen erschöpft habe,
> so bin ich nun auf dem harten Felsen angelangt,
> und mein Spaten biegt sich zurück.
> Dann bin ich geneigt zu sagen:
> 'So handle ich eben.'"
>
> English:
>
> "If I have exhausted the justifications I have reached bedrock,
> and my spade is turned.
> Then I am inclined to say:
> 'This is simply what I do.'"

And `PI 219`:

> German:
>
> "Wenn ich der Regel folge,
> wähle ich nicht.
> Ich folge der Regel blind."
>
> English:
>
> "When I obey a rule,
> I do not choose.
> I obey blindly."

These lines frighten readers because they seem to say that in the end we hit brute behavior. That is a partial truth and a serious distortion. What Wittgenstein means is not that all justification collapses into an animal jerk. He means that the demand for further explicit justification does not go on forever. Practices bottom out in trained forms of response, and those forms of response are not irrational merely because they are not each time accompanied by a fresh explicit proof. If every move required a prior proof of its own legitimacy, no move would ever occur.

This is a point the article must make with special force, because the user's project wants to talk about disciplines, registers, forms of life, and scope, without having them all dissolve into optional perspectives. Wittgenstein's point is that justification has a human shape. It ends in life. That is not an embarrassment. It is the condition of meaningful normativity.

### III.9 — `PI 241-242`: agreement in form of life

The most dangerous late Wittgenstein passages for the user's project are `PI 241-242`, because this is where the easy relativist reading looks most tempting. `PI 241` says:

> German:
>
> "'So sagst du also,
> daß die Übereinstimmung der Menschen entscheide,
> was richtig und was falsch ist?'
> — Es ist,
> was Menschen sagen,
> das wahr und falsch ist;
> und in der Sprache stimmen die Menschen überein.
> Das ist keine Übereinstimmung der Meinungen,
> sondern der Lebensform."
>
> English:
>
> "'So you are saying that human agreement decides what is true and what is false?'
> — It is what human beings say that is true and false;
> and they agree in the language they use.
> That is not agreement in opinions but in form of life."

And `PI 242`:

> German:
>
> "Wenn die Sprache ein Verständigungsmittel sein soll,
> dann braucht es nicht nur eine Übereinstimmung in den Definitionen,
> sondern...
> eine Übereinstimmung in den Urteilen."
>
> English:
>
> "If language is to be a means of communication there must be agreement not only in definitions but also...
> in judgments."

The bad reading is obvious: if truth and falsity arise only inside human agreement, then truth is just what a community agrees to call true. That would indeed be a disaster for the user's project. But it is not what Wittgenstein is saying. He is not offering a plebiscitary theory of truth. He is identifying a condition of linguistic practice. Communication requires more than a lexicon. It requires convergences of training, reaction, measure, application, correction, and result. Agreement in judgments is not the same as agreement in opinions; it is the background regularity without which words would not have stable use. This does not abolish logic, as Wittgenstein immediately says. It shows where logic lives.

The article needs to make the anti-relativist point with absolute clarity. Wittgenstein is not saying that truth is socially invented from nothing. He is saying that the functioning of truth-claims presupposes publicly shared forms of life in which criteria are operative. The user's additional step is to say that there can be many such forms of life, many disciplined language-games, and many real aspects tracked through them, without there being one super-practice that reduces them all to itself. That is not in Wittgenstein as explicit doctrine. But Wittgenstein removes the chief modern obstacle to making such a claim: the demand that meaning must answer to one underlying formal order in the same way everywhere.

### III.10 — Against the Kripkean disaster reading

Because `PI 201-242` are so powerful, they have repeatedly been read as generating a universal skepticism about rule-following. The most famous version is the Kripkean one. Very compressed: the rule itself never uniquely determines its future applications; therefore there is no fact of the matter about what one meant by a rule; therefore correctness is saved only by community response.

Why is this too much? Because it turns Wittgenstein's anti-occultism into a global skeptical result he is trying to avoid. Wittgenstein's point is not that there are no facts of following a rule. It is that these facts do not reside in a hidden interpretation standing behind competent practice. That is a different claim. The article should say plainly: the skeptical reading mistakes the destruction of one bad model of normativity for the destruction of normativity itself.

If the user wants one analytic ally against relativism, it is precisely the non-skeptical reading of rule-following that matters. Rules have life in use. That is not a reduction. It is an account of where they are.

### III.11 — Why rule-following is the spine of the later Wittgenstein

Among the many famous themes in `PI`, rule-following should be treated as the spine for this article. Why? Because it gathers several other themes into one knot: meaning as use, language-games as practices, criteria as public, interpretation as insufficient, bedrock as lived response, form of life as background. All of them are there.

And this is the exact place where the user can most fruitfully connect Wittgenstein to the broader scope/register methodology. Registers are not to be imagined as free-floating "perspectives." They are disciplined forms of meaningful operation. They have internal criteria. They train participants into competent moves. They are not reducible to private intuitions, and they are not founded on one master metalanguage. That is an unmistakably Wittgensteinian structure, even if the user's further metaphysical elaboration is not Wittgenstein's own.

### III.12 — `PI 243`: why the private language discussion begins where it does

The private language argument is often reduced to one slogan: "There cannot be a private language." This is too crude from the first line. `PI 243` begins by noting that a human being can speak to himself, order himself, encourage himself, blame himself, answer himself. So the point is not that solitary linguistic performance is impossible. The point is to analyze what would be required for a language whose decisive signs referred to what only one subject could in principle know, with no public criteria of correctness.

The distinction matters. If one ignores it, the whole argument becomes childish. Wittgenstein is not saying "You cannot talk silently to yourself." He is asking: What would make the use of a sign correct or incorrect if there were no criteria beyond what seemed right to the subject at the moment? That is the real question.

### III.13 — `PI 258`: the diary and the collapse of private correctness

The diary example, with the subject writing "`S`" for a recurring inner sensation, is one of the most decisive philosophical thought-experiments of the century. The subject decides that whenever a certain inner occurrence appears, he will mark it with the sign `S`. The philosophical temptation is to say: that is enough — he can inwardly point to the sensation, associate the sign with it, and remember the association.

Wittgenstein's response is devastating. If the correctness of future applications depends only on what seems right to him, then the distinction between seeming right and being right collapses. No criterion has been established. There is only the illusion of one.

This matters for the user's project because it blocks a whole genre of private authority claims. The mere inward vividness of an experience does not yet give it intelligible articulation. One does not get to secure meaning by declaration from the inside. But again the article must keep the limit of the result. It does not follow that the inner is unreal. It follows that inner life is not self-legislating in total privacy. That is a very different conclusion.

### III.14 — `PI 293`: the beetle in the box

The most famous image in the private language discussion is the beetle. `PI 293`:

> German:
>
> "Angenommen,
> jeder hätte eine Schachtel mit etwas darin:
> wir nennen es einen 'Käfer'."
>
> English:
>
> "Suppose everyone had a box with something in it:
> we call it a 'beetle'."

Nobody can look into anyone else's box. Each person says he knows what a beetle is only by looking at his own. Wittgenstein then says: the thing in the box can drop out as irrelevant to the language-game. Even if the boxes differ, even if one is empty, the use of the word may remain intact.

The force of the example is often misunderstood. Wittgenstein is not denying that people have sensations. He is denying that the role of sensation-language is secured by private ostension to a hidden inner object. If the language-game works, it works because of public criteria governing the expression and use of the term, not because each speaker privately inspects an occult beetle.

This is an immense achievement of philosophical hygiene. It strips away a whole mythology of inner objects without denying the reality of pain, grief, fear, or delight.

### III.15 — `PI 304`: not a something, not a nothing

The corrective against the crude behaviorist reading comes a little later. `PI 304` says:

> German:
>
> "Es ist kein Etwas,
> aber auch nicht ein Nichts!"
>
> English:
>
> "It is not a something,
> but not a nothing either!"

This is one of Wittgenstein's finest sentences, and the article should use it. It marks the precise territory on which so many bad readings fail. The sensation is not an object in the model required by the philosopher's grammar of naming, but it is not therefore annihilated. What has been rejected is one grammar.

That sentence could serve as a motto for a much wider range of the user's project. Very often a philosophical dispute ends badly because one side says: if X is not a substantial object in my preferred grammar, then X must be nothing. Wittgenstein's reply is: no, what may need rejection is not the reality in question, but the grammar trying to force itself on us. This is an extraordinarily useful discipline.

### III.16 — The private language argument and the interior life

Now one can state the result more exactly. The private language argument blocks Cartesian privacy as the final court of sense. It blocks the idea that meaning is fixed by a private inward pointing inaccessible in principle to any public criteria. It does not prove that all interiority is reducible to outer behavior. It does not prove that introspection is worthless. It does not give a full positive psychology.

For the user's project, that limitation is not a defect. It is an opening. The article can responsibly say that Wittgenstein dismantles one bad model of inwardness; he does not by himself build a better model. That is where traditions with more explicit analyses of interior stratification can begin to matter.

But the article must still observe the anti-fabrication rule. One should not pretend that Wittgenstein secretly meant the layered `antahkarana`. He did not. The more exact claim is that the private language argument removes a major obstacle to thinking inward life without Cartesian privatism. That much is textual.

### III.17 — `PI 109`: philosophy against bewitchment

The private language discussion belongs inside a larger therapeutic conception of philosophy. One of the most important programmatic remarks is `PI 109`. It says:

> English:
>
> "Philosophy is a battle against the bewitchment of our intelligence by means of language."

This sentence is frequently taken to authorize anti-metaphysical quietism. That reading sees the battle as directed against philosophy itself. But that is too fast. The battle is against bewitchment. Language seduces intelligence into taking grammatical appearance for metaphysical necessity. That is the target. If a metaphysical question survives that purgation, then the question has at least become cleaner. This is exactly how the user's project should use Wittgenstein — not as a policeman forbidding all large thought, but as a purifier of confused large thought.

### III.18 — `PI 115`: the picture that held us captive

Wittgenstein says in `PI 115`:

> "A picture held us captive."

This is one of his great summaries of philosophical delusion. The captivity is not caused by error in the cheap sense. It is caused by a picture so ingrained that one takes its constraints for the shape of reality. In the earlier work, the picture was that proposition and fact share one decisive logical articulation. In the later work, Wittgenstein repeatedly shows how such pictures arise: from the way words tempt us, from the analogies we overextend, from the demand for hidden mechanism where visible practice is enough, from the wish to turn criteria into objects and forms into substances.

The user's polemic against ontologized grammar can use this line without violence. Hegelian predicative grandiosity is one such picture; the `Tractatus` picture theory itself is another. The late Wittgenstein's importance lies partly in showing that analytic philosophy was not immune to this captivity.

### III.19 — `PI 123-126`: perspicuity, not explanatory domination

Wittgenstein says in `PI 123` that a philosophical problem has the form "I do not know my way about." That is a remarkable demotion of philosophical drama. The problem is not cosmic ignorance; it is disorientation inside language. Then `PI 124-126` insists that philosophy leaves everything as it is, does not found or deduce, does not alter the actual use of language, but makes it surveyable.

This can sound quietist. Yet the actual point is methodological modesty, not intellectual abdication. If the thing needed is a surveyable arrangement of what we already do, then adding explanatory theory is not depth — it is interference. The user should find this point useful, because one of the dangers of comparative metaphysics is over-explanation: forcing materials into a grand synthesis before the actual uses, scopes, and addressees have been sorted. Wittgenstein's perspicuous method is a medicine against premature synthesis.

### III.20 — `PI 309`: the fly-bottle

The famous line comes at `PI 309`:

> German:
>
> "Was ist dein Ziel in der Philosophie?
> – Der Fliege den Ausweg aus dem Fliegenglas zeigen."
>
> English:
>
> "What is your aim in philosophy?—To shew the fly the way out of the fly-bottle."

This is not a joke. The fly is trapped not by lack of intelligence but by moving inside a transparent prison it does not recognize as prison. The bottle is made of pictures, grammatical compulsions, misapplied analogies, and the demand for sublimed explanations. The philosopher's work is not to decorate the bottle. It is to show the exit.

This is the image that justifies using Wittgenstein as purgative rather than as destination. Once out of the bottle, one still has to live. One still has to think. One may still ask substantive questions. But one asks them without the bottle.

### III.21 — `PI 654-655`: proto-phenomena and noting a language-game

Near the end of Part I, Wittgenstein writes:

`PI 654`:

> German:
>
> "Unser Fehler ist,
> dort nach einer Erklärung zu suchen,
> wo wir die Tatsachen als 'Urphänomene' sehen sollten..."
>
> English:
>
> "Our mistake is to look for an explanation where we ought to look at what happens as a 'proto-phenomenon'..."

And `PI 655`:

> English:
>
> "The question is not one of explaining a language-game by means of our experiences,
> but of noting a language-game."

These late remarks concentrate much of the book's method. The demand for a deeper explanation is often itself the disease. One wants a hidden substrate, a hidden essence, a hidden mental mechanism, a hidden ontological key. Wittgenstein's answer is not anti-intellectual laziness. It is a refusal to explain away the very practice whose articulation was wanted. This is exactly why the language-game concept matters: one does not reduce the game to a deeper item standing behind it; one learns to see the game.

### III.22 — Part II §xi: aspect-seeing and the duck-rabbit

The duck-rabbit remarks in Part II §xi, and the larger discussion of aspect-seeing, matter because they show that perception itself is not a neutral intake later processed by interpretation. One sees the picture as a duck, or as a rabbit. The aspect dawns. The phenomenon Wittgenstein names "noticing an aspect" is not an extra mental act laid on top of perception; it is a way the seeing itself is organized.

This matters for the article because it supports a large but precise point: forms of life are not merely afterwards imposed on an otherwise content-neutral given. Our seeing, not only our judging, is already shaped by the ways we inhabit significance. Wittgenstein does not turn this into a metaphysics of world-construction. He keeps it at the level of description. But even at that level the point is strong. The dream of an uninterpreted view from nowhere is shaken.

This makes Wittgenstein particularly important for the user's concern with register. Registers are not masks placed on an untouched base. They are ways in which sense itself comes to presence. The user's stronger metaphysical development is not Wittgenstein's own, but the descriptive pressure runs in the same direction.

### III.23 — What `PI` changes and what it keeps from the `Tractatus`

What has changed from the `Tractatus`? Almost everything at the level of official theory. There is no longer one ideal logical syntax, no longer one account of proposition as picture, no longer one determination of meaningfulness by one formal criterion, no longer a clean single logical boundary between the sayable and the unsayable. Instead there are many language-games, many practices, many criteria, many routes by which words have life.

What has been kept? The concern with limit. The hatred of confusion. The refusal to let philosophy become one more doctrinal science. The sense that what matters is not explained by multiplying theoretical entities. And, in a transformed way, the earlier distinction between saying and showing.

The continuity claim has to be defended on the texts, not asserted as a slogan. The defense runs as follows. In TLP `4.022` ("The proposition shows its sense") and TLP `6.522` ("There is indeed the inexpressible. This shows itself"), Wittgenstein marks two distinct things that show themselves: the proposition's mode of representation, and what cannot be propositionally captured at all. In `PI`, the first kind of showing reappears as the way a language-game shows what it does in being played — the perspicuous presentation of `PI 122` and the "noting a language-game" of `PI 655` are both this. The second kind of showing reappears as the form of life that cannot itself be stated as one more proposition within a language-game but is exhibited in the agreement-in-judgments of `PI 241-242`. The form of life is not a hidden metaphysical substrate; it is what shows itself in being lived. So `PI 241-242` performs, in the register of practice, the same structural office that TLP `6.522` performed in the register of logical form: it locates an enabling background that is not itself a further fact in the field whose enabling it is. That is the continuity, and it is textual, not stipulated.

The limit has moved from logical form to use. The discipline of marking what cannot be stated within the field has survived. What has changed is which field is in view and how the marking is done.

## Part IV — `On Certainty`

`On Certainty` is not a textbook appendix to `PI`. It is a late concentration of the question: How can doubt, knowledge, testing, and assertion function at all? The immediate stimulus is Moore's claim to know propositions such as "Here is a hand." Wittgenstein's interest, however, is not to produce a simple Moore-refutation. It is to understand what sort of certainty is at issue when some things stand fast for us.

This late work matters for the user's project because it gives the clearest Wittgensteinian account of background commitments that are not freshly proved at every moment and yet are not thereby arbitrary. The mature scheme of hinges, world-picture, and form-of-life certainty develops the analytic vocabulary `On Certainty` is most often cited for.

### IV.1 — `OC 94`: inherited background

`OC 94` is one of the central late remarks:

> German:
>
> "Aber mein Weltbild habe ich nicht,
> weil ich mich von seiner Richtigkeit überzeugt habe...
> Sondern es ist der überkommene Hintergrund,
> auf welchem ich zwischen wahr und falsch unterscheide."
>
> English:
>
> "But I did not get my picture of the world by satisfying myself of its correctness...
> it is the inherited background against which I distinguish between true and false."

This sentence should be read slowly. Wittgenstein is not saying that he irrationally accepts whatever tradition hands him. He is saying that the activity of distinguishing true from false already presupposes a background within which such distinctions can be made. One does not begin from nowhere. The world's being available for inquiry already depends on an inherited frame of intelligibility.

That frame is not usually an explicit theory. It is the background of action, language, training, response, trust, and confidence within which inquiry occurs. The article can use this as one of its strongest late supports against the fantasy of contextless reason.

### IV.2 — `OC 95-99`: mythology, river-bed, hard rock

Wittgenstein intensifies the point immediately.

`OC 95`:

> German:
>
> "Die Sätze,
> die dies Weltbild beschreiben,
> könnten zu einer Art Mythologie gehören."
>
> English:
>
> "The propositions describing this world-picture might be part of a kind of mythology."

That line must not be cheapened. "Mythology" here does not mean false fairy tale. It means something closer to enabling frame. The propositions may function more like rules of a game than like freshly verified hypotheses.

Then `OC 97`:

> German:
>
> "Die Mythologie kann wieder in Fluß geraten,
> das Flußbett der Gedanken sich verschieben."
>
> English:
>
> "The mythology may change back into a state of flux,
> the river-bed of thoughts may shift."

And `OC 99`:

> German:
>
> "Ja,
> das Ufer jenes Flusses besteht zum Teil aus hartem Gestein..."
>
> English:
>
> "And the bank of that river consists partly of hard rock..."

These images matter enormously. The background is not an eternal immutable foundation. It can shift. Some parts are hard rock, some parts are sand. What is fixed at one time may later move; what is fluid may later harden.

This is exactly why `On Certainty` is more useful for the user's project than a crude foundationalism would be. It gives structure without absolutizing every local fixity into timeless necessity.

`OC 105`, `OC 110`, and `OC 115` push the point further. `OC 105` reminds us that all testing, all confirmation and disconfirmation, takes place already within a system; the system is not so much the starting point of arguments as their element. `OC 115` adds the warning that a doubt about everything would not be a doubt at all: doubt itself is a move that can only be made against an unshaken background. The grammar of doubt is therefore parasitic on the grammar of standing-fast. This is the structural axis on which the entire late work turns.

### IV.3 — `OC 341-344`: hinges

The famous hinge passage comes later.

`OC 341`:

> German:
>
> "D. h. die Fragen,
> die wir stellen,
> und unsre Zweifel beruhen darauf,
> daß gewisse Sätze vom Zweifel ausgenommen sind,
> gleichsam die Angeln..."
>
> English:
>
> "That is to say,
> the questions that we raise and our doubts depend on the fact that some propositions are exempt from doubt,
> are as it were like hinges..."

And `OC 343`:

> German:
>
> "Wenn ich will,
> daß die Türe sich drehe,
> müssen die Angeln feststehen."
>
> English:
>
> "If I want the door to turn,
> the hinges must stay put."

Then `OC 344`:

> German:
>
> "Mein Leben besteht darin,
> daß ich mich mit manchem zufriedengebe."
>
> English:
>
> "My life consists in my being content to accept many things."

This is one of Wittgenstein's deepest late insights. Doubt is not the primordial state from which certainty must be constructed. Doubt is a move that occurs against a background that already stands fast. If everything were under live doubt at once, no actual doubting could occur. The door turns because some hinges hold. This is not dogmatism. It is the grammar of inquiry.

### IV.4 — `OC 358-359`: certainty as form of life

The late culmination comes in `OC 358-359`.

`OC 358`:

> German:
>
> "Ich möchte nun diese Sicherheit nicht als etwas der voreiligen Verwandtem,
> oder Oberflächlichkeit Verwandtes ansehen,
> sondern als (eine) Lebensform."
>
> English:
>
> "Now I would like to regard this certainty, not as something akin to hastiness or superficiality,
> but as a form of life."

Then `OC 359`:

> German:
>
> "Das heißt doch,
> ich will sie als etwas auffassen,
> was jenseits von berechtigt und unberechtigt liegt;
> also gleichsam als etwas Animalisches."
>
> English:
>
> "But that means I want to conceive it as something that lies beyond being justified or unjustified;
> as it were,
> as something animal."

This is another place where Wittgenstein can be badly misread. He is not reducing certainty to brute instinct in a contemptuous sense. He is locating the level at which not every standing-fast item is held by explicit evidential reasoning. Life itself carries certainties. Practice carries certainties. Embodied orientation carries certainties. The term "animal" is meant to pull us away from the fantasy that every rational activity begins in explicit reflective endorsement.

This again is deeply relevant for the user's methodology. Registers, scopes, forms of life, and standards of uptake are not all newly inferred every time they are entered. They are often inherited, trained, and lived.

### IV.5 — `OC 375-410`: Moore-shaped propositions and the system of knowledge

The middle and late stretches of `OC` repeatedly return to Moore's "Here is a hand" and similar propositions to ask: what kind of saying is this? Wittgenstein's recurring point is that such propositions are not ordinary empirical claims. Their certainty is not the certainty of well-confirmed evidence; their grammatical role is closer to that of a rule than that of a finding.

`OC 375`:

> English:
>
> "Here one must realize that complete absence of doubt at some point,
> even where we would say that 'legitimate' doubt can exist,
> need not falsify a language-game."

`OC 378`:

> English:
>
> "Knowledge is in the end based on acknowledgement."

And `OC 410`:

> English:
>
> "Our knowledge forms an enormous system. And only within this system has a particular bit the value we give it."

These remarks complete the late picture. Knowledge is system-relative not because there is no truth but because what counts as a particular item of knowledge gets its weight from the place it occupies in a wider network. The Moore-shaped propositions — "Here is a hand," "I have never been on the moon," "the earth has existed for many years" — are not at the periphery where empirical verification operates. They are at the center, holding the network in shape. Their role is normative for the practice of doubting and proving rather than itself a result of that practice.

For the user's project this is the most exact textual support `OC` offers. The user's concept of `adhikara` — competence-conditioned authority to engage a means of knowing — is not Wittgenstein's. The article must say so. But `OC 375`, `OC 378`, and `OC 410` together do articulate, in their own register, the thought that knowledge does not begin from nowhere and that what one is competent to assert depends on the system within which the assertion has its weight. That is a structural parallel, not an identity, and the article will mark it as the user's reconstruction (AF1, AF4) rather than as Wittgenstein's explicit doctrine.

### IV.6 — Why `On Certainty` matters for the user's reading

The user's project is concerned with `adhikara`, scope, and the ordered functioning of means of knowing in different contexts. Wittgenstein does not speak that language, and the article must not make him speak it. But one can responsibly say that the hinge doctrine is the closest Wittgenstein comes to acknowledging that practices of knowing always presuppose a background not itself being rejustified at every step. That is the structural point.

The user's reading can then say that what one tradition would articulate as a discipline of scope, competence, or ordered authority — `pramāṇa-vyavasthā` in its widest sense — Wittgenstein articulates as the background, world-picture, and hinge-structure of a form of life. This is a translation, not an identity. It is the user's reconstruction (AF4), not Wittgenstein's own self-description, and the article marks it that way.

### IV.7 — `On Certainty` and the continuity thesis

Why does `On Certainty` belong to the same arc as the `Tractatus` and `PI`? Because the question is still the same question, though transformed.

In the `Tractatus`, the question is: what can be said, and what lies beyond saying? In `PI`, the question becomes: what gives words sense in practice, and what philosophical pictures falsely demand from them? In `OC`, the question becomes: what stands fast in life such that saying, doubting, proving, and asking can occur at all?

These are not three unrelated projects. They are three placements of the limit-problem. In each placement, an enabling structure is exhibited that cannot itself be made into one more item within the field it enables — logical form in TLP, the form of life in PI, the hinge and world-picture in OC. The showing/saying distinction, recast at each stage, is what holds the three works together as one labor.

## Part V — The secondary debates, and where this article stands

The most important debate about the `Tractatus` is the dispute between the standard reading and the resolute reading. Hacker, and in a different way McGuinness, insist that the book does articulate positive distinctions, above all the distinction between what can be propositionally said and what is only shown. Diamond and Conant insist, rightly, that `6.54` forbids us to treat the book as a warehouse of ineffable theses. This article takes a mixed position because the text requires one. On what survives the climb, it is closer to Hacker than to an austere nothing-survives reading. On how the climb works, it is closer to Diamond and Conant than to any reading that domesticates the ladder. The mixed position is not a refusal to choose; it is the recognition that the two readings answer different questions. The resolute reading is right about the grammar of `6.54`. The standard reading is right that the discipline of saying/showing is the residue left when one has climbed.

Cavell matters for a different reason. He shifts attention from knowledge to acknowledgment, and that shift is useful here because Wittgenstein's importance is not exhausted by the epistemology of propositions. He is also a philosopher of the forms in which we stand to one another and to the world without turning every relation into a knowledge-claim. McDowell matters because he shows how normativity can be lived without being occult, and that is precisely the line this article needs in the rule-following sections. The late Wittgenstein is neither a skeptic about rules nor a positivist about behavior. He is a philosopher of minded practice.

What, then, is the article's settled position? The `Tractatus` is not empty. The `Investigations` is not relativism. `On Certainty` is not foundationalism. And Wittgenstein as a whole is not quietism in the sense that matters to the user's project. He is a disciplinarian of sense. He dismantles the fantasy that one grammar can rule all thought. He does not thereby close the possibility of metaphysical thinking; he makes any responsible metaphysical thinking harder. That is why he is useful.

## Part VI — Reading notes

What Wittgenstein actually claims is more exact and more limited than many admirers allow. The early book ties sense to logical picturing and distinguishes saying from showing. The later book ties meaning to use, multiplies language-games, refuses essential definitions, locates rule-following in practice, and blocks private criterial sovereignty. The latest notes on certainty show that inquiry depends on a background that stands fast.

Where does he confirm the user's project? He confirms that one discursive form cannot legislate for the whole. He confirms that the life of meaning is plural. He confirms that what matters is often deformed when philosophy tries to force it into one proposition-form. He confirms that practice, not occult inward pointing, carries normativity.

Where does he challenge the user's project? He resists every easy move from plural practices to positive ontology. He gives no doctrine of one real articulated through many true registers, and no metaphysical account of what shows itself. He leaves the user's stronger realism to be argued elsewhere. That is not a defect; it is a pressure.

What Wittgenstein is for, in this corpus, is therefore exact. He is not the destination. He is the purifier of the route. He breaks the spell of grammar-as-ontology, forces attention back to use, shows that justification ends in life, and leaves the reader in a position where a larger realism, if it is to be defended, must be defended without illusion.
