# 17. Henri Bergson — A Deep Reader

*For the user's thesis. Constellation: the most direct French anticipation of Aurobindo's intuitive-mental register; bridge to Whitehead's process metaphysics and Deleuze's univocity; what Banerji's saṃjñāna/ājñāna mapping picks up and what it leaves to be supplied.*

## Reader's Orientation

### Bio (1859–1941)

Cross-engagement: Abhinavagupta presses the same substrate structure axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `one-self-standing` rather than `process-field-with-no-enduring-substrate` in [primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

Henri-Louis Bergson was born in Paris, 18 October 1859, to a Polish-Jewish father and an English-Jewish mother. He was a brilliant student at the Lycée Condorcet, took the *concours général* in mathematics in 1877, and deliberately turned away from mathematics to enter the École Normale Supérieure in 1878 to read philosophy. The choice mattered: he wrote as a philosopher who *could* have been a mathematician, and his polemic against the spatializing intellect is the polemic of someone who knew, from inside, how it actually proceeds.

Cross-engagement: Adi Sankara presses the same selfhood structure axis in the phenomenological register, but with the value `witness-self` rather than `psychic-individual` in [primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

After his *agrégation* (1881) and lycée teaching, the seed of his first book came at Clermont-Ferrand: while preparing a class on Zeno's paradoxes, he saw that the scientific concept of time was incoherent because it was *space in disguise*. Real time was something else; he named it *durée*. The thesis was elaborated as the *Essai sur les données immédiates de la conscience* (1889).

From 1900 he held a chair at the Collège de France; the lectures were a public phenomenon, attended by Péguy, James, Maritain, Gilson, Wahl, Jankélévitch, the young Merleau-Ponty. He was elected to the Académie française (1914), awarded the Nobel for Literature (1927), chaired the League of Nations' *Commission internationale de coopération intellectuelle*. The 1922 confrontation with Einstein produced *Durée et simultanéité* and accelerated the eclipse of his scientific reputation. In autumn 1940, refusing Vichy's offered exemption from antisemitic registration laws, he stood in line in his dressing-gown, registered as a Jew, resigned all honors, and died of pulmonary congestion contracted on that line, 4 January 1941.

### The Work-Arc

Bergson published four major books over forty-three years, each addressing a different domain with the same method. The arc is unusually clean.

Cross-engagement: Akshapada Gautama presses the same method axis in the methodological register, but with the value `formal-proof-or-inference` rather than `mixed-or-layered` in [primary text](cite://aksapada/nyaya-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

**1889 — *Essai sur les données immédiates de la conscience*** (translated by F. L. Pogson 1910 as *Time and Free Will*). The thesis-book. Domain: psychology and the problem of freedom. Central distinction: *durée vraie* (qualitative multiplicity, lived time) versus *temps spatialisé* (quantitative multiplicity, the time of physics and clocks). The deep self is qualitative, free; the surface self is quantitative, determined; the determinism-freedom debate dissolves once the confusion of duration with extension is exposed.

**1896 — *Matière et mémoire*** (translated by Nancy Margaret Paul and W. Scott Palmer 1911 as *Matter and Memory*). Domain: mind-body and the problem of perception. Central move: the introduction of *image* as a metaphysically neutral term that cuts between realism's "thing" and idealism's "representation," refusing both. Two memories: *mémoire-habitude* (sensorimotor, the body's adjustment of itself) and *mémoire pure* (the survival of the past as past, in itself). The cone-diagram of memory; the brain as organ of attention to life, not generator of thought.

**1907 — *L'évolution créatrice*** (translated by Arthur Mitchell 1911 as *Creative Evolution*). Domain: biology and cosmology. Central concept: the *élan vital*, the creative push of life that diverges radially into incompatible solutions (plant / animal; instinct / intelligence; the closed and the open) rather than progressing along a single line. The polemical targets are explicit: Spencer's mechanistic evolutionism, on which the new is the recombination of antecedent units; the neo-Darwinian and neo-Lamarckian mechanisms taken as exhaustive accounts of life; and the Aristotelian-Leibnizian finalism that runs the line in the opposite direction. Critique of mechanism *and* finalism (both spatialize duration); the *cinematographic illusion* of intellectual representation; intuition as the method that grasps duration from within. *Élan vital* is not "vital force" — Bergson explicitly resisted that translation as a relapse into the mechanistic vocabulary the book is written against; the *élan* is duration acting from within the living, not a quasi-physical fluid added to mechanism. (See his clarifications in the prefaces and essays of *La pensée et le mouvant*, 1934, where he goes back over the misreadings of his own concepts.)

**1932 — *Les deux sources de la morale et de la religion*** (translated by R. Ashley Audra and Cloudesley Brereton 1935 as *The Two Sources of Morality and Religion*). Domain: ethics and religion. Central distinction: closed morality (social pressure, the static obligation of the tribe to itself) versus open morality (the call of the moral hero, the saint, the mystic, in which the *élan* itself speaks); static religion (myth-making in the service of social cohesion) versus dynamic religion (mysticism, in which one rejoins the creative source). Mysticism — and specifically what Bergson calls *complete* or *active* mysticism, of which his named examples are St Paul, St Teresa, St Catherine of Siena, St Francis, Joan of Arc — is treated as the highest form of religion and as cognitively probative: the convergence of independent mystics is evidence about reality.

Around these four books cluster the lectures and shorter pieces: *Le rire* (1900) on comedy; *L'énergie spirituelle* (1919); *La pensée et le mouvant* (1934, the methodological essays — "Introduction to Metaphysics" of 1903 is collected here, and is the single best entry-point to Bergson's notion of intuition); *Durée et simultanéité* (1922) on Einstein, partly retracted, dropped from the *Œuvres*.

*La pensée et le mouvant* deserves a special note. Its two long prefaces, written in 1922 and 1934, are Bergson's own retrospective self-correction against the misreadings that had accumulated around his vocabulary by then: the misreading of *durée* as merely subjective time; the misreading of *intuition* as a vague feeling, a romantic surrender of reason; the misreading of *élan vital* as a quasi-physical "vital force" of the kind he had been arguing against. Reading the four major books with these prefaces in hand is the most direct way of meeting Bergson on his own terms rather than through the standard-translation simplifications. The decisive sentences of those prefaces — that intuition is *thinking in duration* (*penser en durée*) and not feeling instead of thinking; that *durée* is the real that the spatializing intellect dissolves; that the *élan* is duration in its creative aspect and not a force-among-forces — are the clarifications the reader should keep beside every chapter.

### The Central Thread

Bergson is a philosopher of *one* problem, said in many domains. The problem is: **how does intellect falsify the real, and what kind of cognition restores it?** His answer: intellect, as evolved instrument of action on solids, decomposes movement into immobilities, duration into spatial intervals, life into mechanism, novelty into recombination of the already-given. This is not an error to be diagnosed and discarded — intellect is supremely useful for what it was made to do (cut the world into manipulable objects, predict their behavior for purposes of action). It becomes an error only when extended beyond its competence, when one takes the spatial-quantitative model as a description of reality rather than as a tool. The corrective is *intuition*: a cognition that places itself inside the moving object instead of circling it from outside, that "sympathizes" with what it knows, that grasps the qualitative, the durational, the ontologically novel. Intuition is not anti-intellectual; it is supra-intellectual; it requires effort, training, philosophical discipline, and is the special task of metaphysics.

Cross-engagement: Gaudapada (Samkhya commentator) presses the same temporal mode axis in the metaphysical register, but with the value `substance-primary` rather than `process-primary` in [primary text](cite://isvara-krsna/samkhya-karika/1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

The same problem cycles through the books in different domains:

- **Données immédiates**: intellect spatializes psychic states; intuition recovers the deep self.
- **Matière et mémoire**: intellect treats memory as weakened perception stored in the brain; intuition recovers memory as ontologically distinct, a survival of past-as-past, with the brain as organ of selection-for-action, not as container.
- **Évolution créatrice**: intellect treats life as mechanism or as predetermined plan; intuition recovers life as creative push, *élan*, radial divergence.
- **Deux sources**: intellect builds closed societies and static religions on the model of pressure-and-habit; intuition opens to the *élan* in moral heroes and dynamic mystics.

In each book the same gesture: the spatializing/quantifying intellect produces a pseudo-problem that disappears under the right method, and the right method is to slip inside the moving thing instead of circling it from without.

### The User's Interest

Bergson is the most direct French anticipation of Aurobindo's *intuitive-mental* register — the rung at which knowledge ceases to be representational-conceptual and becomes contact, "sympathy" with the inner movement of the thing. Banerji's *Inward Turn* makes this constellation: Aurobindo's saṃjñāna (knowing-with) and ājñāna (direct knowing) track Bergson's distinction between intellect (which goes around the object) and intuition (which enters into it). Bergson reaches the intuitive register with unmatched Western precision; he does not reach the *Overmind* (cosmic consciousness, all individual centers perceived simultaneously as one and as many) and does not envision the *Supermind* (being and becoming non-different). What he lacks is a positive doctrine of *līlā*, a doctrine of *involution* preceding evolution, and the static-and-dynamic non-difference of Saccidānanda. The *élan vital* is the becoming-side of the Real without the being-side: push without ground.

Cross-engagement: G. W. F. Hegel presses the same evolution register axis in the historical-genealogical register, but with the value `sublative-becoming` rather than `durational-creative-growth` in [primary text](cite://hegel/phenomenology-of-spirit/17). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

The other reason is bridging. Bergson sets up Whitehead (process as primary), Deleuze (multiplicity as virtual; intuition as method; the critique of representation), Merleau-Ponty (the lived body, perception as already action). None of those later readings is intelligible without him.

### Glossary

*durée* — real time as lived from within: continuous, qualitative, heterogeneous, indivisible without alteration. "Lasting-as-flowing."

Cross-engagement: Akalanka presses the same soteric end axis in the soteriological register, but with the value `isolation-or-discriminative-release` rather than `not-soteriological` in [primary text](cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/1.34-35). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

*temps spatialisé* — the time of physics, clocks, of any model in which moments are juxtaposed like points on a line. Useful for action, false as an account of inner life.

*intuition* — Bergson's *technical* term, not folk-intuition. It is cognition that places itself *inside* its object, "sympathizes" with it, grasps it durationally; it is *not* a hunch, *not* a feeling-from-outside, *not* romantic immediacy. It is a trained conversion of attention — *thinking in duration*, *penser en durée* — that requires philosophical effort and the prior dissolution of pseudo-problems. Standard-translation usage of "intuition" in folk-psychological registers will systematically mislead the reader; Bergson's intuition is closer in tone to a contemplative method than to anything informal English calls "intuitive." His most precise statement is the 1903 essay "Introduction à la métaphysique," restated and defended in the 1934 prefaces of *La pensée et le mouvant*.

*intelligence* / *intellect* — the faculty oriented to action on solids: analyzes, decomposes, calculates. Pseudo-problematizes whatever is qualitative-durational.

*multiplicité qualitative / quantitative* — qualitative: interpenetrating, heterogeneous, not countable without falsification (notes of a melody, nuances of feeling). Quantitative: juxtaposed, countable (sheep in a flock). Different *kinds*, not degrees.

*élan vital* — the creative push of life, indivisible at its source, dividing radially into incompatible lineages as it descends into matter. A tendency, knowable from within by intuition. Not "vital force"; Bergson explicitly resisted that mechanistic rendering. *Élan* is the gathered launching-out of an athlete before a leap, not a quasi-physical energy super-added to inert matter; the term names duration considered from the standpoint of life and is unintelligible apart from the prior doctrine of *durée*.

*durée* — note on translation. The standard "duration" is too neutral; *durée* is *real* duration, lived from inside, qualitative, indivisible without falsification. Bergson resisted the casual English "duration" because in ordinary usage the word names an interval (a duration of three hours) — exactly the spatialized concept he is arguing *against*. Where the prose underneath says "duration," hear *durée* in his sense: lasting-as-flowing, the inner consistency of becoming.

*mémoire pure / mémoire-habitude* — pure memory: survival of the past *as past*, ontologically distinct from any present perception, not stored in the brain. Habit-memory: the body's sensorimotor adjustment, what is "remembered" by being able to do.

*attention à la vie* — the body's pragmatic orientation, the function of which the brain is the organ.

*image* — metaphysically neutral term for what appears: neither the realist's thing-in-itself nor the idealist's representation. Matter is "an aggregate of images."

*société close / ouverte; religion statique / dynamique* — closed/open society: pressure versus aspiration. Static religion: the myth-making function of intellect (*fabulation*) serving social cohesion. Dynamic religion: contact with the *élan* in mysticism.

*saṃjñāna / ājñāna* — Aurobindonian: knowing-with (sympathetic identity) and knowing-by-direct-contact. Bergson's intuition activates these modes but does not extend to the *prajñāna* of the Overmind or the *vijñāna* of the Supermind.

*intuitive mental* — Aurobindo's name for the rung above ordinary mind and below the Overmind. Bergson's "intuition" most plausibly maps to this register.

## Part I — *Essai sur les données immédiates de la conscience* (1889)

### The book and its problem

The *Essai* is Bergson's doctoral thesis (1889). The English translation by Pogson (1910) was titled *Time and Free Will* — a title that fits the polemical occasion (the freedom-determinism debate of late-nineteenth-century French philosophy) but obscures the method. The literal "Essay on the Immediate Data of Consciousness" is closer to what the book actually does: it asks what consciousness *actually* presents when we stop reading it through the schemata of language and associationist psychology. Three chapters: Chapter I shows that "intensity" as quantitative magnitude is a confusion (psychic intensities are qualities); Chapter II arrives at the distinction between quantitative and qualitative multiplicity; Chapter III shows that the freedom-determinism debate dissolves once duration is no longer confused with extension.

The polemical targets are specific. Fechner's and Weber's psychophysics, with its claim to *measure* sensation, is the immediate antagonist of Chapter I. Kant's first *Critique*, with its doctrine that time is an *a priori* form of inner intuition (time as the form-of-arrangement of inner states, structurally analogous to space as the form of outer states), is the deeper antagonist of Chapter II. Bergson's claim against Kant is that time as Kant treats it is *already* spatialized — a form-of-juxtaposition into which inner states are sorted — and so misses what is actually given to inner experience: not a form of arrangement but a flowing, interpenetrating qualitative whole. Bergson is so *un-Kantianizing* time, returning it from a transcendental form to a directly given content of consciousness. The associationist tradition (Mill, Bain, Taine, Ribot) is the secondary antagonist throughout: its picture of mind as combinable atoms is the projection back into consciousness of spatialized representations of consciousness.

### Intensity as quality, not quantity

The opening polemic is against the psychophysics of Fechner and Weber. Fechner's law (*S* = *k* log *E*) treats sensation as a measurable quantity. Bergson's argument: psychophysics has *defined* sensation as a quantity by a chain of conventions and then claimed to have *measured* it. The question whether sensation admits of measurement is begged. Sensations are qualitative changes; a more intense pain is not a larger pain but a different pain that has reorganized the whole field of consciousness around itself.

> *An obscure desire has gradually become a deep passion. The faint intensity of this desire consisted first in its seeming isolated, almost foreign to the rest of your inner life. But little by little it has penetrated a greater number of psychic elements, tinting them with its own colour…*

The metaphor is dyeing: a drop of dye does not occupy a region, it changes the color of the whole. Increase in intensity is not addition of more of the same, but qualitative reorganization of the whole psychic field. This is already the doctrine of qualitative multiplicity.

### Two multiplicities

Chapter II generalizes into the doctrine of multiplicity. **Quantitative multiplicity**: juxtaposed elements, each outside the others, occupying determinate regions of a homogeneous medium — sheep in a flock, points on a line. Countable; counting requires laying-out-in-space. **Qualitative multiplicity**: interpenetrating elements, each modifying the others, none separable without altering the whole — the notes of a melody as heard, the nuances of a feeling. To count the notes one must abstract to a list of items, which is no longer the melody.

The case of duration itself decides between them. The moments of my listening do not lie alongside one another like points on a line; the earlier notes survive *into* the later. Real time — *durée* — is qualitative multiplicity. Spatialized time is quantitative, real as a useful instrument for action, but not what time most fundamentally is. The canonical statement:

> *Pure duration is the form taken by the succession of our conscious states when our self lets itself live, when it abstains from establishing a separation between the present state and anterior states. It need not, for this, absorb itself entirely in the passing sensation or idea, for then on the contrary it would cease to endure. Nor must it forget anterior states: it suffices that in remembering them it does not juxtapose them to the actual state as one point to another point, but organizes them with it, as happens when we recall the notes of a melody, melted together as it were.*

The decisive image is the melody: notes succeed one another but interpenetrate; they are *not* points-on-a-line. *Succession sans extériorité réciproque*, succession without mutual externality, is *durée*; *extériorité sans succession*, mutual externality without succession, is space. Spatialized time is the false synthesis of the two: succession projected as juxtaposition. The clock measures only simultaneities, not duration; "in space, there is never any but a unique position of the hand and pendulum, since of the past positions nothing remains." Real duration is in the conscious self, where states interpenetrate; outside the self there is only space.

### The deep self and the surface self

Chapter III applies the doctrine to the self. The **surface self** (*moi superficiel*) is the self of language, of social interaction; its states are sharp-edged, named, juxtaposed — quantitative multiplicities, predictable, determined. The **deep self** (*moi profond*) is qualitative: states interpenetrate, every state reorganizes the whole; its acts cannot be predicted from antecedents without falsifying the whole.

A free act is not an act with no cause but an act that emerges from the deep self in its qualitative wholeness. Most acts come from the surface self and are determined; some — those in which we feel ourselves most fully ourselves — come from the deep self and are free. Freedom is rare, fragile, a *quality* of an act.

Underneath the polemic is the methodological target of **associationism**: the picture of mind as combinable atoms. Bergson's claim is that introspection yields not atoms but interpenetrating wholes; the atoms are projections back from spatialized representation. This is, already in 1889, a phenomenological move in the strict sense, and places Bergson at the head of the twentieth-century French phenomenological tradition.

The doctrine of two multiplicities is the seed-form of what Deleuze, in *Bergsonisme*, will name the *virtual*: a multiplicity real without being quantitative, that cannot be analyzed into discrete elements without ceasing to be what it is.

## Part II — *Matière et mémoire* (1896)

### The book and its scaffold

*Matière et mémoire: essai sur la relation du corps à l'esprit* (1896) was Bergson's second book; he himself thought it his most important work. Four chapters: I, on the selection of images for representation and the role of the body; II, on recognition, the two memories, and the brain; III, on the survival of pure memory and the cone-diagram; IV, the ontological synthesis grounding matter-mind duality in differences of *durée*-rhythm.

### Chapter I — Image, body, perception

The opening is a phenomenological reduction:

> *Here is a system of images which I call my perception of the universe, and which is upset from top to bottom by slight variations of a certain privileged image, my body. This image occupies the centre; all the others arrange themselves around it; with each of its movements everything changes, as though one had turned a kaleidoscope. Here, on the other hand, are the same images, but each referred to itself; influencing one another, no doubt, but in such a way that the effect is always proportioned to the cause: this is what I call the universe.*

The decisive word is *même* — "the same images." It is one world: gathered around the body it becomes *my perception*, taken in mutual relations without privilege it becomes *the universe*. Not two ontologies, but one configured differently around different centers.

The body's privilege is pragmatic, not metaphysical: my body is the center of *action*. Perception is the field of *available actions* given the body's situation. **Perception is subtractive, not additive.** The realist pictures the brain as adding representational form to physical input; Bergson reverses: the world is already image, and perception is the *limitation* of the image-totality to what concerns the body's action. The brain is an instrument of *attention to life* — a mode of restriction, not of construction.

This is the structural homologue of Aurobindonian *manas* as an instrument of contact and delimitation: consciousness wider than mind, mind narrower than consciousness, brain narrower still. The body's situation determines what *images* can become *perceptions* for it; the rest of the image-totality remains as it is, neither illuminated by the body's attention nor extinguished by its inattention. *Image* is the rejection of the realism/idealism alternative at the level of vocabulary: an image is not a thing-in-itself awaiting representation (realism) and not a representation in a mind that has no thing-in-itself behind it (idealism); an image is what *is*, before the realism/idealism question is even posed. Once posed, the question is a pseudo-problem generated by spatialized intellect; once dissolved, perception is seen for what it is — a *limitation* of one image to those features of the universe-image that bear on the action of one privileged image-among-images, my body.

### Chapter II — Two memories

Chapter II turns from perception to recognition. The standard view (then and now) is that recognition is the activation of stored memory-traces in the brain. Bergson denies this and proposes the doctrine of the **two memories**.

**Mémoire-habitude** — habit-memory: a sensorimotor sequence (reciting a learned poem). Lodged in the body, *acted* not represented; the body adjusted to a learned action.

**Mémoire pure** — pure memory: the first reading of the poem, with its date, color, qualitative wholeness, survives *as itself*. It is the past *as past*, retained, not a sensorimotor pattern. Recall is a movement of attention from action toward contemplation, not the activation of a trace.

The distinction is ontological. Habit-memory is a present disposition in the body. Pure memory is *not* in the brain. The transcendental argument: a present disposition cannot *be* the past as past; any reduction of memory to a present brain-state collapses the past/present difference constitutive of memory. The empirical argument from aphasia (lesions disturb enactment of memory rather than the memory itself; aphasiacs suddenly recover words that had become inaccessible to action) has been substantially vindicated by neuroscience's later turn to memory-as-reconstruction.

### Chapter III — The cone of memory

Chapter III presents the inverted-cone diagram: apex *S* (present moment of action, body's contact with the plan of perception), base *P* (totality of pure memory, every past moment surviving in itself). The cone is the life of consciousness.

The structure is dynamic. At the apex, consciousness is turned toward action; pure memory is "contracted" to what the present act requires. As consciousness withdraws from action — in dream, reverie, contemplation, philosophical intuition — the cone *dilates* toward the base; more of pure memory becomes accessible. This is the *attention à la vie*.

> *Everything goes on as if, in this aggregate of images that I call the universe, nothing genuinely new could be produced except through the intermediary of certain particular images, the type of which is given to me by my body.*

The body is the locus through which *novelty* enters the image-totality. Free acts are not violations of nature but the precise function of certain images (privileged bodies) within the image-system.

The cone has *intermediate planes* — *plans de conscience* — each the *whole* of pure memory contracted to a different rhythm. The same total content is differently disposed at different planes. This gives a non-substantial theory of the levels of mind: not different minds as quasi-substantial layers but one consciousness, contracting and dilating. (A useful instrument against any reading of Aurobindo's planes as distinct substances; in Aurobindo himself they are degrees of consciousness.)

### Chapter IV — The ontology

Matter and mind are not two substances but *the same image-totality* taken at *different rhythms of duration*. Matter is duration at maximum dilation: moments so close that the difference approaches zero. Mind is duration at maximum contraction: a present moment holding within itself a vast stretch of past.

A perceived red is the contraction of trillions of electromagnetic vibrations into a single felt quality. Consciousness is *temporal contraction*; matter, *temporal dilation*. They are not opposed substances but ends of a single spectrum — a difference of *tension*.

> *If quality, in sensation, is only a certain contraction of the past in the present, that is to say the condensation, into a single simple intuition, of a multitude of vibrations almost identical with one another…*

The Paul/Palmer rendering loses *simple* in *intuition simple*. The one-ness of consciousness is the contraction of the many vibrations of matter; matter is many-spread-out, consciousness is many-contracted-into-one.

Bergson is not a dualist, not a materialist, not an idealist; he is closest to a *neutral monism*, except that the neutral term — image — is dynamic, duration at some rhythm. The structural Indian analog is the Sāṃkhya *guṇa*-spectrum (tamas at the matter end, sattva at the mind end). What Bergson articulates is *matter and mind as different rhythms of the same becoming* — close to the Aurobindonian doctrine that all ontological levels are degrees of one Saccidānanda differently veiled. He lacks the Saccidānanda end (rhythm so high it becomes identity-with-self) and the doctrine of involution by which the high end produces the low end. He has the spectrum but not its source.

### What Bergson achieves and what he does not

Achievements: a non-dualist ontology of matter and mind; the body as locus of action (seed of Merleau-Ponty and embodied cognition); pure memory as ontologically distinct from present brain-state (ancestor of Husserl's retention-protention and Deleuze's virtual); the brain as organ of attention to life.

Lacks: a doctrine of the *origin* of the matter-mind spectrum; the highest term of the spectrum (the *vijñāna*-identity of being-and-knowing); a grounding of the body's privilege beyond the descriptive fact of action. These are precisely where Aurobindonian doctrine supplies what Bergson points toward.

## Part III — *L'évolution créatrice* (1907)

### The book and its scaffold

*L'évolution créatrice* (1907) made Bergson famous outside academic philosophy. William James called it "a sort of marvel." Four chapters: I, critique of mechanism and finalism, life as creative push; II, the radial divergence of evolution (plant/animal; instinct/intelligence); III, the meaning of life and intelligence as action-oriented; IV, the cinematographic illusion and a positive metaphysics of *durée*.

The polemical occasion is also specific. Bergson said in his correspondence and lectures of the period that the book grew out of an attempted assimilation, and then a refusal, of Herbert Spencer's *First Principles* — Spencer's grand mechanistic-evolutionary cosmology, dominant in the late-nineteenth-century Anglophone world and influential in France through Taine and Ribot. Bergson admired Spencer's ambition to take evolution seriously as a metaphysics; he found the metaphysics, on inspection, to be precisely the *cinematographic* false synthesis the book diagnoses — evolution recomposed from already-given units, novelty reduced to recombination, duration spatialized into a homogeneous medium of change. Neo-Darwinism (mutation plus selection, the "modern synthesis" was still decades away but its ancestors were in place) and neo-Lamarckism are the secondary antagonists, both flagged as variant mechanisms; Aristotelian-Leibnizian finalism is the antagonist on the other flank, treating evolution as the unfolding of a pre-given plan. Both fail for the same reason: both *already have the line laid out* and differ only on the direction of running.

### The argument against mechanism and finalism

Mechanism explains the present by the past (push from behind); finalism by the future (pull from ahead toward a pre-given end). Both *spatialize* duration: they differ only in the direction in which an already-laid-out line is run. Neither can think real duration — duration in which the line is being made *as we go*.

> *The more we deepen our grasp of the nature of time, the more we shall understand that duration means invention, the creation of forms, the continuous elaboration of the absolutely new.*

Duration is the bringing-into-being of the new. This is the central doctrine of the book.

### The *élan vital*

The positive concept is the *élan vital*. *Élan* in French is what an athlete gathers before a jump — the gathered-and-launching, not merely the propelling. The *élan* is the creative push that, descending into matter, is forced to divide. Not a substance, not a force in the physical sense, not a soul behind the organism, not finalistic. It is duration considered from the standpoint of the living, creating radially as it descends.

The *radial divergence* is essential. The *élan* does not progress in a single line; it diverges into incompatible solutions: plant/animal (fixing solar energy / moving and consuming); within the animal line, instinct/intelligence (organic instruments / inorganic instruments). Each is a complete solution; none is superior. The popular reading of Bergson as a "philosopher of progress" is wrong. Humanity is not the goal of evolution but one extreme of one branch. What is decisive about humans is that intelligence can, with effort, *turn back upon itself* and recover, in intuition, the *élan* from which it diverged.

### Intuition versus intellect

Intelligence is evolved as the instrument for action on solids. Its categories (substance, identity, the discrete, the calculable) work for inert matter; applied to life — which is duration — they yield a picture everywhere a *snapshot* and nowhere a movement.

The remedy is *intuition* — but the word in Bergson's mouth must be heard *against* its folk-psychological echo, not with it. English-language readers, encountering "intuition," reach for the everyday meaning: a hunch, a vague feeling, a knowing-without-knowing-how. Bergson's intuition is none of these. It is *a method*, with stages and a discipline, and it presupposes the prior labor of intellect (without which there is nothing to be intuited *into*). His most precise statement (1903, "Introduction à la métaphysique"): intuition is "*the kind of intellectual sympathy* by which one places oneself within an object in order to coincide with what is unique in it and consequently inexpressible." Mark *intellectual* sympathy: this is not *anti*-intellectual cognition but *supra*-intellectual cognition. Hear in *sympathie* the Aristotelian *sym-patheia*: a discipline of cognition that goes *with* the durating thing instead of analyzing it from outside.

Bergson returned to this point repeatedly in the 1920s and 1930s as he watched his "intuition" being read as a license for woolly impressionism. In the 1934 preface to *La pensée et le mouvant* he is unsparing: intuition is *thinking in duration* (*penser en durée*); it is harder, not easier, than analytic thought; it requires the analytic effort first and then the further conversion of attention that the analytic effort by itself cannot deliver. The reader who confuses Bergsonian intuition with feeling-instead-of-thinking has in fact dropped *below* intellect rather than risen *above* it; the position is the reverse of Bergson's own.

This is Aurobindo's *saṃjñāna* (knowing-with) and *ājñāna* (knowing-by-direct-contact). Bergson activates these modes; he does not climb to the *prajñāna* of the Overmind or the *vijñāna* of the Supermind. This is Banerji's claim, of which more in Part V.

The 1903 essay matters more than a single sentence can show. It is where Bergson turns intuition into a method with repeatable stages: first dissolve false problems created by spatialized thinking, then enter the movement of the thing from within, then return to concepts after the contact, not before it. That is why "Introduction to Metaphysics" is the compact methodological key to the whole corpus. The four major books each perform this sequence at a different scale.

For the user's thesis, that essay is also where Bergson is closest to disciplined *darśana* rather than to romantic immediacy. Intuition is not a mood. It is a trained conversion of attention. One ceases to circle the object as if it were a fixed thing already exhausted by predicates, and instead follows its inner movement until the conceptual shell cracks. That is a real Western analog to the user's appeal to higher modes of contact-knowledge.

### The cinematographic illusion

How does intelligence represent movement? By taking static snapshots and laying them in a row, then re-animating the row — exactly as the cinema (then a decade old) does at twenty-four frames per second. On the strip there is no movement; the "movement" is supplied by the running-through.

> *Instead of attaching ourselves to the inner becoming of things, we place ourselves outside them in order to recompose their becoming artificially. We take, as it were, snapshots of the passing reality, and, since these are characteristic of the reality, it is enough for us to string them along an abstract, uniform, invisible becoming, situated at the back of the apparatus of knowledge, in order to imitate what is characteristic in this becoming itself.*

The decisive phrase is *au fond de l'appareil de la connaissance*, "at the back of the apparatus of knowledge." The "becoming" supplied by intellect is the abstract uniform homogeneous time supplied behind the snapshots: *temps spatialisé*. We have replaced movement by an immobility-row plus a running-through, then puzzle over how movement is possible, Zeno's paradoxes. The paradoxes are real for the cinematographic representation; they have no purchase on real movement.

This is the structural ancestor of the postmodern critique of representation, and what Deleuze will radicalize as "representation as the medium of repetition without difference." It applies to any representation of consciousness, of evolution, of physics: the genuine durational *becoming* drops out and is replaced by an immobility-row plus smuggled-in continuity.

### Life as path-finding through matter

> *Everything happens as if a vast current of consciousness had penetrated into matter, laden, as all consciousness is, with an enormous multiplicity of interpenetrating eventualities.*

The image: a *current of consciousness* entering matter and *forced to divide* by the resistance it meets — a stream entering a delta. Life is the path-finding through matter of an originally undivided current.

This is structurally an *involution-evolution* doctrine. The current must *enter* matter (involution; Aurobindo's term); it then *evolves* through matter into life and intelligence. Bergson has the second movement but not the first. He says "as if" a current entered; he does not work out the involution-side, because for him philosophy proceeds from intuition of duration as we have it, and we have only the evolutionary side. Aurobindo claims the involutionary side *is* intuitable, but only at higher rungs of the consciousness-scale than Bergson reaches. This is where the Aurobindonian completion inserts.

The book's metaphysics is incomplete by Bergson's own admission. It does not say *why* there is an *élan*, nor what the *élan* is the descent *of*; it does not articulate the static eternal pole of the real (Saccidānanda's *sat* and *cit*; *Brahman nirviśeṣa*). It is a metaphysics of becoming without an articulated metaphysics of being.

## Part IV — *Les deux sources de la morale et de la religion* (1932)

### The book and its scaffold

*Les deux sources* (1932) is the application of *durée*-and-*élan* metaphysics to ethics, society, and religion. The Audra-Brereton English (1935), checked by Bergson, is more reliable than the earlier translations. Four chapters: I, moral obligation as closed-morality / instinct-analog; II, static religion as myth-making function; III, dynamic religion and complete mysticism; IV, "mechanism and mysticism" — the diagnosis of modern industrial civilization, *un supplément d'âme*, the universe as *une machine à faire des dieux*.

### Closed and open morality

*Closed morality* (*morale close*) holds a society together by the pressure of its members on one another, functionally analogous to instinct. Its imperatives are categorical within the group; toward outsiders the pressure relaxes:

> *Murder and pillage, like perfidy, fraud, and lying, become not merely licit; they are meritorious.*

The "universalist" rhetoric of closed morality — every nation describing its wars as defending civilization — is the closed morality borrowing the language of openness while remaining functionally closed.

*Open morality* (*morale ouverte*) is not pressure but *attraction* (*attrait*); not formulas but *persons* — the moral hero, the saint:

> *Why do the saints have imitators? They ask for nothing, and yet they obtain. They do not need to exhort; they have only to exist; their existence is an appeal.*

*Leur existence est un appel*, their existence is an appeal, the doctrine in seven words. The *appel* is the call of the *élan* itself. Closed morality is pressure aiming at conservation; open morality is aspiration aiming at creation. The two are different in kind, not in degree.

### Static religion: the myth-making function

Chapter II: intellect has a catastrophic side-effect for social cohesion — it projects the future (including one's own death), the gap between effort and outcome, and alternative courses of action (including desertion of the social bond). These representations are dissolvent. Nature's countermeasure is *fabulation*: intellect generates compensatory representations (gods, spirits, supernatural sanctions) that neutralize the dissolvent ones. Static religion is intellect's antidote to its own side-effects.

The argument is structurally Darwinian without being reductive. Bergson does not say the gods are illusions; he says the *mechanism* of static religion is compensatory representation. There is a second religion on a different basis.

### Dynamic religion: complete mysticism

Chapter III is the part of *Les deux sources* that the user should read most carefully. Bergson argues that there is a second source of religion — *religion dynamique*, dynamic religion — that does not belong to the closed-morality / static-religion complex at all. Dynamic religion is the contact, in certain individuals, with the *élan vital* itself; the experience proper to it is *mysticism*; the form most fully developed of that experience is what Bergson calls *complete mysticism* (*mysticisme complet*).

> *Complete mysticism is in truth that of the great Christian mystics. Setting aside, for the moment, their Christianity, let us consider in them the form apart from the matter. There is no doubt that most of them passed through states resembling the various culmination-points of ancient mysticism. But they only passed through: gathering themselves up to strain themselves in an entirely new effort, they broke through a dam; an immense current of life seized them again; from their heightened vitality there flowed an extraordinary energy, audacity, power of conception and realization.*

Two things matter here. First: Bergson's typology distinguishes *complete* (Christian) mysticism from what he calls *ancient* mysticism (Hellenic, Indian — he names Plotinus and the Hindu and Buddhist traditions). The ancient mystics, on his reading, achieve the contemplative state — the *henosis* of Plotinus, the *samādhi* of the yogins — but stop there; they do not, with the contemplative power they have acquired, return to act in the world. Complete mysticism is contemplative *and* active: it gathers itself in the contemplative state and then comes back, dramatically, to operate in history. His named exemplars: St Paul, St Teresa of Avila, St Catherine of Siena, St Francis, Joan of Arc.

The user must mark a careful point here, because the surface of Bergson's text is regularly misread on this question. Bergson does *not* simply oppose a "complete" Christian mysticism to an arrested Indian or Greek one. The text is more honest than that. Setting out his typology, he treats Brahmanical, Buddhist, and Jain renunciation as forms of mysticism that *did not, by themselves, become complete* — not because India was incapable of complete mysticism but because pessimism about the efficacy of human action, in his diagnosis, prevented the contemplative state from flowing back into world-transforming activity. Then he says, explicitly:

> *A complete mysticism would have gone all the way. It is met with, perhaps, in India, but much later. It is an ardent charity, it is a mysticism comparable to Christian mysticism, that we find in a Ramakrishna or a Vivekananda, to speak only of the most recent.*

This is decisive and should be marked. Bergson *names* Ramakrishna and Vivekananda — by 1932 they were available to him through Romain Rolland's twin biographies of 1929–1930, *La Vie de Ramakrishna* and *La Vie de Vivekananda et l'Évangile universel*, which Rolland sent him personally — as Indian instances of the complete, active, charity-radiating mysticism whose form he had been articulating. He explains the late arrival, on his account, by the contingent fact that Indian material conditions did not permit world-transforming action until the impact of industrial-scientific civilization, a thesis the user is free to reject (the *Bhāgavata*'s *bhakti-rasa*, the medieval *bhakti* poets, Caitanya's six-month *saṅkīrtana* movement and its thirteenth- to seventeenth-century transformations of Bengal-Odisha society, Tukaram's Vārkarī Pune-region transformation, Mīrā in Rajasthan, Lalla in Kashmir, and the long Sant tradition all are world-transforming active mysticisms long predating European industrial contact). What is fair to say of Bergson is more limited. He places the center of gravity of complete mysticism in the Christian tradition, on the basis of the sources available to him; he names the Indian counterpart explicitly when he sees it; he attributes the rarity of the Indian counterpart, on his reading, to material rather than spiritual causes; and he leaves open whether a fuller picture of the *Bhāgavata*-bhakti, Sufi-bhakti, Caitanya-bhakti, and Vārkarī traditions would have changed his weighting. The user should keep Bergson's *structural* point — complete mysticism is contemplative and active in one motion — and supplement his *historical-cultural* picture with the Indian instances Bergson did not himself extract. Caitanya, Mīrā, Tukaram, the Sufi-Bhakti synthesis, Ramakrishna (whom Bergson does name), Vivekananda (whom he does name), Aurobindo and the Mother are all "complete mystics" in Bergson's own structural sense. The user's thesis, at this point, is *augmenting* Bergson by extending the comparative empirical base on which his typology rests.

Second: dynamic religion is *cognitively probative*. The convergence of independent mystics across traditions on certain features of the experience — the breakthrough of a "current of life," the heightened vitality, the union with what the mystics describe as the source of life and love — is, for Bergson, *evidence about reality*. He treats it the way an empirical inquirer would treat the convergence of independent experimenters on a measurement: as a sign that something is being independently registered. This is one of the most striking moves in twentieth-century Western philosophy of religion: a major academic philosopher arguing, on broadly empirical-scientific grounds, that the mystical tradition is *evidentially weighty*, not merely psychologically interesting.

The Bergsonian formula: religion is the crystallization, by careful cooling, of what mysticism deposited, burning, in the soul of humanity (*la cristallisation, opérée par un refroidissement savant, de ce que le mysticisme vint déposer, brûlant, dans l'âme de l'humanité*). Static religion is the cooled crystal; dynamic religion is the burning deposit; the relation between them is the relation between popularization and the science it popularizes.

Bergson's argument against the reduction of mysticism to pathology is itself a methodological landmark. He notes that mystics characteristically display, *after* the breakthrough, an extraordinary increase in practical capacity — administrative, organizational, world-transforming. *Sainte Thérèse fonde, organize, gouverne. Saint Paul parcourt l'Empire.* The pathological diagnosis fails on the empirical evidence: mystics are not only not less competent than ordinary persons in the practical sphere, they are vastly *more* competent. The convergence-of-witnesses, plus the post-experience increase in practical efficacy, gives mysticism a rational warrant analogous to the warrant of any empirical science. The user's project takes this argument structure intact: convergence across traditions on the supramental claim, and post-realization practical fruitfulness, are evidence in the strict Bergsonian-empirical sense.

### The closing pages

The last chapter ("Mécanique et mystique") turns to Bergson's diagnosis of modern industrial civilization. Mechanism — material technology, the manipulation of matter through intelligence — has expanded enormously, providing a "body" of vast extent (railways, telegraphs, telephones, automobiles, aeroplanes; in the early 1930s as Bergson writes, the first hints of mass radio and the conviction of imminent further expansion). The body has grown; the soul has not kept pace. Modern humanity is a giant body with a small soul, and the disproportion is dangerous: the body's reach is so great that mistakes, formerly local, are now global; the soul's wisdom has not deepened to match.

The remedy, Bergson says, is *un supplément d'âme* — a supplement of soul. The phrase is one of his most quoted. The world needs more soul; the only sources of more soul are the dynamic mystics and those who, taking the mystics as their *appel*, follow them. The closing sentences — *l'univers est une machine à faire des dieux* — *the universe is a machine for the making of gods* — should not be misread. The plural is intentional: not the production of *one* god (a finalistic teleology of cosmic destination toward a divine end) but the production of *many* god-becoming individuals (the saints, the mystics, the moral heroes), through whom the *élan* extends itself.

The local French primary gives the governing formula in its sharpest form:

> Let us not stop at saying, as we did above, that mysticism calls for mechanics. Let us add that the enlarged body awaits a supplement of soul, and that mechanics would require a mysticism.

This is the sentence the user should hold onto. Bergson is not nostalgically opposing soul to machine, nor asking for pious ornamentation draped over industrial civilization. He is making a scale-claim: once the human body has been technologically extended into a world-body, only a corresponding extension of moral-spiritual force can keep the enlarged apparatus from turning predatory. The structure is exact for the user's politics. *Mechanics without mystique* becomes predation; *mystique without mechanics* remains socially impotent. Bergson's late social philosophy is therefore not an appendix to the earlier metaphysics but its political consequence.

This last image is the closest Bergson comes to the Aurobindonian doctrine of conscious evolution toward divinity. He has the structural point: the universe is in the business of producing god-becoming creatures; this is what the *élan* is doing. He does not have the further specifications: that there is a definite consciousness-scale (Higher Mind, Illumined Mind, Intuitive Mind, Overmind, Supermind); that the descent has stages (psychic, spiritual, supramental transformation, in Aurobindo's triple-transformation doctrine); that there is a cosmological terminus (the supramental descent into the earth-consciousness as a stable poise). What he has, again, is the right shape without the full content. *Les deux sources* is, of his books, the one closest to the user's thesis on the religious-mystical side, and the one whose Indian-completion is most directly available.

For the user reading toward *bhakti* / surrender: it is *Les deux sources* Chapter III that the user should hold beside the *Bhāgavata*'s *bhakti-rasa* doctrine, beside Caitanya's *acintya-bhedābheda*, beside Ramakrishna's "honey and bee," and beside Aurobindo on surrender. Bergson's "complete mysticism" is the structural shape of what those traditions specify in detail. The mystic who is contemplative-and-active is the *bhakta* who has achieved *prema* and *sevā* in unity; the *appel* of the mystic-as-exemplar is the *darśana* of the saint that draws the seeker on; the immense current of life that seizes them is the *śakti* of the Divine flowing through the surrendered soul. Bergson saw this from the European side; the user can supply the Indian side without distortion of either.

## Part V — Banerji's Bergson-Aurobindo Mapping

### The argument

Debashish Banerji's essay "The Inward Turn and the Radically Creative Posthuman: Bergson, Deleuze and Sri Aurobindo" — circulated through the Indian Posthumanism Network and reproduced on Banerji's website, with a longer version published in the volume *Critical Posthumanism and Planetary Futures* — is the bridge text between continental philosophy and Aurobindonian *darśana* in the Anglophone scholarship. The user has it extracted at `sources/banerji/07_inward_turn_bergson_deleuze.md`. What Banerji does there is to argue that the *inward turn* of late modern philosophy — the displacement of consciousness from the rational ego (cogito) toward deeper sources of being — has two complementary trajectories that converge on Aurobindo: the Bergsonian (intuition of duration) and the Deleuzean (the third synthesis of time, the aleatory point). Aurobindo extends both — the Bergsonian by *individualizing* duration in the *psychic being* (*caitya puruṣa*), and the Deleuzean by *evolutionizing* the rupture into a doctrine of progressive supramental descent.

> "The psychic being [is] the reincarnating person in each individual, and as such, the form of durational becoming individualizing the pure past of cosmic memory. Bringing the psychic being to the front [is] the *psychic transformation* — the first stage of Aurobindo's Triple Transformation."

(Banerji, *The Inward Turn and the Radically Creative Posthuman*, as extracted at `sources/banerji/07_inward_turn_bergson_deleuze.md`.) The structural claim: Bergson has *durée* but does not have a doctrine of the *individual* form of durational becoming; Aurobindo supplies that doctrine in the form of the *psychic being*, the reincarnating personal center that *individualizes* what Bergson treats as a continuous flow. This is, the user should mark, an extension of Bergson rather than a correction: Bergson's *durée* is not falsified by the doctrine of the psychic being, only specified.

### The saṃjñāna / ājñāna mapping

The mapping the user is most interested in turns on Aurobindo's typology of modes of knowledge in *The Synthesis of Yoga* and the *Record of Yoga*. Aurobindo distinguishes four modes:

- **prajñāna** — apprehending knowledge: the knowledge that operates *through* a representation, holding the object before the mind. This is the mode proper to ordinary mind and to the intellect generally. It is *adequate but indirect*; the object is known *via* the representation.
- **saṃjñāna** — knowing-with: contact-knowledge, in which the knower *touches* the object directly, has it *with* and *in* the act of knowing, but not yet identifies with it. The Sanskrit *sam-* prefix carries the sense of *together-with*; *jñāna* with *sam-* is knowledge that is together-with what it knows. This is the mode proper to the *Intuitive Mind* — Aurobindo's name for the rung of consciousness above ordinary mind and below the Overmind.
- **ājñāna** — knowing-by-direct-contact, *toward* the object, sometimes "knowledge by indwelling": the *ā-* prefix carries the directional sense of "toward" or "into"; *ājñāna* is knowledge that goes-into its object. It is, in Aurobindo, a closer mode than *saṃjñāna*; one *enters* the object rather than merely touching it.
- **vijñāna** — integral knowledge: the mode proper to the Supermind, in which all the other modes operate as one, in which knowing-of, knowing-with, knowing-by-contact, and knowing-by-identity are non-different. *Vi-* here marks not separation but integral distinction; *vijñāna* is the wisdom that *holds* all the modes simultaneously.

The user's thesis turns on the *vijñāna* register at the top of this list and on the differentiated handling of the lower three; the document does not need to name the user's specific construction here. What matters is the mapping: which of these four modes Bergson's "intuition" most precisely activates, and which are left to be supplied from elsewhere.

Banerji's claim — and the user's reading of him — is that **Bergson's intuition activates *saṃjñāna* and *ājñāna*** but does not extend to *vijñāna*. Bergson's "intellectual sympathy by which one places oneself within an object in order to coincide with what is unique in it" is precisely *saṃjñāna* (knowing-with, by sympathy) on its way to *ājñāna* (entering-into). The Bergsonian intuition reaches the *intuitive mental* register and is, of all Western philosophical articulations, the one that most precisely names that register. It does not reach the *Overmind* (the cosmic consciousness in which all individual centers are simultaneously perceived as one and as many, the "Golden Lid" of Iśā 15) and does not envisage the *Supermind* (the *vijñāna*-poise in which being and becoming are non-different).

Concretely: when Bergson says intuition is "the kind of intellectual sympathy by which one places oneself within an object in order to coincide with what is unique in it and consequently inexpressible," he has saṃjñāna (intellectual sympathy = *sam-* knowing) and ājñāna (places oneself within the object = entering into it). When he describes the mystical breakthrough in *Les deux sources* as the moment in which "an immense current of life seized them again" and produced an "extraordinary energy, audacity, power of conception and realization," he is describing what Aurobindo would identify as the touch of the higher *prāṇa-śakti* — close to the Overmind's overflow but not the Overmind itself, which would carry a wholly different kind of *unifying* perception. The Christian mystics Bergson cites do, in many cases, give descriptions of the unifying perception (Teresa, Eckhart, Ruusbroec); Bergson reads these as *consequences* of the breakthrough rather than as defining a *higher* register. Aurobindo would read them as glimpses of the next rung up.

### What Banerji adds

The Banerji extension to Bergson runs in three directions:

**1. Individuation of duration.** Bergson speaks of *the* duration of consciousness as one continuous flow; he does not have a stable doctrine of *the individual* as a permanent center of duration through reincarnation. The *psychic being* — Aurobindo's *caitya puruṣa* — is the reincarnating personal center that individualizes durational becoming across lifetimes. It is, Banerji says, "the form of durational becoming individualizing the pure past of cosmic memory." This phrase will repay attention. *Pure past of cosmic memory* is Banerji's translation back into Bergsonian language of the Aurobindonian doctrine that the universe holds, in its own *citta*, the trace of every becoming; *individualizing* it is the work of the psychic being, which is the personal center that *retains* and *grows through* successive incarnations. The user's thesis — phenomenology of the individual psychic being, NOT Hegelian-universal — is exactly this Banerji move: take Bergson's *durée* and individuate it through the doctrine of the psychic being.

**2. Cosmic time as progressive remembrance.** Banerji writes:

> "Cosmic time may be thought of as progressive remembrance of increasing degrees of involved creative freedom, from matter through life to mind."

This is one of the user's most important sentences in the Banerji essay, and it should be unpacked. *Cosmic time* is not the time of clocks but the time of the universe's becoming. *Progressive remembrance* is the time-form proper to the involution-evolution doctrine: what evolves was first involved (Aurobindo's central thesis), so evolution is structurally a *remembering* — the bringing-back-to-explicit-presence of what was already implicitly present. *Increasing degrees of involved creative freedom* names the gradient: matter has freedom involved at low intensity, life at higher, mind at higher still, the supramental at maximum. Cosmic time is the unfolding of this gradient. This re-frames Bergson's open-ended *élan vital* in two ways: it gives the *élan* a cosmic *content* (not merely a creative push but a remembering of what was involved) and it gives it a *direction* (toward higher and higher degrees of explicit freedom, with the supramental as the limit). Bergson has the push without the content and direction; Aurobindo, on Banerji's reading, supplies both.

**3. Heidegger and Hegel.** Banerji's framing is anti-Heideggerian and anti-Hegelian in a precise way that the user has marked as the "T11" of the user's thesis (the ontology-of-phenomenology thread). Heidegger sees only epistemic conditioning with limited escape — the saving power of the artwork. Hegel sees the dialectical self-realization of Absolute Spirit as totalization. Aurobindo, on Banerji's reading, is between them: he has Hegel's evolutionary dialectic (involution-evolution-transformation maps formally onto thesis-antithesis-synthesis but with an ontological referent that is *real* rather than logical) without Hegel's totalizing absolute Spirit (the individuation in the psychic being is genuine), and he has Heidegger's diagnosis of modernity's enclosure (the over-reach of representation, the technological framing of being) without Heidegger's epistemic-conditioning closure (the supramental beckons; the closure can be opened by yogic praxis).

This is the user's thread, and the user has named Banerji as the precedent. For the present document the relevant point is that **Bergson's metaphysics, individuated through Banerji-Aurobindo's psychic being and re-cosmologized through the involution-evolution doctrine, becomes the user's metaphysics** — at least as far as the becoming-side. The being-side (Saccidānanda's *sat* and *cit* poise; the static eternal pole of the Real that is non-different from the dynamic) requires a further move that neither Bergson nor Banerji's Bergson-Deleuze-Aurobindo synthesis directly addresses.

### What Bergson cannot supply

Three things Banerji's reading must add from Aurobindo, because Bergson does not have them:

**(a) The static-and-dynamic non-difference.** Saccidānanda is *both* sat (eternal being) and ananda (eternal becoming-as-bliss). Bergson has only the becoming-side; for him the eternal-static pole would be a residue of intellectualism. Aurobindo's central claim — that the static and the dynamic are non-different in the supramental, which is structurally the user's "static and dynamic Saccidānanda" thesis — requires the supramental rung of the consciousness-scale, which Bergson does not reach.

**(b) The doctrine of involution.** Bergson has evolution as creative becoming; he does not have the prior descent of consciousness into matter that, in Aurobindo, makes evolution possible by laying down what is to be re-emerged. Without involution, Bergsonian evolution is open-ended creation without a determinate content; with involution, evolution becomes the *remembering* of an already-laid-down content (without thereby reducing to finalism, because the *forms* of remembrance are genuinely creative).

**(c) The doctrine of līlā.** Bergson has no positive doctrine of divine play. The closest he comes is the *appel* of the mystic-exemplar in *Les deux sources*; but this is the *call* to the human, not the *play* of the divine. The user's thesis on *līlā* — the divine play through willful self-veiling, *māyā* as itself a manifestation of the divine — has no analog in Bergson and would not be intelligible to him. This is one of the most precise places where the user's thesis exceeds Bergsonian metaphysics.

The Banerji bridge is real and useful. It is also limited: Banerji is reading Bergson into Aurobindo, not Aurobindo into Bergson. He uses Bergson to give a Western articulation of the *intuitive mental* and to constellate it with Deleuze's third synthesis; he does not claim that Bergson can supply the cosmology. The user's reading should follow Banerji's structure and respect his limits: take from Bergson the *intuitive mental* register articulated with unmatched Western precision; take from Aurobindo (via Banerji) the individuating, cosmologizing, and *līlā*-giving extensions; do not require Bergson to do work he did not undertake.

## Part VI — Deleuze's *Bergsonism*

### The book and its argument

Gilles Deleuze published *Le Bergsonisme* in 1966 (PUF), the small book that, alongside his *Différence et répétition* of 1968 and his readings of Spinoza, Nietzsche, Hume, and Leibniz, established his early position. The English translation by Hugh Tomlinson and Barbara Habberjam appeared in 1988 (Zone Books). It is one of the great works of philosophical reading in the twentieth century: Deleuze takes Bergson, who by 1966 had been almost entirely eclipsed in French philosophy by phenomenology and structuralism, and recovers him as the most powerful resource for a non-representational metaphysics. The Deleuze-Bergson reading is now standard and has done more than any other single thing to keep Bergson alive in academic philosophy.

The book has five chapters, organized around what Deleuze identifies as Bergson's three central methodological commitments: **intuition as method**, **multiplicity as structure**, and **memory as ontology**. The argument develops as follows.

### Intuition as method

Deleuze's first move is to deny that Bergsonian intuition is anything woolly or anti-philosophical. On the contrary: *intuition is a strict method*, with three rules and three corresponding acts.

The three rules: (1) state and create problems (do not accept inherited pseudo-problems); (2) discover genuine differences in *kind* (not merely in degree, which mask kind-differences as quantitative variations); (3) state problems and solve them in terms of *time* rather than *space*.

The three acts: (1) the *critical* moment, in which inherited problems are dissolved by being shown to be confused composites of mixed kinds; (2) the *intensive* moment, in which the genuine differences in kind are recovered (qualitative multiplicity, duration, the two memories, the two moralities); (3) the *integrative* moment, in which the recovered kinds are reunified at a higher level — the level of the *virtual*, where they are seen as differentiations of one indivisible source.

The user should mark how powerful this re-reading is. Deleuze takes Bergson's apparently meandering work — psychology, mind-body, evolution, religion — and reveals an extraordinary methodological consistency. The same three-step in every book: dissolve the pseudo-problem, recover the differences in kind, reunify at the source. The *Essai* dissolves the determinism-freedom debate, recovers the two multiplicities, reunifies at the deep self. *Matière et mémoire* dissolves realism-versus-idealism, recovers the two memories, reunifies in the matter-mind continuum. *L'évolution créatrice* dissolves mechanism-versus-finalism, recovers the two tendencies (life and matter), reunifies in the *élan*. *Les deux sources* dissolves the moral-relativism / moral-universalism debate, recovers the two moralities, reunifies at — well, this is the move Deleuze's reading is least comfortable with, because *Les deux sources* reunifies at the mystical-religious source, and Deleuze has no comfortable category for that. He treats it gently and moves on.

### Multiplicity as virtual

The second central move of Deleuze's reading is the radicalization of Bergson's two-multiplicities doctrine into a doctrine of the **virtual** as a third term. In *Bergsonisme* Chapter 3, Deleuze argues:

The qualitative multiplicity is, in Bergson, the multiplicity of *durée* — a continuous, interpenetrating, heterogeneous, undivided yet differentiated whole. Deleuze names this multiplicity **virtual**. The *virtual* is not the *possible* (a word Bergson detested, because the possible is always merely a retroactive projection of the actual that mistakes itself for the antecedent of the actual); the virtual is the genuine ontological category for "that which is real without being actual, ideal without being abstract." The virtual is *real* in the strongest sense — it is the very stuff of duration; it is not *actual* because it has not yet been differentiated into separated identities; it is *ideal* in the sense of belonging to the order of differences-in-themselves; it is *not abstract* because it is the dynamic source from which all actuality issues.

This is one of the most important moves in twentieth-century metaphysics. The standard categories of Western thought split being into *actual* (real, determinate) and *possible* (ideal, indeterminate, mere logical space), and treat the two as exhausting being. Deleuze, reading Bergson, opens a third category: real-and-indeterminate, real-as-process-of-determination, real-as-differentiation-of-differences. Multiplicity in this radicalized sense is *not* a many of items (which would be quantitative) and *not* a continuous whole that *contains* a many (which would still be implicitly spatial); it is a many-in-becoming, a differentiation-without-prior-undifferentiated-One, a real that is genuinely creative because it is not the resolution of a prior possibility but the production of difference *as such*.

The virtual, Deleuze says, *actualizes itself* — and the actualization is not a resemblance (the actual does not "look like" the virtual; it differs from it) but a *creation*, a making-real of differences that were virtually but not actually distinct. This is Bergson's *invention, création de formes, élaboration continue de l'absolument nouveau*, with the metaphysical machinery exposed.

### Memory as ontology

The third move of Deleuze's reading takes *Matière et mémoire*'s pure memory and makes it the centerpiece of a *non-psychological* ontology. Pure memory, in Bergson, is the survival of the past *as past* in itself. Deleuze radicalizes this: pure memory is **the past as ontological dimension**, the past-in-itself that is not the memory of any present but the *condition of possibility* of any present. The present, on this reading, is what passes; the past is what *is*. Without a past-in-itself, no present could pass into past, and no future could become present.

This is the structural ancestor of Deleuze's own doctrine of the *three syntheses of time* in *Différence et répétition* (1968). The first synthesis — the present that contracts the immediate past and anticipates the immediate future — is Bergsonian habit. The second synthesis — the *pure past*, in which the present passes — is Bergsonian pure memory generalized into ontology. The third synthesis — the future as eternal return of difference, the *aleatory point* that ruptures the cone of memory — is *Deleuze's own* addition, the move beyond Bergson, the move toward Nietzsche and the *eternal return of the different*.

It is precisely this third synthesis that Banerji uses in the *Inward Turn* essay to make the connection to Aurobindo: the aleatory point that ruptures the time-cone is, for Banerji, the structural form of yogic *praxis* — the moment in which the sādhaka, gathering himself in attention, *makes possible* a future not deducible from the past. This is the user's thesis on the radical opening that *līlā* makes possible against the closures of mere remembrance.

### The seed and the germ

In *Différence et répétition*, Deleuze develops what he calls the *germ* or *seed* (he uses *germe* in French, with both senses) as the figure of the virtual that actualizes itself differentially. The germ is the virtual-as-active-source: it is what makes the new, what differentiates, what creates. The doctrine has direct Bergsonian roots: in *L'évolution créatrice*, Bergson speaks of the germ-cell that, in passing from generation to generation, carries forward not the parents' particular accomplishments but the *creative push* that the parents are momentary expressions of. The germ is the *élan* in continuity; it is what the parents were the temporary differentiations of and what the offspring will be the next temporary differentiations of.

Deleuze takes this image and ontologizes it. The germ is not a biological entity but a metaphysical one: the virtual, in its mode of self-active source, *is* the germ of every actuality. Each actual thing is the *fruit* of a germ; the germ is not in the fruit (an actuality cannot contain its own virtual source as part of itself) but is what the fruit *is differentiated from*. This gives Deleuze a way of speaking of the source of differentiation that does not reify the source as another item.

For the user's mapping: the Deleuzean germ is structurally what Aurobindo names the *seed-state* of the supramental — the involved-and-self-actualizing principle that holds all evolution implicitly and unfolds it explicitly. The Aurobindonian doctrine that "what evolves must first have been involved" is, at the metaphysical level, the same doctrine as Deleuze's germ-virtual-actualization triad, with two differences: (1) Aurobindo names the seed-state the *Real-Idea* of the Supermind, with a definite cosmological location; (2) Aurobindo's involution gives the seed a *content* (the determinate forms it will unfold into) that Deleuze's virtual leaves indeterminate. The user can hold both: take Deleuze's metaphysical machinery (virtual / actual / differentiation, germ as source) and supply the Aurobindonian content (the seed-state has a determinate cosmological structure given by the supramental's prior involution).

### What Deleuze gets right and what he leaves out

Deleuze gets Bergson *philosophically* right with extraordinary precision. The Deleuzean reading is the standard now because it is true: Bergson *is* doing what Deleuze says he is doing, and the methodology is *more* rigorous than the fluid prose has led generations of Anglophone readers to suppose. The user should rely on Deleuze for the technical apparatus (intuition as three-step method, qualitative multiplicity as virtual, pure memory as ontological past) and read Bergson directly with that apparatus in mind.

Deleuze leaves out, or deliberately edges around, Bergson's *Les deux sources* — the religious and mystical book. He has no comfortable category for *complete mysticism*; his own metaphysics, although it has a place for the singular and the eventful, has no *teleology* (Deleuze is sharper on this than Bergson) and therefore no place for the mystic as the figure who pulls humanity toward a higher destiny. The Deleuze-Bergson is a Bergson of the first three books; the fourth-book Bergson, the religious Bergson, is unreceived in Deleuze. The user should mark this. Banerji's bridge takes Deleuze on the metaphysics and Bergson on the religion, with Aurobindo as the figure who synthesizes them, which is to say: who has both the rigorous metaphysics of difference (the supramental's individual-and-cosmic differentiation) and the religious destiny (evolutionary supramental as the universe's actual direction). Bergson alone has the religious without the metaphysical machinery; Deleuze alone has the machinery without the religious; Aurobindo has both. This, again, is the user's constellation.

## Part VII — Reading Notes

### Where Bergson confirms

The user's position finds in Bergson a number of independent confirmations from a distinct philosophical tradition. These should be marked.

**Intuition as method against representation.** The user's thesis treats representation — the *prajñāna*-mode operating without the deeper modes — as the diagnostic site of modern philosophical and political pathology (the "monkey describing its own brain," correlationist closure, OOO's reified phenomenology, the postmodern infinite regress). Bergson, working from inside the late-nineteenth-century French academy, arrives at a structurally identical diagnosis: intellect is an instrument of action on solids whose extension to the whole of reality produces precisely the spatializing-quantifying-representational pathology the user diagnoses. Bergson's *intuition*, as Deleuze's reading reveals, is a strict method for restoring contact with the durating real. This converges with the user's call for the *intuitive mental* register and gives it Western technical vocabulary.

**Durée as anti-spatialized reality.** The user's thesis, drawing on Aurobindo and on Prigogine, is that the real is intrinsically temporal, intrinsically becoming, that "from being to becoming" is not a discovery one might make on the way to a more fundamental being-only ontology but is constitutive of the real. Bergson is the most precise Western articulator of this point. *Durée* is real; the spatialized-time of physics is a useful abstraction that has been mistaken for the underlying reality. The user can use Bergson's analyzes (the two multiplicities; the cinematographic illusion; the cone of memory) as instruments for showing, in any given case, how a domain of qualitative-durational reality has been falsified by importation of quantitative-spatial schemes.

**The open society and dynamic religion.** The user's politics centers on *matsya-nyāya* (the Arthaśāstra's prevention-of-predation as the limited but real *telos* of the state), Aurobindo's diagnosis of "collective egoism," and KC Bhattacharyya's anti-capture position; the user's religious commitments center on *bhakti*-surrender and on the recurrence-of-divinity in the saints. Bergson's *Les deux sources* gives the structural distinctions that match these: the *closed society* held together by pressure (analogous to the closed national-tribal-corporate-bureaucratic forms the user opposes) versus the *open society* drawn by aspiration (analogous to the predation-preventing state ordered toward universalization); the *static religion* of fabulation-for-cohesion versus the *dynamic religion* of mystical contact (analogous to the *bhāva-bhakti* / *prema-bhakti* that draws by *appel* rather than enforcing by formula). Karl Popper's *Open Society* (1945) took Bergson's distinction and politically operationalized it; the user can take Bergson's distinction with greater fidelity to its religious-metaphysical depth.

**The body as locus of action, the brain as organ of attention to life.** *Matière et mémoire*'s doctrine that the brain is not the producer of mind but the organ of attention-to-life (the body's pragmatic delimitation of the image-totality) anticipates by half a century the embodied-cognition / 4E-cognition tradition (embedded, embodied, extended, enactive) and confirms the user's anti-reductionist position on the mind-brain question without requiring a metaphysical dualism. Mind is not "in" the brain because the relation is the wrong way around: brain is one image-among-images that *selects* (subtractively) for life-relevant action; mind is the durational reality of which the brain is the action-tuned organ.

### Where Bergson diverges

The divergences are real and the user should mark them.

**No positive doctrine of līlā or divine self-veiling.** Bergson has the *élan* and its creative push; he does not have a doctrine of the divine *playing*, of *māyā* as the divine's own willful self-veiling that is a *manifestation* of the divine rather than a foreign obscuration. The user's T7 — Maya as positively existent (*bhāvarūpa*), as Brahman's own self-manifestation through *svātantrya* (Śiva's sovereign freedom that includes freedom-to-self-veil), as *līlā* — has no analog in Bergson and would not be intelligible in his frame, because Bergson lacks the *static-and-dynamic non-difference* that allows *līlā* to be the *play* of an eternal-and-creative source. He has only the dynamic side; his "creation" is genuinely creative but is not the *play* of an antecedent fullness; it is push without ground.

**The *élan vital* lacks the cosmological completeness of Aurobindo's involution-evolution.** Bergson has evolution; he does not have involution. Banerji's mapping (*cosmic time as progressive remembrance of increasing degrees of involved creative freedom*) supplies what Bergson lacks: the doctrine that what evolves must first have been involved, that evolution is a *remembering* of an already-laid-down content. Without this doctrine, Bergsonian evolution is creative but directionless; with it, evolution is creative *and* directional, with the supramental as the limit. The user's thesis requires the involution-evolution doctrine; Bergson does not deliver it.

**No doctrine of the supramental, the Overmind, or the static-and-dynamic non-difference of Saccidānanda.** Bergson reaches the *intuitive mental*; he does not reach the *Overmind* (the cosmic-individual unifying consciousness that perceives all centers simultaneously as one and as many) and does not envisage the *Supermind* (the *vijñāna*-poise in which being and becoming are non-different). His mysticism in *Les deux sources* is read by him as a heightening of the *élan*-current; he does not distinguish, as Aurobindo does, between the rungs of the higher consciousness and does not theorize the structure of the cosmic consciousness as a definite station. The user's thesis requires these distinctions; Bergson does not have them.

**The static eternal pole of Saccidānanda.** Bergson has, on the side of being, only the limit of duration as it extends into matter — the dispersed, near-zero rhythm of the material end of the spectrum. He does not have the *other* end: the eternal-static pole of the Real, *Brahman nirviśeṣa*, the *sat* of Saccidānanda. The closest he comes is the conclusion of *L'évolution créatrice*, where he gestures toward an "absolute" that would be, presumably, the *élan* itself viewed as totality; but this is a gesture, not a doctrine. The user's thesis is that the Real is intrinsically *both* static and dynamic, that Saccidānanda is the non-difference of being and becoming; Bergson has only the becoming side. To complete him, the user needs the Aurobindonian (and Vivaraṇa-Kashmir-Shaiva) doctrine of *svātantrya*, the sovereign freedom that holds being and becoming as non-different.

**No individuation in the strict Aurobindonian sense.** Bergson speaks of "the deep self" as the locus of free acts; he does not have a doctrine of the *psychic being* as the reincarnating personal center that *survives* across lifetimes and grows by the differentiated development of its specific durational tendency. Banerji's extension of Bergson supplies this individuating doctrine. Without it, Bergson's "duration" is the duration of a flux that has unity in the moment but does not have the trans-incarnational continuity the user's T6 requires.

### How Bergson sets up Whitehead and Deleuze

Two figures who carry Bergson's position forward and the user should hold beside him:

**Alfred North Whitehead** published *Process and Reality* in 1929 (Gifford Lectures 1927–28). The doctrines at the heart of Whitehead — that *actual occasions* are the fundamental units, that they are *concretions* (concrescences) of prior occasions, that "the many become one and are increased by one," that nature is process not substance, that the "fallacy of misplaced concreteness" is the mistaking of a derivative abstraction for the concrete — are recognizably the Bergsonian doctrines of duration, contraction, novelty, and the cinematographic illusion, restated in Whitehead's idiom. Whitehead's acknowledgment is explicit: in the preface to *Process and Reality* he names Bergson, William James, and John Dewey as the predecessors his cosmology principally extends; the relation is one of parallel development with mutual influence. For the user, the Whitehead-Bergson constellation supplies the *process side* of the user's thesis (Aurobindo's becoming-side of Saccidānanda, Prigogine's irreversibility, the Deleuzean differentiation), with Whitehead supplying a more cosmologically explicit articulation than Bergson (Whitehead has actual entities, eternal objects, prehensions, and a doctrine of God as the ordering of the eternal objects toward the actual world).

The Whitehead-Bergson asymmetry: Whitehead has the cosmological apparatus Bergson lacks (eternal objects supply the static-pole content; God's primordial nature supplies the eternal pole) but is less precise than Bergson on intuition-as-method and on the matter-mind continuum. The user should read them as complementary: Bergson on method and on the durational structure of consciousness; Whitehead on the cosmological architecture in which durational structure operates.

**Gilles Deleuze** carries Bergson's position forward as has been described in Part VI. The Deleuzean radicalization of multiplicity into the virtual, of intuition into a strict three-rule method, of pure memory into ontological past, gives the user the contemporary technical apparatus for thinking Bergsonian-Aurobindonian metaphysics in a non-representational register. The aleatory point of the third synthesis is the Deleuzean equivalent of the yogic *kairos* — the moment of opening at which a future not deducible from the past becomes possible. The user's reading of "eternal return as return of difference" (T-Auxiliary) takes Deleuze's reading of Nietzsche (*Différence et répétition* III, *Logique du sens* XXIV) and constellates it with Aurobindo's evolutionary cosmology; this constellation is Banerji's, and Bergson is its bridge.

### Bergson and Aurobindo as parallel projects

The user should hold one final framing. Aurobindo and Bergson were near-contemporaries (Aurobindo 1872–1950, Bergson 1859–1941). They never met, never corresponded (so far as the published archives show), and worked in completely different traditions and languages. Bergson knew of Indian philosophy in the second-hand way of a turn-of-the-century French intellectual; Aurobindo, an extraordinary Cambridge classicist with fluent French, would have read Bergson's books in the original — *L'évolution créatrice* came out in 1907 just as Aurobindo was deepening his yoga in Pondicherry — and there are passages in *The Life Divine* (composed across 1914–1919 in *Arya*) where Aurobindo's vocabulary on evolution, intuition, and the relation of intellect to the higher consciousness echoes Bergsonian formulations closely enough that direct influence is plausible (though never explicit and not, to my knowledge, established by manuscript evidence).

What is striking is that *the same metaphysics is being articulated in two registers*. Bergson's *durée* is Aurobindo's *citta-vṛtti* of the Time-Spirit; Bergson's *élan vital* is Aurobindo's evolutionary supramental in its descent through life; Bergson's intuition is Aurobindo's *saṃjñāna*-and-*ājñāna* operating in what Aurobindo names the *intuitive mental*; Bergson's complete mysticism is Aurobindo's *integral yoga* mystic who is contemplative-and-active. The convergence is not coincidence; it is the convergence of two careful inquirers on the same structural features of the real, viewed from different cultural standpoints. Bergson saw farther than any other modern Western philosopher into this territory; Aurobindo, with the *yogic praxis* and the *darśana*-tradition behind him, mapped the territory more fully. They are best read together, as complementary articulations of one metaphysics — the user's metaphysics — in the user's exact sense of one Real said in many senses.

### Reading sequence

For a reader new to Bergson, the recommended sequence is:

**1. Begin with *Matière et mémoire*, Chapter 1.** The opening pages on image, body, and perception are the most powerful introduction to Bergson's method. Read the French aloud if possible; the prose enacts the durational integration it describes. The user's two-volume *Œuvres* (PUF, "Édition du Centenaire," 1959) is the standard French edition; the Paul/Palmer English (1911, often reprinted) is adequate. Allow several hours for the chapter; it should be read slowly.

**2. Then read *L'évolution créatrice*, Chapter 1.** The critique of mechanism and finalism, the doctrine of the *élan*, the framing of intuition as method. Mitchell English (1911); the French is more vivid. Skip Chapter II on first reading (the radial divergences are detailed and can be picked up later); return for Chapters III and IV when the framework is set.

**3. Read *Les deux sources*, Chapter 3, on dynamic religion.** The mysticism chapter is the most directly relevant to the user's *bhakti* / surrender thread, and is also the chapter least often read by Bergson scholars (who tend to stop at *L'évolution créatrice*). Audra/Brereton English (1935) is reliable. The closing pages of Chapter 4, on *un supplément d'âme* and the universe as a machine for the making of gods, follow naturally.

**4. Then return to the *Essai sur les données immédiates* for the foundations.** Chapter II on the two multiplicities is the most important; Chapters I and III can be read more quickly. Pogson English (1910) is dated but readable.

**5. The 1903 essay "Introduction à la métaphysique"** (in *La pensée et le mouvant*) is the compact statement of intuition-as-method; the single best methodological supplement to the four major books. Read it after the four books to consolidate.

**6. Deleuze's *Bergsonisme* (1966)** is the indispensable second-order reading. After Bergson, read Deleuze, who will reveal the methodological rigor that Bergson's prose can mask.

**7. Banerji's *Inward Turn* essay** is the bridge to the user's wider constellation. It is best read *last*, after Bergson and Deleuze, because it presupposes both and adds the Aurobindonian extension.

**8. Optional further reading**: Vladimir Jankélévitch, *Henri Bergson* (1959; English 2015); Jean Wahl, *Vers le concret* (1932); Maurice Merleau-Ponty, the chapters on Bergson in *Éloge de la philosophie* (1953) and *La nature* (lecture notes 1956–60); Frédéric Worms, *Bergson ou les deux sens de la vie* (PUF 2004), the most authoritative recent French commentary; Suzanne Guerlac, *Thinking in Time: An Introduction to Henri Bergson* (Cornell 2006), a clear English re-introduction.

### Final note

Bergson is the philosopher who, of all moderns, most precisely articulates the *intuitive mental* register from inside the modern Western tradition. He does not climb higher; he does not have the cosmological completeness; he does not know the doctrines of involution, of *līlā*, of the static-and-dynamic non-difference of Saccidānanda. What he has, he has with extraordinary precision and beauty, and for the user's purposes he is indispensable for three things: (i) the technical articulation of intuition-as-method in a Western register; (ii) the diagnosis of the spatializing intellect's pathologies, applicable as instrument to many domains; (iii) the framing of the closed/open and static/dynamic distinctions in morality and religion, which the user can extend without distortion. He is the bridge to Whitehead and Deleuze; he is the figure whom Banerji uses to bring continental philosophy into productive contact with Aurobindo. He stands where the user most needs him to stand: at the place where modern Western philosophy, working from inside its own resources, comes closest to recognizing what the *darśana*-tradition has always known. Read carefully, his books vindicate the user's thesis from a wholly independent direction, and they leave open precisely the work the user's thesis is undertaking.
