# Foucault — Reader's Brief

A deep but bounded reader of Michel Foucault aimed at substituting for direct work with the primary texts when time is short. The user's thesis stands in a complicated relation to Foucault — partly polemical, partly indebted — and this brief tries to mark both edges precisely. References to "the user's thesis" or "the user's position" name a developed Vedānta-Tantra-Aurobindo synthesis whose internal vocabulary is laid out elsewhere; the apparatus is not repeated here.

The brief follows Foucault's own self-periodization, not the standard textbook framing. In the late interviews — the 1983 conversation with Dreyfus and Rabinow eventually printed as "On the Genealogy of Ethics," and the introduction Foucault drafted in 1983-84 for *L'usage des plaisirs* — Foucault retrospectively re-described his entire trajectory as an inquiry into three "modes of objectification" by which human beings are made into subjects: through the formation of *savoirs* (the archaeological axis: *Folie et déraison*, *Naissance de la clinique*, *Les mots et les choses*, *L'archéologie du savoir*); through "dividing practices" and the operations of power (the genealogical axis: *L'ordre du discours*, *Surveiller et punir*, *La volonté de savoir*); and through the way human beings turn themselves into subjects of their own action (the ethical axis: *L'usage des plaisirs*, *Le souci de soi*, *Les aveux de la chair*, and the Collège de France lectures of 1981-82, *L'herméneutique du sujet*). Foucault was insistent, against the early Anglophone reception, that these were not three different projects but three angles on one problem — *the historically specific conditions under which something appears as true, as known, as a possible object of inquiry, and as a possible kind of subject*. This brief follows that order. French is cited from Foucault's own editions (Gallimard 1961, 1963, 1966, 1969, 1975, 1976; Gallimard/Seuil 2001 for the *Hermeneutics* lectures; Gallimard 2018 for *Les aveux de la chair*); English renderings here are working translations from the French, with the standard published translations consulted only where a divergence is consequential and explicitly flagged.

## Reader's Orientation

### Life and arc

Cross-engagement: Karl Marx works the same manifestation status axis in the political-social register. Here the operative value is `socially-or-discursively-stabilized`; Karl Marx lands on that same value in [primary text](cite://marx/capital/1.4). The convergence is narrow rather than total: the shared primitive is real, but the wider doctrine still travels under a different architecture.

Paul-Michel Foucault was born in Poitiers in 1926, into a provincial bourgeois family of physicians; he died in Paris on 25 June 1984, of complications of AIDS, the first major French intellectual to do so publicly. Between those years he produced a body of work that, more than any other twentieth-century French philosopher except perhaps Deleuze, restructured the international Anglophone reception of "theory" — and produced, in passing, the conceptual furniture (*disciplinary power, biopower, panopticism, discourse, the deployment of sexuality, the care of the self*) on which most contemporary social thought now relies whether it knows it or not.

Cross-engagement: Abhinavagupta presses the same selfhood structure axis in the phenomenological register, but with the value `relational-self` rather than `split-or-produced-subject` in [primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

Foucault himself, in the 1983-84 retrospective texts, divided the work into three axes, corresponding to three "modes of objectification" of the human subject: the *axe du savoir* (knowledge), the *axe du pouvoir* (power), and the *axe de l'éthique* (ethics — the relation of the self to itself). In strict chronology these are sequential phases; in Foucault's own re-description they are co-implicated angles on a single question. Both framings are useful, and this brief uses them in conjunction: chronological order gives the texture of the development, but the three-axis self-periodization is what Foucault eventually said the work had been about.

The **archaeological axis** — Foucault's term — is the historian-of-the-*épistémè*. *Folie et déraison: Histoire de la folie à l'âge classique* (1961, his thèse de doctorat) is a history of the conceptual partition between madness and reason in the seventeenth-century "Great Confinement" and the early-nineteenth-century medicalization that closed and re-codified it. *Naissance de la clinique: Une archéologie du regard médical* (1963) does the same work for medicine: a history of the medical *regard* and of how the modern clinic became the site at which an individual body became "a case." *Les mots et les choses: Une archéologie des sciences humaines* (1966) — the surprise bestseller, inseparably coupled with the structuralist moment — generalizes the method into a general archaeology of Western knowledge, with three successive *épistémès* (Renaissance, Classical, Modern) and the famous closing image of "man" as a recent invention, a face drawn in sand at the edge of the sea, soon to be erased. *L'archéologie du savoir* (1969) is the methodological book Foucault wrote both to consolidate and to refit the archaeological project: it loosens the singular *épistémè* of the prior book and substitutes a finer-grained analytic of *énoncés* (statements) and *formations discursives*.

The **genealogical axis** opens with the inaugural lecture *L'ordre du discours* at the Collège de France (December 1970) and culminates in *Surveiller et punir: Naissance de la prison* (1975) and the first volume of *Histoire de la sexualité*, *La volonté de savoir* (1976). Here the analytic of knowledge becomes an analytic of *power-knowledge* (*savoir-pouvoir*). The body is no longer something *represented* in discourse but something *produced* by disciplinary techniques — the timetables of the workshop, the cellular geography of the prison, the examination of the school, the medical-psychiatric examination of the asylum. *Surveiller et punir* opens with the description of Damiens's quartering in 1757 and works, by inversion, to Bentham's Panopticon as the diagram of a new modality of power. *La volonté de savoir* extends the analysis: sexuality is not what is repressed by Victorian power but what is produced, classified, and incited as a domain of knowledge by it. The repressive hypothesis is rejected; *biopouvoir* (the administration of life at the level of the species) is named. The Collège de France courses of 1977-78 (*Sécurité, territoire, population*) and 1978-79 (*Naissance de la biopolitique*) extend the genealogy into *gouvernementalité* and a critique of liberal-political reason.

The **ethical axis** — the late phase — is the surprise of the work, and for the user's project the most consequential moment. Between 1976 and the second and third volumes of *Histoire de la sexualité* (*L'usage des plaisirs* and *Le souci de soi*, both 1984, the latter completed weeks before his death), Foucault re-organizes the entire project. Beginning with the 1980-81 course (*Subjectivité et vérité*), and definitively with the 1981-82 course (*L'herméneutique du sujet*), Foucault turns to ancient *epimeleia heautou* — *souci de soi*, care of the self — and to the techniques by which classical and Hellenistic-Roman subjects constituted themselves as ethical subjects. The fourth volume of the *Histoire de la sexualité*, *Les aveux de la chair*, written before *L'usage des plaisirs* but withheld during his lifetime on Foucault's instructions, was edited by Frédéric Gros and published only in 2018; it treats the early Christian doctrine of the flesh as the hinge between pagan ascesis and the modern *dispositif* of sexuality. With *Les aveux* finally in print, the late project's architecture has come fully into view: a positive ethics of self-formation, recovered through a long history of *pratiques de soi* from classical Greece through Hellenistic Rome through the Christian patristic period.

It must be said at the outset, because the standard Anglophone reception persistently mis-states it, that Foucault explicitly rejected two readings widely attributed to him. **First**, the claim that "Foucault thinks the subject is dead" or "Foucault denies the subject." In the 1982 essay *Le sujet et le pouvoir* and again in the 1984 introduction to *L'usage des plaisirs*, Foucault states plainly that the general theme of his research has not been power but *the subject*, and that the late work on care of the self is the constructive completion of, not a departure from, the earlier critique. The "dethronement of man" in *Les mots et les choses* is the dethronement of a specific historical figure (the empirico-transcendental doublet of the post-1800 *épistémè*), not the abolition of subjectivity. **Second**, the claim that Foucault is a relativist for whom "all truth is power." Foucault rejected this in interview after interview: the genealogical analysis of how a given regime of truth was constituted does not entail that there is no truth; it specifies the conditions under which something *counts* as true within that regime. Foucault is committed to the local, situated truth of his own historical analyzes; the genealogical method is itself a truth-telling practice, a *parrhēsia* on its own terrain. The textbook framings of "Foucault the post-structuralist relativist" and "Foucault the executioner of the subject" are therefore not Foucault's own positions; this brief follows Foucault.

### The central thread

Across the three axes one question runs continuously, and Foucault said so: *what are the historically specific conditions under which something appears as true, as known, as a possible object of inquiry, and as a possible kind of subject?* The archaeological axis answers in terms of the *épistémè* and the rules of formation of statements; the genealogical in terms of power as the immanent condition for the production of subjects and objects of knowledge; the ethical in terms of *pratiques de soi* as the partly free work by which a subject constitutes itself within and against the historical field. The vocabulary changes; the question is one.

Cross-engagement: Theodor W. Adorno works the same finite cognition axis in the political-social register. Here the operative value is `genealogically-produced-illusion`; Theodor W. Adorno lands on that same value in [primary text](cite://adorno/negative-dialectics/2). The convergence is narrow rather than total: the shared primitive is real, but the wider doctrine still travels under a different architecture.

Three thematic vectors organize his thought:

**(a) The dethronement of the universal subject.** The Cartesian *cogito*, the Kantian transcendental subject, and the post-Kantian subject of phenomenology are not, for Foucault, ahistorical givens; they are historically produced figures of a specific *épistémè* (the modern, post-1800), and that figure is dissolving. "Man" is not an eternal object of knowledge; "man" is the strange empirico-transcendental doublet that comes into view when classical representation collapses and a new arrangement opens.

**(b) Power as productive, immanent, capillary.** Power is not first repression and second prohibition; it is, primarily, the productive, dispersed, anonymous network of relations that produces subjects, knowledges, bodies, and pleasures. The juridical-sovereign model (the king, the law, the prohibition, the "no") is the wrong model. Discipline and biopower are the right ones.

**(c) Subjectivity as a constituted field that can be re-worked.** The late Foucault recovers a classical tradition (Stoic, Epicurean, Cynic, early Christian) of *askēsis* — practices on the self, by the self — as a partly free zone of subject-formation. The subject is constituted historically, but the constituted subject can take itself as the object of an ethical work and become otherwise.

### The user's interest

For the user's thesis, Foucault is a productively double figure: largely an ally on the *register-distinction* axis (T1) and the *anti-Cartesian-subject* axis (T11), a polemical opposite where the reception promotes his methodological caution to a totalizing metaphysics. Foucault's *épistémè* — the conditions of possibility for statements within a period, not a claim about the Real — is the closest twentieth-century European analog of the register-thesis. His dismantling of "man" as the empirico-transcendental doublet is congenial to the user's view that the Cartesian, Kantian, and Hegelian subjects are not the deepest fact about subjectivity. Foucault would never take the further metaphysical step (one Real said in many senses); but the register-analytic without the metaphysical step is in him in clean form, and the late-period recovery of *epimeleia heautou* maps with structural precision onto *sādhanā*. The user's polemic engages where the *useful local diagnosis* — schools, prisons, hospitals, sexological discourses do produce the subjects they claim merely to study — is silently raised to a *totalizing metaphysics in which there is nothing but power*. That metaphysics the user rejects, and Foucault himself, by 1982, was already withdrawing from. The detail of agreement and disagreement is laid out in Part VI; what matters at the outset is that this is the relation of a careful reader to a partial fellow-traveler, not of an opponent.

### Glossary

- ***épistémè*** — the historical *a priori* of a culture; the field of possibility within which knowledge can be produced at a given period. Distinguished by Foucault from *Weltanschauung*: an *épistémè* is structural, not phenomenal. *Les mots et les choses* identifies three: Renaissance (similitude), Classical (representation, *mathesis*), Modern (man as empirico-transcendental).
- ***archive*** — in *L'archéologie du savoir*, the system of formation and transformation of statements; not the totality of texts but the law of what can be said.
- ***énoncé*** — the *statement*; the basic unit of analysis in *L'archéologie du savoir*. Not a sentence (*phrase*), not a proposition, but the function by which a sign-set acquires a position in a discursive field.
- ***énonciation*** — the act of stating, distinct from the statement; locus of a subject-position rather than of a person.
- ***dispositif*** — apparatus; a heterogeneous network of discourses, institutions, architectural forms, regulatory decisions, laws, administrative measures, scientific statements, and so forth. The *dispositif* of sexuality, of madness, of security.
- ***biopouvoir / biopolitique*** — the modality of power that takes life itself, at the species level, as its object: birth-rates, mortality, public health, demography, the social body. Distinct from sovereign power (which kills or lets live) and from disciplinary power (which trains the individual body).
- ***disciplinaire*** — the modality of power that produces docile and useful bodies through fine partitioning of space, time, and gesture; the workshop, the prison, the school, the army, the clinic.
- ***panoptisme*** — the diagram of disciplinary power, after Bentham's Panopticon: the architectural arrangement in which surveillance becomes structurally possible without being actual, and the surveilled subject internalizes the surveillance.
- ***savoir-pouvoir*** — power-knowledge; not knowledge serving power, not power producing ideology, but a single field in which the production of knowledge and the exercise of power are co-implicated.
- ***généalogie*** — the historical analytic Foucault adopts from Nietzsche after 1970: a description of how something came to be as it is, with attention to discontinuity, contingency, and the strategic emergence of practices.
- ***archéologie*** — the prior method of *Les mots et les choses* and *L'archéologie du savoir*: a structural analysis of the conditions of possibility of statements within an *épistémè*.
- ***herméneutique du sujet*** — the late project of analyzing how subjects are constituted as subjects of truth — both in the modern Cartesian "*gnōthi seauton*" mode (know thyself, where access to truth is the price of nothing) and in the ancient *epimeleia heautou* mode (care of the self, where access to truth requires a self-transformation).
- ***souci de soi / epimeleia heautou*** — the care of the self; the practice by which a classical or Hellenistic-Roman subject worked on itself in order to become a subject capable of truth.
- ***ascèse / askēsis*** — exercise; the technical practice (of writing, of reading, of meditation, of memorization, of dietary regimen) by which the care of the self is enacted.

Cross-engagement: Bannanje Govindacharya touches the same determination axis, but not in the same register. This section works it as `genealogical-exposure` in the historical-genealogical register; Bannanje Govindacharya approaches it through the logical-dialectical register in [primary text](cite://madhva/tattva-sankhyana/1-4). The point of contact is genuine, but the comparison only holds once the register-shift is kept visible.

## Part I — *Les mots et les choses* (1966)

### The opening scene: Borges and the "table"

Cross-engagement: Friedrich Nietzsche works the same method axis in the historical-genealogical register. Here the operative value is `genealogy`; Friedrich Nietzsche lands on that same value in [primary text](cite://nietzsche/beyond-good-and-evil/22). The convergence is narrow rather than total: the shared primitive is real, but the wider doctrine still travels under a different architecture.

*Les mots et les choses* opens, famously, with Borges. Foucault reports laughter at "a certain Chinese encyclopedia" in which animals are divided into "(a) belonging to the Emperor, (b) embalmed, (c) tame, (d) suckling pigs, (e) sirens, (f) fabulous, (g) stray dogs, (h) included in the present classification, (i) frenzied, (j) innumerable, (k) drawn with a very fine camelhair brush, (l) et cetera, (m) having just broken the water pitcher, (n) that from a long way off look like flies." The laughter, he says, comes from a *malaise*: the limit of our own thought. What the encyclopedia abolishes is not the proximity of things but the very *site* in which they could be juxtaposed.

Cross-engagement: Akshapada Gautama presses the same method axis in the methodological register, but with the value `formal-proof-or-inference` rather than `dialectical-development` in [primary text](cite://aksapada/nyaya-sutra/1.1.1). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

> What is impossible is not the neighboring of things, but the site itself in which they could be neighbors. Where could they be juxtaposed except in the non-place of language?

The thought-figure to retain is *the table*, in two superposed senses Foucault borrows from Roussel: the dissection table on which umbrella meets sewing-machine, and the *tableau* (the schematic table or grid) that lets thought put beings into order, partition them by class, designate their similitudes and differences. *The table* — the pre-discursive, semi-spatial schema beneath all our naming — is what the Borges text annihilates. *Les mots et les choses* will be a history of three successive Western tables, three *épistémès*.

The methodological declaration follows directly:

> What one wishes to bring to light is the epistemological field, the *episteme*, in which knowledges display a history that is not one of increasing perfection, but of their conditions of possibility.

The history will not be a Whiggish history of *progress* (knowledge-improving-toward-truth). It will be a history of conditions of possibility — and Foucault's claim is that this history shows two great discontinuities, one inaugurating the Classical age (mid-seventeenth century), one opening modernity (turn of the nineteenth). Between the *épistémès* there is no smooth transition; there is a structural rearrangement of the field of the sayable.

### The Renaissance épistémè: similitude

The Renaissance — for Foucault, roughly the sixteenth century, with Paracelsus, Crollius, della Porta as canonical figures — operates by *similitude* (*ressemblance*). To know is to find resemblances and trace them across the world. There are four cardinal forms:

Cross-engagement: Judith Butler works the same normativity axis in the political-social register. Here the operative value is `disciplinary-power`; Judith Butler lands on that same value in [primary text](cite://butler/gender-trouble/3). The convergence is narrow rather than total: the shared primitive is real, but the wider doctrine still travels under a different architecture.

**(i) *convenientia*** — convenience, neighborhood-by-juxtaposition: things resemble each other because they are close. The plant resembles the animal because it is rooted to it; the soul resembles the body because it is housed in it. Resemblance is *spatial*.

**(ii) *aemulatio*** — emulation: a similitude that operates without contact, across distance. The face emulates the heavens; the eyes are stars. Resemblance jumps space.

**(iii) *analogie*** — analogy: combines *convenientia* and *aemulatio*; relations of relations. Body : soul :: earth : world; the face is the analogy that lets the human be a *microcosme*.

**(iv) *sympathie*** — sympathy and its twin antipathy: the active force by which things draw together or repel; the gravitational principle of the Renaissance world. Sympathy reduces difference to identity; antipathy preserves singularity. Together they keep the world circulating.

The four figures cohere only because they are *legible*. Resemblance must be *signed*. Hence the doctrine of *signatures*: every similitude leaves a mark — a color, a shape, a number, a pattern — by which it can be read. Knowledge in the Renaissance is hermeneutic in a strong sense: a *deciphering* of the signed surfaces of a world that has been written. This is why, for the Renaissance, *the world is a book*, *Don Quichotte* is the literary key to the close of the *épistémè*: Quichotte is the man who tries to find similitudes when the *épistémè* of similitudes has just collapsed beneath him.

The collapse comes around 1650. Don Quichotte is already a "negative" of the Renaissance; he reads similitudes that are not there. By 1650, what for the Renaissance was *the world's resemblance to itself, signed and decipherable* has become superstition, fable, hallucination. Knowledge is no longer the deciphering of signs but the analysis of representations.

### The Classical épistémè: representation, *mathesis*, *taxinomia*

The Classical age (roughly 1650–1800: Descartes, Port-Royal, Linnaeus, Cuvier-as-yet-not-quite, the *Encyclopédie*) operates by *representation*. The word *représentation* in Foucault is not a casual term: it names the entire operation by which a sign now stands for the thing not by *being similar* to it but by being able to *take its place* in a system of differences. The sign no longer carries the weight of resemblance; it discriminates and orders.

Cross-engagement: G. W. F. Hegel presses the same social formation axis in the political-social register, but with the value `recognitive-institutional` rather than `disciplinary-production-of-subjects` in [primary text](cite://hegel/phenomenology-of-spirit/17). The disagreement is structural, not verbal. Both texts are answering the same pressure-point; they are not locating the pressure in the same way.

Three operations characterize the Classical *épistémè*:

**(i) *Mathesis*** — the project of a universal science of order, modeled on mathematics, in which beings can be brought into comparison along quantitative scales. *Mathesis* is the dream of a calculus of qualities.

**(ii) *Taxinomia*** — the project of an ordered table, in which beings are arranged by visible characters into genera and species; not yet biology (which presupposes life as the inner principle of organization), but *natural history* (which classifies surfaces by visible difference). Linnaeus is the pure case.

**(iii) The general grammar** — language ceases to be the surface of the signed world and becomes the system of signs by which thought marks itself to itself. The Port-Royal grammarians (Arnauld, Lancelot) treat language as a transparent medium of representation; the proposition is the basic unit; the verb *to be* is the link by which thought attaches a predicate to a subject.

These three are not separate sciences; they are three modes of one operation, which is the construction of *the table* — the ordered grid in which signs and things are co-extensive. The *table* of the Classical age is the new "non-place" that succeeds Renaissance similitude. Knowing now means *putting in the table*: laying out a domain into its discriminations, identities, differences.

Foucault's central illustration is *Les Suivantes* (Velázquez's *Las Meninas*, 1656), with which he opens chapter 1. The painting marks representation itself: the painter, the canvas, the mirror, the king and queen present only as reflection. The painting *holds open* the structure of Classical representation precisely because the sovereign subject of representation — the figure for whom the representation is — is not in the painting; he is the empty point in front of the canvas, the void that the structure requires and cannot itself contain. The Classical *épistémè* marks everything *except* the representer.

This is decisive. In the Classical *épistémè* there is no philosophical "man." Representation is sovereign; the human subject is the unmarked condition of the table, never *in* the table. To classify beings is not to classify *oneself*; the classifier is structurally external. This is why, for the Classical age, "anthropology" in the modern sense is impossible.

### The discontinuity around 1800: man enters the order of things

The Classical *épistémè* collapses across the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. Three movements describe the collapse, parallel across three domains:

Cross-engagement: Akalanka touches the same practice-path axis, but not in the same register. This section works it as `critical-genealogical-work` in the ethical register; Akalanka approaches it through the soteriological register in [primary text](cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/1.34-35). The point of contact is genuine, but the comparison only holds once the register-shift is kept visible.

**(i) Natural history → biology.** Cuvier's comparative anatomy (1795 onward) introduces *the organism* as a hierarchical, functional integration whose visible characters depend on an *inner* principle of organization (life). What had been classifiable on the surface of the table now requires reference to a depth that is not on the surface. *Life* enters the field as the unrepresentable condition of biological knowledge.

**(ii) Analysis of wealth → political economy.** Adam Smith and Ricardo introduce *labor* as the source of value. Value can no longer be analyzed in the table of exchanges (Condillac, Turgot); it has a non-representable origin in the productive activity of human beings.

**(iii) General grammar → philology.** Bopp, Grimm, Schlegel introduce *language as a historical organism*. Languages are not transparent media of representation but historically transformed structures with their own laws. *Language* enters the field as the unrepresentable depth of all discourse.

In each case, the visible field of representation is now grounded on something that is no longer on the table — *life, labor, language* — and that something is, in each case, what the human being *is*. The human is alive, the human labors, the human speaks. Hence in each of the three domains there now appears, as the empirico-transcendental point at which the unrepresentable depth meets the visible surface, **"man."**

> As things wind in on themselves, man in turn enters, for the first time, into the field of Western knowledge.

### *Naissance de la clinique*: the medical gaze as bridge

The book that most concretely mediates between the archaeology of *Les mots et les choses* and the disciplinary analytics of *Surveiller et punir* is *Naissance de la clinique* (1963). Foucault's claim there is not merely that doctors began looking more carefully at patients. It is that a new *medical gaze* came into being around the turn of the nineteenth century, one that reorganized space, speech, visibility, and death. The hospital became the site where the sick body could be isolated, compared, recorded, and read as a legible case. Symptoms ceased to be scattered signs awaiting interpretation and became localizable indications within an anatomical field.

That matters for the user's project because it shows Foucault's method working at the level of institutional perception itself. The clinic is not simply a place where knowledge gets applied; it is a place where a new kind of object, the pathological body as case, is constituted. This is the medical analog of the broader claim that "man" enters the modern field as an empirico-transcendental doublet. The body in the clinic is no longer just suffered or narrated; it is systematically rendered visible under rules that define what counts as a lesion, a symptom, an observation, and a valid utterance.

"For the first time" is exact. Before 1800 there was no "man" in this sense — only "human beings" (Renaissance microcosms, Classical subjects of representation), not "man" as the empirico-transcendental doublet who is at once an *object* of knowledge (the body, the laboring agent, the speaking being studied by biology, economics, philology) and the *subject* of knowledge (the one for whom these sciences are produced). This is the structural strangeness of modernity: man is both one being among others and the condition of possibility of the order in which beings appear. The post-Kantian human sciences all live in this doubled condition; they all try to ground the empirical knowledge of man in a transcendental analysis whose subject is also man.

### The closing image: man as a recent invention

The last pages of *Les mots et les choses* contain the most-cited passage Foucault ever wrote. The argument is structural, not nostalgic.

> One thing, at any rate, is certain: man is neither the oldest nor the most constant problem posed to human knowledge. Man is an invention whose recent date the archaeology of our thought easily shows, and perhaps whose end is near. One might wager that he would be erased, like a face drawn in sand at the edge of the sea.

Three things to notice. First, the claim is *historical*, not *normative*: Foucault is not saying "man should disappear," he is saying "the figure of man, as the empirico-transcendental doublet of the post-1800 *épistémè*, is structurally tied to that *épistémè* and will not survive its rearrangement." Second, the rearrangement is already under way: the *human sciences* (psychoanalysis, ethnology, linguistics — the three Foucault names in the closing pages) are dissolving the figure of man from within. Psychoanalysis dissolves the unity of the subject of consciousness; ethnology dissolves the universality of the European model of "man"; structural linguistics dissolves the privilege of the speaking subject. Third — and this is the point at which the user's polemic engages — the *visage de sable* image is genuinely structural-archaeological in Foucault's hands but in the reception it has been taken as a *general metaphysical claim* about the impossibility of the subject. That overreading the user rejects.

### The user's reading

For the user, *Les mots et les choses* is the most important early Foucault, both for what it gets right and for what its reception got wrong.

What it gets right: at the level of the empirical history of European knowledge, the discontinuity-thesis is well-evidenced and important. The figure of "man" *is* a recent invention; the post-1800 human sciences *do* live in the empirico-transcendental doublet; biology, economics, and philology *do* share a deep structural homology that earlier intellectual history had missed. The *épistémè* concept, taken as a register-analytic — i.e., as the claim that statements within a period are formed within a structurally specific field — is a real philosophical achievement, and one which the user's T1 register-thesis can absorb without alteration.

What its reception got wrong, and where the user's polemic engages: the transition from "the figure of man, as a particular historical configuration, is dissolving" to "there is no subject, no enduring center of experience, only the dispersion of discursive positions." This is the move that, on the user's reading, courses through the entire reception of post-1968 French theory and lands as the assertion that the subject is *nothing but* a discursive effect. Foucault himself in 1966 is more cautious; the late Foucault, as we shall see, will explicitly walk parts of this back. But the *visage de sable* has been routinely deployed as a slogan against the very possibility of *psychic being* (caitya puruṣa) in the Aurobindonian sense — a center that is not the Cartesian *cogito*, not the Kantian transcendental, not the Hegelian universal, but is also not a "discursive effect."

The user's response is double. First, the structural archaeology of Western knowledge does not entail metaphysical anti-realism about the subject; the user accepts the local historical thesis without the metaphysical generalization. Second, the very figure Foucault is dissolving — "man" as the empirico-transcendental doublet — is a specifically *Western* figure, with a specifically *Western* trajectory (the Cartesian *cogito*, the Kantian transcendental, the Hegelian Spirit, the post-Kantian sciences of man). The Indic darśanas never installed that figure; *jīva*, *Ātman*, *puruṣa*, *citta*, *caitya puruṣa* are not the empirico-transcendental doublet, and Foucault's archaeology of that doublet leaves them untouched. To wield Foucault's localized critique as a global argument against any candidate for a stable center of experience is to miss what Foucault himself was doing.

There is one further point. The Renaissance / Classical / Modern triad has within it an unspoken continuity Foucault does not thematize: a Western *épistémè* that runs through all three, namely the *épistémè* of *the table* — the assumption that knowledge takes the form of an exhaustive ordered grid in which what *is* shows itself as what *can be classified*. The Renaissance, the Classical, the Modern *épistémès* differ in *how* the table is constituted (similitude / representation / depth-and-finitude), but they share the assumption that something like a table is the right form for knowledge. From the user's vantage — informed by the Indic vocabulary of *adhikāra*, register, standpoint, and the explicit recognition that *vyavahāra* and *paramārtha* speak from different stations — Foucault's three *épistémès* are local refractions of a single deeper *épistémè* of the table, and the Indic traditions never operated under that deeper *épistémè* in the first place. *That* is the local diagnosis the user is willing to credit Foucault with; the global metaphysics he must not be saddled with.

## Part II — *L'archéologie du savoir* (1969)

*L'archéologie du savoir* is the methodological book Foucault wrote in the wake of *Les mots et les choses* in order to do two things at once: to consolidate the archaeological method into a workable analytic, and to retract — or at any rate to refit — some of its more grandiose claims. The book does not narrate any specific *épistémè*; it works at one level of abstraction higher. It is a book about how to do a history of statements (*énoncés*) without falling either into the history-of-ideas style of intellectual history (which presupposes continuous traditions, influences, syntheses) or into a structuralist analysis that would treat discourse as a closed code. The book is the bridge from the early to the middle Foucault.

### The polemic against continuity

The book opens with a sustained attack on the conventional unities of intellectual history: *the book*, *the work* (*l'œuvre*), *the author*, *the tradition*, *the discipline* (in the sense of "physics" or "biology"), *the period*. These, Foucault argues, are not given to us by the texts; they are *imposed* by historical conventions of reading. *The book* presupposes a unity that the actual physical object (covers binding pages of paper) does not necessarily have. *The work* presupposes that the totality of an author's writings forms a coherent expressive whole — but on inspection, what an author's "complete works" comprises is itself a critical-editorial construction. *The author* is a function — the *fonction-auteur* — by which a culture organizes the circulation of certain discourses; not all discourses have it, and even where it operates, it is itself historically variable. *The tradition* presupposes that a continuous influence flows from earlier to later texts, but this continuity is more often retroactive construction than empirical fact.

The methodological imperative is therefore: *suspend* these unities at the outset. Begin not with given continuities but with the dispersed empirical material — actual statements made in actual texts at actual moments. Analyze the regularities, the rules of formation, the relations of co-existence and transformation that hold among them. *Then*, if continuities emerge, they will have been earned, not assumed.

This is not a counsel of pure positivism. Foucault does not propose a textual atomism in which only individual sentences exist and all higher-level unities are illusion. He proposes a substitution: the unities of intellectual history (book, work, author, tradition, discipline, period) are to be replaced by a different and more operational unit — *the discursive formation* (*formation discursive*) — which is defined not by the continuity of a theme or a method but by a system of dispersion that can be mathematically analyzed.

### The *énoncé*

The basic unit of analysis is the *énoncé* — *the statement*. Foucault is at pains to distinguish the *énoncé* from three near-neighbors.

First, the *énoncé* is *not the sentence* (*la phrase*). A sentence is a grammatical-syntactic unit; an *énoncé* may be a sentence, but it may also be a table, a graph, a list of figures, a mathematical formula, a set of map symbols. What matters is not grammatical form but *function*: a sign-set is an *énoncé* when it functions in a discursive field as a possible move within that field.

Second, the *énoncé* is *not the proposition*. A proposition is a logical unit, evaluable for truth-value. Two sentences expressing the same proposition (in different grammatical forms) are logically the same proposition; but they may be very different *énoncés*, made under different conditions, in different fields, with different functions.

Third, the *énoncé* is *not the speech-act* (*l'acte de parole*) of Austin and Searle. A speech-act is performed by a speaker on an occasion; it has illocutionary force; it is a personal act. The *énoncé* is impersonal. It has a *subject-position* (a place that someone may take up in order to make the statement) but it does not have a personal subject. It is not what someone says; it is what *can be said* in that field, by anyone in the appropriate institutional or discursive position.

The *énoncé* is therefore a *function*. It is the function by which a sign-set takes its place in a field; the conditions of its existence are the conditions of *that field's* possibility. To analyze a statement is to analyze: what other statements does it presuppose, exclude, neighbor? what subject-position does it open? what referent does it constitute? what materiality (manuscript, printed page, lecture, archived court record) does it require?

Foucault's example, repeated through the book, is striking: "the species evolves." Considered as a sentence, this is a determinate grammatical object; considered as a proposition, it is true in some logical schemata and false in others. Considered as a *statement*, the same sign-string was an impossibility in 1750 (no concept of species susceptible to evolutionary transformation existed; the field of natural history was a table of fixed characters), a heretical possibility around 1800 (Lamarck, Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire), and a foundational orthodoxy after 1859 — and "the same sentence" in these three moments is *not the same statement*. The statement "the species evolves" in 1859 is constituted by, and constitutes, a different field from the same string in 1750.

### The four axes of the discursive formation

A *discursive formation* is identified by four kinds of regularity, each governing one axis of statement-formation:

**(i) Formation of objects.** What objects does the discourse take? In medical discourse around 1800, "madness" is a new kind of object — not the old *insania* of the Renaissance, not the sequestered *unreason* of the seventeenth century, but a clinical object with internal classification (mania, melancholia, hysteria, dementia), a material substrate (the body, the brain), an institutional locus (the asylum), and a clinical subject-position (the alienist). The object did not pre-exist its discursive formation; it was constituted by the rules that allow it to be made the object of statements.

**(ii) Formation of enunciative modalities.** Who can speak? from where? in what voice? The medical discourse of 1800 specifies: speaker must be a credentialed alienist, speaking from the asylum, addressing other credentialed alienists in the codified genres of the medical text and the case report, taking up the subject-position of the *clinical observer*. Different fields specify different modalities; analyzing a formation means analyzing which modalities it admits.

**(iii) Formation of concepts.** Which concepts can be deployed, in what sequences, with what relations among them? Concepts are not ahistorical philosophical tools; they are field-specific, formed and transformed within the field. The concept "the body" in seventeenth-century anatomy and in early-nineteenth-century clinical medicine is not a *more developed* version of the same concept; it is a structurally different concept, constituted by different rules of formation and entering into different relations with other concepts.

**(iv) Formation of strategies.** What theoretical positions, what choices among possibilities, are open? A discursive formation does not determine a single doctrine; it permits a range of doctrinal possibilities, structured by the field. The analysis of strategies is the analysis of which choices are *available* in the field, not of which choices are *taken*.

The discursive formation is the system of dispersion that organizes statements along these four axes. It is not a *Weltanschauung* (a worldview), not a *theory*, not a *doctrine*. It is the law of formation of a heterogeneous body of statements.

### The *archive*

The most heavily-cited concept of the book — partly because it has been the most heavily over-read — is the *archive*. The archive is *not* the totality of texts produced in a culture, nor the institutions in which texts are stored, nor the body of "what has been said." It is, in Foucault's specific definition, **the system of formation and transformation of statements**.

The archive is what allows certain statements to appear and others not to; it is what enables the subsistence and modification of statements; it is what governs the way in which statements relate to each other. The archive is *not* itself a statement; it is the system that makes the existence of statements possible.

> The archive is first of all the law of what can be said, the system that governs the appearance of statements as singular events.

The archive is the *historical a priori*. It is *a priori* because it is the condition of possibility of statements within its domain; it is *historical* because that condition is itself a contingent product of historical processes, and therefore subject to transformation, displacement, and rupture.

This is the place at which Foucault distances himself from his own *Les mots et les choses*. The latter book had spoken of *the* *épistémè* of an age, in the singular — the Renaissance *épistémè*, the Classical *épistémè*, the Modern *épistémè* — and had given the impression of a single overarching field unifying all knowledge of a period. *L'archéologie du savoir* corrects: there is no *single* archive of an age; there are multiple discursive formations, each with its own rules, intersecting and transforming under their own dynamics. The *épistémè* is not retracted but is now treated as a *complex of relations* among discursive formations, not as a Hegelian-style "spirit of the age."

This is, on the user's reading, a critical correction, and one that brings Foucault closer to the user's register-thesis. There is no single overarching field of formation for "European knowledge in 1800"; there are biological, economic, linguistic, medical, criminological, sexological formations, each governed by its own rules, intersecting partially. Likewise — and this is the user's extension — there is no single overarching field of formation for "Indian knowledge in 800" or "in 1500"; there are Nyāya formations, Mīmāṃsā formations, Vedānta formations (themselves multiple), Tantric formations, each governed by its own rules. The register-thesis (T1) is the claim that these formations *cannot be flattened into a single competing-theories table* without category error. *L'archéologie du savoir* gives a precise European parallel to that claim.

### Transformation, discontinuity, the rules of formation

A long methodological middle of the book is devoted to the analysis of *transformation* — how a discursive formation changes. Foucault is concerned to refuse two equally bad pictures: (i) gradual continuous evolution (the history-of-ideas picture), and (ii) revolutionary total break (a caricature sometimes attributed to him). The actual texture of historical change in discourse, he argues, is more complex. It involves: redistributions among objects, displacements among subject-positions, reconfigurations of conceptual networks, opening or closing of strategic possibilities. Some of these changes happen at one axis without affecting the others; some propagate across all four. Discontinuities are real but local; continuities are real but field-specific.

The methodological upshot is that historians of discourse should describe rather than explain in the causal-genetic sense. The "why" of a transformation is, for Foucault, not first a question about external pressures (economic, political, social) acting on a discourse, but a question internal to the analysis of the field's rules of formation: what redistributions of those rules took place, and how did they propagate? Only after that internal description is in hand does it make sense to ask about the relations between the discursive transformation and other social processes.

This methodological caution will be partly relaxed in the genealogical phase that follows. Beginning with the inaugural lecture *L'ordre du discours* (1970) and definitively with *Surveiller et punir*, Foucault will reconnect the analysis of discourse to the analysis of *power* — and the question "what produced this discursive transformation?" will receive an answer in terms of strategic redistributions of power-relations. But the archaeological tools developed in *L'archéologie du savoir* are not abandoned; they are subsumed within a wider analytic.

### The user's reading of the archaeological method

For the user, *L'archéologie du savoir* is, at the methodological level, perhaps Foucault's most directly useful book — useful precisely *as a method*, available for a register-analytic of the darśanas, without commitment to the specifically Foucauldian metaphysics-by-default that the reception has read into him.

Three points to draw out.

First, the *énoncé* analytic — the insistence on analyzing *what statement is being made, in what field, at what subject-position, with what referent* before evaluating its truth-value — is structurally identical with the user's standpoint-discipline (*adhikāra* + register). To say that "*Brahman is nirguṇa*" and "*Brahman is saguṇa*" are *contradictory* propositions is to make a category error: they are different *statements*, made in different fields (Advaita-paramārtha and Bhakti-vyavahāra), at different subject-positions (the *jñānī* who has reached the roof, the devotee on the stairs), with different referents (the absolute considered as the limit of negation, the absolute considered as immanently engaging the manifest order). They are both true *qua* statements in their own fields; the question of their *propositional* contradiction does not arise once the *énoncé*-analysis is done.

Second, the rejection of the unity-presuppositions of intellectual history (the work, the author, the tradition) is precisely the rejection that the user's polemic against false-genealogical readings of the darśanas requires. Vedānta is not a *single tradition with a continuous theme* (the Hegelianizing reading of "Indian thought from the Upaniṣads to Śaṅkara to Rāmānuja to Madhva"); it is a heterogeneous body of formations, each with its own rules, intersecting selectively. *L'archéologie du savoir* gives the user a precise methodological vocabulary for that heterogeneity.

Third — and this is where the user's polemic re-enters — the archaeological method, taken as method, is *neutral* on the further metaphysical question of whether there is a Real about which the various formations speak. Foucault, by methodological caution, brackets that question; the reception has often promoted the brackets into a denial. The user does not. The user takes the archaeological method as a tool for tracing the field of formation of statements, while holding (T1, T4) that the formations *do* track different registers of one Real. Foucault's method is preserved; his methodological agnosticism is not promoted to a metaphysical thesis.

## Part III — *Surveiller et punir* (1975)

*Surveiller et punir* is the canonical Foucault. It is also the most rhetorically forceful book he wrote: the opening pages — Damiens's quartering on 2 March 1757 — are among the most famous in twentieth-century French prose, and the closing image — the rumble of the battle in the carceral city — is one of the few moments in Foucault that sounds like it might be calling for action. The book's structure is unusual: not a chronological narrative but a sequence of four parts, each treating one *regime* of punishment, in order of approximate emergence — *Supplice* (the body of the condemned, sovereign-spectacular punishment), *Punition* (the eighteenth-century reformers, punishment as semiotic deterrent), *Discipline* (the productive shaping of bodies, the panopticon), *Prison* (the carceral institution and its strategic function).

The book's claim, in one sentence: the modern prison is not a humanitarian advance over the spectacle of public torture but the most visible institution of a much wider modality of power — disciplinary power — which produces individuals as docile-and-useful bodies, and produces the modern soul (the *psyche*, the *subjectivity*, the *personality* of psychological discourse) as the surface on which that power acts.

### The opening: Damiens, 1757

> Damiens had been condemned, on 2 March 1757, to make public amends before the principal door of the Church of Paris, then to be drawn and quartered by four horses, his limbs and body consumed by fire, reduced to ashes, and his ashes scattered in the wind.

The description continues for two further pages, in the sober, technical, quasi-judicial prose of the *procès-verbal*: the horses unaccustomed to pulling, four of them not enough so six were brought; the executioner cutting the tendons and joints with a knife; Damiens crying *Mon Dieu, ayez pitié de moi; Jésus, secourez-moi*. Then, without transition, Foucault produces a second document: the timetable for the inmates of the *Maison des jeunes détenus de Paris*, drafted by Léon Faucher, eighty years later, in 1837. *Article 17.* The roll-call. *Article 18.* Dressing. *Article 19.* The bed-making. *Article 20.* Prayer. *Article 21.* Work. *Article 22.* Lunch. The clock-work of an institutional day.

The juxtaposition is the book's argument in miniature. In less than a century, the punishment of the body (visible, public, spectacular, intermittent, sovereign) has been replaced by the management of conduct (invisible, institutional, continuous, regulatory). The vocabulary of *humanity* will say: the latter is more humane than the former. Foucault's claim is that the latter is *not less harsh* than the former; it is *a different modality* of power, with different objects, different subjects, different effects. The reform is not the softening of punishment but the redistribution of its mechanisms across the social body.

### The body of the condemned: sovereign-spectacular power

Pre-modern punishment, on Foucault's analysis, is essentially *the public re-affirmation of sovereign power on the body of the offender*. A crime is not merely a breach of law; it is an offense against the sovereign whose person *is* the law. The execution must therefore be a counter-offense on the body of the criminal, marked, dramatized, public, in proportion to the affront. The torture (*le supplice*) is not anomalous excess; it is the *meaning* of pre-modern punishment. The body is the visible bearer of the sign of sovereign power, and the spectacular spectacle is the means by which that sign is reaffirmed before the populace.

This is why the popular crowd that gathers at the scaffold is *necessary*: punishment must be witnessed for it to function as the signature of sovereignty. And it is also why the popular crowd is *unstable*: it can identify with the condemned, riot at the executioner, turn the scaffold against itself. The *écrit du condamné* — the speech the criminal often gave from the scaffold, sometimes printed and circulated — could become a counter-discourse of the people. Sovereign-spectacular punishment is theatrical, and like theater, it can be lost.

This is the first of the book's reasons for the eventual replacement of the spectacle: not humanitarian, but political. The crowd is unreliable; the spectacle is unmanageable; sovereign-spectacular power produces, in the very act of its exercise, the conditions of its own undoing.

### The reformers: punishment as semiotic deterrent

Part II treats the eighteenth-century reformers — Beccaria above all, but also the long catalog of pamphleteers (Servan, Le Trosne, Mably, Brissot) — who proposed a thoroughgoing reorganization of penal practice. Their proposal was *not* the prison; that came later. Their proposal was punishment as *signe*: a calculated correspondence between crime and penalty, in which the penalty served as a *legible sign* of the wrong, deterring future offenders by the publicly available image.

> The art of punishing must therefore rest on a whole technology of representation. To find the fitting punishment for a crime is to find the disadvantage whose very idea will make the idea of the offence definitively unattractive.

The reformers' punishment is so still public, but no longer spectacular in the sovereign-affirming sense. It is a *semiotic* punishment: a system of public works, of signs read in the public square — the road-mender for the highwayman, the public laundry-worker for the slanderer — calibrated so that the punishment *marks* the crime in a manner that the spectator can read.

What is striking about the reformers' program is that it was *not the program that won*. By 1810 — within a generation of the first reformist proposals — the dominant penal practice in France, England, the United States was *the prison*. And the prison is not a semiotic sign read by the public; it is a sequestered institution, invisible, productive, and continuous. Foucault's question is: how did this happen? what happened that the reformers' proposal lost out to a program they had not designed and would not have endorsed?

The answer is not located within the reformers' debates at all. It is located in a parallel and earlier development: the slow elaboration of *disciplinary techniques* across multiple non-judicial domains throughout the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

### Discipline: the production of docile-and-useful bodies

Part III is the book's analytic center. Foucault's claim is that, throughout the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, in the army, the convent, the workshop, the school, the hospital — in every institution that needed to produce coordinated, productive, predictable activity from large numbers of human bodies — a new technology of power was being elaborated. This technology has three traits:

**(i) It works on the body, not on the soul.** Discipline trains gestures, postures, movements; it shapes the body's capacities by repetition, decomposition, recomposition. The soldier is *made*: from the formless paste of the conscript, the well-drilled musketeer is produced. The schoolboy is made: from the inattentive child, the disciplined student. The labourer, the patient, the inmate.

**(ii) It is continuous, not intermittent.** Sovereign-spectacular power flares occasionally (the execution, the festival of submission); disciplinary power is woven into every minute of the day. The timetable is its emblem. *Article 21*: from 5:30 to 6:30, work; from 6:30 to 7:00, prayer; etc.

**(iii) It produces, it does not (only) repress.** The crucial and counterintuitive claim: discipline does not principally *prohibit*. It *fabricates*. It produces the docile soldier, the trained student, the productive labourer, the cured patient, the rehabilitated convict. It is *positive* in the technical sense: it manufactures certain capacities, dispositions, and forms of subjectivity.

Discipline operates by four basic procedures:

**(a) Distribution in space.** The cellular partitioning of bodies. Each body has its place; each place has its body. The dormitory cell, the school desk, the work-station, the bed in the ward. Space is rendered analytic: divided so that movements can be observed, traced, and corrected.

**(b) Control of activity.** The timetable inherited from the monastic offices, adapted to the workshop. Activities are decomposed into elementary gestures, each gesture timed, each gesture in fixed sequence with the next. Foucault's example is the prescription of musket-loading in eighteenth-century military manuals: the operation is broken into fifteen elementary movements, each separately rehearsed, each timed.

**(c) Organization of geneses.** Time is itself partitioned: into stages of training, into a developmental sequence in which capacities are progressively built up. The military exercise leads through phases; the school curriculum proceeds through grades; the hospital regime through stages of recovery. The body's becoming is rendered analytic.

**(d) Composition of forces.** Individual bodies are combined into ranks, files, units, classes, wards. The single body's force is multiplied by the geometry of its combination. The army is not the sum of soldiers but the disciplined arrangement of them.

Across these four operations, three further mechanisms are deployed:

**(1) Hierarchical observation.** The disciplinary architecture is built so that supervision is structurally possible at every moment. The school's vitrified partitions, the barracks' dormitories with NCOs at intervals, the hospital ward with the supervisor's elevated table. The architecture *is* a power.

**(2) Normalizing judgment.** Every micro-deviation from the prescribed gesture, posture, or schedule is recorded and corrected. *Punishment* in the disciplinary regime is not the spectacular reprisal but the constant minor correction: the demerit, the extra exercise, the reproof. The norm — the prescribed correct comportment — is the reference against which judgment operates.

**(3) The examination.** The combinatory of the two: at fixed intervals, the subject is examined, the deviations from the norm are recorded, the case file accumulates. The examination is what *fixes* an individual as a "case" — a case in the sense of a clinical case, an educational case, a penological case. Through the examination, each subject is made knowable in his individuality, locatable in a hierarchy, intervenable through the exercise of corrective power.

> The examination, as the at once ritual and "scientific" fixation of individual differences, as the pinning of each to his own singularity, indicates the emergence of a new modality of power in which each receives individuality itself as a status.

This is the inversion that Foucault thinks is the most important political fact of the modern period: in older regimes, *individuality* was a privilege of the powerful — kings, lords, saints, persons named in genealogies and immortalized in monuments; the masses were undifferentiated. In the disciplinary regime, *individuality* is a procedure applied *downward*: to the schoolchild, to the worker, to the patient, to the prisoner. Each is *made* an individual, in the precise sense of being differentially measured, examined, recorded, classified. The "individual" of modern liberalism, the "individual" of the human sciences, the "individual" who is the subject of rights — this individual is the disciplinary product, not the disciplinary presupposition.

### The Panopticon

The last chapter of Part III treats Bentham's *Panopticon*, which Foucault elevates from a peripheral architectural curiosity to *the diagram of disciplinary power as such*. The principle is well-known:

> At the periphery, a ring-shaped building; at the center, a tower. It is enough to place a supervisor in the central tower and in each cell to confine a madman, a patient, a convict, a worker, or a schoolboy.

The architecture's principle is that the cell is illuminated by an outer window so that the supervisor in the tower can see every gesture inside it, while the inmate, by inverse arrangement, sees only the tower, not whether it is occupied.

The architecture has three structural features that make it the diagram of disciplinary power.

First, it *inverts the dungeon*. The dungeon shuts in, deprives of light, hides. The Panopticon shuts in, *floods with light*, exposes. The cell is illuminated by the outer window so that the supervisor in the tower can see every gesture inside it.

Second, it *dissociates the seeing-being-seen pair*. The supervisor in the tower sees but is not seen; the inmate in the cell is seen but does not see. The asymmetry is what produces the disciplinary effect.

Third — and most crucially — it *automates the functioning of power*. Whether or not a supervisor is actually in the tower, the inmate cannot know. The inmate must therefore behave as if surveillance were continuous, even when it is not. The inmate *internalizes the gaze*. Power is exercised not by an actual watcher but by the *structural possibility* of being watched. Power becomes anonymous and continuous; it is in the architecture, not in the person.

> By its principle of asymmetry, the Panopticon guarantees the dissociation of the pair seeing-being-seen: in the peripheral ring one is totally seen without ever seeing; in the central tower one sees everything without ever being seen.

The Panopticon is therefore not (or not only) a building. It is a *diagram*: a generalizable form of organization that can be installed wherever bodies must be made productive — not only the prison, but also the workshop, the school, the asylum, the hospital, the army camp, the colonial settlement. Bentham's term *panopticism* names not a building but a *generalized modality* of power.

### The "soul" of the modern individual

Part IV treats the prison itself: how the disciplinary techniques developed across non-penal institutions colonized the penal system; how the prison, despite its manifest failure to *correct* the criminal (its recidivism rate has been catastrophic since the early nineteenth century), functions strategically to *produce* delinquency as a controllable, marked, knowable population; how delinquency in turn becomes a useful instrument in the broader disciplinary management of the working classes.

The most-cited passage of the book — and the most consequential for the user's reading — comes earlier, near the close of Part I:

> The soul is the effect and instrument of a political anatomy; the soul is the prison of the body. The man we are told about and invited to liberate is already in himself the effect of a subjection far deeper than himself. A "soul" inhabits him and brings him into existence, and that soul is itself one piece in the power exercised over the body.

This is Foucault's most concentrated formulation of the productive-power thesis. The soul — the modern *psyche*, the subjectivity, the personality, the inner depth of the human individual — is *produced* by the operations of disciplinary power. Not "fabricated as illusion" (Foucault explicitly disavows this reading); the soul is *real*, in the sense that it has effects, that it is the site of psychological and educational and clinical knowledge, that it is what the human sciences study. But it is not a substance that pre-exists power; it is the surface, produced by power, on which power continues to act.

This is the inversion of the Christian-Cartesian-Kantian-humanist picture of the human being. In that picture, the soul (or the *cogito*, or the transcendental subject, or the rational personality) is the *given*, and the operations of training, education, discipline, punishment are *imposed upon* it from outside. In Foucault's picture, the operations come first, and the soul is what the operations *produce*. The "real man" we are invited to liberate from oppressive institutions is *himself* the product of those institutions; "liberation" — at least in its psycho-humanist sense — is a deeper subjugation, because it presupposes the very figure of the inner self that the disciplinary regime has fabricated.

### The user's reading

For the user, *Surveiller et punir* is the place where Foucault has to be read most carefully — because it is the place where the empirically rigorous local diagnosis is most easily over-extended into a totalizing metaphysics, and the user accepts the diagnosis but rejects the metaphysics.

What the user accepts. At the level of the empirical history of modern European institutions — schools, prisons, hospitals, factories, armies — Foucault's analysis is largely correct, and his vocabulary (disciplinary power, normalization, the examination, panoptic surveillance) is now indispensable for thinking about the actual functioning of these institutions. The user accepts the productive-power thesis at this level; the critique aligns with the user's politics (the matsya-nyāya analytic of the predatory state, the Vivekanandian morphology of predation, the Bhattacharyya analytic of categorial capture). The "soul" of the modern individual *is*, to a very substantial extent, a product of bio-disciplinary management; the empirical *jīva* under conditions of modernity *is* shaped by the techniques Foucault describes.

What the user rejects. The further claim — silent in Foucault, loud in the reception — that there is *nothing more* than this, that the soul-as-product-of-discipline is the *only* candidate for what subjectivity is. This is the move from local diagnosis to totalizing metaphysics, and it is exactly the move the user's polemic against postmodern theory targets. Subjectivity is not exhausted by the disciplinarily-produced empirical subject. There is a *psychic being* (caitya puruṣa) — a center that is not the empirical *jīva*, not a product of biopolitical management, not a discursive position. This center is *not* given to the immediate self-reflection of the *cogito*; it is reached only by *sādhanā*, and the *sādhanā* itself transforms the empirical subject through which the access is achieved. So Aurobindonian psychic-being doctrine *also* says that the immediately-given subject is not the deepest fact about the self; *Surveiller et punir* and Aurobindo agree on the dethronement of the Cartesian-Kantian universal subject. They diverge on what comes next: for Foucault (or at least for the Foucault of 1975), what comes next is power-relations all the way down; for Aurobindo, what comes next is the discovery, through inward turning, of a center that is neither the empirical *jīva* nor the universal subject but the singular individual divine.

A second point. The Panopticon analysis is most directly aimed at the *ascending* gaze of the supervisor over the surveilled inmate — power as that which *sees without being seen*. There is a striking inversion in the mystical traditions: in the *darshan* of the icon, the *seeing* is reversed — it is the deity who sees the worshipper, and the worshipper offers herself to that gaze. This is *not* the panoptic structure (the deity is *seen* by the worshipper as well; the asymmetry is in the *quality* of the gaze, not its directionality). The user marks this as one of the places where Foucault's analytic, taken globally, fails to think the structure of devotional looking, because the only structure of asymmetric vision he can imagine is disciplinary. In the registers of *darshan* and *bhakti* (T6, T9, T10), there is an asymmetry of gaze that is not power-over but the very condition of *līlā*; this is a phenomenon for which Foucault's middle-period analytic has no available analysis.

A third point. The book's final image — *le grondement de la bataille*, the rumble of the battle in the carceral city — sounds like a call to resistance, but it is striking that *Surveiller et punir* itself never specifies the standpoint from which resistance could be conducted. If the soul is power's product, what subject of resistance remains? The user's reading: this is the structural lacuna of the middle Foucault, and it is precisely what the late Foucault (the 1981–82 lectures, the second and third volumes of the *Histoire de la sexualité*) will return to address — by recovering, however partially, an ascetic-subject standpoint from which the power-network can be worked on rather than only undergone.

## Part IV — *Histoire de la sexualité* (1976; 1984; 1984; 2018)

The *Histoire de la sexualité* is the project on which Foucault was working at his death and which he never completed in the form he had originally announced. Its first volume, *La volonté de savoir* (1976), proposed a six-volume sequence that was abandoned. The second and third volumes, *L'usage des plaisirs* and *Le souci de soi*, appeared in June 1984, weeks before his death; both are studies of classical and Hellenistic-Roman ethics, very different in tone and method from the first volume. The fourth, *Les aveux de la chair*, on early Christian patristic doctrine of the flesh, was complete at his death but, on his instructions, not published until 2018. The result is a project that in its actual existing form does *not* describe the trajectory the first volume's prospectus sketched; the late Foucault rewrote his program in the very act of executing it.

For the user's purposes, two arguments matter. First, the argument of Volume 1 against "the repressive hypothesis" and for biopower. Second, the argument of Volumes 2 and 3 (and the lecture courses contemporaneous with them) for the *care of the self* as a constitutive practice of subjectivity.

### Volume 1: against the repressive hypothesis

The conventional reading of the historical relation between sex and modern power runs as follows: pre-modern Europe was relatively tolerant of sexual variety; Victorian-bourgeois society imposed a thoroughgoing repression; the twentieth century has gradually liberated us from that repression. Sexuality is the natural truth of the human; power, in this story, *suppresses* sexuality; liberation consists in *speaking about* sex against the repressive prohibition.

Foucault calls this story the *repressive hypothesis* and rejects it. Not because there are no prohibitions, censorships, denials — there are; but because they are local mechanisms within a wider field that the repressive hypothesis fundamentally mis-describes. The wider field is *not* one of suppression but of *incitement to discourse*: a sustained, multi-institutional, multi-disciplinary push, beginning in the seventeenth-century Counter-Reformation pastoral and extending through eighteenth-century medicine, nineteenth-century psychiatry, late-nineteenth-century sexology, twentieth-century psychoanalysis, to *put sex into discourse* — to extract from each individual subject a confession, a self-description, a clinical case-narrative.

> My object is to define the regime of power-knowledge-pleasure that sustains, among us, discourse on human sexuality. It is, in short, a matter of the great discursive fact, the putting of sex into discourse.

The Counter-Reformation pastoral demanded an ever-finer, ever-more-detailed confession of sexual thoughts, desires, and acts; this is not repression but *production* of a discursive economy of sex. Eighteenth-century medicine took up the same terrain in a different register, treating sexual practice as a domain of health, hygiene, and population management. Nineteenth-century psychiatry produced the catalog of perversions — the homosexual, the fetishist, the masochist, the exhibitionist, each as a *type* of person rather than (as the older juridical apparatus had treated them) someone who had performed a particular act. Sexology took these typologies and elaborated them into a science. Psychoanalysis claimed to liberate sex from the prohibition by extracting and interpreting the deepest confession.

In each case, the operation is the same: not *suppress* sex, but *produce* sex as a domain of knowledge and a vector of power. *The deployment of sexuality* (*le dispositif de sexualité*) is the heterogeneous network — discursive, institutional, medical, juridical, pedagogical — by which "sex" is constituted as the truth of the modern individual.

Three consequences follow.

**(i) Sexuality is not the natural that power suppresses; sexuality is what power produces.** "Sex" — in the sense of an internal truth of the self that one *has* and that *defines* one — is not a biological given that institutions either acknowledge or deny. It is the historical product of a specific *dispositif*. Where the seventeenth century saw certain *acts* (the act of sodomy, the act of adultery), the nineteenth century sees a *type of person* (the homosexual, the adulterer); the type is the product of the deployment.

**(ii) The "liberation of sex" is not the escape from power but the next move within it.** When the twentieth-century critic urges us to speak about sex, to confess sex, to liberate sex from repression, she is not breaking the deployment; she is operating it. The discursive incitement is what the deployment of sexuality has *always* required. The critic of repression is the deployment's most efficient agent.

**(iii) Power is not principally prohibition.** Here Foucault generalizes from the particular case of sexuality to a general thesis about power. The juridical-sovereign model of power — power as the source of the *no*, of the law, of the prohibition — is a misreading of how power actually works in modern societies. Power is *positive*, *productive*, *capillary*, *immanent*. It produces objects (the homosexual, the masturbating child, the hysterical woman), subjects (the confessing penitent, the patient, the analysand), and pleasures (a whole new economy of pleasures elicited by the operations of power on bodies). It is not the censor; it is the network of relations that produces the censored *and* the censorship as positive effects.

### Biopower and biopolitics

Volume 1 closes with the introduction of *biopower* — the modality of power that takes "life itself" as its object. The argument: from the eighteenth century onward, alongside the older sovereign power (the right to take life or let live) and the disciplinary power of *Surveiller et punir* (the training of individual bodies), there emerges a third modality. This new modality operates not on individual bodies but on the *species body* — on the population as a biological aggregate. Its objects are: birth-rates, mortality-rates, fertility, public health, longevity, the geography of disease, the management of food supply, the control of urban populations, the regulation of working conditions.

Sexuality is the privileged hinge between disciplinary power and biopower. As discipline, sexual practice is trained in the individual body (the masturbating child, the converted homosexual, the hysteric); as biopolitics, sexual practice is regulated at the population level (birth-rates, eugenics, hygiene, family planning). The deployment of sexuality is the operative connector between the two regimes.

The political consequences of biopower are sweeping. Modern racism, in Foucault's analysis (developed more fully in the 1976 lecture course *Il faut défendre la société*), is the form taken by the sovereign right to kill *within* a regime of biopower: to kill, in the name of the life of the population, those whose existence is held to threaten that life. Modern wars of extermination, in this analysis, are not atavisms; they are biopolitical phenomena. The argument is at its most consequential in the analysis of fascism and totalitarianism, where biopolitics and sovereignty are explicitly fused.

### Governmentality and liberal reason

The 1977-78 and 1978-79 Collège de France courses, *Security, Territory, Population* and *The Birth of Biopolitics*, are essential here even though they sit outside the published volumes. They introduce *governmentality*: not rule by law alone and not discipline alone, but the complex art of governing populations through security, circulation, probability, and managed freedom. Liberalism on this view is not simply the ideology of less government. It is a specific governmental reason that governs more effectively by letting certain processes run, measuring them, modulating them, and intervening only at strategic thresholds.

This matters for the user's thesis because it expands Foucault beyond prisons and sexuality into the ordinary management of modern life. Markets, public health, policing, welfare, urban circulation, and administrative expertise all become intelligible as techniques that work through apparently free subjects rather than against them. The result is a more comprehensive map of modern power: sovereignty, discipline, biopolitics, and governmentality are distinct but overlapping modes, and any reading of Foucault that stops at the prison misses how fully he came to understand liberal modernity as a regime that produces freedom in order to administer it.

### Volumes 2–3: the late turn

If Volume 1 had announced a six-volume sequence on the deployment of sexuality from the Counter-Reformation onward, Volumes 2 and 3 instead jumped backward two millennia, to the classical Greek and Hellenistic-Roman ethical traditions, and approached *aphrodisia* (the Greek term for sexual practice) from a fundamentally different angle. The shift is methodological: the analytic of power recedes; the analytic of *pratiques de soi* comes forward. The question is no longer "how does power produce subjects?" but "how does a subject constitute itself as an ethical subject in relation to a given moral code?"

Foucault distinguishes four dimensions of any ethics:

**(a) The *substance éthique*** — the part of the self that is the material of ethical work (for Christianity: the flesh, the desire; for classical Greece: *aphrodisia* as activity, not as desire).

**(b) The *mode d'assujettissement*** — the mode of subjection: how is one called to recognize one's moral obligations? (for Christianity: divine law; for classical Greece: a *kalon* aesthetic of the beautiful life).

**(c) The *travail éthique*** — the ethical work: the techniques and practices by which one transforms oneself (askēsis: self-examination, dietetic regimen, *epistolē*-writing, philosophical exercises).

**(d) The *téléologie*** — the telos: what kind of subject does one wish to become? (for Christianity: the saved soul, freed from the flesh; for classical Greece: the master of one's pleasures, the free man, the ethical subject of one's life as a beautiful work).

The classical Greek ethical regime of *aphrodisia* is not a regime of prohibition (it is largely indifferent to *which* objects of desire are pursued, within bounds) but a regime of *self-mastery*. The free male citizen is enjoined to be the master of his pleasures, not because the pleasures are intrinsically wrong, but because the inability to master them is a sign of an unfree self, incapable of governance — first of himself, secondarily of others (his household, his city). *Sōphrosunē* — temperance, self-mastery — is the ethical telos.

The Hellenistic-Roman regime (*Le souci de soi*) intensifies the work on the self while shifting its register. The Stoic, Epicurean, and Cynic schools elaborate techniques of self-examination, of attention to the present, of regulation of desire, of preparation for death — and these techniques are addressed not to a small political elite but to a wider class of free men, in a world in which political agency has contracted (the end of the Greek polis, the rise of the Roman administrative empire). The care of the self becomes more intensive, more universalist, more solitary. The marriage relation becomes the primary site of regulated *aphrodisia*, with new emphases on conjugal fidelity that anticipate (without yet being) the Christian sexual ethic.

The Christian regime — treated only summarily in *Le souci de soi*, but developed at length in the long-suppressed *Les aveux de la chair* (written before *L'usage des plaisirs*, withheld during Foucault's lifetime, edited by Frédéric Gros and published only in 2018) — completes the inward turn. *Les aveux* is the structural hinge: it shows how the patristic codification of the *flesh* (*la chair*) — the Augustinian rendering of *concupiscentia* as the indelible mark of fallen interiority — is the relay station between the pagan ethics of *aphrodisia* (where the substance was the act) and the modern *dispositif* of sexuality (where the substance is the desire-as-truth-of-the-self). In the Christian regime the substance shifts from *act* to *desire*: the inner movements of concupiscence become the primary terrain of moral attention. The *mode d'assujettissement* becomes obedience to divine law as transmitted through the institutional church. The *travail éthique* becomes the confession, the spiritual direction, the examen of conscience. The *téléologie* becomes purity from the flesh in this life and salvation in the next. The entire infrastructure of the Christian sexual subject — confessing, self-suspicious, vigilant of the inner movements of desire — is in place by the patristic period; what the early modern period adds is the institutional intensification of the practice and its eventual relay through medical, psychiatric, and pedagogical institutions. The fourth volume so completes the architecture of the project: Volume 1 names the modern *dispositif*, Volumes 2–3 recover the pagan ascetic alternative, and Volume 4 traces the Christian-flesh hinge by which the second became the first.

### The user's reading

For the user, the *Histoire de la sexualité* is the most strategically interesting Foucault, because it is here that two distinct trajectories cross: the genealogical critique of how modern subjectivity is produced (the Volume 1 argument) and the recovery of an ancient practice of self-formation that was *not* a function of the modern *dispositif* (the Volumes 2–3 argument).

What the user accepts from Volume 1. The historical thesis that "sexuality" as we now have it — sex as the inner truth of the self, the privileged site of personal authenticity, the domain of confession and therapeutic knowledge — is a specific historical product of a specific *dispositif*; this is correct, and the user accepts it. The user accepts also the critique of the repressive hypothesis: the picture of liberation-from-repression is the deployment's own self-understanding, not its critique. And the user accepts, with some qualifications, the analytic of biopower as a description of how the modern state operates on the population.

What the user rejects, again, is the totalizing extension. Sexuality may be a historical construct; *erotic experience* — including, in Indic terms, the entire register of *kāma* and its sublimation into the higher *rasas*, including *bhakti-rasa* and the path of *viraha-bhakti* (T10) — is not exhausted by the modern Western *dispositif* of sexuality. The Tantric traditions, in particular, treat sexual energy as a mode of the divine *śakti* and have an analytic of erotic experience that is *neither* the Christian-confessional regime *nor* the medico-psychiatric *dispositif* Foucault analyzes. Foucault's thesis is local; promoted to a totalization, it would erase the Indic regimes from the field of possible erotic experience.

What the user accepts, decisively, from Volumes 2–3. The recovery of the *care of the self* (*epimeleia heautou*) as a *practice of freedom* — as a domain in which the constituted subject can take itself as the object of ethical work and become otherwise — opens, within Foucault's own framework, a margin of free practice that the middle period had seemed to close. *Sādhanā*, in the Indic vocabulary, is precisely this: a disciplined work on the empirical self by which the empirical self is transformed and, in the limit, the *psychic being* (caitya puruṣa) emerges into operative relation with the surface personality. Foucault, working entirely from European sources, recovers an outline of this kind of practice — Stoic, Epicurean, Cynic, eventually Christian — and recognizes its philosophical seriousness in a way that the middle Foucault had bracketed. The Foucault of 1984 is much closer to the Foucault the user can read with sympathy than the Foucault of 1975.

A specific point on the four-dimensional analytic of ethics. The four dimensions (substance, mode, work, telos) translate well into the Indic register-analytic. Different darśanas constitute the *substance éthique* differently: for Yoga, the *cittavṛtti*; for Vedānta, the *avidyā*; for Tantra, the *śakti*; for Bhakti, the *prema-bhāva*. The *mode d'assujettissement* is also field-specific: for Mīmāṃsā, the *vidhi* (Vedic injunction); for Vedānta, the *śruti*-authorized inquiry; for Bhakti, the personal call of the *iṣṭa-devatā*. The *travail* — *sādhanā* — varies accordingly. The *telos* — *mokṣa*, *sāyujya*, *kaivalya*, *bhakti-paramā*, *supramental transformation* — depends on the field. Foucault's analytic, *taken as method*, is well suited to a register-sensitive analysis of these regimes; what he has demonstrated for European ethical history can in principle be done, with the appropriate cultural-historical sensitivity, for the Indic ethical traditions, and the result will not be a single universal "ethics of the subject" but a plurality of regimes of self-constitution within a plurality of fields.

## Part V — *L'herméneutique du sujet* (1981–82): Foucault's positive ethics

The late lecture course at the Collège de France, taken with the second and third volumes of the *Histoire de la sexualité* and the posthumous *Les aveux de la chair*, is not a coda to the genealogical project. It is, on Foucault's own account in the introduction to *L'usage des plaisirs*, *the project the genealogical work was leading to* — a positive ethics of the subject's self-formation, traced through the long European archive (Plato, Stoics, Epicureans, Cynics, early Christians) from which European philosophy after Descartes had cut itself off. To read Foucault as ending with *Surveiller et punir* and *La volonté de savoir* is to take the middle term of his work for the whole. The late period is *constructive*, not destructive; the question is no longer "how is the subject produced by power?" but "how does a subject constitute itself in relation to truth, through a free practice of work on itself?"

The 1981–82 lecture course *L'herméneutique du sujet* (transcribed and published in French in 2001) is the densest and most philosophically explicit late Foucault. It is, on the user's reading, the place where Foucault comes closest to articulating a position with which the user can be in deep agreement — and the place where the limits of his own framework, against the Indic resources, become most visible.

The course is built around a single textual-historical thesis that Foucault states in the first lecture and develops over the following twenty-three sessions. The thesis: in the Greek philosophical tradition from Socrates through to the Hellenistic-Roman period (roughly 5th century BCE through 3rd century CE), there was a constitutive coupling between two principles — *gnōthi seauton* (know thyself) and *epimeleia heautou* (care of the self) — and the second was the *broader, prior, and more fundamental* term. *Gnōthi seauton* was a specific *moment* within the more general practice of *epimeleia heautou*. The Cartesian moment — Descartes's *Meditations* of 1641 — uncoupled these. *Gnōthi seauton* was elevated to the founding act of philosophy, while *epimeleia heautou* was demoted to "spirituality," excluded from the domain of legitimate cognition, and eventually relegated to therapy or religion. The "Cartesian moment" is, on Foucault's diagnosis, the moment at which European philosophy lost something philosophically essential — namely, the recognition that *access to truth requires a transformation of the subject*.

### The "Cartesian moment"

What Foucault calls the *moment cartésien* is not a chronological event located in 1641 alone. It is a structural reorganization of the relation between subject and truth that takes shape across the early modern period and that crystallizes in Descartes. The reorganization can be stated as follows.

In the ancient regime — for Plato, the Stoics, the Epicureans, the Cynics, even (with significant transformations) for the early Christian Fathers — the subject did not have automatic access to truth. To become capable of truth, the subject had to undergo a *transformation*: a *conversion* (in the broad sense, not the narrow Christian sense), an *askēsis*, a re-formation of the self. Truth was not the *destination* of an unchanged subject but the *prize* of a changed one. The price of access to truth was the work of the self on the self.

In the Cartesian regime, this coupling is broken. Truth becomes accessible *as such* to any subject who exercises the methodical discipline of clear and distinct ideas. The subject does not have to *become other* to be capable of truth; the subject has only to *think correctly*. *Gnōthi seauton* is preserved — Descartes's *cogito* is the foundational self-recognition — but it is severed from *epimeleia heautou*, the practice of self-formation. Knowledge becomes possible without ascesis; truth becomes a property of method, not of the self that wields the method.

The consequence is sweeping. The entire architecture of ancient self-cultivation — the Stoic exercises (Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations*, Epictetus's *Discourses*), the Epicurean *philia*-based practice, the Cynic *parrhēsia*, the Platonic *anamnēsis* — is split off from "philosophy proper" and relegated to a residual category that nineteenth-century commentators will call "spirituality" or "personal ethics." A Marcus Aurelius is no longer counted as a philosopher in the same sense as a Descartes or a Kant. The Hellenistic schools, which had been *primarily* philosophical schools of life, are read by post-Cartesian historians as *only* schools of doctrine, with their practical-ascetic component thrown away as historical ornament.

Foucault's claim is that this is a historically specific impoverishment, not a *philosophical advance*. The ancient coupling of truth and self-transformation was not a confusion that Descartes correctly clarified; it was a recognition that the Cartesian moment lost. And the loss is consequential: the modern subject, conceived as the unchanging epistemic operator who applies methods to objects, is incapable of the kind of access to truth that the ancient practices made possible — because it is incapable of *becoming other than it is*, and therefore incapable of the kind of truth that requires becoming other.

### *Parrhēsia* and the practice

The lectures spend extensive time on *parrhēsia* — frank speech, courageous truth-telling — as a practice of the care of the self. The *parrhēsiast* is the one who speaks the truth at risk to himself, in conditions where speaking the truth might be dangerous. The figure ranges from the Athenian democratic citizen who speaks freely in the assembly, through the philosophical advisor (Plato to Dionysius of Syracuse), to the Cynic on the street challenging conventional opinion (Diogenes), to the Christian martyr.

What is philosophically important about *parrhēsia* is that it integrates a practice of self-formation with a practice of truth-telling. To speak frankly requires that one *be* a certain kind of subject — that one have done the work on oneself to be capable of saying what one means under conditions of risk. The truth-telling and the self-formation are not separate activities; they are aspects of one practice.

Likewise the practices of *hupomnēmata* (notebooks), of *meletē* (premeditation, mental rehearsal), of *anachōrēsis eis heauton* (withdrawal into oneself), of the *examen de conscience* — these are not exercises performed *in addition to* philosophical thought; they are constitutive of being the kind of subject for whom philosophical thought is possible.

### The user's reading

For the user, *L'herméneutique du sujet* is by some distance the most directly resonant Foucault. Three points.

First, the central thesis — that access to truth requires a transformation of the subject — is, in the Indic tradition, simply *uncontroversial*. The entire tradition operates on this principle. *Brahmavidyā* is not accessible to the unprepared; the *adhikārī* — the qualified candidate — must have undergone the four-fold preparation (*sādhana-catuṣṭaya*: *viveka*, *vairāgya*, *śamādi-ṣaṭka-saṃpatti*, *mumukṣutva*); and even granted the preparation, *brahma-jijñāsā* is not the same operation as ordinary cognitive inquiry. Likewise, *bhakti* requires the cultivation of the *bhāva*; *tantric* attainments require the *dīkṣā* and the practice; *yogic* perceptions require the *aṣṭāṅga* progression. There is no *darśana* in which truth is the indifferent prize of correct method applied by an unchanged subject. The Cartesian *moment* the late Foucault diagnoses is, from the Indic vantage, a peculiarly Western detour, and the recovery of *epimeleia heautou* — partial as Foucault makes it, embedded as it is in his genealogical apparatus — is the West's slow, halting return to a position that the Indic traditions have held continuously.

Second, the structural homology between *epimeleia heautou* and *sādhanā* is precise. *Sādhanā* is the *travail éthique* in Foucault's sense; the *substance* on which it works is the *citta* (in the Yogic register), the *avidyā* (in the Vedāntic), the *aham-bhāva* (in the Bhakti); the *mode d'assujettissement* is the *guru-paramparā* and the *śāstra*; the *telos* varies by darśana. This is not just an analogical correspondence; it is the same structural form. Where Foucault recovers — in Marcus Aurelius, in Epictetus, in Seneca — practices that the post-Cartesian European tradition had forgotten, the Indic traditions had preserved analogous practices unbroken from the *Brahmasūtra* and *Yogasūtra* through Śaṅkara, Rāmānuja, Madhva, Caitanya, the Tantras, to Aurobindo. The user's *integral yoga* is the late inheritance of an unbroken transmission of *epimeleia heautou*-equivalents that the West had to laboriously rediscover.

Third, the *limit* of Foucault's late position. Even in 1982, Foucault remains methodologically agnostic about the *destination* of *epimeleia heautou*. He asks: what subject does the practice produce? He does not (cannot, on his methodological principles) ask: is the subject produced by the practice in *contact* with anything beyond the field of the practice itself? Is the *parrhēsiast*'s truth a feature of his transformed self, or is it a feature of *the truth*? Is the Stoic's *prosoche* (attention) trained on the inner field of representations, or on something accessible *through* that field? Foucault — at least in the formal lectures, and within his methodological self-restraint — does not say. The Indic traditions are not under that restraint; they say. The *psychic being* (caitya puruṣa) is *not* a product of the practice; it is what the practice *uncovers*. The *ātman* is not constituted by *sādhanā*; it is realized through *sādhanā*. The *supramental*, in Aurobindonian terms, is not a culturally formed subjectivity; it is a pre-existent register of the Real that becomes operative through the work.

This is the precise edge at which the user's polemic re-enters even the most sympathetic late Foucault. The *recovery of practice* is welcome and consequential; the agnosticism about what the practice puts the practitioner in contact with is the Cartesian shadow that even the late Foucault has not fully dispelled. To read the late Foucault as if he had finished the work is to mistake a partial recovery for a completed one. The user reads the late Foucault as a Western philosopher edging back, against the weight of the entire post-Cartesian apparatus, toward something the Indic traditions had never lost — and to give him credit for the edging-back without confusing it with arrival.

## Part VI — Reading notes

These notes consolidate the user's position on Foucault and stage three things: (1) the points at which Foucault *confirms* the user's thesis, (2) the points at which he *diverges*, and (3) the user's polemic — the precise location at which Foucault is right (and useful) and the precise location at which the standard reception of Foucault is over-extended into something the user must reject. A reading sequence closes the brief.

### Where Foucault confirms the user's thesis

**T1 — Register-distinction.** Foucault's *épistémè*, refined by *L'archéologie du savoir* into the four-axis discursive formation, is the closest twentieth-century European approximation of the user's claim that statements within different darśanas operate within different fields of formation and are not in propositional competition. Where the user reads the apparent disagreement between Advaita's *nirguṇa Brahman* and Bhakti's *saguṇa Brahman* as a register-difference rather than a contradiction, Foucault would (correctly, on his own terms) read them as *énoncés* in different discursive formations, with different objects, subject-positions, conceptual networks, and strategic possibilities. The Foucauldian analytic, taken as method and stripped of the methodological agnosticism, gives the register-thesis a precise European-philosophical articulation. *L'archéologie du savoir* is therefore the Foucault book the user can borrow most directly, with the fewest reservations.

**T11 — Anti-Cartesian-subject.** Foucault's dismantling of "man" as the empirico-transcendental doublet of the post-1800 European *épistémè* is congenial to the user's position that the Cartesian *cogito*, the Kantian transcendental subject, and the Hegelian universal subject are not the deepest fact about subjectivity. The user agrees: these are constructions internal to a specifically Western *épistémè*, and they are dissolving. The Aurobindonian psychic being (caitya puruṣa) is *not* the figure of "man" Foucault dethrones; it is a different candidate for the center of subjectivity, on a different axis, accessed by different practices. So the dethronement of "man" leaves Aurobindonian psychic-being doctrine untouched; in fact, it clears the ground.

**The constituted-subject-can-self-transform-via-ascesis thesis.** The late Foucault's recovery of *epimeleia heautou* maps with structural precision onto the user's account of *sādhanā*. The empirical subject is constituted by historical, cultural, biological forces (Foucault); the empirical subject is also reachable as the field of practice on which a transformation can be undertaken (Foucault, late); through this practice the subject can become otherwise (Foucault, late). The Indic equivalent runs: the empirical *jīva* is constituted by *karma*, *saṃskāra*, cultural and biological inheritance; the *jīva* is also reachable as the field of *sādhanā*; through *sādhanā* the *jīva* is transformed and, in the limit, the *psychic being* takes operative possession of the surface personality. The structural homology is exact. (The metaphysical destination is different, but that is a separate question.)

**The four-dimensional analytic of ethics.** The substance / mode / work / telos schema of Volumes 2–3 is portable: it can be applied to the Indic ethical traditions and produces, at each darśana, a coherent description of what is being worked on, by what authority, through what techniques, toward what end. This is a usable export from late Foucault into the Indic register-analytic.

**The diagnosis of disciplinary modernity.** *Surveiller et punir*'s analysis of how schools, prisons, hospitals, factories, armies produce docile-and-useful bodies, and of how the modern soul is the surface on which that production registers, is largely correct as an empirical analysis of modern European institutions. The user's politics — matsya-nyāya as the analytic of the predatory state, Vivekanandian morphology of predation, Bhattacharyya's *Swaraj in Ideas* on categorial capture — is fully compatible with Foucault's diagnosis at the empirical level. The disciplinary apparatus is real, productive, and politically consequential; the user agrees with this part of Foucault unreservedly.

### Where Foucault diverges

**No positive doctrine of the divine.** Foucault has none, by methodological principle. The *dispositif*, the *épistémè*, the practice of the self — all of these are immanent to a historical field. There is no place in Foucault's analytic for a Real beyond the field, for a Brahman, for a *paramārtha* register, for the supramental. This is not, in itself, an *error*; it is a methodological self-restriction. But when the methodological self-restriction is promoted (in the reception, often without explicit argument) to a metaphysical thesis — *there is no Real beyond the field of practice* — the user diverges sharply. The user's metaphysics (T1, T6, T7, T9) explicitly affirms one Real, said in many senses, of which each darśana, each register, each tradition speaks truly in its own way. Foucault's framework cannot accommodate this; the user holds it nonetheless.

**Genealogy explains genesis but not validity.** Foucault's genealogical method shows how a discourse, a practice, an institution, a subject-formation came to be as it is. It does not show, and on its own principles cannot show, whether the resulting discourse, practice, institution, or subjectivity *tracks anything true*. The genealogy of the discourse on madness shows how the modern category "madness" was constituted; it does not show whether there is or is not a *real* psychic phenomenon (or family of phenomena) in the vicinity of which that constitution operates. The user holds (against an over-extended genealogical reading) that *the genesis of a category does not falsify what the category tracks*. The Vedāntic concept of *avidyā* has a long history of formation; its genealogy is interesting; but the genealogy does not tell us whether the empirical phenomenon of *avidyā* is real. Bhattacharyya's principle — *cancellation is not falsification* — is the precise corrective. Foucault's reception has often used genealogy *as if* it were a falsification of the genealogically-tracked phenomenon; the user rejects this conflation.

**The reduction of subjectivity to power-relations.** This is the move the user most centrally rejects. At the empirical, social, institutional level, subjectivity is heavily shaped by power-relations — the user accepts this. But power-relations are not the *whole* of subjectivity. There is a *psychic being* whose existence is not constituted by, and not exhausted by, the disciplinary apparatus. There is a *supramental subject* whose realization is the destination, not the starting-point, of *sādhanā*. Foucault would say: these are themselves the products of a specific cultural-religious *dispositif* — namely, the Aurobindonian one. The user's response is that the production-of-the-discourse-of-the-psychic-being and the *reality* of the psychic being are different questions; Foucault's method addresses the former without thereby foreclosing the latter. To use the genealogy of Aurobindo's discourse as an argument *against* the reality of the psychic being is exactly the genealogy-as-falsification mistake.

**No analysis of devotional looking.** The Panopticon describes one structure of asymmetric vision: the supervisor sees, the inmate is seen, and the asymmetry is the form of disciplinary power. But there is another structure of asymmetric vision — the *darśana* of the icon, the *bhakti* relation in which the deity sees the devotee and the devotee offers herself to the gaze. The asymmetry here is *not* power-over but the very condition of *līlā* and *prema-bhāva*. Foucault's framework, at least in its middle-period formulation, has no analytic for this register. *Bhakti* is, structurally, *not* available within his middle-period analytic. (The late period is more open, but never thematizes this case.) For the user — for whom the path of *viraha-bhakti* and surrender-from-fullness is one of the central registers (T10) — this is a major lacuna.

### The user's polemic, stated precisely

The user's stance toward Foucault is therefore three-layered.

**Layer 1: empirical-historical Foucault is largely accepted.** The accounts of how knowledge of madness, of the clinic, of the prison, of sexuality have been historically constituted; the analysis of disciplinary power; the diagnosis of biopower as a modality of modern state-form; the recovery of *epimeleia heautou* in the late lectures — at the level of the empirical-historical register, these are mostly correct, often illuminating, and useful for the user's politics and methodology.

**Layer 2: methodological Foucault is conditionally accepted.** The analytic of *énoncés*, discursive formations, the four-axis specification of a field, the genealogical attention to discontinuity and contingency, the four-dimensional analytic of ethics in Volumes 2–3 — these are usable methodological tools, available for application beyond the European archive that Foucault himself worked. The user can deploy them on the darśanas, on the Indic ethical traditions, on Aurobindo's relation to the colonial-modern *dispositif*. The condition: methodological agnosticism is a virtue *as method*, but should not be promoted to metaphysical denial.

**Layer 3: metaphysical Foucault — the totalizing reduction of subjectivity to power-relations and discourse — is rejected.** This is the move that the user calls the *category error* of postmodern theory: ontologized grammar, the reification of methodological epoché into metaphysical thesis. Power-relations are real; *power-relations are not all that is real*. Discursive formations are real; *discursive formations are not all that is real*. The user's polemic against postmodern theory is most precisely directed at this third layer, not at Foucault's empirical work and not at his methodology.

The third-layer overreading is more characteristic of the *reception* of Foucault than of Foucault himself, who was, by the standards of his successors, methodologically careful. The Foucault of 1982 is not the Foucault of "post-Foucauldian theory" as it has been deployed in Anglophone critical discourse since the 1990s. The user is therefore engaging not only with Foucault but with the Foucault-effect — the institutional mobilization of the work in directions Foucault himself would not always have endorsed.

A specific form of this polemic. In *Surveiller et punir* the soul is "*effet et instrument d'une anatomie politique; l'âme, prison du corps*" — the soul as effect and instrument of political anatomy, the soul the prison of the body. Within the local Christian-modern apparatus this analysis tracks something. But to read this as a *general* claim about the soul — that the only soul there is, in any tradition, in any practice, in any candidate metaphysics, is the disciplinary product of a political anatomy — is to use a local diagnosis as a global metaphysics. The user's response: *the modern Christian-disciplinary soul is one historical-cultural product, and its genealogy as Foucault traces it is largely correct; the soul of the bhakta who is wept upon by Krishna, the soul of the yogin in nirvikalpa-samādhi, the psychic being of Aurobindonian sādhanā, are different entities altogether, with different conditions of existence, accessed by different practices, and they are not derivable from, or exhausted by, the Foucauldian analysis of political anatomy.*

### A reading sequence

For someone who must learn Foucault and has limited time, the following sequence balances accessibility with philosophical importance. It is *not* the chronological order, and it is *not* the systematic order; it is a learning order.

**(1) Begin with *Surveiller et punir*, Chapter 1 of Part I ("Le corps des condamnés") and the entirety of Part III ("Discipline").** This is the most accessible Foucault and the most rhetorically gripping. The Damiens opening will fix the contrast with disciplinary modernity in the reader's mind permanently; the analysis of disciplinary techniques in Part III will give the reader the empirical sense of what Foucault is talking about.

**(2) Continue with *Surveiller et punir*, the chapter on the Panopticon at the end of Part III.** This is the most-cited single passage in Foucault's corpus and the diagram of disciplinary power.

**(3) Move to *L'herméneutique du sujet*, the first three lectures (6, 13, 20 January 1982).** These open the late position with great clarity. Read alongside Frédéric Gros's "course context" appended to the Gallimard/Seuil edition, which orients the reader in the wider trajectory of the late work.

**(4) Then *Histoire de la sexualité* I (*La volonté de savoir*), Parts I and II (the introduction "We 'Other Victorians'" and the entire "Repressive Hypothesis" section).** This gives the productive-power thesis at its sharpest.

**(5) Then *Les mots et les choses*, the Préface (on the Borges encyclopedia and the project of an archaeology) and the closing chapter on the figure of "man."** Skip the long substantive chapters on natural history, general grammar, and the analysis of wealth, on a first reading; they require background the average reader will not have. The Préface and the closing pages give the philosophical structure.

**(6) Finally, if time permits, *L'archéologie du savoir*, Parts I–III.** This is the methodologically densest book; do it last because by this point the reader will have empirical-analytic anchors against which to measure the abstract methodological claims.

For the user's specific purposes — engagement with Foucault as a partial ally and a partial polemical target — the most consequential reading is **the late Foucault** (item 3 above and the second and third volumes of the *Histoire de la sexualité*). It is in the late period that Foucault's framework opens, however partially, onto the practice-of-self analytic that the Indic traditions have always centered. The middle Foucault (items 1, 2, 4) is critical for the empirical-political diagnosis and for understanding why Foucault's reception has been what it has. The early Foucault (items 5, 6) is methodologically foundational but is the most easily over-read into postmodern relativism, and should be approached with the user's polemic against the third-layer metaphysical reduction firmly in view.

A final note. The user's relation to Foucault is *not* the relation of an opponent; it is the relation of a careful reader who finds substantial parts of the diagnosis accurate, substantial parts of the method useful, and the metaphysical totalization that the reception has often built on the work unacceptable. To read Foucault non-caricaturally is to take seriously the local rigor of his historical work, the genuine philosophical seriousness of the late turn, and the real philosophical claim — anti-Cartesian, anti-universal-subject, anti-juridical-power — that the work makes; and to refuse, simultaneously, the silent generalization of these local claims into a totalizing metaphysics in which there is nothing but power, nothing but discourse, nothing but the field of practice. The user accepts the *épistémè*-thesis as a register-analytic; rejects the silent metaphysical agnosticism dressed as ontology; accepts the disciplinary-power diagnosis at the empirical level; rejects the production-of-the-soul thesis as a totalizing claim; accepts the recovery of *epimeleia heautou* as a partial Western retrieval of what Indic *sādhanā* never lost; and reads the late Foucault as a Western philosopher edging back, against his own apparatus, toward a position the user can engage as a partial fellow-traveler, without ever confusing partial agreement for arrival.
