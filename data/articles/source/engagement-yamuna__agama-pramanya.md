# Engagement: Yāmuna's *Āgama-Prāmāṇya*

## 1. What the text is

*Āgama-Prāmāṇya* is an independent Sanskrit polemical treatise by Yāmunācārya (Ālavandār; traditionally c. 10th-11th century CE), an early and decisive architect of the Śrīvaiṣṇava line that later runs through Rāmānuja and Vedānta Deśika. It is not a bhāṣya on a mūla-text, but a self-standing defense of the Pañcarātra revelation against charges of Veda-bāhyatva, social illegitimacy, and doctrinal incoherence; its printed form is a single continuous work with a detailed contents-page rather than internal adhyāya-divisions, and the surviving edition adds an editor's preface and table of contents before the main text. Structurally, the body runs to roughly 87 printed pages in the pure-Sanskrit file and moves from pramāṇa-theory, to cosmological and scriptural defense, to anti-Pāśupata differentiation, to the *Brahma-sūtra* `utpattyasambhavāt` objection, and finally to social-ritual boundary questions around *bhāgavata* status, *devalaka*, *nirmālya*, and *nivedya*.

Cross-engagement: a real divergence with Abhinavagupta on the substrate structure axis. The metaphysical register is shared; the values are not — `one-qualified-by-real-internal-distinctions` here, `one-self-standing` at [primary text](cite://abhinavagupta/tantraloka/1.16).

## 2. Sampling and scope statement

Engaged at depth: roughly pp. 1-26, 42-64, and 72-84 of the pure-Sanskrit text, plus selected closing lines on p. 87. Those are the stretches for which I followed the argument stepwise, translated passages, and checked how Yāmuna turns objections into constructive theses. Structural summary only: roughly pp. 27-41, 65-71, and 85-87, where I traced the argumentative role of the sections but did not translate every quotation or sub-argument. Flagged but not fully engaged: some OCR-damaged technical asides in the middle pages, and the many quoted *smṛti/purāṇa* citations whose exact wider source-context I did not reconstruct beyond what this file itself gives.

Cross-engagement: against Adi Sankara: `dependent-real` rather than `sublatable-not-null` for the manifestation status axis ([primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/1.1.1)).

## 3. Structural walk-through

The contents-page in the raw OCR gives the text's best explicit segmentation. The opening cluster, up to the heading `vṛttyartha-devatā-pūjanādi-niṣedhaḥ` (contents p. 1; body through pure-Sanskrit pp. 1-10), stages the hostile case: Pañcarātrins are said to be non-Brāhmaṇas, *bhāgavatas* are socially suspect, and temple-service practices supposedly mark them as outside Vedic respectability. This is not yet the text's constructive core; it is the social disqualification strategy that Yāmuna must first neutralize.

Cross-engagement: Anandabodha ([primary text](cite://sarvajnatman/samksepa-sariraka/1.319-352)) and this section take opposite turns on the identity relation axis — `non-otherness` against `body-soul-qualification`.

The next unit, `vedasāmyena pañcarātra-tantra-prāmāṇya-nirūpaṇam` together with `pṛthivy-ādīnāṃ vicitra-sanniveśatvādina kāryatvānumānam` (contents pp. 11-12; pure-Sanskrit pp. 11-18), shifts from social suspicion to epistemology and cosmology. Yāmuna argues that the world is inferably an effect and that a superhuman knower of *dharma/adharma* is therefore not gratuitous. Polemically, this opens conceptual room for a revelatory text that is neither reducible to ordinary human speech nor blocked by the limits of perception.

The broad argument marked by `veda-pañcarātrayoḥ sāmyena prāmāṇya-nirūpaṇam` (contents p. 18; pure-Sanskrit pp. 18-26) is the treatise's first load-bearing move. Yāmuna denies that verbal cognition is intrinsically invalid unless backed by some external guarantee; defects belong to defective speakers, not to *śabda* as such. This is the point at which Pañcarātra ceases to be defended merely as venerable custom and is claimed as a genuine pramāṇa for otherwise inaccessible religious means.

The largest middle cluster runs from the comparison with rival Tantras through `nārāyaṇa-praṇītatvena pañcarātra-tantra-prāmāṇya-vyavasthāpanam` and `tantrāntarāṇāṃ ... prāmāṇya-samarthanāsaṃbhavaḥ` (contents pp. 23-46; pure-Sanskrit roughly pp. 23-51). Here Yāmuna confronts the obvious parity objection: if one grants Pañcarātra because it claims divine origin, why not Pāśupata and the rest? His answer is asymmetrical. Rival Tantras are excluded because their content conflicts with Vedic and Vedāntic teaching, whereas Pañcarātra is said to condense the Vedic revelation of Nārāyaṇa/Viṣṇu as supreme cause and supreme object of worship.

The next unit, beginning with the contents headings on *dīkṣā* and the `utpattyasambhava-catuḥsūtrī` (contents pp. 52 and 63; pure-Sanskrit pp. 52-64), is the work's direct intervention in Vedānta polemics. Yāmuna takes up the familiar charge that Pañcarātra teaches impossible origination, especially in connection with the vyūhas and with *jīvotpatti*. His strategy is to deny the opponent's reading: the text should not be taken to teach literal production of independent entities from one another, but differentiated manifestation of the one Vāsudeva.

The section marked `veda-pañcarātra-śāstrayoḥ svataḥ-prāmāṇya-nirūpaṇam` (contents after p. 63; pure-Sanskrit pp. 64-71) presses the parity claim further. If Veda does not wait on a separately established proof of speakerly defectlessness before counting as authoritative, then Pañcarātra should not be put under a stricter burden once its divine provenance and Veda-concordance are admitted. This is the text's most systematic pramāṇa-theoretic claim.

The final third returns to community-formation. The contents headings on `gotrādy-asādhāraṇa-dharma-jñāna-nibandhana-brāhmaṇya`, suspicion of *vaiśya-vrātya* descent, and `sātvata-bhāgavata-śabdārtha-nirūpaṇam` (contents pp. 70-77; pure-Sanskrit pp. 72-79) argue that shared labels do not force shared caste-status, that *brāhmaṇya* is socially and genealogically recognizable, and that *bhāgavata* naming does not make Vaiṣṇavas non-Brāhmaṇa by definition. This is a polemic against linguistic and ritual shortcuts: one cannot infer social ontology from a word alone.

The closing unit, from `devalakādi-śabdārtha-nirūpaṇam` through `nirmālya`, `bhagavan-nivedita-vastu-upayoga`, `devatāntara-nirmālya-upayoga-niṣedha`, and `bhojyābhojya-vyavasthā` (contents pp. 79-84 and following; pure-Sanskrit pp. 79-87), makes clear what is at stake. Scriptural authority cashes out in concrete ritual boundaries: who may serve, who may consume, what counts as pure, and why Viṣṇu-offerings are not to be assimilated to the remnants of other cults. The treatise ends with lineage-praise centering Nāthamuni, so the final effect is institutional as much as theoretical.

## 4. Load-bearing passages — engaged at depth

**1. Locus:** pure-Sanskrit p. 34, in the defense of verbal authority.

Cross-engagement: Akalanka's reading at [primary text](cite://umasvati/tattvartha-sutra/1.34-35) fills the epistemic authority axis with `plural-pramana-realism`. The present section fills it with `scripture-dominant`.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `naiva śabde svato doṣāḥ prāmāṇya-paripanthinaḥ | santi kintu svatas tasya pramāṇatvam iti sthitiḥ ||`

**Close English:** “There are no faults in verbal testimony, arising from itself, that obstruct its validity; rather, the settled view is that its validity belongs to it from itself.”

**Engagement:** This is the text's cleanest methodological thesis. Yāmuna is not saying that every utterance is true; he is relocating the burden of defeasance. *Śabda* does not begin under suspicion and then acquire authority only by external certification. It begins as authoritative unless some speaker-defect or contradiction undercuts it. That is exactly the move Pañcarātra needs.

Word by word: `na eva` = “not ”; `śabde` locative singular, the locus in which the relevant property is denied; `svataḥ` = “from itself”; `doṣāḥ` nominative plural; `prāmāṇya-paripanthinaḥ` = `prāmāṇyasya paripanthinaḥ`, “obstructors of validity”; `santi` = the principal finite verb, “are”; `kintu` = “rather”; `svatas` again marks source; `tasya` genitive singular, “of it”; `pramāṇatvam` nominative singular, “authoritativeness”; `iti sthitiḥ` = “such is the established position.” The principal predication is `doṣāḥ santi`, with `śabde` as locus; the second hemistich is elliptical but amounts to `tasya pramāṇatvaṃ svatas [asti]`. The compound `prāmāṇya-paripanthinaḥ` is a ṣaṣṭhī-tatpuruṣa: those “hostile to” or “blocking” validity. The philosophical force of the grammar is that invalidity is derivative; validity is default.

**2. Locus:** pure-Sanskrit p. 12, cosmological inference in support of a competent revealer.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `vivādagocharā bhāvāḥ kāryā viśvambharādayaḥ | vicitra-sanniveśatvān narendra-bhavanādivat ||`

**Close English:** “The entities that are under dispute, such as earth and the rest, are effects, because they possess complex arrangement, like a king's palace and the like.”

**Engagement:** This is a standard-looking inferential move, but in this treatise it serves a very specific function. Yāmuna is not trying to build a free-standing natural theology for its own sake. He is trying to block the claim that only ordinary, publicly inspectable human production can license discourse about religious means and ends. If the world itself bears inferential marks of intelligent production, then a superhuman knower of *dharma* is no longer a gratuitous posit.

**3. Locus:** pure-Sanskrit p. 43, the saturation of Vedic language with Nārāyaṇa-identity.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `śrūyate hi sa vedānteṣv ātmā nārāyaṇaḥ paraḥ | satyaṃ jñānam anantaṃ ca tad viṣṇoḥ paramaṃ padam ||`

**Close English:** “For it is heard in the Vedāntas that that Self is the supreme Nārāyaṇa; [it is] reality, knowledge, and endlessness, and that is the supreme station of Viṣṇu.”

**Engagement:** The important point is not the isolated quotation but the argumentative use of quotation-clusters. Yāmuna piles *śruti*, *smṛti*, and *purāṇa* on top of one another in order to make “Pañcarātra is Vedic in substance” look like the simplest reading of the authoritative archive. The constructive thesis is therefore not merely “our Tantra is old” but “our Tantra is a condensed restatement of what the Veda already discloses about the supreme cause.”

**4. Locus:** pure-Sanskrit p. 64, transition into the `vijñānādibhāve vā tadapratishedhaḥ` discussion.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `pañcarātrasya kṛtsnasya vaktā nārāyaṇaḥ svayam | idaṃ mahopaniṣadaṃ caturveda-samanvitam ||`

**Close English:** “Nārāyaṇa himself is the speaker of the whole Pañcarātra; this is a great Upaniṣad furnished with the four Vedas.”

**Engagement:** This is the hinge of the anti-asymmetry argument. If this traditional memory is admitted, Pañcarātra is not a merely sectarian supplement hanging beneath Veda; it is a Veda-suffused revelation in its own right. Yāmuna's bolder move is then to say that the prāmāṇya-question should be handled symmetrically: both Veda and Pañcarātra disclose what is not otherwise available, and both rest on divine rather than ordinary human cognition.

**5. Locus:** pure-Sanskrit pp. 76-77, the semantic defense of *sātvata/bhāgavata* naming.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `na caika-śabda-vācyatvād ekajātīyatā bhavet | mā bhūd ācārya-śabdatvād brāhmaṇasya nikṛṣṭatā ||`

**Close English:** “Nor does identity of caste follow from being denoted by a single word. Otherwise, because both are called ‘ācārya,’ the Brāhmaṇa would have to become degraded.”

**Engagement:** This is a genuinely sharp local argument. Yāmuna refuses the easy move from lexical extension to social identity. The point is not modern liberalism; it is analytic discipline. Shared word-use underdetermines ontology and status. That matters because a large part of the anti-Pañcarātra case trades on word-level conflations: *bhāgavata*, *sātvata*, *devalaka*, temple-servant, non-Brāhmaṇa. Yāmuna's reply is that such inferences are semantically sloppy.

**6. Locus:** pure-Sanskrit pp. 82-84, in the discussion of *nirmālya* and Viṣṇu-offerings.

**Sanskrit (IAST):** `viṣṇo-nivedyakaṃ śuddhaṃ munibhir bhojyam ucyate | anya-nivedya-nirmālyaṃ cāndrāyaṇaṃ caret ||`

**Close English:** “What has been offered to Viṣṇu is said by the sages to be pure and fit to be eaten; for the leftover of what has been offered elsewhere one should perform a Cāndrāyaṇa expiation.”

**Engagement:** The treatise ends where its practical stakes always lay: in liturgical exclusivity and embodied religious distinction. This is not an appendix detachable from the earlier pramāṇa-argument. On Yāmuna's view, scriptural authority is shown precisely in its power to determine purity, hierarchy, and access where common social inference is insufficient. The ontology of divine offering is therefore strong: Viṣṇu's consecrated remainder is not a symbol, nor religiously interchangeable matter.

## 5. The text's relation to the position

This work sits squarely on the Yāmuna → Rāmānuja → Vedānta Deśika line. It is upstream of the *Śrī-bhāṣya*'s defense of Pañcarātra and downstream of the broader Vaiṣṇava insistence that divine form, divine names, and liturgical mediation are not optional pedagogical fictions. It is not, however, a primary source for the Bhartṛprapañca → Bhāskara → Madhva → Caitanya → Aurobindo realism-recovery trajectory in the strict sense, nor does it enter the Vivaraṇa-school disputes about *avidyā* that matter for T7. Its polemical world is different: Mīmāṃsaka restriction, anti-Pañcarātra Vedāntins, and rival Tantras, above all Pāśupata.

Cross-engagement: Anantakrishna Sastri works the truth-structure axis in the epistemological register too, but the value is `hierarchical-standpoint-truth`, not `single-absolute-truth` ([primary text](cite://sankara/brahma-sutra-bhasya/2.1.14)).

Whom does it refute? By named or quasi-named type: Pāśupata, Śaiva, Kāpālika, Kālāmukha, Bauddha, Jaina/Ārhata, and other *veda-bāhya* systems. By anonymous *pūrva-pakṣin*: the Veda-only critic who says Pañcarātra is socially borne by non-Brāhmaṇas, doctrinally guilty of *jīvotpatti*, textually tainted by apparent Veda-nindā, and epistemically too human to count as pramāṇa. The constructive thesis is narrower than a grand metaphysics: Pañcarātra is Veda-concordant revelation of Nārāyaṇa-Viṣṇu and therefore authoritative for worship, initiation, and salvation.

For the position's specific load-bearing claims, the fit is uneven. On T2, the text helps indirectly rather than directly: it does not theorize `mithyā`, but it is a strong early witness against any flattening of divine manifestation, image, mantra, or ritual relation into mere unreality. On T9 it is much more directly useful: this is a hard Vaiṣṇava critique of any framework that would render divine plurality and worship merely secondary. On T11 it contributes only modestly: Yāmuna is not doing an ontology of phenomenology, but he is insisting that scriptural and ritual disclosure reach realities not capturable by ordinary empiricism. On T7, almost nothing substantial should be claimed; *Āgama-Prāmāṇya* is not a text about *avidyā* as *bhāvarūpa*, nor about Brahman as both *āśraya* and *viṣaya* of ignorance.

The principal resistance is equally clear. This is not a charter for “all paths” pluralism. Yāmuna ranks traditions, excludes rival Tantras, and insists on asymmetry in favor of Viṣṇu-centered revelation. Vallabha and Nimbārka later offer parallel realisms in different registers, and Madhva later radicalizes a different anti-Māyāvāda pressure; but *this* text itself is sectarian, juridical, and boundary-policing. If the position uses it, it should use it for the reality of Vaiṣṇava disclosure and the critique of reduction, not as a warrant for universal harmonization.

## 6. What the user can productively take

This text gives the corpus a strong pre-Rāmānuja primary source for the claim that Pañcarātra is not a post-Vedic excrescence but a Veda-concordant disclosure of Nārāyaṇa. It is especially useful wherever the position needs hard evidence that divine form, divine name, consecrated offering, and liturgical relation are ontologically serious rather than symbolic concessions.

Cross-engagement: a real divergence with G. W. F. Hegel on the normativity axis. The ethical register is shared; the values are not — `divine-command-or-grace` here, `ethical-life-in-institutions` at [primary text](cite://hegel/phenomenology-of-spirit/17).

It also sharpens a limitation. Any synthetic use of Vaiṣṇava anti-Māyāvāda materials has to answer Yāmuna's exclusivism rather than quietly absorb it. He will support a realism of manifestation and relation; he will not support a soft ecumenism.

Finally, the text strengthens one very specific corpus-line: the Śrīvaiṣṇava insistence that authority, ontology, and ritual practice are continuous. If the position wants to say that manifestation is real and that forms of worship disclose rather than conceal the Real, Yāmuna supplies an early, disciplined, and unapologetically primary witness.
