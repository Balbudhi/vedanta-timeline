# Translation Poetics for *Koi Hor Nahi Hai Mera*

Derived from a multi-pass reading of Sri Aurobindo, *The Future Poetry* (CWSA vol. 26).
Source on disk: `/Users/eeshan/Dev/vedanta-timeline/data/sources/english/aurobindo/the-future-poetry.txt`.

This document is in three parts:

1. **Principles** — what Aurobindo demands of a poetic word that carries spirit, distilled into translation constraints.
2. **Models** — the English poets and traditions Aurobindo himself names as bearers of the register this song requires.
3. **The song** — opinionated guidance for *Koi Hor Nahi*, followed by line-by-line proposals for the four lines you named (invocation, refrain, v1a, v1b).

## Part 1 — Aurobindo's principles, distilled as translation constraints

Aurobindo wrote this book to argue for one thing: the **Mantra** — "a rhythmic revelation or intuition arising out of the soul's sight" (Ch. "Poetic Vision and the Mantra"). It is not decoration; it is the word that *makes the hearer see*, the word in which "the unity of a divine rhythmic movement" meets "a depth of sense and a power of infinite suggestion welling up directly from the fountain-heads of the spirit within us" (Ch. "Style and Substance"). Translating a devotional song into English is, for him, an attempt at exactly this.

From a close reading, ten principles bear directly on our task. Each is a constraint on word-choice.

### P1. The soul is the hearer, not the intellect

> "neither the intelligence, the imagination nor the ear are the true or at least the deepest or highest recipients of the poetic delight … the true creator, the true hearer is the soul. The more rapidly and transparently the rest do their work of transmission, the less they make of their separate claim to satisfaction, the more directly the word reaches and sinks deep into the soul, the greater the poetry."
> ("The Essence of Poetry")

**Constraint:** Choose the word that reaches the soul fastest. Any word that makes the reader stop to admire it (or to decode it scholastically) has already failed. Reject all diction whose first effect is to flatter taste.

### P2. The poet is a seer — make the reader *see*

> "The essential power of the poetic word is to make us see, not to make us think or feel; thought and feeling must arise out of the sight or be included in it, but sight is the primary consequence and power of poetic speech."
> ("Style and Substance")

**Constraint:** Each rendered line must produce, in the English reader's mind, a *picture* — concrete, bodied, of one event. Not the idea of devotion but the image: the small fist on the cloth. "Liturgical" diction (*hem, beseech, supplicate, eternal flame*) fails this test because it gives a category, not a picture.

### P3. Rhythm carries the consciousness; you cannot translate sense without rhythm

> "Rhythm is the premier necessity of poetical expression because it is the sound-movement which carries on its wave the thought-movement in the word."
> ("Style and Substance")

> "The vital power of the poetic spirit … given the native power in him, has all the vitality of a thing fully felt and lived."
> ("The Breath of Greater Life")

**Constraint:** Each line must have a cadence that the mouth and ear find inevitable — not lumpy, not iambic-padded. Where the Punjabi opens with two long-vowelled syllables (*Mā merī*), the English must open with weight too. Where it ends with a release (*…jai!*), the English must release. If a literal rendering is rhythmically clumsy, choose freedom over fidelity.

### P4. Reject the half-poetry of "intellectual or rhetorical commonplace"

Aurobindo gives Pope's *"God sees with equal eyes as lord of all / A hero perish or a sparrow fall"* as a cautionary example: an idea that, rightly seen, "might touch the very heights of poetic revelation" is "falsified" by being made "intellectual and rhetorical, not poetic figures" (Ch. "Style and Substance").

**Constraint:** "Victory always be Yours" is in this register — abstract piety. So is "I have held onto Your hem; I have no one else but You" — it summarises rather than embodies. Replace abstractions with their living image.

### P5. The "inmost in the inmost way" — the most direct utterance is the highest

> "this new turn of the poetic voice is characteristically an endeavour to see and to say *our inmost in the inmost way*."
> ("Recent English Poetry – II")

**Constraint:** Do not paraphrase the meaning. *Say what the child says,* exactly, with no translatorial overlay. Strip "translator's English" — interpretive insertions like "eventually," "anyway," "just," "even then" — wherever they bury the bare cry. Punjabi devotional address is naked; the English must be naked.

### P6. Spiritual *Ananda* is found through, not above, the concrete

> "The poet's business most really, most intimately is not with the outward physical life as it is or the life of the passions and emotions only for its own sake or even with some ideal life imaged by the mind … but with the life of the soul *and with these other things only as its expressive forms*."
> ("The Breath of Greater Life")

> "the beauty and delight of the thousand-coloured, many-crested, million-waved miracle of life made a hundred times more profoundly meaningful by the greatness and the sweetness and attracting poignancy of the self-creating inmost soul."
> ("The Soul of Poetic Delight and Beauty")

**Constraint:** The clutched cloth, the breath stopping, the refusal to turn around — these *are* the spirit. Do not "elevate" them into "hem of Your garment" or "we shall depart this life." The child's gesture in its bare physical accuracy IS the spiritual content.

### P7. Reverence is not produced by formality; the highest reverence is intimate

Aurobindo, on Whitman's "The Younger melts in fondness in his arms" (the soul as child to God-as-Elder-Brother): "one of the seers of old time reborn in ours might so have expressed himself in a modern and intellectualised language" ("Recent English Poetry — IV"). The *figure* he singles out for praise is *exactly* a child melting in a parent's arms — not a worshipper at a shrine.

**Constraint:** "My Mother" with capital M is the right move, but the surrounding diction must be conversational and warm, not vestmented. Reverence in this song lives in the way a child says *Maa* — not in any English Latinism.

### P8. The childlike register is *not* a lower register — Aurobindo names it as one of the rarest

Of Blake: *"casts into some echo of the language of the luminous children of those shores the songs of their childhood and their innocence … here we have something unique, a voice of things which had not been heard before nor has it been heard since"* ("The Poets of the Dawn – III"). Of Wordsworth's "philosophy of childhood": *"the first clear intimations dimmed and finally passed leaving behind an unillumined waste of mere thought"* — i.e. the childhood-voice was the *high* register, and what came after was the fall ("Poets of the Dawn – II").

**Constraint:** When the user says "the childlike register is missing," he is naming what Aurobindo says is the *highest* of the available registers, not a softer or simpler one. Hold this register *without simpering*. Do not write babytalk. Write the way a real five-year-old speaks to his mother when he means it: short clauses, repeated words, no ornament, blunt insistence.

### P9. Repetition is not redundancy — it is the body of devotion

> "The lyric is a moment of heightened soul experience … sometimes prolonged and repeating or varying the same note." ("The Form and the Spirit")

The song *itself* is built on this: the refrain "Koī hor nahī ae merā" recurs maybe twenty-four times across the song. The repetition IS the devotion, the way a child clinging says "you, you, you, you."

**Constraint:** Do not vary the refrain to avoid monotony. English likes synonym-variation; resist it. The English refrain must remain word-for-word identical every time it appears, just as in Punjabi.

### P10. The word must do the alchemical work *itself* — the translator cannot smuggle in feeling by explanation

> "the inspired word comes, as said of old the Vedic seers, from the home of Truth … and it is only at best the most outward or mechanical part of it which admits analysis."
> ("The Word and the Spirit")

> "much of the work is done not so much by the language as the subtle sense suggestion of the rhythm and word music, the sound doing the alchemic labour of transfiguration."
> (ibid.)

**Constraint:** Do not add adjectives or adverbs to compensate for what you suspect the reader will miss. ("True, eternal sacred flames" is exactly this — three modifiers attempting to recover a single Punjabi compound that worked by sound and reverence alone.) Trust the bare noun. Trust the bare verb.

## Part 2 — The poets Aurobindo points to as models for this register

Aurobindo's text names, with care, the English-language voices that he believes carry spirit-into-word. I draw four of these forward as direct models for *Koi Hor Nahi*. I note in each case **what Aurobindo says** and **why it bears on us**.

### Model A — **William Blake** (childlike directness as the highest, not the lowest, voice)

Aurobindo on Blake: *"casts into some echo of the language of the luminous children of those shores the songs of their childhood and their innocence … he becomes limpid to us and sheds upon our earth some clear charm, felicity, wonder of a half divine otherwhere"* ("Poets of the Dawn — III"). He calls these "the luminous simplicities of Blake."

This is the central model for our song. Blake's *Songs of Innocence* — "Little Lamb, who made thee?" — and his *Auguries of Innocence* show how a child's voice can carry the deepest theology with no loss of doctrinal weight. The diction is monosyllabic and the syntax is short. **Adopt this for v1a and v1b: short declaratives. No subordinate clauses. No abstract nouns.**

### Model B — **Wordsworth at his Nature-and-childhood best**

Aurobindo: *"in the luminous simplicities of Blake, in the faery melodies of Coleridge, most of all perhaps in the lyrical cry and ethereal light of Shelley … We find it in Wordsworth's [moments of] revelations of the spiritual presence in Nature."* He admires Wordsworth's *"direct simplicities and spontaneities of the soul's emotion"* — and condemns Wordsworth when *"he intellectualises his poetic statement overmuch."*

This is the warning for us: Wordsworth at his best ("They flash upon the inner eye / Which is the bliss of solitude") is monosyllabic and present-tense; at his worst he moralises. **For the invocation, use Wordsworth's *clear-light* manner: simple grammar, a present-tense address, vowels open enough to hold reverence.**

### Model C — **Whitman — the soul as child, intimate with the Divine as parent**

Aurobindo quotes Whitman *approvingly* and at length on this passage:

> "Surrounded, copest, frontest God, yieldest, the aim attained,
> As filled with friendship, love complete, the Elder Brother found,
> The Younger melts in fondness in his arms."

He says: *"one of the seers of old time reborn in ours might so have expressed himself in a modern and intellectualised language … it is in some degree an indication of that which the twentieth century is slowly turning to lay hold of."*

Notice exactly what Whitman does: the speaker is the *Younger*, the divine is the *Elder Brother* — the figure is family-affection, not vassalage. The line that crystallises Whitman's spiritual sense is one in which a child melts into a parent. **This is licence — from Aurobindo — to render the refrain in exactly this family-of-the-spirit register.** "I'm holding on to You" is closer to Whitman than "I have held onto Your hem."

### Model D — **Tagore's English self-translations (*Gitanjali*) as warning AND permission**

Aurobindo writes more about Tagore than about almost anyone in this book. The crucial verdict ("Conclusion"):

> "at the subtlest elevation of all that has yet been reached stands or rather wings and floats in a high intermediate region the poetry of Tagore, not in the complete spiritual light, but amid an air shot with its seekings and glimpses, a sight and cadence found in a psycho-spiritual heaven of subtle and delicate soul experience transmuting the earth tones by the touch of its radiance."

And on the **method**:
> "what is given … is not the formal steps of the Sadhana, but the strongly felt movement and the living outcome … the spirit and power and body of sweetness and beauty and delight."

So: Tagore in English (which is the closest English analogue we have to *this exact translation task* — Bengali devotional lyric → English) works by **bare lyrical present-tense address**: "Thou hast made me endless, such is thy pleasure." "Light, my light, the world-filling light." Short. Vocative. Almost no Latinate words. **Permission** to use *Thou/Thy* if it helps, but only if it is integrated; otherwise modern *You/Your* is fine. Tagore is also the warning: in his weakest Gitanjali pieces he drifts into the very "psycho-spiritual" abstraction that Aurobindo names — "infinite gift," "endless wonder." Do not drift there.

### Models held in reserve (not pressed in proposals below, but available)

- **Christina Rossetti** ("In the bleak midwinter" — "What can I give Him, poor as I am? … I would give my heart") — child-speaker offering the only thing it has to a divine mother/child. Closest English-poetic analogue we have to *v1b*'s defiant little-one register.
- **Hopkins** — Aurobindo does not name him, but Hopkins's "Glory be to God for dappled things" and his "Margaret, are you grieving" both work in compressed, monosyllabic devotional intimacy. Useful for the closing "Koī hor nahī ae merā."
- **George Herbert** — *not* a poet Aurobindo discusses, and his Anglican formality is exactly what we should NOT borrow. Mention only as the trap.

## Part 3 — Specific guidance for *Koi Hor Nahi*

The song is a child speaking to the Mother. Aurobindo's text gives us, surprisingly cleanly, the directive: this is *the* register the future of devotional poetry must recover. Operationalised:

**Do:**
- Use monosyllables wherever a polysyllable would do. *Hold* not *grasp*. *Hand* not *grip*. *Cry* not *lament*. *Mother* not *Goddess*.
- Use the simple present and simple future. No perfect tenses where simple ones will do.
- Use **vocative address**. The child IS talking *to* the Mother in every line. English should make this audible: "Mother, ..." or "..., Mother" — drop the comma-vocative back in wherever the Punjabi has it.
- Use **contractions only sparingly** ("I'm" but probably not "won't") — the register is intimate, not slangy.
- **Let the refrain repeat verbatim.** Twenty-four times if necessary.
- **Trust short lines.** A five-word English line is almost always better than an eight-word "explanation" of it.

**Do NOT:**
- Do not write "May victory always be Yours." Do not write "we shall eventually depart this life." Do not write "we will not return" when the verb is *turn*. These are the failures the user already named — and Aurobindo would name them too as the "rhetorical poetic manner" (P4).
- Do not use "Thy/Thee/Thou" *unless* you can keep it consistent across all twenty-something lines of the song. If you can, it's gorgeous; if you can't, it's worse than nothing. (I lean against, in the proposals below.)
- Do not capitalise mid-sentence words ("Hem," "Skirt") for piety. Capitalise only the pronouns referring to the Mother (Your, You) — which is already the convention in the source data.
- Do not "explain" *darśan* or *jai* or *putt*. Find an English image; *let it carry*.
- Do not vary the refrain for variety. The repetition IS the devotion (P9).
- Do not add adjectives. (See P10.)

**One overarching test.** Read each proposed English line aloud as if a small child, half-sobbing, half-clinging, were saying it to his mother. If your mouth refuses — if the words feel too dressed-up, too grown-up, too explained — reject and re-do. This is the *Aurobindo test*: does the soul hear it?

## Part 4 — Proposed translations for the four lines

For each line: primary rendering (the one I recommend), 2–3 alternates at different settings, and a short rationale grounded in the principles and models above.

### LINE 1 — Invocation
**Punjabi:** *Mā merī sachīyā jotā wālī mātā, terī sadā hī jai*
**Word gloss:** Mother — my — true/genuine/eternal — sacred flames / jyoti — bearer of — Mother Goddess — Your — always — victory/glory/praise
**Current:** *My Mother, O Mother of the true, eternal sacred flames — may victory always be Yours.*

**Primary proposal:**
> *Mother, my own Mother, Keeper of the True Flame — glory to You, always, always.*

**Alternates:**
- (more vocative, less Anglicised) *Maa, my Maa, You who hold the True Flame — glory to You, always.*
- (Tagore-Gitanjali register, simpler) *Mother, my Mother, You bear the eternal flame. To You all glory, now and always.*
- (most spare, closest to a child's voice) *Mother, my Mother. You hold the True Flame. Glory to You. Always.*

**Rationale.** The user named two failures: "My Mother" is clinical (it isn't endearment), and "victory always be Yours" is abstract. Both fixes follow from the principles.

- *Mā merī* → "Mother, my own Mother" — the doubling reproduces the Punjabi's affectionate redundancy (*Mā* then *mātā*) and gives English a way to be tender without baby-talk. (P5 — say it in the inmost way; P7 — reverence is intimate.) Keeping the optional "Maa" version is for the user; "Maa" is a real English-language vocative in diaspora English now, and may be the most honest choice if the rendering is for a primarily diasporic audience.
- *sachīyā jotā wālī* → "Keeper of the True Flame." The current "of the true, eternal sacred flames" piles three adjectives onto one noun. That is exactly Aurobindo's "alankāra" failure (P10): the translator does not trust the noun. "True Flame," capitalised, *is* the eternal/sacred; the capitalisation does the work three adjectives were doing. "Keeper of" reads as title rather than as description — which is what *wālī* actually is in Punjabi: a title. Compare "Lord of the Dance," "Mistress of the Way" — same grammar.
- *terī sadā hī jai* → "glory to You, always, always." The user said the current is "too abstract/general" and that the Punjabi feels "general and personal at the same time." "Glory to You" is a vocative that reaches across — the *to You* is the possessive intimacy he was hearing. The doubled "always, always" reproduces the *sadā hī*'s emphatic particle (*hī*) and gives the line a rhythmic close — also Whitman-ish, also exactly the kind of repetition a child uses when meaning something completely.

### LINE 2 — Refrain
**Punjabi:** *Main laṛ phaṛyā ae terā, koī hor nahī ae merā*
**Word gloss:** I — the hem/edge of Your garment — have grasped/held — is (aux.) — Yours — anyone else — there is not — mine
**Current:** *I have held onto Your hem; I have no one else but You.*

**Primary proposal:**
> *I'm holding on to You — there's no one else for me.*

**Alternates:**
- (keeps the cloth image, but as English speakers actually use it) *I've caught hold of Your saree, Mother — there's no one else for me.*
- (closer to the literal grip, more physical) *My fist is in Your cloth — I have no one but You.*
- (most Whitmanic — the child melting in the arms) *I'm clinging to You — there is no one else, no one but You.*

**Rationale.** This is the line where the user was most specific: *the image is a child grabbing his mother's skirt — concrete, fistful, refusing to let go.* "Held onto Your hem" reads as liturgical for two reasons: "hem" in English is biblical (the woman touching the hem of Christ's garment), and "held onto" is past-perfect, which in English distances. Both failures are addressable.

- **Tense.** The Punjabi *phaṛyā ae* is grammatically a perfect, but its felt sense is the English **present continuous** — the child is grabbing the saree *right now,* and is still grabbing it. English's "have held onto" pushes it into completed past, which sterilises it. "I'm holding on" reads as the action in progress. (P2 — make us see it; P3 — get the rhythm of the lived moment.)
- **Image.** The primary proposal drops the cloth-image altogether ("holding on to You" — bare, child-direct). This is on Aurobindo's permission (Model C, Whitman: "the Younger melts in fondness in his arms" — no garment, just the body of the parent). The user described the gesture as *a child grabbing something of his mother's*; in English what a child grabs is the mother *herself*, or her *leg* / *hand* / *dress* / *saree*. "Hem" is not a word any English-speaking child says, ever. The alternates retain the cloth if the user wants it: "saree" is the natural English-via-diaspora word and immediately produces the picture of *laṛ*. "Fist in Your cloth" is the most physically faithful to the Punjabi.
- **Second half.** "I have no one else but You" reads stiff because of "no one else but" — that's three small words doing the work of one. "There's no one else for me" is the way the sentence falls in real speech — and *for me* recovers the possessive intimacy of *merā* that "but You" loses. (P5.)

**On the refrain repeating verbatim.** Whichever version is chosen, it must appear word-for-word identical every one of the ~24 times the refrain occurs (P9). Do not let a future editor "vary it for English flow." The Punjabi singer sings it identical every time; so must the English.

### LINE 3 — Verse 1a
**Punjabi:** *Asī sāh chhaḍ jāvānge*
**Word gloss:** We — breath — letting go — we will go
**Current:** *We will eventually let go of our breath.*

**Primary proposal:**
> *We'll stop breathing one day.*

**Alternates:**
- (most direct, child-blunt) *One day we'll stop breathing.*
- (slightly more elevated, but still bare) *Our breath will go.*
- (temporal sub-clause — sets up v1b's "and then…") *When our breath goes…*

**Rationale.** The user's complaint was strictly grammatical: *"It's saying 'we will stop breathing.' Or 'when we stop breathing.' You have to get the grammar right."* He's correct. *Sāh chhaḍ jāvānge* in Punjabi is a future event, not a long process; "we will eventually let go" reads as if the speakers are slowly choosing to die. The image is simpler: *one day our breath stops*.

- The primary uses the future contraction "we'll" — keeps the child-conversational tone (P8) and avoids the over-formal "We shall."
- "Stop breathing" is the literal English of *sāh chhaḍ jāvānge* and it has the right awful matter-of-factness — a child can say "one day we'll stop breathing" the way a child says "one day Daddy will die" — flat, simple, terrifying because not elaborated. (P2 — make us see; P5 — the inmost way; P10 — trust the bare verb.)
- The "When our breath goes…" alternate is structurally appealing because the Punjabi *v1a* and *v1b* are syntactically joined (*X*; *then Y*). If the user wants the two-line couplet to feel like one sentence in English, choose this alternate and join it to v1b's primary.

### LINE 4 — Verse 1b
**Punjabi:** *Pher bājā māroge, asī muṛ nahīyo āvānge*
**Word gloss:** Then — cries/calls (loud sound) — You will strike/raise — we — back / again — not (emphatic) — we will come
**Current:** *Then You will call out to us, but we will not return.*

**Primary proposal:**
> *Then You'll cry out for us — and we won't turn around.*

**Alternates:**
- (louder, closer to *bājā*'s percussive force) *Then You'll be calling and calling for us — we won't turn back.*
- (most childlike-defiant — the user's "child exalting") *Then You'll shout — and we still won't turn round.*
- (most spare and most ritual-feeling) *Then You will cry for us. We will not turn.*

**Rationale.** The user named two specific corrections: *bājā māroge* is not "call out" — it is **loud, struck, raised** ("Bāj is loud — cry, scream. Mār = strike"). And *muṛ nahīyo āvānge* is "we will not **turn around**," not "we will not return" — the image is a child stamping away with his back to the mother, refusing to look back. That's "childlike defiance, a child exalting." Both are corrected:

- *bājā māroge* → "cry out" is a defensible English compound for a loud raised voice (cf. *cry out in pain, cry out the name*), and the verb "cry" carries the mother's emotion (she is crying out for the child *because she loves him and the child has died*) without explanation. The "shout" alternate is more percussive; "be calling and calling" reproduces *māroge*'s iterative force. (P2, P10.)
- *muṛ nahīyo āvānge* → "and we won't turn around" — this is the user's correction taken straight. The English idiom for the gesture is "turn around" (or "turn back," or just "turn"). Critically, *turn around* in English means the bodily act of pivoting — exactly the child's gesture. *Return* in English is abstract and bureaucratic and lost the gesture entirely. (P5, P6.)
- The em-dash between the two clauses replicates the *pher … asī …* hinge in the Punjabi: cause/effect, but also pause-for-emphasis. The child says "then You'll cry —" and pauses, and then twists the knife: "— and we won't turn around." (P3.)

**Childlike defiance: a note.** The user said this is "a child exalting." I would resist the temptation to make this funny or cute in English. The Punjabi has zero irony — the child means it. The English should not wink at the reader. The em-dash and the bare *won't* do the entire job. Do not add "no, we won't!" or "not us!" — that would be the babytalk failure (P8). The flat, stubborn future tense is the entire defiance.

## Appendix — what I did not do

- I did not retranslate the rest of the song (v2 through outro/closing). The user asked for the four lines named in his brief. The same principles apply to the rest, and several of the existing renderings are already strong (e.g. "*putt* — a deeply tender word in Punjabi" → "son" is correct and bare).
- I did not propose alternative typographic treatments (italics, line breaks, etc.) for the rendered HTML. That is a separate question.
- I did not propose changing the Romanisation or word-mapping infrastructure. The `{n:phrase}` slot system in `assets/song.js` will need each chosen translation to be re-slotted by hand; the proposals above are written so that the slot-mapping is straightforward.
